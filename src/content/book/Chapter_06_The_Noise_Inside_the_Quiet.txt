Chapter 6 - The Noise Inside the Quiet

Silence isn't quiet. Anyone who's been alone at 3 AM knows this. The rest of the world shuts up and your skull gets louder, like the noise was always there and the daytime was just drowning it out.

I used to think the problem was the bad days—the ones where getting vertical felt like negotiating a hostage situation with my own nervous system. But the bad days have a clarity to them. You know where you stand. The ground is gone and you're falling and at least falling is a direction.

It's the quiet days that'll kill you. The ones where nothing is specifically wrong, where the dogs are fed and the bills are paid and nobody's dead today, and your brain decides this is the perfect moment to open every file cabinet it owns and dump the contents on the floor.

*»You've had this day. The one where everything is technically fine and you're somehow worse than when everything was on fire. Tell me I'm wrong.«*

---

Three in the morning. The air purifier wheezes its mechanical lullaby from the corner. Badge is snoring at the foot of the bed, twitching through some dream where he's probably chasing something he'll never catch—lucky bastard, even his subconscious has clear objectives. Huck is wedged against my ribs like he's trying to hold me together through osmosis.

And my brain is running a highlight reel of every mistake I've ever made. Not the big ones. Not Riley. Not the things that actually broke me. No, the OCD saves those for special occasions. Tonight it's the B-roll. The throwaway footage.

That thing I said to a coworker in 2009. The exact words. The exact inflection. The way her face changed—did it change? Did I imagine the change? I need to know precisely whether her expression shifted or whether I'm manufacturing a reaction that didn't happen, and I need to know right now, at 3:17 AM, fourteen years after the fact, or I will not be permitted to sleep.

That's not hyperbole. That's the machinery.

The OCD doesn't do "probably." Doesn't do "close enough." Doesn't do "let it go, man, she almost certainly doesn't remember this and neither should you." It does exact. It does complete. It does solve this with total precision or I will keep the lights on in here all night, and by lights I mean the interrogation kind, and by here I mean the inside of your skull.

---

People think OCD is about hand-washing. About straight lines and locked doors and alphabetized spice racks. And sure, sometimes it wears that costume. But the version that lives in my head isn't interested in organizing my pantry. It's interested in organizing reality. Every piece of it. Down to the molecular level. Before I'm allowed to do anything else.

Trauma hands it the raw material. Here's a catalog of every time something went wrong, every person who left, every moment the floor disappeared. And the OCD says thank you, I'll need to cross-reference all of this before we can proceed.

Proceed with what?

Anything. Everything. Getting out of bed. Making coffee. Answering a text from someone who just wants to know if I'm alive.

*»When's the last time you responded to a simple text without drafting it three times in your head first? When's the last time "hey, how are you" didn't feel like a trap with a wrong answer?«*

The morning light comes through blinds I haven't adjusted in months. Not because I can't reach them. Because the moment I think about adjusting them, the gears engage. What angle. How much light. Whether the new position will create a glare on the TV that I'll notice later and need to fix again. And now adjusting the blinds isn't adjusting the blinds anymore—it's a project, a commitment, a chain of decisions that terminates somewhere around heat transfer calculations and I haven't even put my feet on the floor yet.

So the blinds stay. And I stay. And the light does whatever it wants.

---

Sad country music playing from the kitchen speaker. On a loop. The landing. The corner. The three steps. My body knowing before my brain caught up.

The OCD cataloged that morning with the precision of a crime scene photographer. Every detail. The things that were wrong. The things that were missing. The things that should never have been there. It filed all of it in high definition under "play on repeat without warning," and that's exactly what it does. At 3 AM. At noon. In the middle of a conversation. In the shower. While I'm trying to decide what to eat for dinner and suddenly I'm not in my kitchen anymore, I'm on that landing, and the music is playing, and—

This is the noise inside the quiet.

Not the memory itself—I could handle the memory if it just showed up, did its damage, and left. It's the way my brain won't let it just be a memory. It has to be examined. Verified. Cross-referenced. Was every detail exactly right? Was the song exactly that song? Because if any element isn't precisely accurate, then the whole memory needs to be reconstructed from scratch, and we need to go through it again. Carefully this time.

Again.

*»You don't need me to explain what this does to a person. You already know. Maybe not this specific machinery, but you know what it's like when your own mind won't let you put something down.«*

---

Justin died on a Tuesday. Or a Wednesday. I think it was a Tuesday but I'm not completely sure anymore, and that uncertainty—that tiny gap between what I know and what I can verify—sits in my chest like a splinter I can't reach.

Most people would let that go. Tuesday, Wednesday, what difference does it make? He's gone either way. But my brain doesn't have a "what difference does it make" setting. It has "resolve this or I will keep circling it like a dog that can't find a comfortable position on the bed, except the bed is your consciousness and the dog never sleeps."

I spent an evening once trying to reconstruct the exact timeline. Not because it mattered. Not because anyone was asking. But because the not-knowing had become its own kind of noise, a low hum underneath everything else, and I thought if I could just pin down the day—Tuesday or Wednesday, which was it—the hum would stop.

It didn't stop. Because once I confirmed the day, I needed to confirm the time. And once I had the time, I needed to verify what I was doing when I got the call. And once I reconstructed that, I needed to know exactly what the nurse said, word for word, and whether my memory of her voice was accurate or whether I'd layered something over it in the years since—some emotional filter that changed the tone from clinical to kind or kind to clinical, and which version was true?

This is what the OCD does with grief. It takes the rawest, most human thing you can feel and turns it into a research project with no due date and no passing grade. You can't just miss someone. You have to miss them correctly. Precisely. With full documentation.

---

The quiet hours are the worst because there's nothing to compete with the noise. During the day, the world provides interference—obligations, conversations, the dogs demanding walks, the basic friction of existing around other humans. The signal gets jammed. Not silenced, not even quieted, but at least buried under enough static that you can function.

But at night, the static clears. And the signal comes through clean and loud and relentless.

I've tried everything. Podcasts. Audiobooks. TV shows I've seen enough times that they function as white noise. Music, though music is dangerous because certain songs are tripwires and I don't always remember which ones until I'm already falling. I've fallen asleep to the same nature documentary about deep-sea creatures probably three hundred times because David Attenborough's voice doesn't remind me of anyone who's dead and the anglerfish don't have any unresolved emotional significance.

Yet.

Give the OCD time. It'll find a way to make the anglerfish about something.

*»What's your version of this? What's the thing you play on repeat to drown out the thing that plays on repeat? What's your David Attenborough?«*

---

People tell you to meditate. To sit with the silence. To breathe through it.

And I want to grab them by the collar and say: I am sitting with the silence. The silence is deafening. The silence is a room full of voices all talking at once, all demanding answers to questions that don't have answers, all insisting on precision in a universe that doesn't do precise. Telling me to sit with the silence is like telling someone in a hurricane to sit with the breeze.

Therapy helps, sometimes. But there's a specific frustration in trying to explain this to someone whose brain has a dimmer switch. Their thoughts get loud and they turn them down. They have a technique for it. A breathing exercise. A grounding practice. Name five things you can see.

I can see five things. I can see five thousand things. I can see the dust mote traveling through the beam of light from the window and I need to know whether it's moving left or right and whether that direction means something and whether the movement pattern is consistent with the HVAC system operating normally or whether something is wrong with the filter and when was the last time I changed the filter and was it exactly three months ago like the package recommended or was it closer to four and does that difference matter and—

That's not a thought I can turn down. That's a machine I can't turn off.

---

The whiteboard hangs crooked on my wall. Some days I look at it and the tasks feel possible—not easy, not simple, but like doors I could walk through if I committed to not looking back. Other days I look at it and each task is an entrance to another hallway of calculations I'll never finish.

The OCD doesn't care that the tasks are simple. Simple is irrelevant. A glass of water is simple until you need to verify the exact temperature, the precise mineral content, whether the ice has melted to the correct ratio, whether the glass itself is the right glass or whether using this glass means something about your mental state that needs to be analyzed before you can drink.

You think I'm exaggerating. I'm not. I wish I were. I wish this was a bit, something I'm hamming up for effect, something that sounds worse on paper than it is in practice. But the truth is I'm actually underselling it, because the real version—the full, unedited, unabridged version of what happens inside my head between the moment I decide to drink water and the moment water enters my mouth—would take pages. And by the time I finished writing it, the OCD would need me to go back and verify that every word was precisely accurate.

---

There was a night, maybe six months after Riley, when the noise got so loud I drove to the gym at 2 AM. Not my gym—that was closed. A 24-hour place off the highway, fluorescent-lit, populated by exactly the kind of people you'd expect at a 24-hour gym at 2 AM: insomniacs, night-shift workers, and one guy who clearly lived there and was using the locker room as his primary residence.

I didn't work out. I sat in my car in the parking lot for forty-five minutes with the engine running, trying to decide whether going inside was the right decision. Not in the normal way people weigh decisions—pros and cons, do I feel like it, will it help. In the way where my brain needed to know, with certainty, that walking through that door would produce a specific result, and it couldn't know that, because you can't know that, because the future doesn't come with footnotes, and so I sat there running scenarios while the gas gauge dropped and the night got colder.

I did eventually go in. Did some half-assed work on the heavy bag. Drove home. Sat in my driveway for another twenty minutes processing whether the trip had been worth the gas money, whether the workout had been sufficient to qualify as exercise, whether I should have stayed longer, whether staying longer would have changed anything or just moved the noise to a different room.

The noise came home with me. It always comes home with me.

*»You're waiting for the part where I tell you what finally worked. What technique or breakthrough or moment of clarity cut through the static. I don't have that part. I have a parking lot and a heavy bag and a brain that won't quit.«*

---

The cruelest trick of the OCD is that it disguises itself as thoroughness. As responsibility. As the voice of reason insisting on due diligence. You can't tell someone your brain won't let you make a decision without them nodding sympathetically and saying "I'm like that too, I'm such a perfectionist!" As if what I'm describing is a personality quirk. A charming flaw. Something you'd list on a dating profile between "loves dogs" and "can cook."

This isn't perfectionism. Perfectionism is wanting things to be good. This is needing things to be exactly right before your nervous system will stand down, and "exactly right" is a target that moves every time you get close to it, and your nervous system never stands down, and you're exhausted in a way that sleep can't touch because the exhaustion isn't physical—it's the fatigue of a mind that's been running computations nonstop since you were old enough to think.

I remember being seven. Maybe eight. Lying in bed unable to sleep because I needed to remember, with total accuracy, every word of a conversation I'd had at recess. Not because anything important happened. Nothing happened. But my brain had flagged it for review and review meant complete reconstruction, word by word, gesture by gesture, and any gap in the record was unacceptable and I would lie there, a little kid in the dark, trying to remember whether Tyler said "cool" or "awesome" because the difference mattered to a part of my brain that I didn't yet know had a clinical name.

I thought everyone did this. Thought every kid lay awake reconstructing their day with forensic precision. Thought the noise was normal. That every mind was this loud and everyone just dealt with it.

Finding out they don't—that most people can actually just stop thinking about something—was like finding out everyone else had been breathing underwater this whole time and nobody told me I was the only one drowning.

---

Dani used to text me late. Not for any particular reason—she was a night person, and I was a person who was always awake, and we existed in that overlap the way two insomniacs share a waiting room. No agenda. No expectations. Just two people awake at an hour when most people aren't, saying whatever came to mind.

The OCD was quieter when she texted. Not silent—it's never silent—but the volume dropped, like the signal found some interference it couldn't overpower. I don't know if it was her specifically or just the fact of another human consciousness reaching through the dark to say I'm here, are you there, what are you thinking about?

I'm thinking about whether the exact shade of blue in the sky today matched the shade from yesterday and whether the difference means the atmosphere is changing in a way I should be concerned about. But I'd text back something normal. Something human. Something that didn't sound like the inside of my head looks.

She never knew about that. About the translation work happening between what I thought and what I typed. About the noise she was accidentally quieting just by being awake at the same time. I never told her. And now I can't.

The OCD has opinions about this, obviously. Runs the analysis nightly. Should I have told her? Would it have changed something? Was there a precise sequence of words that would have altered the outcome? It presents these questions as solvable. As though somewhere in the data there's an answer I haven't found yet, and if I just think hard enough, thoroughly enough, I'll crack the code of a conversation I'll never get to have.

---

Here's what the noise sounds like at 3 AM, if I'm being honest. Not a metaphor. Not a description dressed up in literary clothing. Just the raw feed:

Did I lock the door. I locked the door. Did I check the lock or just think about checking the lock. There's a difference. Get up and check. But if I get up to check I'll need to verify that the checking was real and not imagined. What if I check and then doubt the check. Then I'll need to check again. And again. Until checking becomes its own loop and the door was never the point.

Is Ripley breathing normally. Count the breaths. Was that a normal interval between breaths. What's normal. Look it up. But if I look it up I'll fall into a research spiral about canine respiratory rates and then I'll need to compare Ripley's rate to the median and if it's off by any margin I'll need to decide whether that margin warrants a vet visit and it's 3 AM and the vet isn't open and now I have a new thing to hold until morning.

Riley would have been forty-two this year. Was he thirty when he died or thirty-one. I should know this. I should know this with certainty. He was my best friend. What kind of person doesn't remember the exact age their best friend was when—

And that one. That one right there. That's the one that draws blood.

Because the OCD doesn't distinguish between checking a lock and reconstructing the details of the worst night of your life. It applies the same standard to both. Total accuracy or we're not done here. And some things aren't meant to be processed with that kind of precision. Some things are meant to be approximate, blurry, held loosely in the place where grief lives without sharp edges. But my brain doesn't have that place. My brain has filing cabinets and they demand exact labels and the labels have to be right.

---

The dogs shift in their sleep. Badge stretches, groans, resettles. The air purifier continues its asthmatic hymn. Outside, the world is doing what the world does at 3 AM: nothing. Holding its breath.

I'm not holding mine. I'm counting it. In and out. Trying not to catalog the duration of each inhale, the pause at the top, the length of the exhale, the ratio between them. Trying not to turn breathing into another equation.

Sometimes I manage. Sometimes the noise thins enough that I can hear the actual quiet underneath—the kind that most people get to live in every day, the kind that's genuinely empty, genuinely still. Those moments last seconds, maybe. A few breaths where the machinery idles and I get to just exist without annotation.

Then the gears catch again. And the noise comes back. And I'm right back in it, awake in the dark, solving problems that don't have solutions, answering questions that don't have answers, trying to reach a standard of certainty that doesn't exist anywhere outside of my own impossible demands.

*»I'd tell you it gets easier but I'm not going to lie to you at 3 AM. That's the one time of day lying feels pointless.«*

The noise doesn't stop. It doesn't thin with time or soften with practice or respond to the well-meaning suggestions of people whose brains have a volume knob. It just continues, running its program, doing its work, building its case for why nothing can ever be left alone, why nothing can ever be good enough, why every thought must be chased to its end and every end must be verified and every verification must be verified again.

And you learn to live in it. Not with it—that implies some kind of peace, some accommodation, some handshake deal between you and the noise. You live in it the way you live in weather. It's just there. It's always there. And sometimes it's a drizzle you can walk through and sometimes it's a storm that pins you to the floor and you never know which one you're getting until you open your eyes in the morning and listen for how loud the room is.

Tonight, it's loud.

The ceiling holds its familiar shadows. The dogs breathe their simple, unanalyzed breaths. The air purifier wheezes. And my brain keeps going, keeps churning, keeps demanding answers to questions that exist only because it asked them.

I don't fight it anymore. Fighting it is just more noise. I just lie here, in the noise inside the quiet, and wait for the part of the night where exhaustion finally outweighs precision and my mind lets me slip under for a few hours before it starts again.

That's not peace. That's not healing. That's barely even a strategy.

But it's 3 AM, and it's all I've got.
