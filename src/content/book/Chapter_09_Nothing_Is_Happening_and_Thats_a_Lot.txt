PART III: The Weight of Waiting
(Stagnation, liminal space, nothing happening and that being a lot)

Chapter 9 - Nothing Is Happening, and That's a Lot

Nothing is happening.

I need you to understand the specific weight of that sentence. Not "I'm taking a break" nothing. Not "I'm recharging" nothing. Not any of the curated, Instagram-worthy nothings that people post about with a candle and a book and a caption about self-care. This is the other kind. The kind where your system has shut down to minimum power and you can feel it running — just barely, like a computer in sleep mode, the little light pulsing to prove it hasn't completely died — but you can't access any of the programs. Can't open any of the files. Can't do anything except watch the light pulse and know that somewhere inside the machine, something is technically still happening, even though it doesn't look like anything from the outside and it sure as shit doesn't feel like anything from the inside.

I disappeared.

Not dramatically. Not in a way that made the news or warranted a search party or even a concerned phone call. I just… stopped. Stopped answering. Stopped showing up. Stopped participating in the endless exchange of communication and obligation and basic human reciprocity that keeps people connected to each other. I withdrew into this house, into this room, into this bed, and I waited for the world to notice.

It didn't.

The phone didn't ring. The door didn't knock. The texts didn't come. I lay there in the dark, day after day, and the silence stretched out around me like water filling a hole, and nobody — nobody — came looking. Not because they're bad people. Not because they don't care in whatever abstract way people care about the idea of you. But because the world adapts to absence faster than you'd think. You stop showing up and people route around you like water flowing around a stone, and within weeks it's like the stone was never there at all. The stream looks the same. The water doesn't remember.
»You've tested this, haven't you? Not consciously, maybe. But you've gone quiet — stopped texting first, stopped reaching out, stopped being the one who maintains the connection — just to see what would happen. Just to see if anyone would notice the gap. And now you know. The answer is the silence you're sitting in right now.«
That silence is its own kind of violence. Not the sharp kind — not a blow or a betrayal or anything you can point to and say that's what hurt me. It's the violence of irrelevance. The slow, quiet confirmation that you were right about yourself all along. Every voice that ever told you you were nothing, every frequency that carried the weight of not-wanted, every kitchen table courtroom that found you guilty of existing — they were all telling the truth. The evidence is in. The verdict is the empty phone. The sentence is this room.

I wanted to close the gap. I need to be honest about that. Not in the past tense, not in the safely retrospective way that implies it's over and I've moved past it. I wanted to actually not exist. To finish the equation that started in the kitchen with the linoleum and the light fixture and the word "too dumb" and continued through every loss, every departure, every person who left or died or simply stopped showing up. The through-line was clear: subtract yourself and the world loses nothing. The math was clean. The conclusion was obvious.

But I couldn't even do that.

And that's the thing that breaks you in a way none of the other things do — not the losses, not the silence, not the absent phone, not even the voice that says you shouldn't exist. It's the discovery that you can't live and you can't die and you're stuck in between, in this gap that has no name and no exit and no resolution, wanting to close it from either direction and not having the energy to move either way. Too tired to live. Too tired to die. Just lying there in the exact center of nothing, which turns out to be the heaviest place in the universe.
»If you know exactly what this gap feels like — the specific weight of wanting to stop existing but not being able to stop existing — then we don't need to explain it to each other. We just know. And if you don't know, I hope to God you never learn. Because it's the loneliest coordinate on any map, and there's no one there to find you.«
The days blur. That's not a metaphor — they literally blur. Tuesday could be Saturday could be three weeks ago. The only time markers are the dogs' internal clocks, which are more reliable than mine. They know when it's feeding time to within about fifteen minutes, which means they have a better relationship with the structure of reality than I do. They need to eat. I'm the one with thumbs. So I get up. Fill three bowls. Stand there watching them eat with their perfect, uncomplicated focus, their whole world narrowed to the thing in front of them, and I think: that used to be enough. The dogs used to be the reason. "Nobody could give them what I give them," I told myself. "Nobody would take care of them the way I do."

And then even that turned. Because the voice doesn't leave anything alone. It took the one thing tethering me to the planet and made it into more evidence: "They deserve so much better than what you've been giving them. Look at you. Look at what their owner has become. They deserve someone who can walk them, play with them, be present for them — not this. Not a body in a dark room that can barely manage to pour kibble into a bowl."

Even the dogs became proof I was failing.

The ceiling has a water stain in the corner from when the roof leaked. A nearly perfect circle where a smoke detector used to be. A crack that runs like a fault line toward the window, getting a little wider every winter. I know these things the way most people know their own face — from the kind of long, repeated, involuntary study that only happens when you spend enough mornings, afternoons, and nights unable to look anywhere else. I've counted the stains. Seventeen. Counted them twice, sometimes three times, because my brain demands the number be exact before it will permit any other thought, and by the time the count is settled I've lost another hour to a ceiling I could draw from memory with my eyes closed.

Time becomes elastic in stagnation, following rules that have nothing to do with clocks or calendars. Minutes stretch like taffy while hours disappear without evidence. You look up from your phone — the same three apps, scrolled in the same order, Instagram Reddit Instagram, a thumb migration that goes nowhere but at least involves a body part doing something — and realize six hours have passed. Six hours of moving your thumb across a screen, watching other people's lives happen in bright squares while yours contracts to the dimensions of a mattress and a ceiling and a phone that doesn't ring.

The treatment cycle happens in here somewhere. Outpatient, then inpatient — six weeks of residential where they cracked me open, where therapists who actually knew what they were doing got past the part of me that had been running the show for thirty-five years and found the parts underneath that had been waiting that long to be heard. Then back to outpatient. The PTSD treatment hokey-pokey. You put your trauma in, you pull your trauma out. And you keep ending up in the same waiting room with the same magazines from three years ago. But the treatment happens and then it ends and then you're back here. In this room. With this ceiling. In this nothing.

The house is quiet. Not peaceful quiet — minefield quiet. Every genre of music is mapped to someone I've lost. Country belongs to Riley. There are songs that belong to Dani. So the house stays silent. The air purifier hums. The dogs breathe. David Attenborough sometimes, because penguins don't have unresolved emotional significance. That's the soundtrack of nothing. That's what it sounds like when a person disappears and the world doesn't notice and the only sound in the room is a machine filtering air that nobody else is breathing.

I scroll through the same three apps. Not looking for anything. Not engaging with anything. Just moving my thumb in a pattern that approximates participation in the world without requiring any. The same posts. The same faces. People getting married, having babies, going on trips, posting sunsets and gym selfies and the clean, bright, forward-moving version of life that I watch the way you watch fish in an aquarium — it's right there and I'm separated from it by glass I can't break.
»When's the last time you actually knew what day it was without checking your phone? When's the last time you ate a meal that wasn't whatever you could reach from your bed? When's the last time you felt like a person instead of a collection of half-finished thoughts and abandoned plans? Don't answer. We both know.«
The shame sits where it always sits — heavy in the chest, wet cement that hardens a little more each day you don't move. What kind of person lies in bed in the middle of the afternoon counting water stains? What kind of adult can disappear completely and have nobody notice? The shame wants you to believe you're uniquely pathetic. That everyone else has figured out the basic mechanics of being alive and you're the only defective unit on the assembly line. But the shame is also the only thing that acknowledges you exist, which is more than the silent phone is doing, so you sit with it. You let it stay. Because even shame is company when the alternative is nothing.

Nothing is happening. And that sentence has more weight than anything I've written in this book so far. Because at least the other chapters had stories — Riley, Vanuatu, the gym, the kitchen with the linoleum. Those were things that happened. This chapter is about the thing that happens when nothing happens. The slow, heavy, unremarkable experience of existing in a holding pattern that nobody would call living but that your body insists on continuing because bodies are stubborn and hearts are relentless and lungs don't know how to read the room.

I think about the gap. The space between existing and not existing. How narrow it is. How wide it is. How it changes depending on the day, the hour, the particular quality of the silence. Some days the gap is a canyon I couldn't cross if I wanted to. Some days it's a crack in the sidewalk I could step over without trying. The terrifying days are the ones where I can't tell which one it is — where the distance between here and not-here feels ambiguous, negotiable, like a number that keeps changing depending on how you round it.

The crack in the ceiling gets a little wider every winter. I watch it the way you'd watch a slow leak — knowing it's getting worse, knowing eventually it'll become a problem someone has to deal with, knowing that someone is me and I'm not dealing with anything right now except the basic physics of remaining horizontal while conscious. I wonder sometimes if that's what's happening to me. If I'm the crack. Getting a little wider every winter. Following the path of least resistance. Getting worse at a pace that's easy to ignore because who's measuring? The ceiling doesn't file reports on its own deterioration. Neither do I.

Nothing is happening. And that's a lot. More than a lot. That's the full weight of being alive compressed into this single, endless moment where time moves like honey and every breath feels negotiable and the phone doesn't ring and the door doesn't knock and the only proof that I exist at all is a water stain I've memorized, a crack I'm becoming, and three dogs who need to eat whether I deserve to feed them or not.

That's the weight of waiting. Not the waiting itself. The knowing that you're the only one who can end it, and you can't. The knowing that nobody's coming. The knowing that the silence isn't a pause — it's the answer.

And still, somehow, being here. In the nothing. In the weight of it. Not because I chose to stay but because leaving takes something I don't have and staying takes nothing at all, and nothing is the only thing I've got in surplus.

So I stay.

And the ceiling holds its seventeen stains. And the crack gets wider. And the dogs breathe. And the phone stays silent.

And nothing keeps happening.

And it weighs everything.
