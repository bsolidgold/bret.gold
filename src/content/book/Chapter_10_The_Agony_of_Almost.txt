Chapter 10 - The Agony of Almost

There’s a particular kind of torture that lives in the space between your fingertips and what you’re reaching for. Not the clean break of missing entirely, not the solid impact of failure that at least gives you something to push against. This is different. This is the brush of skin against possibility, the moment your fingers graze what you need before it slips away, leaving you with the phantom sensation of what almost was.

Almost is a special kind of hell that nobody writes self-help books about.
»You know this feeling, don’t you? The way almost sits in your throat like a stone you can’t swallow or spit out. The way it makes you replay that moment over and over, each time ending exactly where it always does - one breath short of enough.«
I almost saved Riley.

Not in some dramatic, movie-moment way where I burst through the door at the last second. No, I mean I almost saw it coming clearly enough to do something. Almost recognized the signs for what they were instead of what I wanted them to be. Almost asked the right questions. Almost pushed past his deflections. Almost, almost, almost.

The night before, we’d been in the kitchen. He was making something - probably one of those healthy grain bowls he was always perfecting, the kind where everything was precisely measured and arranged. He took care of himself that way, at least physically. Made it even harder to see what was happening underneath. I was bitching about something at work - can’t even remember what now, isn’t that fucked up? Here’s the last real conversation I had with my best friend and I wasted it on some forgettable workplace drama.

He said something. Just a throwaway comment, the kind that slides past you in normal conversation. Something about being tired. Something about things being easier soon. Something that, in retrospect, was him saying goodbye in the only way he knew how.
I almost heard it.
I felt something shift in the room, like the air pressure changed for just a second. That animal part of your brain that knows when something’s wrong before your conscious mind catches up. I looked at him, really looked at him for maybe three seconds. Saw something in his eyes that made my stomach drop.
And then I let it go.
Because that’s what we do with almost-knowing, isn’t it? We negotiate with our instincts, rationalize away the alarm bells, tell ourselves we’re being paranoid. We choose the comfort of maybe-I’m-wrong over the discomfort of what-if-I’m-right.
“You good?” I asked.
“Yeah, man. Just tired.”
Almost. I almost pushed. Almost said “No, really, are you okay?” Almost sat down at that kitchen table and refused to move until he told me the truth. Almost grabbed his shoulders and shook him until all the words he was swallowing came tumbling out.
Instead, I said, “Get some sleep,” and went downstairs.
Twelve hours later, I found him.
»That familiar nausea just hit you. The way your body remembers every almost-save, every almost-catch, every moment you could have changed everything if you’d just moved a little faster, pushed a little harder, seen a little clearer.«
The thing about almost is that it doesn’t fade like regular failure. Failure has clean edges - you tried, you failed, you move on. But almost? Almost has hooks. Almost has teeth. Almost keeps you suspended in that moment just before the fall, playing it over and over, each replay showing you exactly how close you were, exactly how little would have needed to change.
With Justin, it was different but the same. I almost convinced him to let me manage his prescriptions. We had the conversation, sitting in my living room, him fidgeting with the label on his beer bottle, me trying to find the right words that wouldn’t sound like judgment.
“Just until you get through this rough patch,” I said.
He almost said yes. I saw it in his face - that moment of relief when someone offers to carry part of your weight. For maybe ten seconds, he was going to let me help. I could see him doing the math, weighing his pride against his exhaustion, his need for control against his need for support.
Then his phone buzzed. His girlfriend. The moment broke.
“Nah, man. Iva’s got it. We have a system.”
Almost. He almost let me in. I almost became the barrier between him and the pills that would kill him. Almost changed the trajectory of everything that came after.
The system didn’t work. Iva loved him, but love isn’t always enough when you’re wrestling someone else’s demons. She’d hide the pills, he’d find them. She’d ration them out, he’d convince her he needed more. The dance of addiction and enabling, each step perfectly choreographed to end exactly where it did - with me standing in a hospital room, watching machines breathe for someone who was already gone.
»Go ahead. Name yours. The conversation you almost had. The hand you almost reached for. The truth you almost told. You don’t have to say it out loud — just let it surface. It’s already there anyway, isn’t it? It’s always there.«
Almost-moments become a special kind of haunting. They don’t visit like regular memories - clean and contained, filed away in the past where they belong. They seep into everything, coloring every decision, every interaction, every moment of hesitation with the weight of what almost was.
I almost answered Riley’s last text immediately. It came through at 11:47 PM, two nights before he died. Just “you up?” - the kind of message that could mean anything or nothing. I typed a response, then deleted it. Typed another, deleted that too. Told myself I’d reply in the morning when I could give him my full attention.
The morning never came. Not for him.
The response I never sent sits in my head like a splinter: “Yeah, what’s going on?” Five words. Seventeen characters. Maybe enough to start a conversation that could have changed everything. Maybe not. That’s the torture of almost - you never get to know.
This is what almost does to your mind: it creates an infinite loop of alternate endings, each one so close to possible that you can taste it. You become a mathematician of catastrophe, calculating and recalculating the angles of approach, the velocity of intervention, the exact force needed to change an outcome that’s already carved in stone.
If I had left work five minutes earlier that day… If I had pushed harder when he deflected… If I had recognized the signs for what they were… If, if, if, almost, almost, almost.
The geography of almost is measured in millimeters and microseconds. The space between your hand and theirs when you almost reached out. The pause between their question and your almost-honest answer. The fraction of a moment when you almost chose differently, almost moved faster, almost saw clearer.
But almost doesn’t count. Almost doesn’t save anyone. Almost just leaves you suspended in that space between what was and what could have been, trapped in the amber of a moment that will never resolve.
»You’re trying to breathe through it right now, aren’t you? That specific weight that almost puts on your chest. Not the clean pain of loss, but the jagged edge of proximity. Of being so close you could feel the heat of a different outcome.«
My phone holds a museum of almosts. Unsent messages that almost said what needed saying. Half-dialed numbers when I almost called. Photos I almost took on what turned out to be last times. Each one a monument to the moments when I stood at the edge of action and chose hesitation instead.
There’s a voicemail from Justin that I almost deleted a hundred times but couldn’t. It’s from two weeks before he died, his voice slurred but warm, telling me about some joke he’d heard that he’s too fucked up to remember properly. At the end, right before he hangs up, there’s this pause. This breath. This space where he almost says something else.
I’ve listened to that pause a thousand times. Trying to decode what lived in that silence. What almost got said. What might have changed everything if it had found its way into words.
The pause lasts 2.7 seconds. I know because I’ve timed it. 2.7 seconds of almost that I carry like a stone in my pocket, smooth from all the times I’ve turned it over, examining it from every angle, looking for the message that almost made it through.
Almost-grief is different from regular grief. Regular grief has stages, processes, a general trajectory toward something that might eventually resemble acceptance. But almost-grief? That stays fresh. That keeps you locked in the moment just before the ending, unable to move forward because some part of you is still trying to reach back and grab what slipped through your fingers.
The worst part about almost is how it makes you doubt every instinct, every decision, every moment of action or inaction that comes after. Because if you almost saved them but didn’t, if you almost helped but couldn’t, if you almost saw it coming but missed it - how can you trust yourself with anything ever again?
»Your throat is closing up. Because this is the real agony of almost - not just the loss, but the way it makes you question every choice that comes after. The way it turns you into someone who second-guesses every instinct, who hesitates when they should move, who moves when they should wait.«
I almost didn’t write this chapter.
Almost convinced myself that nobody wants to read about the distances we fail to close, the reaches that fall short, the saves that don’t quite save. Almost decided it was too heavy, too dark, too much truth for a world that prefers its survival stories with triumphant endings.
But then I remembered all the other people carrying their own almosts. All the reaching that fell short. All the moments that live in that space between success and failure, suspended forever in the amber of what could have been.
And I thought maybe, almost certainly, someone needs to say this:
Your almosts count. Even if they didn’t change the outcome. Even if they fell short. Even if they left you with nothing but the ache of proximity. They count because they were attempts. Because they were reaches. Because they were moments when you tried to close the distance between what was and what could be.
Almost doesn’t save anyone. But it proves you tried to. And in a world where so many people don’t even reach, where so many stay safely outside the range of almost, that means something.
Even if it’s not enough. Even if it’s almost nothing.
Almost nothing is still something.
And sometimes, that's the only thing worth carrying.
The Weight of Almost
The mathematics of almost are measured in the narrowest margins - the quarter-second hesitation before speaking, the half-step back when you should have stepped forward, the single degree of angle that made you miss what you were reaching for. These measurements become obsessions, each one calculated and recalculated in the laboratory of regret that your mind becomes after almost fails you.
Almost has its own gravity, different from the weight of actual loss. Loss is heavy but solid - you can learn to carry it, to build your muscles around its definite mass. But almost? Almost is liquid weight, shifting and reshaping itself with every movement, never letting you find your balance, never letting you adjust to its burden.
I almost got to Justin in time. The call came at 2:17 AM - I know because I’ve stared at that timestamp in my phone log so many times it’s burned into my retinas. Iva, crying, barely able to get the words out. Something was wrong. He was in the emergency room. She couldn’t explain - or wouldn’t - just kept saying I needed to come.
The drive to the hospital takes twenty-eight minutes normally. That night it felt like twenty-eight years, each mile stretching into impossible distances. Not knowing what I was driving toward. Not knowing if I was already too late. Just knowing that the panic in her voice meant everything was about to change.
I almost made it before they intubated him. The elevator doors were closing as I rounded the corner. I saw the gap narrowing, saw my reflection in the polished steel getting smaller, saw my last chance at goodbye disappearing into a line of light.
My hand hit the door just as it sealed shut.
Almost.
»Funny how the universe has a comedian’s sense of timing, isn’t it? Always letting you close enough to taste it. Never close enough to swallow. If you designed a torture device specifically for hope, it would look exactly like almost.«
By the time the next elevator came, by the time I found the right floor, the right room, the right horrible scene - he was already gone in every way that mattered. The machines were keeping his body running, but Justin, the real Justin, the one who laughed too loud at his own jokes and gave terrible advice with absolute confidence - that Justin had left the building while I was watching elevator doors close.
Almost is a timestamp. A measurement. A precise coordinate where possibility died but hadn’t stopped moving yet.
The doctor said if they’d gotten to him sooner. If someone had noticed earlier. If, if, if. The time it takes to make coffee. The time it takes to listen to a song. The twenty-eight minutes I drove as fast as I could, not knowing what I was racing toward, just knowing I was racing against something I couldn’t see.
Those minutes of almost that I’ll carry forever.
This is what almost does - it turns you into an archivist of distances. You start measuring everything in terms of how close you came, how far you fell short, what small adjustment might have changed everything. Life becomes a series of calculations you can never quite solve.
The space between my hand and Riley’s door handle when I almost checked on him the night before: eighteen inches.
The number of words in the text I almost sent: “Hey, you seemed off tonight. Want to talk?”
The seconds between when I heard something strange and when I decided it was nothing: maybe four, maybe five, maybe a lifetime.
»You’ve done this math too, haven’t you? Measured your own almosts down to the millimeter, timed them down to the microsecond. Replayed the tape so many times you’ve worn grooves in it. And every time, you land on the same answer — the one that doesn’t help.«
Almost creates its own kind of insomnia. Not the regular kind where you can’t fall asleep, but the kind where you can’t stay asleep because your mind keeps returning to that moment of almost, examining it from every angle like a detective at a crime scene that never stops being fresh.
3:17 AM becomes my almost hour. The time when my brain decides to replay every almost-save, every almost-catch, every almost-moment with high-definition clarity. It’s like my subconscious has a Pinterest board of failures, and it insists on scrolling through it when my defenses are down.
The almost-moments stack up like evidence:
Almost noticed Riley was isolating more than usual
Almost recognized the farewell tour he was doing, visiting people one last time
Almost heard the goodbye hidden in “take care of yourself”
Almost understood why he was giving things away
Almost, almost, almost
Each one a thread that, if pulled, might have unraveled a different ending. But threads only work if you grab them in time. Almost-grabbing just leaves you with empty hands and the ghost sensation of what might have been.
The Echo of Unfinished Endings
Unfinished endings don’t fade - they ferment. They sit in the dark corners of your consciousness, transforming from simple regret into something more complex, more toxic, more impossible to metabolize. They become part of your psychological microbiome, altering how you process every experience that comes after.
Here’s one that fermented for years before I even understood what it was.
I came home from Germany — from the Air Force, from a life that had started making a kind of sense even if I was drowning in guilt about what I was supporting — and walked straight into an almost that should have been a sure thing. I had a girl. We got engaged. Fresh start, clean slate, all that horseshit you tell yourself when you’re desperate enough to believe in new chapters. I was out of the military, out of the church by choice, out of a country I’d actually started to love, and here was this one thing that was supposed to be mine. This one thing that was going to work.
She changed her mind. Which, fine — people change their minds. That’s a clean wound. But she didn’t just change her mind. She cheated on me and then tried to ghost me. Just… vanished. Like the engagement was a conversation she’d already forgotten, a subscription she’d let lapse, something that happened to someone else.
When I finally cornered her — and I’m not proud of the word "cornered" but that’s what it took — she looked at me like I was speaking a language she’d never learned. Like the ring, the plans, the future we’d talked about in embarrassing detail were things I’d invented. She acted like we weren’t engaged. Not like it was over. Like it had never happened.
*»You ever have someone rewrite history right in front of you? Look you dead in the eyes and tell you the thing you both lived through didn’t happen? There’s a word for that. It’s a word that gets thrown around too much these days, but when it actually happens to you, it doesn’t feel like a buzzword. It feels like the floor disappearing.«*
I drove home from that conversation — if you can call a ten-minute exercise in being erased a conversation — and sat in my car in the driveway of a house where nobody was particularly glad I’d come back to. Family who didn’t want me. Friends who’d written me off for leaving the church. A fiancée who’d decided I was fiction.
Almost had the life. Almost had the girl. Almost had the fresh start. Almost, almost, almost.
So I did what I always do when almost swallows me whole — I found something to survive inside of. January 2006, I walked into a jiu-jitsu gym for the first time. Some strip-mall dojo that smelled like rubber mats and someone else’s sweat, and a guy I’d never met choked me unconscious within twenty minutes, and I thought: *okay. This is honest. This, at least, isn’t pretending to be something it’s not.*
Another almost-salvation. The gym became my church after I’d burned down the old one, became my family after the real one decided I wasn’t worth the trouble. For twenty years, that mat was the most honest relationship in my life. But that’s a different chapter, and a different kind of almost.
Riley’s goodbye lives in my head as an unfinished conversation, constantly evolving, never resolving. Every few days, I have a new response to things he never quite said. I win arguments we never had. I answer questions he never asked. I finish sentences he started but never completed.
“I’ve been thinking…” he said, that last night in the kitchen.
And then nothing. Subject changed. Moment passed. Sentence abandoned like a car on the side of the highway, still running but going nowhere.
I’ve finished that sentence a thousand different ways:
“I’ve been thinking about getting help.” “I’ve been thinking about ending things.” “I’ve been thinking about telling you something.” “I’ve been thinking about saying goodbye.”
Each completion creates its own alternate universe where I respond differently, where the conversation continues, where the ending changes. But none of them are real. They’re just ghosts of almost-conversations, haunting the spaces where real words should have lived.
»You have your own unfinished sentences, don’t you? The words that trail off into silence, that hang in the air like smoke from a fire that’s been out for years but somehow still burns.«
Justin left me a voicemail two days before the end. I’ve transcribed it because sometimes reading it hurts less than hearing his voice:
“Hey man, it’s me. Just wanted to… I don’t know. Shit. This is stupid. Call me back, I guess. Or don’t. Whatever. I just… yeah. Anyway. Later.”
Thirty-seven seconds of almost saying something. Thirty-seven seconds of circling around words that wouldn’t come. Thirty-seven seconds that I’ve parsed like ancient text, looking for meaning in the pauses, significance in the stammers.
What was he trying to say? Was it a cry for help disguised as casual check-in? Was it goodbye dressed up as small talk? Was it nothing, or was it everything?
The not-knowing is its own form of torture. The unfinished ending becomes a wound that can’t heal because it never properly formed. It’s all jagged edges and exposed nerves, no clean lines to stitch together, no clear borders to define where the injury ends and the rest of you begins.
These unfinished endings multiply, creating a symphony of incompletion:
The relationship that just… stopped, with no breakup, no closure, just silence
The project abandoned two paragraphs from completion
The apology that died in my throat
The confession that never made it past my lips
Each one adds its voice to the chorus of almost that follows me around, a Greek chorus narrating all the ways I’ve failed to complete things, to close loops, to finish what I started.
»What's the sentence you never finished? Not the one you're thinking of second — the one that showed up first, before you had time to choose a safer one. That's the one that matters. That's the one that's still running on a loop somewhere in the back of your skull, waiting for an ending it's never going to get.«
The echo of unfinished endings changes how you approach new beginnings. You start everything expecting it to remain incomplete. You hold back that crucial ten percent, keeping it in reserve for an ending that might never come. You practice goodbyes in the middle of hellos, already preparing for the almost that seems inevitable.
I can’t watch movies anymore without wondering about the conversations that happen after the credits roll. The words that don’t get said. The almost-moments that the camera doesn’t capture. Because I know now that most of life happens in those spaces, in the almost and the after and the unfinished.
I couldn’t go back to that house. Not after finding him there. Not after that Saturday morning turned every corner, every doorway, every inch of that space into a crime scene that only existed in my head. I moved out immediately - grabbed what I could carry and left everything else behind. Never spent another night there. Couldn’t. The almost of that house was too heavy - almost home, almost safe, almost the place where we were supposed to keep building memories instead of where everything ended.
There was no note. No explanation. No neat little paragraph that would have explained everything or nothing. Just absence. Just the echo of conversations we never had, words he never wrote, explanations that never came. Sometimes I think that’s worse - the blank page where answers should have been. At least a note would have been something to analyze, to parse, to torture myself with. Instead, there’s just nothing. Just questions. Just almost-understanding scattered with never-knowing.
When Almost Feels Like a Cage
Almost builds its own architecture around you, bar by bar, until you’re living in a cage made entirely of could-have-beens and should-have-dones. The bars aren’t solid - they’re made of moments, of possibilities, of the ghosts of different outcomes. But they’re strong enough to hold you, to define the boundaries of where you can go and who you can become.
I live in that cage now. Every decision filtered through the mesh of previous almosts. Every reach calculated against the memory of falling short. Every moment of potential action paralyzed by the weight of all the times action almost mattered but didn’t quite.
The cage manifests in specific ways:
I don’t answer phones on the first ring anymore - what if it’s another almost-too-late call?
I over-analyze every conversation, looking for hidden goodbyes, for the things people almost say.
I can’t let people leave without saying exactly what I mean, which makes every goodbye feel like a funeral.
I respond to every text immediately, terrified that waiting will turn into never.
I’ve become exhausting to be around, this person who treats every interaction like it might be the last, who can’t let anything stay light, who drags the weight of almost into every room.
»You recognize this cage, don’t you? The way almost starts to define the shape of your life. The way it makes you either grab too hard or not at all. The way it turns you into someone you don’t recognize.«
The cage has its own geography. Safe zones where almost can’t reach - mindless TV, repetitive tasks, anything that doesn’t require emotional investment. Danger zones where almost lurks - meaningful conversations, new relationships, any situation that carries the possibility of falling short.
I’ve mapped this territory so carefully that I can navigate it without thinking. Avoid these topics. Deflect those questions. Stay surface-level here. Don’t get too close there. Build walls before bridges. End things before they can almost-matter.
The cage becomes comfortable after a while. Not pleasant, but familiar. The known pain of limitation feels safer than the unknown pain of reaching and possibly falling short again. You start to decorate your cage, make it homey, convince yourself it’s not a prison but a choice.
But sometimes, in the quiet moments when your guard is down, you feel the bars. Feel how they’ve shaped you into something smaller than you were meant to be. Feel how almost has become not just something that happened to you, but something you’ve become.
And then there’s the almost that doesn’t fit inside the cage at all. The one that spans so many years it becomes less like a moment and more like a weather pattern. The one I’ve been circling around this whole chapter without naming because naming it makes it too real and too real is where I live whether I want to or not.
Dani Dickson.
I don’t even know where to start with Dani because we never had a proper start. We had a series of almost-starts. Collisions that looked like beginnings but kept dissolving before they could solidify into anything with a name. On-again, off-again for close to a decade, which sounds like a romantic comedy premise until you’ve actually lived inside it, and then it just sounds like slow drowning with intermissions.
She was a musician. Talented in that effortless way that made you want to hate her except you couldn’t because she was also genuinely kind about it — never performed her talent, never weaponized it, just had it the way some people have freckles. She’d play something and the room would change temperature. I don’t know how else to describe it. The air moved differently when she played.
She was my dream girl. And I say that knowing how fucking stupid it sounds, how teenage and reductive and not at all adequate for what she actually was. But that’s the thing about almost-relationships — they freeze the other person in this amber of potential, this idealized version that never has to survive the mundane erosion of actually being together long enough to figure out who leaves the dishes in the sink and who forgets to pay the electric bill. We never got there. We never got to be ordinary together.
We were extraordinary at almost.
The timing was never right. That’s the polite version. The honest version is that we kept choosing the wrong moments to be brave and the wrong moments to be scared. I’d be ready and she wouldn’t. She’d reach out and I’d be buried in something I couldn’t climb out of. We orbited each other for years — close enough to feel the gravity, never close enough to land.
*»You’ve had one of these, haven’t you? The person who’s always almost. Who exists in your life like a song you can never quite remember the words to — you know the melody, you can hum a few bars, but the actual lyrics stay just out of reach. And every time you think you’ve finally got them, they slip away again.«*
She made me feel at home wherever we were. That’s the detail that kills me. Not the music, not the way she looked, not any of the things you’d put in a eulogy. It was that specific, impossible-to-manufacture feeling of home that she carried with her. Some people have it. Most people don’t. She did. And I spent a decade almost living inside it.
We had this check-in thing. When we hadn’t talked in a while — which happened, because on-again-off-again means there are gaps, sometimes weeks, sometimes months — one of us would text the other: "Are you alive?"
That’s it. Three words. Part joke, part genuine concern, part acknowledgment that we still existed to each other even during the off-again stretches. It was our shorthand for: I haven’t forgotten about you. I’m still here. Are you?
It was the same phrase I used with Riley.
I texted her earlier that year. "Are you alive?" Standard check-in. The kind of text that takes two seconds to send and carries the weight of everything we’d never quite managed to say to each other. I sent it and went about my day and waited for the response that always came — sometimes hours later, sometimes days, but it always came.
It didn’t come.
I didn’t panic. That’s the thing about almost-relationships — they train you not to panic during silences because the whole thing is built on silences, on gaps, on the understanding that the connection persists even when the communication doesn’t. She’d text back. She always did. We always found our way back to each other’s orbit. That was the deal. That was the one thing that was reliable about our unreliable thing.
I found out she was dead through Instagram.
Not a phone call from a mutual friend. Not a text from her family. Not any of the ways you’re supposed to find out that someone you loved — someone you almost-loved, someone you loved in the specific, inadequate way you love someone you can never quite hold onto — is gone. I was scrolling, probably at 2 AM because that’s when I do my worst decision-making, and I stumbled onto a post by her brother. It took me a few seconds to understand what I was reading. His words were gentle and careful and final in a way that made my vision go white around the edges.
*»Have you ever learned something through a screen that should have been delivered in person? Something so enormous that your phone suddenly feels too small to contain it, like the words should be spilling out of the edges, flooding your hands, soaking into the floor?«*
I went back to my messages. Scrolled up. Found the "Are you alive?" sitting there, delivered, read, unanswered. Read. She read it. Or her phone marked it as read. Or someone going through her phone after she was gone opened it and it got marked as read and I’ll never know which one and the not-knowing is its own specific corridor of hell.
One of the last things she ever sent me was a Billie Eilish song. "everything i wanted." I listened to it when she sent it and thought it was just Dani being Dani, sharing music the way she always did, the way musicians communicate when words aren’t enough. I didn’t hear it as a message. I didn’t decode it. I just added it to the playlist of songs that made me think of her and moved on with my day.
After I found out, I listened to it again. Sat in my car in the dark and let it play on repeat until the lyrics stopped being lyrics and started being a letter I’d received weeks ago and never opened. *I had a dream / I got everything I wanted / Not what you’d think / And if I’m being honest / It might’ve been a nightmare.* There’s a line about having someone who’d catch you if you fell. About that being enough.
I have a tattoo for her now. I’m not going to describe it because some things are just mine, and I’ve given enough of myself in these pages. But it’s there. Permanent in a way we never were. Fixed in a way our timing never allowed. A mark on my body for a person who left a mark on everything else.
The agony of almost with Dani is different from the agony of almost with Riley or Justin. With them, almost means I almost saved them. I almost intervened. I almost changed the ending. With Dani, almost is the whole story. We almost had it. Not once, not twice, but over and over for a decade. We almost got the timing right. Almost chose the same moment to be brave. Almost built the life that kept shimmering on the horizon like heat off asphalt — visible, tangible-looking, never actually there when you reached it.
And the "are you alive?" sits in my phone like a headstone. Same phrase I used with Riley. Same three words, same casual cruelty of a question that assumes the answer is yes because the alternative is something your brain won’t process until it has to.
*»Sometimes the patterns in our lives are so on the nose that you’d reject them as bad writing if they showed up in a novel. "Are you alive?" asked twice, answered with silence twice. No editor would let that through. Too neat. Too symbolic. But here we are.«*
I don’t listen to that Billie Eilish song anymore. Not because it hurts — everything hurts, that’s baseline, that’s just Tuesday — but because I’m afraid that if I listen to it enough times I’ll stop hearing Dani in it. That it’ll become just another song instead of the last thing she tried to tell me. And I can’t afford to lose that. I’ve already lost everything else about us that was solid. I’m not giving up the ghost of a message I was too thick to hear when hearing it might have mattered.
That’s the thing about the agony of almost. With some people, you get one almost, one moment, one missed catch. With Dani, I got a decade of them. A whole collection. An anthology of almost, beautifully bound in could-have-been, sitting on a shelf I can see but can’t reach.
And now she’s gone and the almost is permanent. No more chances for on-again. No more orbiting back toward each other after months of silence. No more texts that say "are you alive?" and getting the answer that lets you exhale.
Just the question, hanging there. Unanswered. Unanswerable.
Finding Peace (or Something Like It)
Peace doesn’t arrive like you’d expect - not as resolution or acceptance or any of the neat stages grief is supposed to follow. It comes in fragments, in moments so small you might miss them if you’re looking for something bigger. It comes not as the absence of almost, but as brief instances where almost loosens its grip just enough for you to breathe.
I found a piece of it at 4 AM on a Tuesday, sitting on my bathroom floor for reasons I couldn’t explain, when I suddenly realized I’d gone twelve minutes without calculating distances. Twelve minutes where my mind wasn’t measuring the space between what was and what almost was. It wasn’t peace exactly, but it was the absence of active torture, and sometimes that’s close enough.
»Did you just take a breath? A real one? Because sometimes that’s what peace looks like - not the absence of pain but a momentary softening of its edges.«
The thing about making peace with almost is that you can’t approach it head-on. You can’t wrestle it into submission or therapy-speak it into dissolution. Almost is too slippery for that, too quantum. The more directly you look at it, the more it shifts into something else.
Instead, peace comes sideways, through unexpected angles:
Through the exhaustion that finally makes you stop calculating. Through the moment you realize you’re torturing yourself with math that will never add up differently. Through the accidental discovery that some things can stay unfinished without the world ending. Through the grudging acceptance that almost is just another word for tried.
That last one took me two years to even consider. Two years of hating my almost-saves, my almost-catches, my almost-enoughs. Two years before someone said, “At least you reached. Most people don’t even try.”
I wanted to punch them. It felt like consolation prize bullshit, the kind of thing people say when they don’t know what else to offer. But it stuck in my head like a splinter, working its way deeper until I couldn’t ignore it anymore.
Almost means you showed up. Almost means you tried. Almost means you gave a shit when giving a shit hurt. Almost means you reached even when reaching meant risking everything.
This isn’t about redemption or silver linings or finding the good in the bad. Fuck that. This is about survival mathematics - finding the minimum viable peace that lets you continue existing without the weight of almost crushing you completely.
Moving Forward After Almost
Moving forward doesn’t mean leaving almost behind. It travels with you, but maybe - if you’re lucky, if you work at it, if you can find the right angle - it becomes something you carry instead of something that carries you.
I still have the text I never sent to Riley. It’s saved in my drafts, dated two days before he died. Twenty-three words that might have changed everything or nothing: “Hey man, you’ve seemed really off lately. I’m worried about you. Can we talk? Like really talk? I’m here if you need me.”
I used to open it every day, editing it, perfecting it, like somehow getting the words exactly right retroactively would change what happened. Now I look at it maybe once a month. Not to torture myself (mostly), but to remember that I was trying. That I saw something. That I almost acted on it.
That’s what moving forward looks like - not forgetting the almost, but changing your relationship with it. Letting it be part of the story without letting it be the only story.
»You’re still here, reading this. Still carrying your own almosts. That’s not nothing. That’s not almost-something. That’s real survival, the kind that doesn’t make it into motivational speeches.«
The whiteboard on my wall - the one with those four simple tasks - has a fifth line now, added in fading blue marker:
“Almost counts”
Not in the horseshoes-and-hand-grenades way. Not as consolation. But as acknowledgment. As witness. As a reminder that reaching and falling short is still reaching. That trying and failing is still trying. That almost-saving someone means you were the kind of person who tried to save someone.
Moving forward looks like:
Answering texts without analyzing them for hidden goodbyes. Having conversations that don’t all feel like potential last ones. Letting some things stay unfinished without it feeling like moral failure. Accepting that almost is part of the human experience, not a personal failing.
I’m not there yet. Not even almost there. But I’m somewhere different than I was, and maybe that’s enough movement to call it forward.
The almosts are still there. They’ll always be there. Riley is still dead. Justin is still gone. The distances I failed to close are still measured in my muscle memory. But I’m learning to live with them instead of in them. To carry them instead of being buried by them.
And maybe that’s all any of us can do with our almosts - learn to carry them with something approaching grace, even when grace feels impossible, even when carrying feels like too much, even when almost is all we have.
Because here’s what I’ve learned about almost: it’s not nothing. It’s not enough, but it’s not nothing. And in the arithmetic of survival, not-nothing might be the best we can hope for.
Sometimes that has to be enough. Even when it almost isn’t. Especially when it almost isn’t.
That’s the agony and the anchor of almost - it keeps us suspended between what was and what could have been, but it also proves we were reaching. And as long as we’re reaching, we’re still alive.
Still trying. Still almost-something. Which is infinitely more than nothing.
