PART IV: The Shaky Inhale of Hope
(Fragile, imperfect, slow progress)

Chapter 13 - Hope Isn't Loud

Hope showed up in a fucking grocery store, of all places. Between the frozen peas and the overpriced organic bullshit, while some terrible pop song from 2011 played through speakers that hadn't been cleaned since the store opened. I was standing there, trying to remember why I needed groceries when eating felt optional, when the song shifted to something else—something Riley used to play on repeat during our better days, before everything went to shit, before that Saturday morning changed the geography of my entire existence.
And for maybe three seconds—maybe—something in my chest unclenched. Not dramatically. Not like those movie moments where the music swells and the character has some profound realization about the meaning of life. Just a slight loosening, like a fist that's been clenched so long it forgot it could do anything else finally remembering how to release.
That's it. That's the whole fucking story. No breakthrough. No transformation. No sudden understanding that everything happens for a reason or that what doesn't kill you makes you stronger or any of that packaged, palatable bullshit. Just three seconds of something that wasn't quite pain, standing between the frozen vegetables and processed dinners, while strangers pushed their carts past me like I wasn't having some microscopic moment of not-quite-drowning.
*»You're waiting for more, aren't you? For the part where this tiny moment becomes something bigger, something worth writing about. Yeah. I was too.«*

I drove home with the wrong groceries. Half the stuff in the bags made no sense—rice milk, which I've never bought in my life, and a jar of olives, which I actively hate. But I'd been standing in that aisle and the song ended and the unclenching stopped, and I just started grabbing things so I'd look like a person who had his shit together. Like a person who came to the grocery store on purpose.

Huck met me at the door. Did that thing where he shoved his nose into every bag like I'd brought him something, which I hadn't, because I'd been too busy pretending to be a functioning adult to remember dog treats. He didn't care. Tail going, whole body vibrating with the conviction that my arrival was the most important event of the day. Maybe the century.

I put the rice milk in the fridge and the olives in the cabinet and stood there in my kitchen, and the three seconds were already gone. Already felt like something I might have imagined. Already felt like the kind of thing you don't tell anyone because it sounds like nothing—because it is nothing—because three seconds of your chest not hurting in a grocery store is not a story anyone would care about.

But I cared about it. I cared about it the way you care about finding a dollar in an old coat pocket. Not life-changing. Not meaningful. Just unexpected evidence that not everything has been taken from you.

The Morning After Justin

The morning after Justin died, I made coffee. Not because I wanted it. Not because I thought caffeine would help. But because making coffee is what you do in the morning, and my body moved through the motions while my mind was somewhere else entirely—still in that hospital room, still listening to machines that weren't doing their job anymore, still holding a hand that couldn't hold back.

Steam rose from the mug. The kitchen filled with that familiar smell. The coffee maker gurgled its mechanical morning prayer, same as always, same as every morning, same as if nothing had happened.

And for just a moment—while I stood there holding a mug I didn't want, breathing in steam from coffee I wouldn't drink—something in me registered that water still boiled at the same temperature. Coffee still brewed in the same amount of time. The sun still rose even though Justin wouldn't see it.

That's not hope exactly. I don't know what to call it. It's the thing that exists next to hope, the way a shadow exists next to a wall. Not the thing itself but proof that the thing is nearby, somewhere, casting itself onto surfaces you weren't looking at.

I drank the coffee eventually. It tasted like nothing. Like hot water pretending to have meaning. But I drank it anyway, because that's what you do with coffee. You drink it. Even when it doesn't help. Even when you can't taste it. Even when the person who used to steal sips from your mug will never steal another sip from anything.

*»You've done this, haven't you? Gone through the motions of something so ordinary it felt obscene. Made breakfast after a funeral. Paid a bill the same day your world collapsed. Brushed your teeth like your teeth mattered. Like any of it mattered.«*

What I Didn't Expect

I didn't expect the humming.

About six weeks after Justin—I'd stopped counting exact days by then, which maybe means something or maybe just means I'm bad at math—I caught myself humming while I was filling the dogs' water bowl. Not a real song. Not even a recognizable melody. Just this absent, tuneless vibration in my throat that stopped the second I noticed it.

I stood there with the hose running, water overflowing the bowl, trying to figure out what had just happened. My body had done something without consulting me. Something that wasn't crying or clenching or lying very still. Something that might, if you squinted at it from the right angle, look almost like a person who wasn't in the middle of falling apart.

It scared the shit out of me.

Not because it happened. Because of what it might mean. Because if I was humming—if my body was capable of producing a sound that wasn't a sob or a scream—then maybe the worst was over. And if the worst was over, that meant I'd survived it. And if I'd survived it, that meant I had to figure out what came next. And "what comes next" is the most terrifying question you can ask someone who's been putting all their energy into getting through right now.

I turned off the hose. Wiped the water off my hands. Went inside and sat on the floor in the hallway for about twenty minutes. The dogs came and lay against me, one on each side, like they were trying to hold me together through sheer body heat. Ripley put her head on my thigh with a sigh that said, in dog, *I don't know what's happening but I'm here for it.*

*»That sigh. Dogs have this way of saying everything therapists charge two hundred dollars an hour to approximate.«*

I didn't hum again for weeks. But I remembered that I had. Filed it in the same place I'd filed the grocery store, this growing catalog of moments that didn't quite hurt. Not a collection of evidence for some case I was building about getting better. Just... a drawer I kept accidentally putting things in.

The Faded Whiteboard

Morning light was doing that thing where it moved across my ceiling—the same ceiling I'd memorized every crack and shadow of during the nights I couldn't sleep. But the light shifted, just slightly, and caught the whiteboard on the wall. Crooked. Faded. The words almost invisible now because I haven't rewritten them. Haven't replaced the marker.

There's something honest about their faded state. Something true about having to squint to read them. I thought about making them bold and clear again. But no—let them be barely there. Let them be the thing you have to already know about to see.

Some mornings the light hits just right and those words stop feeling like accusations. Stop feeling like evidence of my failure to perform basic human functions without written instructions. For just a moment, they become something else—not encouragement, not permission. Just a record. Proof that I've been here before, in this exact spot, feeling this exact way, and I made it to the next day. That's all they say now. Not "do these things." Just "you've done these things. You can probably do them again."

Probably. The most honest word in the English language.

What Accumulates

Over time—and I'm talking months, years, not the neat arc of a recovery memoir—something started to accumulate. Not hope, exactly. More like evidence that time was passing and I was still in it.

The grocery store moment didn't change my life. Didn't spark some journey. Didn't even make me feel better for more than those three seconds. But I remembered it. Filed it away. And the next time I was in that store, some part of me was listening for that song, waiting for that feeling, holding space for the possibility of another three seconds.

That's the thing I didn't expect. Not the hope itself, but the waiting for it. The listening for it. The refusal to close off the possibility that it might show up again, even though closing off would have been so much easier. Even though expecting nothing is the only reliable way to avoid disappointment.

I started buying groceries for next week instead of just tomorrow. Didn't notice when it happened. One day I just looked down at my cart and there was a bag of rice that would take more than a day to get through, and I thought—huh. Apparently I'm planning to be alive on Thursday.

I stopped counting the days since it happened. Not a conscious decision, not a milestone I celebrated. Just one morning I woke up and realized I didn't know the exact number anymore, and the not-knowing didn't feel like betrayal. Just felt like Tuesday.

I answered "How are you?" with "Okay" instead of "Fine." Not because okay was true, but because it was closer to true than fine, and the distance between lying and not-lying had started to matter to me again. Which meant something. Probably.

*»None of these are victories. I want to be clear about that. I'm not building toward some moment where I tell you it all added up to healing. It didn't add up to anything. It just accumulated, the way dust accumulates on a shelf you keep meaning to clean. You don't decide to collect dust. It just happens because time passes and you're still there.«*

Riley and Justin and the Math of Staying

I think about Justin sometimes, about those last weeks when hope must have been so thin for him—if it was there at all. I wonder if he had his own grocery store moments, his own three seconds of unclenching that weren't enough to outweigh everything else. If the math just stopped working. If the accumulation went the other direction, the drawer filling up with moments that hurt until there wasn't room for anything else.

And Riley. Whether hope was even a variable in his calculation by the end, or if the equation had simplified itself down to something that didn't include it anymore. He planned it so carefully—the speaker, the songs, the timing. That's not the math of someone weighing hope against despair. That's the math of someone who's already finished weighing.

I can't save them retroactively. Can't go back and make their three seconds last longer, make their drawers hold different things. But I carry them. I carry the way Justin's hospital room smelled like plastic and wrong. The way Riley's door felt under my hand. I carry those things next to the humming and the grocery store and the coffee that tasted like nothing, and somehow it all fits in the same body. The worst things and the almost-okay things, coexisting in the same set of bones.

That might be the closest thing to hope I can honestly describe. Not that it gets better. Not that the weight lifts. Just that you can carry more than you think. Not because you're strong—fuck strong—but because that's apparently what bodies do. They carry things. It's not a talent. It's just physics.

The Grocery Store, Again

The other day I was back in that grocery store. Same aisle, same shitty speakers, same fluorescent light that makes everyone look vaguely deceased. A different song was playing—nothing I recognized, nothing connected to anyone I've lost. Just a song.

And nothing happened.

No unclenching. No three seconds. No flicker of whatever that was. Just me, standing between the frozen peas and the organic bullshit, being a person buying groceries. Which—and I need you to hear this—is also the whole fucking story. Because a year ago I wouldn't have been in that store at all. A year ago the grocery store was an ordeal that required two days of psychological preparation. And now it was just a store. I was just shopping. The song was just a song.

*»That's it. I don't have a better ending than that. A man in a grocery store, not falling apart. I know it doesn't sound like hope. But go back and read the first page of this chapter and tell me it's not.«*

Even if it stays just an ember forever—not a flame, not a fire, nothing that warms you or lights your way. Even if all you get are three-second moments in grocery stores and coffee that tastes like nothing and dogs who sigh against your leg like that's a form of therapy. That's still something.

Not the loud kind of something. Not the kind that conquers anything or transforms anything or makes a good story at parties.

Just the quiet, stubborn kind that doesn't require victory. Doesn't demand you become someone better or stronger or more healed. Just asks you to keep showing up at the grocery store, keep making the coffee, keep filling the dog bowl, keep being the person who's still here even though being here is the hardest thing you've ever done.

The whiteboard hangs crooked. The dogs need feeding. Somewhere a song is playing that might make someone's chest unclench for three seconds.

None of it means anything. All of it means everything.

And we keep showing up anyway.
