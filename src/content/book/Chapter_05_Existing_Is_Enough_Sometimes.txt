PART II: When Breathing Feels Like Work
(Survival in the in-between — heavy, slow, stuck)

Chapter 5 - Existing Is Enough (Sometimes)
The whiteboard hangs on my wall like a monument to basic existence, its surface marked with four lines that shouldn't need to be written down, shouldn't require conscious effort, shouldn't feel like mountains to climb:
Wake up.
Get out of bed.
Take a shower.
Go outside.
In their simplicity lies an unbearable weight, each word a reminder of how fundamentally trauma can reshape your relationship with the basic acts of being alive. These aren't goals or aspirations—they're negotiations with existence itself, daily contracts we make with our own survival.
»How tight is your chest right now? You know exactly how it feels when these basic acts become monuments. When existing requires a checklist. When survival looks like four lines on a whiteboard that mock you with their simplicity.«
The whiteboard didn't exist yet when Riley died. There were no four lines, no checkmarks, no system. There was just a body that forgot how to do the things it used to do without thinking. Waking up meant remembering, meant existing in a world where my best friend had chosen to leave it. The simple act of consciousness became a burden too heavy to bear, each moment of awareness a fresh encounter with loss. Getting out of bed took three days—three days of lying there, counting breaths, watching shadows crawl across the ceiling while the world outside continued without me. Taking a shower felt like climbing Everest, the simple mechanics of standing under water requiring a kind of strength I wasn't sure I possessed. And going outside? That seemed impossible, a step too far into a world that kept spinning, kept moving, kept existing as if nothing had changed, as if my entire universe hadn't just collapsed around me.

The whiteboard came later—about a year later—when I realized those four tasks had never gotten easy again. That they weren't going to. That I needed to see them written down, needed the quiet accountability of a checkmark, because my body had learned a new default and that default was stillness. The whiteboard wasn't born from one bad morning. It was born from hundreds of them.

After Justin died, the lines evolved into something even more fundamental. Each checkmark became a testament to the kind of courage that nobody writes books about—the raw, unglamorous bravery of continuing to exist when existence itself feels optional. The victories became simultaneously enormous and embarrassingly small: managing to lift your head from the pillow after hours of staring at nothing. Putting both feet on the floor even if just to move to the couch. Standing under water while unsure if it's tears or shower drops running down your face. Opening the front door just enough to take one breath of outside air.

The world doesn't have metrics for these kinds of victories. There are no medals for getting out of bed when your bones feel filled with lead. No celebrations for the profound courage it takes to step into a shower when just existing feels like too much work. No recognition for the raw bravery of opening your front door when the world outside feels too loud, too bright, too real. Try explaining these triumphs to someone who measures success in promotions and purchases and Instagram-worthy moments. Try telling them that sometimes, victory looks like checking a box next to a basic human function, like managing to exist for one more day in a world that keeps taking pieces of you.
»There's a pit in your stomach now. Because these are your victories too, aren't they? The ones you never talk about. The ones that feel too small to celebrate but too heavy to ignore. The ones that live in the space between breathing and breaking.«
The whiteboard doesn't judge. It maintains its quiet vigil, patient and unchanging, neither pushing nor pulling but simply existing as a reminder that survival sometimes looks like four simple lines. It doesn't rush. Doesn't compare today's victories to yesterday's failures. Doesn't demand explanations for the days when even one checkmark feels impossible. It just waits, holding space for whatever version of survival we can manage on any given day.

In the space between heartbeats, between the moment you open your eyes and the moment you remember why breathing feels like labor, there's a ceiling. White. Textured. The kind of surface that holds shadows in its valleys and creates patterns that shift with the angle of morning light. I've memorized every inch of it during these heavy mornings—the slight water stain in the corner from that time the roof leaked, the almost-perfect circle where a smoke detector used to be before I took it down because its blinking red light kept catching my eye at night, the barely visible crack that runs like a fault line toward the window. These imperfections have become familiar companions in the quiet hours when existence feels too heavy to bear.

My body carries a peculiar kind of weight during these moments. Not the kind that comes from physical exhaustion or too little sleep, but the kind that lives in your bones, that makes your chest feel like it's being compressed under the weight of everything you're carrying. Everything you've lost. Everything you're afraid to lose. It's the kind of weight that transforms simple existence into an act of defiance against gravity itself.
»Your throat just got tight, didn't it? Because you know this weight. This particular kind of gravity that has nothing to do with physics and everything to do with grief.«
Some days, just existing feels like climbing a mountain with weights strapped to your ankles, each breath a deliberate act of defiance against the gravity of loss, each moment of consciousness a negotiation with yourself about whether you have the strength to keep your eyes open, to keep breathing, to keep existing in a world that keeps taking pieces of you. Today is one of those days, when the simple act of being alive feels like an Olympic sport nobody trained you for.

The light changes angle slightly, shifting the shadows on the ceiling. Time passing, marked only by the slow crawl of darkness across textured white paint. Within me, the voice of should rises like a tide: should get up, should shower, should eat something. But the phone on the nightstand is silent. No messages piling up. No notifications from a world waiting for my participation. The silence where the buzzing should be is its own kind of confirmation—that I could lie here for another day, another week, another month, and the world wouldn't so much as hiccup. That the space I occupy would simply close around my absence like water filling a hole. That everything the voice in my head has been saying—that I'm nothing, that I'm dispensable, that nobody would notice or care—has receipts now. Actual, verifiable, empirical evidence in the form of a phone that doesn't ring.

This is one of those days when survival doesn't look like victory. When healing doesn't look like progress. When existence doesn't look like living. And maybe that has to be okay. Because sometimes, in the depths of grief, in the shadows of trauma, in the quiet spaces between heartbeats, existing is enough. It has to be.

There's a corner in my living room where Justin's recliner used to be. One of those electric-assisted ones—I bought it for my shoulder surgery recovery, and Justin fell asleep in it the night he came over with a repurposed prescription bottle full of weed and the instruction to "get off that opioid shit ASAP." He snored so loud I thought the neighbors were going to call someone. We watched Family Guy reruns and laughed and he fell asleep in that chair and I don't think anyone else ever sat in it after him. It stayed in my house for years after he died. Way longer than it was welcome—this piece of furniture that had become a memorial I never asked for, holding the shape of a person who wasn't coming back. I finally got rid of it. The corner is empty now, which isn't better. Just different. The absence of the recliner is its own kind of presence.

The house is quiet. Not peaceful quiet. Quiet because music is a minefield. Every genre is mapped to someone I've lost. Country belongs to Riley—sad country songs on a Bluetooth speaker, on a loop, in a kitchen I can never go back to. There are songs that belong to Dani. There are songs that belong to phases of my life I can't revisit without the floor giving out. So the house is quiet. The air purifier hums. The dogs breathe. That's the soundtrack. David Attenborough sometimes, because penguins don't have unresolved emotional significance. Yet.

The treatment cycle is its own version of nothing-happening dressed up as something. Outpatient, then inpatient—six weeks of residential where they cracked me open and got to the real shit underneath, the parts that have been locked away since I was a kid. Then back to outpatient. The PTSD treatment hokey-pokey. You put your trauma in. You pull your trauma out. You put your trauma in and you shake it all about. And you keep ending up in the same waiting room with the same magazines from three years ago, wondering if the hokey-pokey really is what it's all about.

The shame arrives like an unwelcome companion, bringing with it a voice that insists I should be better by now. Should be stronger. Should be healing faster. But trauma doesn't follow should. Grief doesn't operate on a timeline. Loss doesn't fade just because we think it's been long enough. The weight of existing becomes most noticeable in these quiet moments, in the space between tasks, in the pause between breaths, in the silence that fills the room when I finally stop trying to pretend that I'm okay.

Each simple task becomes its own mountain to climb—responding to a text message feels like writing a novel, making a phone call feels like giving a speech to thousands, getting dressed feels like preparing for battle, eating feels like solving a complex puzzle. But here's the truth about weight that nobody tells you: it doesn't have to be lifted to be carried. Sometimes, just holding it steady is enough. Sometimes, just continuing to exist under its pressure is its own kind of victory.

The voices start their chorus in these moments of perceived failure:
"You should be over this by now."
"Other people have it worse."
"Just push through it."
"Stop being lazy."
"Get your shit together."
"Why can't you just be normal?"

They disguise themselves as motivation, as tough love, as the kind of hard truth that's supposed to spark action and inspire movement. But they're really just shame wearing a productivity mask, guilt masquerading as guidance.

I couldn't be there when they cleaned out Riley's house. The physical space where I found him had become a gravity well of trauma, a place where the air itself felt heavy with absence and questions that will never have answers. More shame. More weight. More voices whispering about weakness and failure and all the ways I should be stronger, better, more.
»You know these voices too. The ones that measure your worth in productivity, that count your value in tasks completed, that see rest as resistance and stillness as surrender.«

~~~

The ceiling maintains its familiar patterns as evening approaches—same texture, same shadows, same slight water stain in the corner, same almost-perfect circle where the smoke detector used to be, same crack running like a fault line toward the window. But something has shifted in the space between morning and now, something barely perceptible yet undeniably present. It's not progress in any traditional sense, not healing as the world would measure it, but rather a subtle shift in the weight of existing, like learning to breathe underwater.

The whiteboard maintains its quiet vigil on the wall, patient and unchanging, with its four simple lines that have become both challenge and comfort.

Today brought two checkmarks. Tomorrow might bring three, or perhaps just one, or maybe none at all. And in this moment of quiet reflection, that feels okay. Because beneath the weight of expectations, beyond the metrics of traditional success, there's a profound truth: I'm still here. Still breathing. Still existing in a world that keeps taking pieces of me but hasn't taken all of me yet.

The empty corner where Justin's recliner used to be still holds its gravity well of memory. The memories of coming home to Riley's terrible one-man karaoke sessions remain, quiet with the particular silence of absence. The grief still sits in my chest like a stone, and the trauma still lives in my bones like a second skeleton. Yet somehow, through all of this, through the weight and the shame and the endless negotiations with existence itself, something remains. Something continues. Something survives.

The dogs shift at the foot of the bed, adjusting to the evening cool. They've been here all day. They don't know what today cost me. They don't know about the whiteboard or the checkmarks or the voices or the empty corner. They just know their person is still here. And for them, that's enough.

Maybe they're onto something.
