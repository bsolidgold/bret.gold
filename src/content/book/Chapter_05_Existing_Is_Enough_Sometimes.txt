PART II: When Breathing Feels Like Work
(Survival in the in-between — heavy, slow, stuck)

Chapter 5 - Existing Is Enough (Sometimes)

The ceiling has a water stain in the corner from when the roof leaked. A nearly perfect circle where a smoke detector used to be before I took it down because its blinking red light kept catching my eye at three AM. A crack that runs like a fault line toward the window, getting a little wider every winter. I know these things the way most people know their own face — from the kind of long, repeated, involuntary study that only happens when you spend enough mornings unable to look anywhere else.

Today is one of those mornings. The whiteboard is across the room, crooked as always, its letters so faded they're more memory than ink. Four tasks I could recite in my sleep. Four tasks I will not be completing today.

I know this already. It's 7 AM and I already know. Not because I've tried and failed — because the trying isn't even available as an option yet. It's like someone removed a gear from the machine overnight and the whole transmission just grinds when I reach for it.

The dogs don't care about my transmission. Badge is standing next to the bed staring at me with the particular intensity of a dog who needs to eat and has identified me as the obstacle between him and breakfast. Huck is doing his low whine — the one that starts quiet and escalates until it becomes genuinely impossible to ignore. Ripley is still asleep because Ripley operates on her own timeline and always has.

This is what gets me vertical. Not the whiteboard. Not willpower. Not some inner light flickering to life. A dog that needs to eat and will not stop staring at me until I do something about it.

I swing my legs over the side of the bed. Sit there for a minute. Maybe five. Maybe fifteen — time doesn't really work right on these mornings. It pools and stretches and I lose track. Eventually I stand up. Walk to the kitchen. Scoop food into three bowls. Stand there watching them eat with their perfect, uncomplicated focus, their tails going, their whole world narrowed down to the thing in front of them, and I think: that must be nice. To want one thing and just do it. To not have a committee meeting about whether you deserve to eat today.

*»You've envied an animal for that. For the simplicity of just being hungry and eating. For not having to justify the basic act of sustaining yourself.«*

I go back to bed.

That's the day. That's the whole day. I fed the dogs. I went back to bed. The whiteboard got zero checkmarks.

∽∽∽

People who haven't been here think the bad days are dramatic. They picture crying, screaming, throwing things. And sometimes it is that — sometimes the flood comes and you're on the bathroom floor making sounds you didn't know a human could make. But most of the time, the bad days don't look like anything. They look like a person lying in bed in a dim room at 2 PM on a Tuesday with their phone face-down on the nightstand and three dogs arranged around them like sentries guarding something that doesn't want to be guarded.

The phone buzzes. I know it's buzzing because I can hear it vibrate against the wood. I don't look at it. Whoever it is can wait. Or they can't wait and that's their problem. I know that's shitty. I know people are trying. I know there's probably a text in there from someone who actually cares, and ignoring it is the kind of thing that makes people stop trying, and I know that too, and knowing it makes me feel worse, which makes me less likely to pick up the phone, which will make them less likely to try again, and the whole thing spirals into a guilt loop that ends with me lying in the same position staring at the same ceiling feeling worse than I did before the phone buzzed.

So I don't look at it.

The air purifier wheezes in the corner. It's the most consistent relationship in my life — it shows up every day, does its job, doesn't ask questions, doesn't need me to be okay. I've thought about this more than a grown man should think about an air purifier. That's what these days do. They turn your brain into a machine that finds meaning in appliances because the real meaningful things are too heavy to pick up.

There's my protector again. Analyzing the air purifier instead of sitting with the fact that I haven't moved in six hours.

∽∽∽

I should tell you about the treatment cycle.

I've done outpatient therapy. Then inpatient — six weeks of residential treatment where I talked about things I'd been avoiding for decades. Hours a day in a room with therapists who were the first ones to actually break through the analyzer and get to the real shit underneath. Then back to outpatient. Then the question of what comes next, which is a question I'm not prepared to answer because the honest answer is I don't know and nobody seems to accept that as a response.

The treatment hokey-pokey. You put your trauma in. You pull your trauma out. You put your trauma in and you shake it all about. Except nobody's shaking anything — you're just sitting in a room telling a stranger about the worst things that ever happened to you and hoping that eventually the telling makes them weigh less. Sometimes it does. Sometimes it doesn't. Sometimes you walk out of a session feeling like someone took a layer of skin off and you have to drive home with all your nerve endings exposed to the air.

The residential stint was different from everything before it. The therapists there didn't get the analyzer. They got the flood. They broke through to the parts of me that had been locked away since I was a kid — the parts that carry the real weight, the ones the protector has been standing in front of for thirty-five years saying "nothing to see here, move along." They saw through it. And when they did, what came out was years of grief I'd never actually processed. Riley. Justin. Vanuatu. Air Force shit I didn't even know I was carrying. All of it, in a room, with people who weren't going to let me think my way out of feeling it.

*»If you've ever been in a room where someone finally called your bluff — where someone saw through the version of you that has everything together and got to the version that doesn't — you know what I'm talking about. It feels like the floor disappearing. It feels like the hole in your chest getting bigger instead of smaller. It feels like the worst thing in the world until you realize it might be the only thing that actually helps.«*

I don't want to make it sound like a breakthrough montage. It wasn't. I didn't walk out healed. I walked out raw. I walked out knowing things about myself that I can't un-know, carrying tools I'm still not sure I know how to use, and facing the same whiteboard in the same bedroom in the same house where everything happened. The whiteboard didn't care that I'd just spent six weeks doing the hardest work of my life. It still had four tasks. I still had mornings where zero of them got done.

∽∽∽

The house is quiet in a way that has nothing to do with peace. There's no music. Not because I like silence — because music is a minefield. Every genre is mapped to someone I've lost. Country belongs to Riley. There are songs that belong to Dani. There are songs that belong to phases of my life I can't go back to without the floor giving out. So the house is quiet. The air purifier hums. The dogs breathe. That's the soundtrack.

Some people leave the TV on for background noise. I do that sometimes — David Attenborough usually, because nature documentaries are the only thing that feels neutral. Penguins don't have unresolved emotional significance. Fish don't remind me of anyone. It's the only content I can consume without risking a tripwire.

The silence isn't comfortable. It's just the least dangerous option.

There's a corner in the living room where Justin's recliner used to be. One of those electric-assisted ones — I bought it for my shoulder surgery recovery, and Justin fell asleep in it the night he came over with a repurposed prescription bottle full of weed and the instruction to "get off that opioid shit ASAP." He snored so loud that night I thought the neighbors were going to call someone. We watched Family Guy reruns and laughed and he fell asleep in that chair and I'm pretty sure nobody else ever sat in it after him.

The recliner stayed in my house for years after Justin died. Way longer than it was welcome. I'd walk past that corner every day and feel the specific weight of a piece of furniture that had become a memorial I never asked for. It sat there holding the shape of a person who wasn't coming back, occupying space in my living room the way his absence occupied space in my chest.

I finally got rid of it. Took a while to work up to it. The corner is empty now, which isn't better, just different. The absence of the recliner is its own kind of presence. Like the room remembers what was there and holds the space open, just in case.

∽∽∽

A good day — and I use that word the way someone uses the word "fine" when they're not fine but they need the conversation to move on — a good day is two checkmarks. Maybe three if something external forces the third one. Like a doctor's appointment I can't cancel, or one of the dogs needing to go to the vet, or the particular kind of guilt that builds up after enough ignored texts that the social pressure finally outweighs the paralysis.

A good day looks like this: I wake up. That's one. I get out of bed — actually out, not the thing where I sit on the edge for an hour and then lie back down. That's two. I stand in the shower for twenty minutes doing nothing but letting the water hit my back because the act of standing upright in warm water is about all I can manage, and I count it. That's three.

Going outside is the hardest one. Always has been. It's not the outside itself — it's the implication. Going outside means being visible. It means existing in a space where other people can see you and form opinions about the state of your hair and your face and your general vibe of someone who has not been handling things well. The driveway is as far as I usually get. Stand there. Let the air hit my face. Watch a neighbor drive past and wave and wonder what they'd think if they knew that the wave I just gave them was the most significant social interaction I've had in four days.

*»Three checkmarks and you feel like you ran a marathon. And the world would look at your day and see absolutely nothing. That's the gap nobody talks about — between what it costs you and what it looks like from the outside.«*

Four checkmarks — all four tasks, one day, all completed — has happened. Not often. When it does, it doesn't feel like victory. It feels like exhaustion. Like I spent every unit of energy I had on the basics and there's nothing left for anything else. No cooking dinner, no returning calls, no working on the thing I'm supposed to be working on. Just four checkmarks and an empty tank and the knowledge that tomorrow I'll wake up and the board will be blank again and we'll start the whole thing over.

∽∽∽

I've been in this house since a few months after Riley died. Eleven years. The whiteboard has been on the wall for most of them. The letters have been rewritten a few times when they faded past the point of readability, but mostly they just sit there in various stages of ghostliness. Some mornings I can barely see them. Some mornings they're the loudest thing in the room.

The dogs have changed. Red and Adrian — the dogs who were there when I found Riley, the ones who found him first — they're gone now. Badge and Huck and Ripley are the ones who share the bed now, who need their bowls filled, who stare at me with their simple demands that have nothing to do with healing or progress or any of the words people use when they want your pain to have a trajectory.

The house has changed around me too, in the slow way that houses change when one person lives in them for a long time. Things break and get half-fixed or not fixed at all. The curtain in the bedroom has a tear I've been meaning to repair for years. The level is still in a box somewhere — same box, probably, that it was in when I hung the whiteboard crooked. Things accumulate. Dust. Mail. The particular kind of entropy that sets in when the person maintaining the space is using all their energy on four tasks that most people do before they've finished their first cup of coffee.

*»You know what your house looks like. You know what the dishes situation is. You know about the pile. Everyone has a pile. Don't lie.«*

This is what survival looks like. Not the version in the books. Not the version with the arc and the turning point and the moment where the music swells. This version. The ceiling and the dogs and the faded whiteboard and the corner where the recliner used to be. The quiet house with no music. The phone face-down on the nightstand. Two checkmarks on a good day. Zero on the others.

The word "sometimes" in this chapter's title is doing a lot of heavy lifting. Existing is enough — sometimes. And sometimes it's not. Sometimes existing is just the thing that happens because the dogs need to eat and you're the only one here to feed them. There's no profundity in that. There's no lesson. There's just a man and three dogs and a whiteboard and another morning where the ceiling is the same and the crack has gotten a little wider and the air purifier is still wheezing and I'm still here.

I don't know why. But I'm still here.
