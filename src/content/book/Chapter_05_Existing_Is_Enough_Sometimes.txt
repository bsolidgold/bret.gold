PART II: When Breathing Feels Like Work
(Survival in the in-between — heavy, slow, stuck)

Chapter 5 - Existing Is Enough (Sometimes)
The whiteboard hangs on my wall like a monument to basic existence, its surface marked with four lines that shouldn’t need to be written down, shouldn’t require conscious effort, shouldn’t feel like mountains to climb. In their simplicity lies an unbearable weight, each word a reminder of how fundamentally trauma can reshape our relationship with the basic acts of being alive. These aren’t goals or aspirations—they’re negotiations with existence itself, daily contracts we make with our own survival.
»Go ahead and tell me you've never had a day where brushing your teeth felt like a heroic act. Tell me you've never measured a whole day's worth by whether you managed to leave one room. I'll wait.«
The whiteboard didn’t exist yet when Riley died. There were no four lines, no checkmarks, no system. There was just a body that forgot how to do the things it used to do without thinking. Waking up meant remembering, meant existing in a world where my best friend had chosen to leave it. The simple act of consciousness became a burden too heavy to bear, each moment of awareness a fresh encounter with loss. Getting out of bed took three days—three days of lying there, counting breaths, watching shadows crawl across the ceiling while the world outside continued its relentless motion. Taking a shower felt like an act of war against my own body, the simple mechanics of standing under water requiring a kind of strength I wasn’t sure I possessed. And going outside? That seemed impossible, a step too far into a world that kept spinning, kept moving, kept existing as if nothing had changed, as if my entire universe hadn’t just collapsed around me.

The whiteboard came later—about a year later—when I realized those four tasks had never gotten easy again. That they weren’t going to. That I needed to see them written down, needed the quiet accountability of a checkmark, because my body had learned a new default and that default was stillness. The whiteboard wasn’t born from one bad morning. It was born from hundreds of them.

After Justin died, the lines evolved into something even more fundamental. Each checkmark a testament to the kind of courage that nobody writes books about—the raw, unglamorous bravery of continuing to exist when existence itself feels optional. The victories became simultaneously enormous and embarrassingly small:  Managing to lift your head from the pillow after hours of staring at nothing. Putting both feet on the floor even if just to move to the couch. Standing under water while unsure if it’s tears or shower drops running down your face. Opening the front door just enough to take one breath of outside air.

The world doesn’t have metrics for these kinds of victories. There are no medals for getting out of bed when your bones feel filled with lead. No celebrations for the profound courage it takes to step into a shower when just existing feels like too much work. No recognition for the raw bravery of opening your front door when the world outside feels too loud, too bright, too real. Try explaining these triumphs to someone who measures success in promotions and purchases and Instagram-worthy moments. Try telling them that sometimes, victory looks like checking a box next to a basic human function, like managing to exist for one more day in a world that keeps taking pieces of you.
»There’s a pit in your stomach now. Because these are your victories too, aren’t they? The ones you never talk about. The ones that feel too small to celebrate but too heavy to ignore. The ones that live in the space between breathing and breaking.«
The whiteboard doesn’t judge. It maintains its quiet vigil, patient and unchanging, neither pushing nor pulling but simply existing as a reminder that survival sometimes looks like four simple lines. It doesn’t rush. Doesn’t compare today’s victories to yesterday’s failures. Doesn’t demand explanations for the days when even one checkmark feels impossible. It just waits, holding space for whatever version of survival we can manage on any given day.

What I’m learning, slowly and painfully in the space between these four lines, is that victory doesn’t always wear a medal, courage doesn’t always shout, and progress doesn’t always look like progress. Sometimes it looks like one checkmark. Sometimes it looks like trying again tomorrow when today was too heavy. The profound truth hiding in these simple lines is that survival doesn’t always look like victory—sometimes it looks like one foot in front of the other, sometimes it looks like nothing more than continuing to breathe when breathing itself feels like an act of defiance against gravity.

Only at the very end of this brutal lesson do we understand: these small victories aren’t small at all. They’re the foundation upon which all other survival is built, the quiet courage that makes any other kind of courage possible. In their simplicity lies their power—four lines that remind us that sometimes, existing is enough. One breath at a time, one checkmark at a time, one day at a time, until existing becomes a little easier, until breathing becomes a little lighter, until the victories start feeling a little less impossible.

In the space between heartbeats, between the moment you open your eyes and the moment you remember why breathing feels like labor, there’s a ceiling. White. Textured. The kind of surface that holds shadows in its valleys and creates patterns that shift with the angle of morning light. I’ve memorized every inch of it during these heavy mornings—the slight water stain in the corner from that time the roof leaked, the almost-perfect circle where a smoke detector used to be before I took it down because its blinking red light kept catching my eye at night, the barely visible crack that runs like a fault line toward the window. These imperfections have become familiar companions in the quiet hours when existence feels too heavy to bear.

My body carries a peculiar kind of weight during these moments. Not the kind that comes from physical exhaustion or too little sleep, but the kind that lives in your bones, that makes your chest feel like it’s being compressed under the weight of everything you’re carrying. Everything you’ve lost. Everything you’re afraid to lose. It’s the kind of weight that transforms simple existence into an act of defiance against gravity itself.
»You’ve tried to describe this to someone who doesn’t get it, haven’t you? And they said something like "just get up and move around, you’ll feel better." As if the weight pinning you to that mattress was a choice you were making for fun.«
Some days, just existing feels like climbing a mountain with weights strapped to your ankles, each breath a deliberate act of defiance against the gravity of loss, each moment of consciousness a negotiation with yourself about whether you have the strength to keep your eyes open, to keep breathing, to keep existing in a world that keeps taking pieces of you. Today is one of those days, when the simple act of being alive feels like an Olympic sport nobody trained you for.

Nobody posts this version of the highlight reel. There’s no content strategy for the days when victory looks like managing to swallow past the lump in your throat, when success is measured in breaths taken, in seconds survived, in the simple, brutal act of continuing to exist. The people screaming about discipline and no excuses from behind ring lights and carefully angled cameras — their metrics for progress don’t account for the profound courage it takes to simply stay when everything in you wants to disappear.

The light changes angle slightly, shifting the shadows on the ceiling. Time passing, marked only by the slow crawl of darkness across textured white paint. Within me, the voice of should rises like a tide: should get up, should shower, should eat something, should answer the messages piling up on my phone. But in this moment, in this space between breathing and breaking, between surviving and surrendering, perhaps existing is enough. Perhaps the most profound act of courage is simply staying, continuing to breathe when breathing itself feels like labor.

This is one of those days when survival doesn’t look like victory. When healing doesn’t look like progress. When existence doesn’t look like living. And maybe that’s okay. Maybe that has to be okay. Because sometimes, in the depths of grief, in the shadows of trauma, in the quiet spaces between heartbeats, existing is enough. It has to be.

The thing about trauma is that it fundamentally rewrites your relationship with existence itself. It teaches your body new ways to carry weight, embedding itself into your muscle memory like an unwanted skill, like your cells remember every loss, every goodbye, every moment someone who was supposed to stay decided to leave. These memories don’t just live in your mind—they take up residence in your body, transforming simple acts of being alive into exercises in endurance.

Each morning becomes a complex negotiation with gravity, not the kind that keeps planets in orbit, but the kind that lives in your chest, that makes your limbs feel like they’re made of depleted uranium, that turns basic tasks into monuments of effort. The simple mechanics of existing—breathing, moving, engaging with the world—become weighted with the invisible mass of everything you carry. Everything you’ve lost. Everything you’re afraid to lose.

I learned young that hunger was something you managed, not something you mentioned. When your food is rationed out each morning by a mother who treats feeding you like a chore she resents, you learn to make smallness an art form. You learn that need itself is an imposition.
»How old were you when you learned that needing something was the same as being a burden? Six? Eight? Some of us learned that lesson before we could spell the word.«
The daily rituals of being alive transform into intricate calculations of necessity versus capacity. Getting out of bed becomes an act of defiance against the weight pressing you into the mattress. Taking a shower feels like preparing for battle, each step a conscious decision to continue moving forward when stillness feels safer. Eating becomes a complex equation of necessity versus the effort required to swallow, to convince your stomach that yes, this is something we need to do, even when your body seems to have forgotten the basic mechanics of sustaining itself.

These tasks aren’t goals or aspirations, but necessary negotiations with existence. Simple things. Basic things. Things that shouldn’t need to be written down, things that shouldn’t require conscious effort. Yet here they are, transformed by grief and trauma into mountains that must be climbed daily, each one a testament to the weight of continuing to exist when existence itself feels optional.

The weight shows up in unexpected places, manifesting in the slight tremor of my hand when reaching for the glass of water that’s been sitting untouched on my nightstand for days, in the way my chest tightens when a song that Riley used to listen to on repeat catches me off guard, in the sudden vacuum of air when I look at the corner where Justin’s recliner used to be. These moments of heaviness aren’t constant—they ebb and flow like tides of gravity, sometimes receding enough to let me pretend I’m okay, sometimes surging with enough force to pin me in place.
»Why are you finding it hard to breathe? Because you have your own weights, don’t you? Your own songs that stop your heart, your own corners that steal your air, your own glasses of water that sit untouched while you try to gather the strength to reach for them.«
Some days, the weight becomes almost manageable, almost invisible. Those are the days when I can almost pretend that I’m healing, that I’m moving forward, that I’m progressing toward something that looks like recovery. But then there are the other days—the days when the weight becomes visible, tangible, manifesting as a physical presence pressing down on my chest, making each breath feel like a negotiation with my own body. These are the days when success looks different, when victory is measured not in accomplishments or progress, but in breaths taken, in moments survived, in the simple, stubborn act of continuing to exist despite everything inside screaming for release.

The shame arrives like an unwelcome companion, bringing with it a voice that insists I should be better by now.   Should be stronger. Should be healing faster.   But trauma doesn’t follow should.  Grief doesn’t operate on a timeline.  Loss doesn’t fade just because we think it’s been long enough.   The tonnage becomes most noticeable in these quiet moments, in the space between tasks, in the pause between breaths, in the silence that fills the room when I finally stop trying to pretend that I’m okay.

Each simple task becomes its own mountain to climb—responding to a text message feels like writing a novel, making a phone call feels like giving a speech to thousands, getting dressed feels like preparing for battle, eating feels like solving a complex puzzle. But here’s the truth about weight that nobody tells you: it doesn’t have to be lifted to be carried. Sometimes, just holding it steady is enough. Sometimes, just continuing to exist under its pressure is its own kind of victory.

There’s a particular kind of shame that blooms in the spaces between action, a specific flavor of self-loathing that grows in the moments when you can’t quite convince your body to move, your mind to focus, your heart to beat with any purpose beyond basic survival. It’s a shame that feeds on stillness, that grows stronger in the quiet moments when the world’s expectations collide with your limited capacity for existing.
»There’s that pit in your stomach again. You know this shame intimately. The kind that whispers that you should be doing more, being more, healing faster. What’s wrong with you?«
The world doesn’t pause for grief or trauma. It continues its relentless motion, generating emails that pile up unanswered, phone calls that go to voicemail, calendar invitations that feel like mountains to climb. Each notification becomes a reminder of your inability to keep pace with the world’s expectations, each unanswered message another brick in the wall of shame that builds itself around your stillness.

The voices start their chorus in these moments of perceived failure: 
“You should be over this by now.”
“Other people have it worse.”
“Just push through it.”
“Stop being lazy.”
“Get your shit together.”
“Why can’t you just be normal?”

They disguise themselves as motivation, as tough love, as the kind of hard truth that’s supposed to spark action and inspire movement. But they’re really just shame wearing a productivity mask, guilt masquerading as guidance.

I couldn’t be there when they cleaned out Riley’s house. The physical space where I found him had become a gravity well of trauma, a place where the air itself felt heavy with absence and questions that will never have answers. More shame. More weight. More voices whispering about weakness and failure and all the ways I should be stronger, better, more.
»You know these voices too. The ones that measure your worth in productivity, that count your value in tasks completed, that see rest as resistance and stillness as surrender.«
There’s a whole industry built on the idea that suffering is a choice you can outrun if you just want it badly enough. That pain is fuel. That if you’re not grinding through it, you’re not trying hard enough. Their carefully curated feeds don’t show the days when victory looks like managing to brush your teeth, when success is measured not in miles run or weights lifted, but in breaths taken, in moments survived—in the simple, brutal act of continuing to exist. They sell a version of struggle that always ends in a transformation montage, a highlight reel that makes trauma look like a stepping stone and rest like a character flaw.

But here’s what I’m learning, slowly and painfully, in the spaces between breaths and heartbeats and moments of overwhelming shame: Trauma doesn’t operate on society’s timeline. Grief doesn’t follow a productivity schedule. Healing doesn’t conform to corporate deadlines. The shame wants us to believe that if we’re not constantly moving, we’re not trying. That if we’re not visibly struggling, we’re not really healing. That if our survival doesn’t look like their definition of success, we’re somehow failing.

But what if the most profound acts of courage happen in stillness? What if the deepest healing happens in the spaces between action? What if survival sometimes looks like saying no, staying still, choosing stillness over motion? I couldn’t be there for Justin’s mom that day. Couldn’t face Riley’s house. Couldn’t push through or rise above or any of the other phrases people use when they want pain to look prettier than it is.

The shame of that lives in my chest like a stone, but I’m still here. Still breathing. Still existing. And some days, that’s enough. It has to be. Because the alternative—the alternative is not surviving at all. And I’ve lost too many people to that alternative to believe in shame’s lies anymore.

~~~

The ceiling maintains its familiar patterns as evening approaches—same texture, same shadows, same slight water stain in the corner, same almost-perfect circle where the smoke detector used to be, same crack running like a fault line toward the window. But something has shifted in the space between morning and now, something barely perceptible yet undeniably present. It’s not progress in any traditional sense, not healing as the world would measure it, but rather a subtle shift in the gravity pressing down, like learning to breathe underwater.

The whiteboard maintains its quiet vigil on the wall, patient and unchanging, with its four simple lines that have become both challenge and comfort.

Today brought two checkmarks. Tomorrow might bring three, or perhaps just one, or maybe none at all. And in this moment of quiet reflection, that feels okay. Because beneath the weight of expectations, beyond the metrics of traditional success, there’s a profound truth: the world keeps taking pieces of me, but it hasn’t taken all of me yet.

The empty corner where Justin’s recliner used to be still holds its gravity well of memory. The memories of coming home to Riley’s terrible one-man karaoke sessions remain ,quiet with the particular silence of absence. The grief still sits in my chest like a stone, and the trauma still lives in my bones like a second skeleton. Yet somehow, through all of this, through the weight and the shame and the endless negotiations with existence itself, something remains. Something continues. Something survives.

The world outside maintains its relentless motion, generating expectations and demands and the kind of success that looks good on resumes and social media feeds. But in here, in this quiet space between heartbeats, between breaths, between moments of overwhelming gravity—a different kind of victory emerges. It’s not the kind that wins medals or generates likes or earns promotions. It’s the kind that lives in the simple, stubborn act of continuing to breathe when breathing feels like labor, in the quiet courage of staying present when presence feels like too much work.

Nobody writes books about these kinds of victories. But in the space between grief and survival, between trauma and persistence, between loss and continuing, these are the victories that matter most.

What I’m learning, in this long journey through loss and survival, is that courage doesn’t always wear a cape, strength doesn’t always lift weights, and success doesn’t always climb ladders. Sometimes courage looks like opening your eyes when sleep feels safer. Sometimes strength looks like breathing through the moments when breathing feels impossible. Sometimes success looks like whatever version of survival we can manage that day.

The ceiling holds its patterns in shadow and light as evening approaches, a silent witness to another day of choosing to stay. The world continues its relentless motion, carrying its expectations and demands like banners into battle. But in here, in this quiet space between presence and absence, between weight and weightlessness, something profound emerges: you made it through another day. You chose to stay.

And in the end, perhaps that’s not just enough—perhaps that’s everything. Because in a world that keeps taking pieces of us, that keeps expecting motion and progress and performative healing, the simple act of continuing to exist becomes its own kind of revolution. A quiet rebellion against the gravity of loss. A gentle defiance of trauma’s undertow. A stubborn insistence on breathing when every breath feels like a decision you have to make on purpose.

The light fades, the shadows shift, and still we remain. Still we breathe. Still we choose to stay. And in that choice—that simple, profound, everyday choice—lies a victory that doesn’t fit on any poster or in any book. We’re still here. And maybe that’s the most important victory of all.
