Chapter 12 - Between Before and After

There’s a particular kind of vertigo that comes with existing in the space between what was and what might be. Not the dramatic, cinematic moment of transformation that self-help books promise, but the long, disorienting stretch of time where you’re no longer who you were but haven’t yet become whoever you’re supposed to be next. It’s like being caught between floors in a broken elevator—not quite here, not quite there, just suspended in the mechanical hum of waiting for something to shift.
»You know this space, don’t you? This liminal hell where nothing fits quite right anymore. Where your old life feels like clothes you’ve outgrown but your new life hasn’t arrived yet. Where every mirror shows you a stranger wearing your face, and you can’t tell if you’re becoming or unbecoming.«
I’ve been living here for what feels like years now—in this indefinite pause between the person I was before Riley’s door, before Justin’s machines went quiet, before my shoulder surgery forced me to write instructions for basic existence on a whiteboard. Not because I needed the reminder, but because seeing it every day felt more honest than pretending I had my shit together.
The before is clear enough. Sharp-edged and definite, carved into memory with the precision of trauma. I know exactly who I was at 6:42 AM on that Saturday morning, thirty seconds before I saw Riley. I can map the exact geography of my ignorance, the specific weight of not-yet-knowing that I’ll never fully inhabit again. That version of me—the one who hadn’t yet learned how thin the membrane is between here and gone—he’s preserved in amber, frozen in the moment before everything changed.
But the after? That’s where things get complicated. Because despite what every grief narrative wants you to believe, there’s no clean moment of emergence. No butterfly breaking free from its chrysalis. No phoenix rising from perfectly photogenic ashes. Just this extended season of being neither caterpillar nor butterfly, neither ash nor phoenix, neither who you were nor who you might someday be.
The Geography of Limbo
Time moves differently in transitional spaces. It pools and eddies, speeds up and slows down according to laws that have nothing to do with clocks or calendars. A single hour can feel like a week when you’re waiting for something to change. A month can disappear in what feels like an afternoon when you’re too exhausted to mark its passage.
I’ve started measuring time in different units entirely. Not days or weeks, but in the space between text messages I can’t quite bring myself to answer. Not months or years, but in the accumulation of dust on things I used to care about. The guitar in the corner hasn’t been touched in—how long? The books on my shelf have developed that particular patina of abandonment that comes from being looked at but never opened.
»You have your own measurements, don’t you? Your own way of marking time in this in-between space. Maybe it’s the number of times you’ve reheated the same cup of coffee. Maybe it’s the episodes of that show you’re rewatching because anything new requires too much attention. Maybe it’s the growing space between “I should” and “I will.”«
The world outside continues its relentless forward motion, generating birthdays and deadlines and milestones that feel like they belong to a different timeline entirely. Friends get married, have kids, change jobs, buy houses—all while you’re stuck in this temporal eddy, watching their lives stream past through the window of social media like you’re watching a movie about people you used to know.
They send invitations to things. Parties, dinners, casual get-togethers that feel anything but casual when you’re trying to figure out how to explain that you’re not quite a person right now. That you’re in between versions of yourself and neither one is particularly good at small talk. That showing up requires a kind of performance you can’t quite manage—pretending to be the person they remember while hiding the fact that person doesn’t exist anymore.
So you develop elaborate architectures of avoidance. Sorry, can’t make it. Something came up. Maybe next time. Each excuse another brick in the wall you’re building between yourself and a world that keeps expecting you to be definable, to be progressing, to be anything other than this formless in-between thing you’ve become.
The geography of limbo has its own landmarks. The couch that’s developed a permanent impression of your body. The corner where you stand sometimes, not going anywhere, just existing in the space between rooms. The bathroom mirror you avoid because the face looking back doesn’t match any version of yourself you remember or anticipate.

The Most Literal In-Between

Sometimes the between-before-and-after isn’t metaphorical. Sometimes it’s just a seventeen-year-old standing on a sidewalk with a garbage bag of clothes, watching a door close behind him.

My dad kicked me out. I don’t even remember the exact words — not the way I remember Riley’s door or the sound of Justin’s machines. It wasn’t cinematic. There was no dramatic speech, no slow-motion hallway walk, no swelling soundtrack. Just a decision someone made about whether I was worth the trouble of keeping around, and the answer was no. One of those things that should shatter a kid but instead just sort of... lands. Like a fact. Like weather. Like something that was always going to happen and you were stupid for thinking it wouldn’t.

Before the actual kicking-out, there was the slow rehearsal for it. My dad had a new girlfriend. He’d all but abandoned me at the house to go live with her — I don’t mean emotionally abandoned, I mean physically gone. The fridge was empty. Not "we’re running low on groceries" empty. Empty. The kind of empty where you open it out of habit and then stand there feeling like an idiot because you already knew. You opened it twenty minutes ago. Nothing materialized in the time between.

*»You’ve opened that fridge. Maybe not the same one, but the same empty. The one that’s less about food and more about the specific humiliation of needing something this basic and not having it.«*

His girlfriend didn’t want me around. I was — what? An inconvenience. A reminder that he had a life before her. She had a son, younger than me by about eight years, and I was the wrong kid in the wrong house. She called the cops on me once. I can’t even remember what I supposedly did — something unreasonable she wanted me to do that I wouldn’t. That’s what it was. Not that I did something wrong. That I wouldn’t comply with something absurd, and her response was to call the police on a teenager in his own house. Or what he thought was his own house. Turns out it was hers, and I was just borrowing the space.

The kicking-out itself was almost a relief. Almost. The way a diagnosis can be a relief — not because the news is good but because at least the waiting is over. At least the thing you’ve been bracing for has finally landed.

But then you’re standing on a sidewalk. Seventeen. And the geography changes completely.

Here’s what nobody tells you about being homeless at seventeen: it’s boring. Not the romantic, on-the-road, Kerouac bullshit. Not the gritty-but-triumphant overcoming story. It’s just boring and embarrassing and logistically exhausting. Where do you sleep? Depends on the night. Where do you eat? Depends on who’s around. Where do you keep your stuff? You don’t have stuff. That’s sort of the point.

I’d already lost my close friend group — we’d had to move out of the neighborhood in Sandy where I actually had people, where I’d built something that looked like belonging. Couldn’t afford it anymore. So that was already gone before the sidewalk. Before the garbage bag. The geography of my life had been shrinking for a while, and this was just the final contraction.

Six to eight months. That’s how long I was homeless. I say "six to eight" because I genuinely don’t know exactly. Time gets weird when you don’t have a fixed address. Days blur into this shapeless thing where the only structure comes from trying to figure out the next night, the next meal, the next few hours. You’re not thinking about the future because the future is an abstraction that requires a foundation to build on, and your foundation just closed a door on you.

*»Six to eight months. Say it out loud if you want. Let it sit in your mouth. That’s not a weekend. That’s not a rough patch. That’s a season of a kid’s life spent trying to solve a problem that no kid should have to solve.«*

Jerry’s dad changed everything. And by "everything" I mean the specific, narrow everything of having somewhere to sleep. Jerry was a friend, and his dad just — offered. That was it. No speeches. No conditions. No paperwork or formal arrangement. Just a man seeing a kid who needed a room and saying yes.

I don’t know how to talk about Jerry’s dad without it sounding like a Hallmark movie, and I hate that. Because it wasn’t heartwarming. It was a band-aid on a wound that went down to the bone. It was someone being decent when decency felt like a foreign concept. I was grateful in the way you’re grateful for a tourniquet — yes, thank you, I’m not bleeding out anymore, but I still got fucking stabbed.

The room at Jerry’s house was mine and not mine. The food in the fridge was available and not mine. The family dinners were inclusive and not mine. I existed in the most literal between-before-and-after I’d ever known — no longer homeless but not homed. No longer abandoned but not claimed. Occupying space in someone else’s life while the space where my own life should have been sat empty.

I went to school. I want to make sure that’s in here because it sounds insane. I was a homeless seventeen-year-old attending high school like everything was fine. Sitting in classrooms learning about the Pythagorean theorem while my entire survival infrastructure had collapsed. Teachers asking about homework while I was calculating whether I’d eat that night. The surreal normalcy of it — the absolute batshit disconnect between what was happening in those fluorescent-lit rooms and what was happening in my actual life — that’s the part I still can’t fully explain.

*»Ever had two realities running simultaneously? The public one where you perform normalcy so convincingly that nobody asks questions, and the private one where you’re solving problems that would make the people around you uncomfortable if they knew? Yeah. Seventeen.«*

You’d think getting kicked out would feel like a clear dividing line. Before: kid with a roof, even a shitty one. After: kid without. But it didn’t feel that clean. It felt like I’d been falling for years and just hadn’t hit the ground until that moment. The food scarcity, the moving, the girlfriends who didn’t want me around, the neighborhood I couldn’t afford — those were all just stations on the way down. The sidewalk with the garbage bag was just where the falling finally stopped.

Or maybe it didn’t stop. Maybe I’m still in the space between that fall and whatever landing is supposed to come next.

The Geography of Losing God (and Everyone Who Came With Him)

The other literal between-before-and-after — the one that hollowed me out in a different way — happened slower. No single door closing. Just a gradual realization that I was standing in a building I no longer believed in, surrounded by people whose love had a condition I could no longer meet.

I served a two-year mission in Vanuatu. Gave everything to it. Learned a language that didn’t have a written form by pure immersion — no textbooks, no Rosetta Stone, just listening and failing and listening again until the words started to mean something. Almost got killed for baptizing a woman whose father was a chief and didn’t appreciate the interference. Carried a machete everywhere I went. Had a dog named Badge who probably saved my life more than once.

I did the thing. All of it. The white shirts, the name tags, the door-knocking, the praying, the believing — or at least the trying to believe hard enough that it would become real. I gave the church two years of my life in one of the most remote places on earth, and I meant it. That part matters. I wasn’t going through the motions. I was all in.

Then I joined the Air Force and got stationed in Germany. And somewhere in the middle of all that distance — from Utah, from the church buildings, from the people who reinforced the belief system every Sunday — something started to crack. Not dramatically. Not a lightning bolt of atheism or some intellectual revelation. Just a quiet, steady erosion. Like standing in a river and feeling the sand shift under your feet, grain by grain, until you realize you’re not standing where you started.

*»You know what I mean. Maybe not about God specifically, but about the moment you realized something you’d built your entire identity around wasn’t real anymore. Or wasn’t yours anymore. Same thing, really.«*

I didn’t just leave Mormonism. I left all of it. Not just the specific theology, not just the cultural stuff — the whole framework. God, Jesus, the afterlife, the idea that someone was keeping score, that suffering had a purpose, that there was a plan. All of it. Gone. Not angrily. Not rebelliously. Just... gone. Like waking up one morning and discovering a room in your house that you thought was full of furniture is actually empty, and you can’t remember when the furniture left or if it was ever really there.

The decision itself wasn’t the hard part. The hard part was coming home.

My family didn’t want me. That’s not self-pity — it’s just geography. I’d been kicked out at seventeen, been homeless, gone on a mission, joined the military. There was no home to come back to. No bedroom with my stuff in it. No place at a dinner table. And now, on top of all that existing not-belonging, I’d added the one thing that could make it worse: I’d left the church.

In Mormon culture — and I don’t know how to explain this to people who didn’t grow up in it — leaving the church isn’t just a personal decision about faith. It’s a social catastrophe. The church isn’t just where you go on Sundays. It’s your neighborhood, your social network, your emergency contacts, your Friday nights, your community service, your identity. When you leave, you don’t just lose God. You lose everyone who came with Him.

Friends I’d known since childhood stopped calling. Not all at once — it was more of a fade. Invitations that stopped arriving. Conversations that got shorter. That particular look people get when they’re being polite but have already reclassified you in their heads. You go from being one of them to being a project — someone to pray for, to worry about, to bring back into the fold. And when it becomes clear you’re not coming back, you go from project to ghost.

*»There’s a word for what happens when the structure that held your social life together dissolves and nobody builds a new one. I think the word is "Tuesday." Or "Wednesday." Or whatever day of the week you’re reading this alone.«*

So there I was. No family that wanted me. No church community. No military community anymore — I’d separated from the Air Force. An ex-fiancee who’d cheated on me and then acted like the engagement had never happened. Standing in the most complete version of between-before-and-after I’d ever experienced: every single structure that humans use to feel like they belong to something — family, faith, community, partnership — stripped away simultaneously. Not one by one, giving you time to adjust. All at once, like someone pulled a tablecloth out from under the entire dinner setting of my life.

I didn’t know what to do with that kind of alone. The kicked-out-at-seventeen version of alone at least had practical problems to solve — where to sleep, what to eat. This was different. This was existential. This was standing in the middle of a life that had no container for it, no framework, no community, no one waiting for you anywhere.

Jiu-jitsu eventually filled some of that space. Years later, walking into a gym and finding a room full of people who didn’t care what you believed or didn’t believe, who only cared whether you showed up and whether you’d roll. A community built on the simple, honest foundation of shared suffering on a mat — no theology required, no tithing, no dress code beyond whatever you were willing to sweat through. But that’s a different chapter, a different between.

The point is the gap. The space between having every structure and having none. Between being somebody’s son, somebody’s missionary, somebody’s airman, somebody’s fiance, somebody’s ward member — and being nobody’s anything. That’s the between-before-and-after that doesn’t get talked about enough. The kind where the "before" was already broken and the "after" hasn’t shown up yet and you’re just standing there, in the middle of your own life, with absolutely nothing holding you in place.

*»If you’re waiting for the part where I tell you how I got through it, I don’t have that for you. I got through it the way everyone gets through it — slowly, badly, without any sense that I was getting through anything. One day I was in it and then more days happened and eventually I was in something else. That’s not a strategy. That’s just time doing what time does.«*

The Weight of Unfinished Transformations
There’s a special kind of exhaustion that comes from being constantly in transition. It’s not the clean fatigue of completed effort or the satisfying tiredness of achievement. It’s the bone-deep weariness of holding multiple versions of yourself in suspension, never quite able to let go of who you were, never quite able to grasp who you’re becoming.
The old me—the one from before—he still has his moments. Still reaches for his phone to text Riley something funny. Still starts to tell stories that include Justin like he’s going to walk through the door any minute. That version of me lives in muscle memory and unconscious habit, in the automatic responses that haven’t gotten the memo that everything has changed.
But there’s this other version too—shadowy, unformed, more possibility than person. The me that might exist on the other side of all this. The one who’s learned to carry these losses without being crushed by them. The one who’s figured out how to be in the world again without constantly looking for the exits. I can almost see him sometimes, like a figure in peripheral vision that disappears when you turn to look directly.
»Be honest—do you even remember the last time you felt like a finished version of yourself? Or has it always been this? One foot in someone you used to be, the other in someone you’re not sure you’ll ever become. Neither one fitting right.«
The problem is, you can’t just decide to be done with transition. Can’t just wake up one morning and declare yourself transformed. The in-between has its own timeline, its own requirements, its own mysterious process that resists every attempt to rush or control it.
I’ve tried to force it. Tried to accelerate through this liminal space like it’s just a particularly long hallway between rooms. Made lists of who I want to be on the other side: stronger, wiser, more capable of handling loss without falling apart. But transformation doesn’t respond to project management. Becoming doesn’t give a shit about your timeline.
So here I sit, suspended between versions of myself like a software update that’s stuck at 67% and has been for months. Not quite the old operating system, not quite the new one. Just this glitchy in-between state where some functions work and others throw error messages, where the whole system runs slower than it should, where you’re never quite sure if you’re making progress or corrupting data.
The Rituals of Waiting
In the absence of clear direction, we create our own structures. Small rituals that mark time, that create the illusion of progress, that give shape to days that would otherwise blur into an endless stream of sameness.
Small rituals become altars to basic functionality. Not because checking boxes means anything profound, but because they create nodes in the day, points of reference in the temporal fog. Getting up becomes an event. Getting dressed becomes an achievement. Each tiny act becomes a conquest.
These aren’t the kinds of milestones anyone else would recognize. They don’t make good stories at parties. Nobody’s putting them on a highlight reel. But in the geography of transition, they’re everything. They’re proof that you’re still here, still trying, still moving even when movement feels impossible.
I’ve developed other rituals too. The specific order in which I check apps on my phone—Instagram, Reddit, Twitter, repeat—like a rosary of digital distraction. The way I arrange objects on my nightstand, creating small geometries of control in a world that feels fundamentally chaotic. The careful choreography of avoiding certain songs, certain places, certain memories that threaten to collapse this fragile in-between space I’m trying to inhabit.
»What’s yours? The app you open without thinking? The route you drive that doesn’t go anywhere? The thing you rearrange on the counter because at least that’s one thing you can control? We all have them—these tiny liturgies of coping that nobody taught us but we perform religiously anyway.«
Some days, the rituals are enough. They create a kind of scaffolding that holds you upright, that gives shape to the shapelessness of being between selves. Other days, they feel like playing house in a burning building—maintaining these small pretenses of normalcy while everything around you is fundamentally not normal at all.
But we keep performing them anyway. Keep checking boxes and arranging objects and maintaining routines that might mean nothing but at least mean consistently nothing. Because in the absence of transformation, in the long wait between before and after, consistency becomes its own kind of progress.
The Company We Keep in Limbo
The strangest thing about existing in transitional space is discovering how many other people are secretly here too. Not the ones posting their highlight reels and transformation stories, but the quiet ones. The ones who understand what it means to be between versions of yourself. The ones who don’t ask “how are you?” because they know the answer is too complicated for casual conversation.
They recognize each other, these in-between people. There’s something in the eyes, maybe. A particular quality of exhaustion that has nothing to do with sleep. A way of occupying space that’s both present and absent. A careful way of speaking that avoids both past and future tense, living instead in the eternal, indefinite now.
We find each other in unexpected places. In waiting rooms where everyone is suspended between diagnosis and prognosis. In support groups where the word “recovery” feels like a foreign language nobody quite speaks fluently. In the comments sections of posts about grief, about loss, about the strange territory between what was and what might be.
There’s comfort in this strange communion of the unfinished. In knowing you’re not the only one stuck in transition, not the only one who can’t quite figure out how to be a person right now. Not the only one measuring progress in microscopic increments that wouldn’t register on anyone else’s scale.
The Acceptance That Isn’t Quite Peace
Eventually—and I use that word loosely because time has no meaning here—something shifts. Not dramatically. Not suddenly. Just a subtle adjustment in how you hold the weight of being in between. A slight loosening of the grip on who you used to be. A tentative allowance for who you might become.
It’s not acceptance in the way self-help books describe it—some moment of serene understanding where everything clicks into place. It’s messier than that. More like exhausted surrender. Like your psyche finally gets too tired to keep fighting against the reality of where you are and just… stops. Lets you be neither here nor there. Lets you exist in the indefinite space between definitions.
»Maybe you’ve been there. That moment where you stop wrestling with it—not because you’ve made peace, but because you’re too tired to keep fighting something that doesn’t fight back. It just sits there. And eventually, so do you.«
This isn’t giving up. It’s not giving in either. It’s something else—a recognition that maybe the in-between isn’t just a waiting room. Maybe it’s its own valid space. Maybe being between selves isn’t a problem to be solved but a reality to be inhabited, however temporarily, however uncomfortably.
Things are still off-kilter. I’ve stopped seeing that as something that needs fixing. Started seeing it instead as an honest acknowledgment that off-kilter is where I live right now, and that’s okay. Or if not okay, at least true. And sometimes true is all we get.
The Slow Geography of Becoming
There’s no map for this territory. No guidebook for navigating the space between who you were and who you’ll be. No timeline for how long you’ll have to live in this suspended state where nothing quite fits and everything feels temporary.
But maybe that’s the point. Maybe the in-between isn’t meant to be rushed through or optimized or transformed into content. Maybe it’s meant to be exactly what it is—a space of not-knowing, of not-yet-being, of existing in the question rather than forcing an answer.
Some mornings, I wake up and for a split second, I forget. Forget that Riley is gone. Forget that Justin isn’t coming back. Forget that I’m different now, changed in ways I’m still discovering. In that brief moment of forgetting, I’m nobody—not the before version, not the after version, just consciousness without context.
And sometimes, in that space between sleep and waking, between forgetting and remembering, I catch a glimpse of something. Not who I was. Not who I’ll be. But who I am, right now, in this indefinite space between definitions. This person who exists in the in-between, who’s learning to breathe in transitional air, who’s finding ways to be present in a timeline that doesn’t quite make sense.
It’s not comfortable. It’s not inspiring. It’s not the kind of transformation story that sells books or gets speaking engagements. But it’s real. It’s honest. It’s what actual change looks like—not a butterfly emerging from a cocoon, but something stranger, more gradual, more uncertain.
»You’re still here, aren’t you? Still existing in your own in-between space. And maybe that’s enough. Maybe that’s everything. Maybe the courage isn’t in transforming but in being willing to exist in the space where transformation might eventually happen. Or might not. And being okay with not knowing which.«
The future remains unwritten. The past remains unchangeable. And here, in the space between before and after, we continue to exist. Not quite who we were. Not yet who we’ll be. Just present in the indefinite now, learning to live with the questions, learning to breathe in transitional air, learning that sometimes the most profound act of courage is simply staying in the in-between until it’s ready to let us go.
Or until we’re ready to let it go.
Whichever comes first.
If either ever does.
And somehow, that’s where we live now. In this suspended space between definitions, with nothing but the in-between. And maybe—just maybe—that’s all we need.
