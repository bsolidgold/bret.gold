PART I: The Pause Before the Storm
(Anticipation, fear, the weight of what's coming)

Chapter 1 - The Stillness Between Waves
Most people think growth feels like something. Like a moment you can point to and say, that’s when it happened. They think it’s cinematic—a montage set to an epic soundtrack.
»Look at you, curled up in your nest of unwashed blankets, hungry but too hollow to eat, reading this and hoping—really fucking hoping—that someone’s finally going to tell you how to fix the broken thing inside you. That empty space behind your ribs that feels like it’s getting bigger every day. I see you. I am you. And I wish I could lie to you about what’s coming.«
Sweat dripping, fists clenching, slow-motion shots of triumph against a glowing skyline. They think it feels good. Like momentum. Like clarity. Like the pieces falling into place in real-time.

But real growth? The kind that actually matters? It doesn’t feel like any of that. It feels like nothing. Like pulling the same hoodie over your head every morning at 6:43 AM, the fabric worn soft from repetition, and staring at the same spot on the floor—that dark smudge by the baseboard that might be a scuff mark or might be a shadow or might be slobber flung from your dog’s jowls or it might be nothing at all. Like opening Instagram for the fourteenth time in an hour, your thumb moving on autopilot: scroll, pause, scroll, close app. Open app. Scroll again. The same posts. The same faces. The same hollow attempt to feel something, anything, besides the drag of being awake.

It feels like being stuck.

Not trapped-in-quicksand stuck, not buried-under-rubble stuck, not any kind of stuck that would make a good story later. It’s the kind of stuck that creeps in quietly, so gradually you don’t notice until it’s too late. Like water seeping under a door during a storm, pooling in the corners of your mind until you realize you’re standing knee-deep in something you can’t escape.

Most of the time, growth feels like treading water. The endless, exhausting work of keeping your head above the surface when every muscle burns with the effort of not drowning. There’s no shoreline to swim toward, no clear direction that leads to safety. Just you and the water and the growing certainty that letting go would be easier than fighting to stay afloat.

But here’s the thing about floating: you’re not sinking.

And as pointless and exhausting as it seems—just existing in that stillness, just holding on—it still counts. Even if it doesn’t feel like movement, even if it doesn’t look like progress from the outside. Staying afloat means you’re still here.

I’ve spent more time than I’d like to admit existing in that stillness. Not just days or weeks—years. Staring at ceilings, counting the spiderwebbed cracks in the corner that spread a little wider every winter. Scrolling through Reddit until my eyes blur, reading the same threads over and over because, hey, at least they’re familiar, at least they’re safe. Laying in bed until my body aches, and its parts feel foreign to me, like a crumpled marionette discarded on a mattress that’s gone concave from the weight of my inertia.

Time doesn’t flow in the stillness—it bleeds. Days seep into each other like watercolors left in the rain, formless and heavy. Tuesday could be Saturday and Saturday could be any day at all, distinguished only by the shifting angles of sunlight through dusty blinds and the occasional buzz of a phone I’ve lost the energy to check.

Here’s something they don’t tell you about this kind of survival: it’s boring. It’s not loud or explosive or thrilling. It’s quiet. It’s painfully quiet. And it happens in the tiniest, most unremarkable moments.

It happens when you force yourself to drink a glass of water because you know your body needs it, even though the cup feels too heavy and the water tastes like metal and nothing feels right but you do it anyway. It happens when you answer one text message—just one—because you hope someone out there might notice if you do. It happens when you stand in the shower for an hour, forehead pressed against cold tile, because the idea of stepping out and facing the day feels impossible but at least you’re vertical, at least you’re trying.

No one celebrates those moments. There are no gold stars for getting out of bed.
»You want to know the really pathetic part? Part of you is still waiting for someone to notice. To see how hard you’re trying. To understand what it costs you to do these basic fucking human things that everyone else seems to manage without thinking. Like maybe if someone just acknowledged how much energy it takes to exist, it wouldn’t feel like such a waste of oxygen.«
No applause for brushing your teeth. No parade for eating something other than stale Doritos and warm Mountain Dew for dinner. But those moments count. Every single one of them. Even if no one sees them. Even if no one knows how much they cost you.

There was a morning in late February—one of those gray, formless mornings when winter feels eternal—where I stared at my bedroom wall for what felt like hours. Dogs snoring. The air purifier wheezed in the corner, its rhythmic rattle making the room feel colder than it was. The sun carved thin lines through the gaps in my curtains, turning dust motes into silent snow, each particle catching light then disappearing like a dying star. I was still. Not peaceful still. Not meditative still. Paralyzed still. My body felt like concrete, each limb weighted with invisible sandbags, while my brain sparked with static so loud it felt physical. I was stuck in that place between sleep and wakefulness where reality feels just thin enough to break if you press too hard.

I couldn’t get up. Couldn’t move. Couldn’t even cry. I just… existed. Listening to the air purifier’s asthmatic wheeze, watching shadows creep across the wall, feeling the weight of every breath like stones piling on my chest.

I think people assume moments like that come with some kind of grand emotional climax—that you’ll hit rock bottom and have a revelation, some lightning-strike epiphany that snaps you out of it. That’s what movies have taught us to expect: the dramatic turning point, the moment where everything changes.

But most of the time, there’s nothing. Just silence. Just the weight of existing. Just the slow, steady certainty that this is all there is.

And then, eventually—almost accidentally—I moved. Not because I wanted to. Not because I had some sudden burst of motivation or clarity. I just… moved. Swung one leg out from under the blanket. Then the other. Stood up. Walked to the bathroom. Filled a cup with water that still tasted like metal but I drank it anyway.

It didn’t feel like victory. It didn’t feel like progress. It felt mechanical. Like I was just checking off boxes on some invisible survival list.

But it mattered.

I know it mattered because six months later, I found myself staring at a whiteboard hung crookedly on my bedroom wall, four simple tasks written in shaky letters with a blue marker that was already starting to dry out: Wake up. Get out of bed. Take a shower. Go outside
»You’ve got lists too, haven’t you? Don’t lie. The pathetic little checkboxes you make for yourself, trying to prove you’re still human, still functioning, still here. The ones you can’t even look at most days because they’re just four more ways you’ve failed before noon.«
Four steps. Four stupidly simple steps that shouldn’t have been hard. But they were. They were so hard. And they would stay on that board for years, long after their original purpose had faded, like scars that never quite heal but eventually become part of your skin’s geography.

The whiteboard wasn’t my idea—not exactly. It came later, after the shoulder surgery, when I knew I’d be facing a different kind of stillness. A medically mandated stillness. The kind that comes with painkillers and physical therapy and the particular challenge of trying to brush your teeth with your non-dominant hand.

But that’s getting ahead of the story.

First, you need to understand what it feels like to be so stuck that even the simplest tasks feel monumental. To be so lost in the stillness that even breathing feels like an act of defiance. Because without that understanding, what came next—the whiteboard, the surgery, the slow crawl back to something resembling normalcy—won’t make sense.

And maybe that’s the point. Maybe none of it makes sense until you’ve been there. Until you’ve felt the gravity of consciousness pressing down on you so hard that even opening your eyes in the morning feels like lifting mountains.

The whiteboard wasn’t anything special. Just a basic Office Depot model, twenty-four by thirty-six inches, with aluminum metal edges that gave an I belong in an office feel. I hung it crooked—not intentionally, but my level was in a box somewhere and I couldn’t muster the energy to find it. The surface had a handful of scratches, remnants from its previous life in my garage where I’d used it to track workout routines I never actually completed. Now it hung opposite my bed, its blank surface reflecting morning light like a dull mirror.

I dug it up three days before shoulder surgery. Not because I wanted to. Not because I felt inspired. Because I knew what was coming—the fog of painkillers, the isolation, the particular kind of crazy that sets in when you can’t wipe your own ass without a strategic battle plan. I knew myself well enough to know that post-surgery me wouldn’t trust his own brain. Wouldn’t be able to distinguish up from down, need from want, essential from optional.

So I prepared.

The bidet came first—a basic model from Amazon because wiping my ass with my left hand wasn’t an option unless I wanted to test the limits of human flexibility and dignity. Then the electric toothbrush, because the idea of manipulating a manual one with my non-dominant hand seemed like an invitation to either chip a tooth or stab myself in the gums. The nylon rope for the shower was my physical therapist’s idea—something to loop over my neck and clutch in the hand of my recovering arm, holding the weight of the temporarily-useless limb; an insurance policy against becoming another slipped-in-the-shower statistic.

But the whiteboard? That was all me. A desperate attempt to give structure to what I knew would become structureless days. To break down survival into steps so basic they’d be insulting if they weren’t so necessary.

I wrote the list with a blue Expo marker that squeaked against the surface, the sound sharp in my pre-surgery bedroom where everything felt too quiet, too still, too much like waiting.

Wake up.
Get out of bed. Take a shower. Go outside

Four simple tasks that shouldn’t have felt impossible. Four basic human functions that were about to become my entire world. I knew it was coming. Could feel it in my bones, in my breath, in the way my hand shook while writing those words.

But I just stood there. Watching. Waiting. Living in that space between now and next, between whole and broken, between standing and falling.

The marker squeaked against the whiteboard’s surface. The sound felt sharp in the pre-surgery quiet of my bedroom. Everything felt sharp. The edges of the mirror. The fluorescent light buzzing overhead. The smell of antibacterial soap that I’d been using to prep my skin.

I should have been sleeping. Should have been resting. Should have been doing any of the hundred things the doctor told me to do to prepare.

Instead, I just stood there. Existing in that moment before everything changed. That pocket of time where change feels both inevitable and impossible. Where you can still pretend, just for a second, that maybe this time will be different.

Maybe this time the glass won’t break. Maybe this time you’ll move fast enough. Maybe this time gravity will forget how to pull.

But gravity never forgets. And glasses always break. And change always comes.

No matter how still you stand. No matter how long you wait. No matter how hard you try to stretch that moment into forever.

The fall always happens.

And maybe that’s the point. Maybe that’s why we freeze. Maybe that’s why we wait. Because in that suspended space between recognition and consequence, we get to feel whole one last time. Get to exist in the moment before the break. Get to remember what it feels like to be unbroken, even if we know—with perfect, aching clarity—that wholeness is about to end.

Later, much later, when the surgery was done and the pain meds had worn off and I was staring at those four instructions like they were written in some ancient, indecipherable language, I thought about that moment in the bathroom. About the way time had stretched. About how peaceful it had felt, right before everything changed.

I thought about all the glasses I’ve watched fall. All the conversations I’ve felt fracture. All the moments I’ve seen coming but couldn’t stop.

And I realized something: it’s not the impact that breaks you. It’s not the shatter or the spray or the mess on the floor. It’s not even the cleanup—the careful gathering of sharp edges, the methodical piecing together of what can be saved and what has to be thrown away.

It’s the moment before. The suspended space where choice lives. Where you could move, could reach, could change everything—but you don’t.

Maybe because you’re tired. Maybe because you’re scared. Maybe because part of you needs to see how the pieces land.

Or maybe because sometimes, in that strange stillness between recognition and consequence, you find something like peace. Something like acceptance. Something like understanding that not every fall needs to be caught.

Not every glass needs to be saved. Not every bridge needs to stay standing. Not every piece needs to be gathered.

Sometimes you just need to let yourself feel the weight of the moment before it breaks. Let yourself exist in that suspended space where everything is still possible and nothing has shattered yet.

Because that moment? That tiny pocket of time between seeing and falling? That’s yours.

For as long as you can hold it. For as long as gravity allows. For as long as time stretches.

Until it doesn’t. Until gravity wins. Until the glass breaks. Until everything changes.

And then?

Then you breathe. Then you move. Then you start again.

Because that’s what we do. That’s all we can do.

Watch. Wait. Fall. Rise.

And somewhere in between—in that sharp, quiet space where time stretches and breath catches and everything feels both inevitable and impossible—we find ourselves again.

Still reaching. Still hoping. Still alive.

Even as we fall.

The first few days after surgery passed in a hydrocodone haze. Time stretched and compressed like putty, measurements becoming meaningless. Minutes felt like hours; hours disappeared entirely. The whiteboard hung on the wall, its blue writing swimming in and out of focus as I drifted between sleep and something adjacent to wakefulness.

But eventually, the fog lifted. The pain settled into something manageable. And I was left with just… me. My thoughts. And those four simple instructions staring at me from across the room.

At first, they worked. They really did. There was something grounding about having them laid out so plainly, something almost comforting about their simplicity. They were like handrails bolted into the walls of my mind—something to grab onto when the floor felt unsteady.

Wake up. Check.
Get out of bed. Check. Take a shower. Check. Go outside Check.

On good days—and there were good days, scattered like loose change through the weeks—the steps felt manageable. Not easy, never easy, but doable. I’d catch myself in the bathroom mirror, right arm strapped to my chest in a sling that already smelled like stale sweat, and think: Okay. I can do this. One step at a time.

The shower became a daily ritual of careful choreography. Loop the nylon rope over my neck. Let it hang down my chest like some kind of primitive safety harness. Turn the water on with my good hand. Wait for the temperature to stabilize. Step in. Let the water hit my back while I lean against the tile, the rope clutched in my immobilized hand like a lifeline. Stand there until the hot water runs cool or until my legs start shaking, whichever comes first.

Some days, that was victory enough.

But then there were the other days. The heavy days. The days when everything felt impossible and those four simple steps felt like a mockery of everything I couldn’t do.

Because some mornings, even waking up felt impossible. Even the basic act of opening my eyes felt like it required strength I didn’t have, energy I couldn’t find. Some days, gravity pinned me to the mattress like an entomologist’s latest addition to their collection, and the whiteboard’s cheerful blue letters felt like they were laughing at me from across the room.

You ever stare at a task so simple—so laughably, insultingly simple—that it feels impossible? That’s what those words became to me. They stopped being instructions and started feeling like accusations. Like razor blades wrapped in familiar phrases, cutting deeper with every glance.

Why can’t you do this? What kind of pathetic excuse for a human being can’t even get out of bed? Everyone else manages this. Children manage this. Elderly people with actual physical limitations manage this. But you? You just lay here, burning daylight, wasting oxygen, taking up space that could be used by someone who actually deserves to exist. Someone who can handle the bare minimum requirements of being alive.

Why is this so hard for you? Are you really this broken? This useless? This weak? Four fucking steps—four basic, kindergarten-level tasks that literal toddlers can handle—and you’re lying here paralyzed by them like they’re asking you to split an atom or solve world hunger. Do you have any idea how ridiculous this is? How embarrassing? How absolutely, fundamentally pathetic?

»Get up. Just get up. Just move your worthless body out of this bed and do something—anything—to justify the resources you’re consuming. The air you’re breathing. The space you’re occupying. The concern you’re causing in people who are foolish enough to still care about you. People who would be better off if you just disappeared. People who are probably relieved when you don’t answer their texts because at least then they don’t have to pretend to be interested in your endless, circular dysfunction anymore.«

The whiteboard stares back, its simple instructions now twisted into evidence of your failure. Each word a witness testifying against you in the endless trial your brain is conducting. Each task a reminder of how far you’ve fallen, how deep this hole goes, how thoroughly you’ve managed to break the most basic functions of being human.

»Wake up? You’re awake. You’ve been awake for hours, staring at nothing, contributing nothing, being nothing.«

»Get out of bed? Sure. Just move. Just fucking move. Just convince your leaden limbs that they remember how to function like a normal person’s body.«

»Take a shower? Stand upright for more than thirty seconds without your knees buckling from the sheer effort of staying upright. Pretend you deserve to be clean.«

»Go outside? Face the world with your hollow eyes and your unwashed hair and your collection of failures pressed against your chest like a shield. Let everyone see what you’ve become.«

The words blur together, but the accusations stay sharp. Each letter a knife, each instruction a noose, each task another piece of evidence in the case your brain is building against you. The case that proves, beyond any reasonable doubt, that you are fundamentally, irredeemably broken. That you are less than nothing. That you are a waste of skin and bones and consciousness.

And still, the whiteboard stays. Silent. Patient. Waiting for you to prove it wrong.

I would lay there, paralyzed by the sheer absurdity of it all. A grown man, staring at a whiteboard, completely and utterly defeated by four sentences. The marker’s ink had started to fade, some letters more ghost than substance, but they were still readable. Still demanding. Still there.

It’s hard to explain that kind of paralysis to someone who’s never been there. How something as small as “take a shower” can feel like climbing Everest in flip-flops. How the sunlight filtering through your blinds can feel like an enemy, like it’s pointing out your failure with every golden beam. How each task feels like it weighs a thousand pounds, and your strength is measured in ounces.

But some days… some days, I did it.

Some days, I’d drag myself out of bed, my body creaking like an old house settling. I’d shuffle to the bathroom, each step an act of deliberate defiance against the gravity trying to pull me back into bed. I’d lean my forehead against the shower wall, letting the water run over me while I clutched that nylon rope and held on to the tiled wall like it was the only thing keeping me tethered to the earth. The rope would leave marks on my palm—thin, red lines that lasted hours—but they felt like proof. Proof that I was trying. Proof that I was still here.

Some days, I walked outside. Just to the end of my driveway and back. Just far enough to let the sunlight hit my face. Just enough to remind myself that the world was still out there, still moving, still happening, even if I felt frozen in place.

And on those days, those four tasks felt monumental. They felt like victory—not the kind with fireworks and applause, but the kind that happens quietly, in the privacy of your own head. The kind where you close your eyes at the end of the day and think, I actually fucking did it. I made it through today.

The whiteboard stayed on my wall long after my shoulder healed.
»You’ve got one too, don’t you? Not a whiteboard maybe. But something. A note in your phone. A Post-it hidden where no one else can see it. Those desperate little reminders of how to be a person. The ones that make you feel like you’re rotting from the inside out because what kind of broken thing needs instructions for existing?«
Long after I stopped needing the sling. Long after the physical therapy exercises became muscle memory and I could wash my hair with two hands again.

But the thing about habits—about grooves worn deep into your routines—is that they don’t disappear just because the immediate need for them is gone. They linger. They become part of the landscape of your days, like furniture you’ve owned so long you don’t really see it anymore.

Eventually, the letters faded into memories of themselves, more suggestion than substance. The blue ink turned grayish, then barely visible, like a ghost of intention. I stopped actively reading the words. They were just there. Another fixture in a room full of fixtures. Another part of the background noise of existing.

But every now and then—on the really bad days, on the heavy days, on the days when the static in my head is too loud to think—I notice them again. Really notice them. And sometimes, I’ll follow them.

Not because I want to. Not because I feel inspired. But because they’re there. Because they’re simple. Because they feel like something I can do, even if I can’t do anything else.

Wake up.
Get out of bed. Take a shower. Go outside

The whiteboard never saved me. It didn’t fix me. It didn’t erase the darkness or make the noise stop. But it gave me something to hold onto. Something small. Something real.

There’s a strange kind of comfort in routines like that. In grooves so worn into your brain that you can fall into them without thinking. Even when everything else feels impossible, there’s something about having a path—any path—that makes it easier to take one step.

I don’t know if I’ll ever take the whiteboard down. Maybe I should. Maybe keeping it is like holding onto crutches long after your leg has healed. But every time I think about removing it, I remember those days when those four simple tasks were the only thing between me and the abyss.

They’re still there now, those faded blue letters. Almost invisible unless you know where to look. A quiet reminder that even when everything felt impossible, I kept going.

I followed the steps. I checked the boxes. I survived.

And sometimes, that’s enough.
