PART V: Memory as a Mirror
(The past, sharp edges, stories we carry)

Chapter 17 - The Sharp Edge of Memory
Memory doesn’t knock. It kicks the fucking door down at 2 AM, drags you from whatever fragile peace you’ve managed to construct, and forces you to relive moments your body has been trying to forget since they happened. It arrives without warning—triggered by something as innocent as the smell of burnt coffee, the particular angle of afternoon light through blinds, the way someone laughs with their whole body thrown back—and suddenly you’re not here anymore. You’re there. You’re then. You’re drowning in a moment that technically ended years ago but never really stopped happening.
»You have one too, don’t you? A smell, a sound, a quality of light that drops you straight through the floor of the present into a moment you thought you’d left behind. You don’t even get a warning. Just—gone. Somewhere else entirely.«
I can still smell the specific combination of disinfectant and fear that filled the hospital room where Justin died. Not metaphorically. Not poetically. I mean I can actually smell it sometimes—walking through a grocery store, sitting in traffic, lying in bed at 3 AM. My brain just decides, without any input from me, that now would be a great time to perfectly recreate that exact sensory experience. The fluorescent lights that made everything look slightly green. The mechanical wheeze of the ventilator. The way his hand felt cold but not cold enough, like his body was stuck between states, unable to commit to either living or dying.
The smell hits first—that particular cocktail of industrial-strength floor cleaner mixed with the sour undercurrent of human fear that no amount of disinfectant can mask. Then comes the sound—not just the ventilator, but the whole symphony of dying. The occasional beep of monitors. The shuffle of tired nurses’ shoes on linoleum. The barely audible whisper of his girlfriend crying in the corner, trying to be quiet, as if her silence could somehow change the trajectory of what was happening.
My body responds before my mind can even register what’s happening. Shoulders pull up toward my ears. Jaw clenches so tight I can hear my teeth creak. Hands ball into fists, nails digging crescents into palms—a sharp, present pain to anchor me to now instead of then. But it doesn’t work. It never works. The memory has its hooks in deep, and it’s going to play out whether I want it to or not.
That’s the thing about traumatic memory—it doesn’t fade the way normal memories do. It doesn’t soften around the edges or blur into something more manageable. It stays sharp. Precise. Like a piece of glass lodged in your brain that cuts fresh every time you brush against it.
Nobody who’s actually lived this calls it a gift. But there’s always someone ready to tell you to “process” your trauma, to “integrate” it, to transform it into some kind of fucking superpower. As if remembering the exact sound of Riley’s door creaking under his weight is going to make me stronger. As if being able to perfectly recall the pattern of bruises on his neck is something I should be grateful for, something I can channel into a keynote speech or a chapter titled “What Doesn’t Kill You.”
They’ll tell you about post-traumatic growth, about resilience, about how suffering builds character. They’ll package it in neat little quotes suitable for Instagram, overlay them on sunset photos, and pretend that’s what healing looks like. What they won’t tell you is that some memories don’t transform into anything useful. They just sit there, sharp as the day they were made, waiting to ambush you in the cereal aisle because someone walked by wearing the same cologne Riley wore.
What a spectacular load of shit.
The truth is messier. Less marketable. The truth is that I can be having a perfectly normal day, maybe even what passes for a good day, and then I’ll catch a glimpse of someone with Justin’s exact shade of dirty blonde hair, that specific way he had of pushing it back when he was nervous. And suddenly I’m not in the present anymore. I’m back in that hospital room, watching machines breathe for someone who’d already decided to stop, counting the space between beeps like they were a countdown to something I couldn’t prevent.
The Weight of Memory: Why Some Moments Refuse to Fade
Memory is supposed to work like sediment in a river—the sharp edges worn smooth over time, the details settling into something softer, more bearable. That’s what they tell you. Time heals all wounds. The pain will fade. You’ll forget the worst parts.
But trauma memory doesn’t follow those rules. It exists outside of time, preserved in perfect, excruciating detail like an insect trapped in amber. Every sensation catalogued. Every emotion preserved. Every second stretched into its own eternity that you get to relive whenever your brain decides you haven’t suffered enough today.
»You know exactly what I’m talking about. That one memory that feels more real than your actual present. The one that makes right now feel like the dream and back then feel like the only reality that matters.«
There’s actual science behind this—something about how extreme stress changes the way memories are encoded, how trauma gets stored differently in the brain. The amygdala goes into overdrive, the hippocampus can’t properly file things away, and suddenly you’ve got memories that exist in a permanent state of “happening right now” no matter how many years have passed.
The amygdala, that ancient alarm system in our brains, doesn’t understand past tense. When it gets triggered, it responds as if the danger is happening right now, this second, flooding your system with the same chemicals it did during the original trauma. Your rational brain might know that Riley’s been dead for years, but your amygdala is convinced you’re still standing in that doorway, still seeing what you saw, still unable to prevent what’s already happened.
But knowing the science doesn’t make it stop. Understanding the mechanism doesn’t prevent the replay. You can know exactly why your brain is doing this to you and still be completely powerless to stop it from happening.
I have this one memory of Justin—not from the end, but from maybe six months before. We were sitting in my backyard, sharing a joint he’d brought over, and he was telling me about this dream he’d had where he could fly but only backwards. He was laughing so hard he couldn’t breathe, tears streaming down his face, and for just that moment, everything felt possible. Everything felt survivable.
The details of that memory are so vivid I could paint them: the way the late afternoon sun caught the smoke, turning it golden. The specific sound of his laugh—not his polite laugh or his nervous laugh, but that deep, genuine one that came from his belly. The way he kept trying to demonstrate the backward flying with his hands, nearly dropping the joint twice. The smell of fresh-cut grass mixing with the smoke. The feeling of hope, real hope, that maybe he was going to be okay.
That memory hurts worse than the hospital ones. Because in that moment, I thought we had time. I thought we’d have a thousand more afternoons like that. I thought the story would end differently.
The sharp memories aren’t always the obviously traumatic ones. Sometimes they’re the beautiful moments that hurt because of what came after. The last normal conversation before everything changed. The final photo where everyone is still alive. The ordinary Tuesday that you didn’t know would be the last ordinary Tuesday you’d ever have.
I have this photo on my phone from two weeks before Riley died. We’re at a bar, he’s mid-story about something, hands gesturing wildly, face lit up with that infectious enthusiasm he had when he was really into telling you something. I’m laughing so hard my eyes are almost closed. The person taking the photo caught us in this perfect moment of pure, uncomplicated friendship. Looking at it now feels like looking at people I don’t recognize—people who didn’t know what was coming, who still believed in tomorrow’s promises.
These memories become landmarks in the geography of our consciousness—immovable points around which everything else has to navigate. You build your life around them like a city built around ancient ruins, incorporating them into the architecture because removing them would mean destroying everything else in the process.
Every decision, every relationship, every moment of joy or peace has to account for these memories. They’re the invisible force that shapes how you move through the world. Why you can’t listen to certain songs. Why you avoid certain streets. Why you check on people with an intensity that probably scares them. Why you say “I love you” like you’re running out of time, because you know, with brutal clarity, that you always are.
The weight of these memories isn’t just emotional—it’s physical. I can feel them in my body like stones I’m carrying. Some days they’re river rocks, smooth and manageable. Other days they’re jagged boulders that make every movement difficult. But they’re always there, always heavy, always demanding to be accounted for in the physics of existing.
The Battle to Forget: The Impossible Task of Letting Go
I’ve tried everything to forget. Or at least to make the memories less sharp, less immediate, less capable of reaching through time and eviscerating me without warning.
Therapy was first. Sitting in beige rooms with well-meaning professionals who suggested techniques like “thought stopping” and “cognitive restructuring” as if trauma is just a filing error in need of reorganization. They’d have me close my eyes and imagine putting the memory in a box, locking it, putting it on a shelf. As if my brain gives a shit about imaginary boxes. As if trauma respects the boundaries of visualization exercises.
“When you feel the memory surfacing,” one therapist said, “try snapping a rubber band on your wrist. The physical sensation will ground you in the present.”
So I walked around for months with rubber bands on my wrists, snapping them so often I had permanent welts. All I accomplished was training my brain to associate the sting of rubber on skin with the memories I was trying to escape. Now I can’t see a rubber band without thinking of Riley’s face that morning.
Then came medication. Little pills that promised to smooth the edges, to create distance between me and the memories. And they did, sort of. They created distance between me and everything. The memories became less sharp, but so did everything else. Food tasted like cardboard. Music sounded like it was playing underwater. I could look at photos of Riley and Justin and feel… nothing. Which was somehow worse than feeling everything.
»Which ones have you tried? All of them? Yeah, me too. And how’s that working out for us?«
Exercise was supposed to help. Running until my lungs burned, lifting weights until my muscles failed, pushing my body to its limits so my mind wouldn’t have energy left for remembering. But you can’t outrun something that lives inside you. I’d be mid-sprint on the treadmill and suddenly smell that hospital disinfectant, and then I’d be running from something entirely different, something that already caught me years ago.
Meditation was another suggestion. “Observe your thoughts without judgment,” they said. “Let them pass like clouds.” But my thoughts weren’t clouds. They were birds of prey with talons that knew exactly where to grip. I’d sit there, trying to breathe mindfully, and instead I’d be breathing in sync with the ventilator that kept Justin alive those last few days. Every inhale a reminder of mechanical breath. Every exhale a countdown to silence.
Then there are the less approved methods. The ones they don’t put in self-help books. The ones that actually work, temporarily, but cost more than they save.
Drinking until the memories blur into each other, until the sharp edges go soft and watery. But alcohol is a liar. It promises to help you forget, but really it just delays the remembering. And when the memories come back—and they always come back—they’re angry about being suppressed. They return with interest, sharper than before, accompanied by shame and headaches and the taste of regret.
Working yourself into exhaustion so complete that even your dreams can’t find you. Eighteen-hour days, every minute scheduled, no space for memories to surface. But exhaustion has its own price. Your body keeps the score, and eventually, it demands payment. You collapse—physically, mentally, emotionally—and in that collapse, all the memories you’ve been outrunning catch up at once.
Building walls of anger so thick that nothing can get through, including the things you actually want to let in. Anger feels better than grief. It feels active, powerful, like you’re doing something instead of just enduring something. But anger is exhausting to maintain. It burns hot and fast and leaves you empty, and in that emptiness, the memories creep back in.
The cruelest joke is that trying to forget actually makes the memories stronger. It’s like that psychological experiment where they tell you not to think about a white elephant, and suddenly white elephants are all you can think about. The more energy you put into not remembering, the more power you give to the memory.
I spent months trying to forget the exact shade of gray Riley’s face had turned when I found him. Not gray like a color in a paint store. Gray like absence. Gray like everything that made him Riley had leaked out somewhere I couldn’t see, leaving just this shell that looked like him but wasn’t. I tried to overwrite it with other images, tried to blur it with substances, tried to bury it under new experiences. But all I accomplished was building a shrine to that particular shade of gray in my mind. Now I see it everywhere—in morning fog, in concrete, in the shadows under people’s eyes when they haven’t been sleeping.
The memory doesn’t just resist forgetting; it actively fights back. It recruits allies—songs that were playing in the background, smells that were in the air, textures and temperatures and specific qualities of light. They all become part of the memory’s network, tripwires that can activate the whole system without warning.
There’s a Counting Crows song that was playing in the background the last time Justin and I hung out before everything went wrong. Now I can’t hear Adam Duritz’s voice without being transported back to that night, to the way Justin kept saying he was fine, he was handling it, don’t worry about him. The way I believed him because I wanted to. The way I didn’t push harder because it was easier not to. That song is ruined forever now, along with anything that sounds remotely like it.
And then there’s the one that doesn’t even need to be playing. It just needs to almost play. I’ll be in a store, or someone’s car, or scrolling past a playlist, and the algorithm serves up Billie Eilish. "everything i wanted." Three notes in and the floor drops out.
Dani sent me that song. One of the last things she ever sent me. I didn’t know it was one of the last things. You never know. That’s the whole point, and it’s also the cruelest part — you don’t get a label that says THIS MESSAGE MATTERS MORE THAN THE OTHERS. It just sat in my phone like every other link, every other song someone shares because it reminded them of something. I probably listened once, maybe twice, texted back something like "yeah that’s a good one" and moved on with my day.
Now it’s a tripwire buried in every streaming service, every coffee shop speaker, every car commercial that uses thirty seconds of it. I don’t have to seek it out. It seeks me out. The opening notes land and suddenly I’m not wherever I am — I’m in the specific silence of scrolling through Instagram and seeing that she’s gone. I’m rereading our last exchange. I’m staring at "You alive?" sitting there in blue with no response beneath it, the check-in that became a question I can never stop asking.
*»How many songs have you lost? Not disliked — lost. Confiscated by grief without your permission, turned into something you have to lunge for the skip button to avoid.«*
The thing about a song as a tripwire is that you can’t decommission it. You can’t explain to Spotify’s algorithm that this particular track isn’t just a track. You can’t put a content warning on a melody. It just exists out there in the world, armed, patient, waiting for you to forget to be on guard.
There’s this thing that happens when you try to forcibly forget—you start forgetting everything else instead. The good memories, the neutral ones, the everyday moments that should be easy to recall—they all get caught in the crossfire. You try to delete one file and end up corrupting the whole hard drive.
I can’t remember the last movie Justin and I watched together, but I can remember the exact pattern of the floor tiles in the hospital waiting room. I can’t recall what Riley and I talked about the week before he died, but I can describe in perfect detail the way the morning light hit his bedroom door that Saturday. The trauma memories are in 4K ultra-high definition while everything else is a grainy photocopy of a photocopy.
What Memory Leaves Behind: The Invisible Scars We Carry
The memories write themselves into your body in ways that are impossible to explain to someone who hasn’t been there. It’s not just that you remember—it’s that your entire system remembers. Your muscles remember. Your nervous system remembers. Your fucking cells remember.
My shoulders still tense when I hear sirens, even though the ambulance for Riley arrived too late to matter. It’s automatic, faster than thought. The sound hits my ears and my shoulders are already up around my ears before my conscious mind even registers what’s happening. My body is preparing for impact, for bad news, for another ending I can’t prevent.
My throat still closes when I smell certain hospital cleaners, even though Justin’s been gone for years. It’s not a choice or a decision. It’s a physical response as involuntary as blinking. That specific combination of bleach and something medicinal hits my nostrils and my throat constricts like someone’s got their hands around it. I have to leave stores sometimes, abandoning full carts, because the cleaning aisle ambushed me with sense memory.
My hands still shake when someone doesn’t text back quickly enough, because some part of me is perpetually frozen in that moment of discovering what silence really means. The last text I sent Riley was “you alive?” It was our thing—our check-in when we hadn’t talked in a while. He never answered. Now every unanswered text feels like a countdown to catastrophe.
»How many of your decisions today were actually made by something that happened years ago? How many times did you flinch, avoid, check, double-check—not because of anything happening now, but because of something that already happened and won’t stop happening?«
And then there are the memories that aren’t even memories. Not really. Not in any way you could point to and say this happened, then this, then this. They’re more like — I don’t know. Smudges. Shapes behind frosted glass. You know something is there. You know it happened. But you can’t make it hold still long enough to see it clearly.
I was young. Too young to have language for it. A babysitter — her hands, something about the room, something about being told this is what we do now. I remember fragments. Pieces that don’t connect to a timeline. Her making me touch her. Her touching me. The specific quality of confusion that comes from knowing something is wrong before you have any framework for what wrong even means at that age. Five, six, maybe older — I don’t know. That’s the thing. I don’t know. I can’t pin it to a year or a season or a specific afternoon because the memory won’t let me. It exists as sensation more than sequence. A heaviness in my hands I can’t explain. A flinch response to certain kinds of proximity that I didn’t understand for decades.
There might be more. That’s the part that never stops being sharp — the might. The not knowing. The edges of this memory dissolve into something I can’t access, and I don’t know if that’s because there’s nothing else there or because something in me locked the rest of it away so thoroughly that I’ll never get to it. Both possibilities are their own kind of violence. Either I’m making it worse than it was, or it was worse than I can remember. There’s no version of this uncertainty that offers relief.
People want trauma to be a story. Beginning, middle, end. This happened to me, here is how it affected me, here is what I did about it. But some trauma is just — a blur you carry in your body. A knowledge without details. A wound with no clear edges, which means it can never fully close because you can’t find the borders of it. The sharpest thing about this memory is its refusal to fully exist. It just sits there, half-formed, daring me to look directly at it, dissolving every time I try.
These body memories become a kind of parallel narrative to your life. You’re having a conversation at work while your nervous system is reliving a trauma from five years ago. You’re at a party while your muscles are braced for an impact that already happened. You’re trying to fall asleep while your body is fighting a battle that ended before you even knew it had begun.
It’s like living in two timelines simultaneously. In one timeline, you’re a functional adult going through the motions of a normal life. In the other timeline, you’re perpetually stuck in the worst moments of your existence, reliving them on a cellular level. Your body doesn’t know the difference between then and now. It responds to remembered danger as if it’s immediate danger, flooding you with adrenaline for threats that no longer exist.
The memories change how you move through the world. You start avoiding certain routes because they pass places where the membrane between past and present feels too thin. I haven’t driven past the hospital where Justin died in three years. I take a fifteen-minute detour rather than spend thirty seconds driving by a building that holds too much history.
You develop elaborate rituals to prevent triggering cascades—checking your phone in specific patterns, avoiding certain songs, creating buffer zones around dangerous dates on the calendar. Riley’s birthday. The anniversary of finding him. The day Justin went into the hospital. The day he came out in a box. These dates are circled in invisible red on a calendar that exists in my bones.
The rituals become their own kind of prison. You think you’re protecting yourself, but really you’re building your life around the memories, letting them dictate your movements, your choices, your existence. The trauma becomes the architect of your life, designing every room around its presence.
But the memories find ways around your defenses. They’re patient. They wait for you to let your guard down, for that moment when you think maybe you’re finally okay, and then they strike with surgical precision. A stranger’s laugh that sounds too much like someone who’s gone. A news story that parallels your own too closely. A random Tuesday that suddenly feels exactly like another Tuesday when everything fell apart.
Last week, I was in line at the coffee shop, completely fine, thinking about nothing more significant than whether to get an extra shot of espresso. Then someone walked in wearing the same brand of work boots Riley always wore—those tan Carhartt ones with the black laces. The exact same wear pattern on the toes. The same way of walking in them, that specific heavy-footed gait. And suddenly I’m not in the coffee shop anymore. I’m standing in Riley’s doorway again, staring at those boots, wondering why they’re touching the ground when they should be flat on the floor.
The sharp edges of memory carve patterns into your behavior that become invisible to you but obvious to everyone else. The way you always sit facing the door. The way you check and recheck that people are still breathing when they sleep. The way you say goodbye like it might be the last time, every time, because you know how quickly last times can arrive without warning.
People notice, even if they don’t say anything. They see the way you flinch at certain sounds. The way you go quiet when certain topics come up. The way you hold on a little too long when you hug, like you’re trying to memorize the feeling. They might not know the specifics of what you’re carrying, but they can see its weight in how you move through the world.
The memories affect relationships in ways that are hard to explain. How do you tell someone new that you need them to text you back within a reasonable timeframe, not because you’re needy or controlling, but because silence has become synonymous with death in your mind? How do you explain that you can’t watch certain movies, listen to certain songs, go to certain places, not because you don’t want to, but because your body will revolt, will throw you back in time to moments you can’t afford to relive in public?
You become a cartographer of your own trauma, mapping the safe routes through your days. This restaurant is okay, but not that one. This park is safe, but avoid it on Saturdays. You can handle this friend’s problems, but that friend’s issues hit too close to your own wounds. You learn to navigate the world like it’s full of invisible landmines, because for you, it is.
Finding Space for Memory: Learning to Coexist with the Past
Here’s what they don’t tell you in therapy: sometimes the goal isn’t to heal from the memory or integrate it or transform it into something else. Sometimes the goal is just to learn to coexist with it, like learning to live with a chronic illness or a difficult roommate who’s never moving out.
The memories are going to be there whether you fight them or not. They’re going to surface at inconvenient times, in inappropriate places, with no regard for your schedule or your emotional capacity. They’re going to remain sharp, possibly forever. And maybe that’s okay. Maybe that has to be okay.
»This is the part where I’m supposed to offer you hope, right? Some silver lining? Some transformative wisdom? Sorry. All I’ve got is this: we’re both still here. Sometimes that’s the only victory we get.«
I’ve started to think of my sharp memories less as wounds and more as weather. They roll in like storms—sometimes with warning, sometimes without. They do their damage, leave their mark, and eventually pass. And just like with weather, I can’t control them, but I can learn to prepare for them, to shelter during them, to clean up after them.
When I feel a memory storm approaching—when I notice the telltale signs of tension in my shoulders, the slight dissociation that precedes a full flashback—I try to prepare. I find a safe space if I can. I text someone who knows what’s happening, not for them to fix it, but just so someone knows where I am, metaphorically speaking. I remind myself that I’ve survived this storm before, that it will pass, that I’ll still be here when it’s over.
During the storm, when the memory has me fully in its grip, I’ve learned to stop fighting. Fighting makes it worse, makes it last longer, makes the damage more severe. Instead, I try to just… witness it. To observe it happening to me without resistance. It still hurts. It still cuts. But accepting the pain somehow makes it more bearable than fighting against it.
There’s this thing I do now when a sharp memory surfaces. Instead of fighting it or trying to escape it, I just… acknowledge it. “Oh, there you are,” I’ll say to the memory of finding Riley. “Yes, I remember you,” I’ll tell the image of Justin in that hospital bed. Not with affection, not with acceptance exactly, but with a kind of resigned recognition. Like greeting a neighbor you don’t particularly like but have to live next to anyway.
Sometimes I even talk to the memories directly. “You can have five minutes,” I’ll tell them. “Five minutes to do your worst, and then I need to get back to my life.” It sounds insane, negotiating with your own trauma, but somehow it helps. It puts a boundary around the unbounded. It gives structure to chaos.
The memories still hurt. They still cut. But maybe they’re supposed to. Maybe some things shouldn’t be forgotten or softened or transformed into something more palatable. Maybe the sharpness is honoring what was lost, what was survived, what mattered enough to leave permanent marks.
I’ve started to understand that the sharpness of these memories is proportional to the depth of what was lost. The reason the memory of finding Riley cuts so deep is because our friendship ran deep. The reason Justin’s hospital room haunts me is because his presence in my life was significant enough to leave a wound that size when it was torn away.
The pain is a measure of the love. The sharpness is a testament to the significance. These aren’t just traumatic memories—they’re monuments to connections that mattered, to people who left marks on my soul that will never fully heal. And maybe that’s how it should be.
There’s a strange comfort in accepting that some wounds don’t heal. It takes the pressure off. You stop waiting to “get better,” stop measuring your progress against some imaginary timeline of recovery. You stop feeling like a failure for still hurting years later. You just accept that this is part of you now, these sharp edges, these memories that refuse to play by the rules.
The Weight and the Gift
The sharp edges of memory are proof that we’ve lived, that we’ve loved, that we’ve lost things worth grieving. They’re evidence of our capacity to be marked by experience, to be changed by connection, to carry others with us even after they’re gone.
This isn’t some bullshit about trauma making us stronger or everything happening for a reason. Fuck that. Some things just hurt, and they keep hurting, and there’s no greater purpose to it. Riley didn’t die so I could learn some important life lesson. Justin’s death wasn’t part of some cosmic plan to help me grow. They died because sometimes people die, and it’s terrible and senseless and leaves wounds that never fully close.
But there is something to be said for the fact that we’re still here, carrying these sharp memories, and somehow continuing to exist despite their weight. Not thriving, necessarily. Not transformed into better versions of ourselves. Just… continuing. Getting up each day and carrying our sharp edges into the world, careful not to cut ourselves or others too deeply.
The memories of Riley and Justin cut me fresh almost daily. They surface without warning, reducing me to a puddle of grief in grocery stores and traffic jams and Tuesday afternoons when I thought I was fine. They make me difficult to be around sometimes, prone to sudden departures and unexplained silences and the kind of sadness that has no solution.
But they also mean that Riley and Justin are still here, in a way. Not in some spiritual afterlife sense, but in the very real sense that they’ve become part of my structure, woven into my consciousness so thoroughly that forgetting them would mean forgetting myself.
Every time the memory of Riley surfaces, painful as it is, it means he’s not completely gone. He exists in that space between memory and presence, more real in those moments than the present moment itself. When I smell the particular combination of cigarettes and cheap cologne he always wore, he’s there. When I hear someone laugh the way he did—full-throated and unself-conscious—he’s there. The pain is the price of keeping him with me.
The same with Justin. Every sharp memory of those final days, every flashback to that hospital room, every moment of grief that ambushes me—it’s all proof that he mattered. That his life had weight and meaning and impact that continues to ripple through time. The memories hurt because the loss was real. The edges stay sharp because the love hasn’t dulled.
»Your own sharp memories are surfacing now, aren’t they? The ones you carry like broken glass in your pockets, careful not to move too quickly lest they cut through the fabric and draw blood. I see you. I know what you’re carrying.«
We don’t get to choose which memories stay sharp and which ones fade. We don’t get to edit our consciousness for comfort or convenience. We just get to carry what we carry, to learn the geography of our own damage, to navigate the minefield of our own minds with whatever grace we can muster.
Some days that grace looks like crying in your car in a parking lot because a song came on that you can’t handle. Some days it looks like leaving a social event early because someone’s story hit too close to your own trauma. Some days it looks like sitting with the pain, letting it wash over you, not trying to fix or change or transform it into something else.
And maybe that’s enough. Maybe carrying the sharp edges, feeling the cut of them, bleeding from them sometimes—maybe that’s just part of what it means to have truly lived, to have truly loved, to have truly lost.
The memories remain sharp because what they’re connected to mattered. The pain persists because the love was real. The edges cut because we’re still soft enough to be cut, still human enough to bleed, still alive enough to feel the weight of what we’ve carried this far.
I think about all the people walking around with their own sharp memories, their own invisible wounds that won’t heal. The woman at the grocery store who flinches when she sees someone who looks like her dead daughter. The man at the gym whose hands shake when he hears a certain song. All of us carrying our broken glass, trying not to cut ourselves or each other, trying to move through the world without leaving blood trails.
There’s a weird solidarity in knowing we’re not alone in this. That there are others navigating their days around memories that ambush them, others who understand what it’s like to be having a normal conversation while part of your brain is reliving the worst day of your life. We don’t talk about it much—it’s not exactly small talk material—but sometimes you can recognize another carrier of sharp memories. Something in the eyes. Something in the way they hold themselves. Something in the careful way they move through the world.
So we keep carrying them. These sharp edges. These perfect, terrible, preserved moments that refuse to fade. We carry them not because we want to, not because we choose to, but because they’ve become part of us. And we continue, despite their weight, despite their edges, despite the way they cut fresh with every step.
Because what else is there to do? What else is there but to keep walking, keep breathing, keep existing with our pockets full of broken glass and our hearts full of sharp-edged love?
The memories don’t get easier. They don’t transform into wisdom or strength or any of that bullshit. They just remain what they are: sharp edges we learn to carry, wounds that won’t quite close, proof that we were there, that it happened, that it mattered enough to leave scars.
Sometimes I wonder what Riley would think about how I carry his memory. Would he be sad that thinking of him still hurts this much? Would he want me to “move on,” whatever that means? Or would he understand that the pain is inseparable from the love, that forgetting him would be worse than remembering him, even when remembering cuts deep?
I think he’d get it. He knew about carrying sharp things. He had his own collection of memories that wouldn’t heal, his own wounds that stayed fresh no matter how much time passed. Maybe that’s why we understood each other so well—we both knew what it was like to be walking wounded, to be functioning while bleeding internally from cuts nobody else could see.
Justin would probably make some dark joke about it. He had that kind of humor—the kind that comes from staring into the abyss so long that you start finding it funny. He’d probably say something about how at least the memories are free entertainment, no subscription required. He’d find a way to make me laugh about it, even while acknowledging the weight of it.
And somehow, we keep going. Not because we’re strong. Not because we’re brave. But because the alternative—letting go of the memories, even the sharp ones—would mean letting go of the people we carry with us. And that’s a price we’re not willing to pay.
So we bleed a little, every day. We flinch when the memories surface. We carry our sharp edges like the broken, beautiful disasters we are.
And we keep going.
Because that’s what you do when you’re carrying sharp memories. You learn to move carefully. You learn to bleed quietly. You learn to exist in a world that’s always going to have corners that cut.
The truth that nobody wants to hear is that some things don’t get better. Some wounds don’t heal. Some memories don’t fade. And that’s not a failure of healing or a lack of effort or a sign that you’re doing recovery wrong. It’s just the nature of certain losses—they leave marks that last forever.
And maybe, someday, we’ll learn that the sharpness isn’t the enemy. Maybe it’s just proof that we’re still capable of feeling deeply, of remembering truly, of carrying our dead with the honor they deserve.
But until then, we just keep going. One sharp memory at a time. One careful step after another. One more day of carrying what can’t be put down.
Because that’s what survival actually looks like. Not the motivational poster version. Not the recovered, healed, transformed version. Just this: continuing to exist with our sharp edges, our fresh cuts, our memories that refuse to play by the rules.
The last time I visited Riley’s grave, I brought a beer—his favorite kind, the cheap stuff he always kept in his fridge. I sat there for a while, not saying anything, just existing in the space between memory and present. The sharp edges were all there—the memory of finding him, the weight of his absence, the guilt of surviving when he didn’t. But there was something else too. Something quieter. Something that felt almost like peace.
Not the peace of resolution or healing or moving on. Just the peace of accepting that this is what carrying someone looks like. This is what love looks like when the person you love is gone but the love itself remains. Sharp. Painful. Permanent.
And somehow, we keep carrying it.
Because it’s all we have. These memories that cut. These wounds that won’t close. These people we carry in the sharp edges of our consciousness.
And maybe that’s not a burden. Maybe it’s a privilege. To have loved enough to hurt this much. To have connected deeply enough to be permanently marked. To carry people with us in the only way we can—in memories that refuse to fade, in pain that refuses to diminish, in love that refuses to die even when the people we love do.
So here’s to the sharp edges. Here’s to the memories that ambush us. Here’s to the wounds that won’t heal. Here’s to carrying our broken glass carefully through the world, trying not to cut anyone else while we bleed quietly from our own impossible collection of things too sharp to hold but too precious to release.
Here’s to continuing anyway. Here’s to getting up tomorrow and doing it all again. Here’s to the messy, painful, beautiful disaster of carrying memories that refuse to play by the rules.
Because that’s what we do. That’s who we are. The carriers of sharp memories. The walking wounded. The ones who know that some things don’t get better, they just get carried.
And somehow, impossibly, we keep going.
