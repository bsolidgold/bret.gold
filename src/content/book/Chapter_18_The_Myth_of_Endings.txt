Chapter 18 - The Myth of Endings
They tell you grief has stages. Five neat little boxes you’re supposed to check off like items on a fucking grocery list. Denial, anger, bargaining, depression, acceptance. As if loss could be that clean. As if you could graduate from one feeling to the next like you’re collecting badges in some twisted scout program for the bereaved.
What they don’t tell you is that grief doesn’t end. It just changes shape. Transforms. Learns to hide in smaller spaces. Gets better at surprise attacks when you thought you were finally safe.
»Be honest—how long have you been waiting for your grief to end? For that magical moment when it’s “over” and you can go back to normal. Spoiler alert: normal died with them.«
Riley’s been gone for almost ten years now. Ten fucking years. You’d think that would be enough time for something to feel finished, resolved, tidied up into a story with a beginning, middle, and end. But here’s the thing about suicide—it doesn’t give you an ending. It gives you an eternal question mark. A sentence that just stops mid-thought, leaving you to wonder forever what the rest might have been.
I still find myself reaching for my phone to text him sometimes. My thumb hovering over his name in my contacts—a name I can’t delete because deleting it feels like admitting something I’m not ready to admit. The message window opens, cursor blinking, waiting for words that will never be read. “You alive?” I used to text him. Our stupid check-in that wasn’t stupid at all. Now the question just hangs there, unanswered and unanswerable.
The world loves endings. Craves them. Needs them to make sense of the chaos. We’re raised on stories that resolve, problems that get solved, wounds that heal into barely visible scars. Every movie, every book, every Instagram post about “healing journeys” sells us this myth that there’s a point where things conclude. Where you can close the book and say “The End” and mean it.
But real life doesn’t work like that. Real life is all middle. No clean beginnings, no satisfying conclusions. Just this endless, messy middle where things keep happening and un-happening and re-happening in loops that would make a film editor weep.
The Stories That Won’t Stop
Justin’s death came with its own resistance to ending. The official story—“complications from cancer treatment”—was technically true but fundamentally dishonest. The real story, the one about opioids and addiction and a system that failed him, that story doesn’t get an ending either. It just gets absorbed into the larger narrative of an epidemic that keeps claiming people while we pretend we’re making progress.
His mom still texts me sometimes. Usually around his birthday or the anniversary of his death. “Thinking of you,” she writes. Three words that carry the weight of everything we can’t say. Everything that doesn’t have an ending. Everything that just continues, like background radiation from a disaster that happened years ago but never really stopped happening.
I want to tell her about the last time I saw him. How he sat in that recliner—the one that would haunt my living room for three years after he was gone—and we both knew but didn’t say. The knowing hung between us like fog, thick and choking, but we just kept talking about bullshit. About TV shows. About anything except the fact that he was dissolving in front of me, becoming less solid with each visit, like watching someone fade out of a photograph in real-time.
»You have your own unfinished stories, don’t you? Your own ghosts who refuse to stay properly dead. Your own questions that will never get answers no matter how many times you ask them into the dark.«
The morning I found Riley, time split into before and after. But even that’s a lie we tell ourselves to make sense of trauma. There’s no clean line. The before bleeds into the after. The after reaches back and rewrites the before. Memory becomes this fluid thing that won’t stay in its proper timeline.
I can still hear those sad country songs playing on repeat from his Bluetooth speaker. Sometimes I’ll be in a store and one will come on and suddenly I’m not in aisle seven looking at pasta sauce—I’m back in that dining room, time moving like cold honey, everything too sharp and too soft at the same time. The ending that was supposed to happen—me saving him, us getting through this, him getting help—that ending just evaporated, leaving only the reality of what did happen. What is still happening. What won’t stop happening no matter how many years pass.
The playlist still exists on his Spotify. I know because I check sometimes, like picking at a scab that won’t heal. Thirty-seven songs about loss and leaving and being too broken to fix. A suicide note written in other people’s lyrics, playing on eternal repeat in the digital ether. Every time Spotify suggests one of those songs to me, it’s like getting a message from the dead. A reminder that endings are just stories we tell ourselves to make the ongoing bearable.
People want you to have closure. They need you to have it, actually, because your open wounds make them uncomfortable. They want to hear that you’ve “moved on,” that you’ve “found peace,” that you’ve reached that mythical state of acceptance they’ve heard so much about. They want your grief to have an arc, a narrative structure they can follow and feel satisfied by.
“How are you doing with everything?” they ask, years later, and what they’re really asking is: “Is it over yet? Can we stop feeling weird about this? Have you reached the ending?”
But there is no ending. There’s just learning to carry the weight differently. Learning to breathe around the permanent stone in your chest. Learning to function with pieces of yourself missing, phantom limbs of friendship that still ache when the weather changes.
The Persistence of Presence
I kept thinking that one day I wouldn’t need those reminders anymore. That eventually I’d reach some point where existing didn’t require instructions. Where the basic functions of being human would become automatic again. But that ending never came. The words faded, the marker got replaced, the words got rewritten, and the cycle continued. Because maybe that’s what survival actually looks like—not reaching an end point, but accepting the endlessness.
No progression. No graduation. No level where you unlock “Normal Functioning Human” achievement and get to stop reading basic instructions for existing.
»You’re looking for the lesson here, aren’t you? The part where I tell you what I learned, how I grew, what wisdom I gained from all this loss. But that’s just another ending we’re supposed to provide. Another neat bow to tie around the mess.«
Vanuatu taught me about the myth of endings too, though I didn’t realize it at the time. When I left, I thought I was closing a chapter. Completing a mission. Reaching a conclusion. But Badge is still there in my mind, that stub tail wagging, those alert ears swiveling toward dangers I’ll never know if they came to pass. The story didn’t end when I got on that plane. It just changed locations, shifted from external reality to internal landscape, where it continues playing out in dreams and unexpected moments of memory.
Sometimes I dream about him. Twenty years later and I’m still dreaming about a dog on an island thousands of miles away. In the dreams, he’s always watching, always waiting, always positioned between me and some unseen threat. I wake up and for a moment I can feel the humidity, smell the frangipani and rotting fruit, hear the specific quality of silence that comes before violence in the jungle.
The men with the bush knives—did they ever stop looking for me? Did they forget, forgive, move on? I’ll never know. That story has no ending either, just a cessation of my participation in it. Somewhere in the islands, that narrative might still be continuing, generating consequences I’ll never see, creating ripples I’ll never feel.
The woman I baptized—is she still alive? Still believing? Did that choice, which seemed so important at the time, lead to happiness or more suffering? The story continues without me, endless branches of possibility sprouting from that single moment in warm ocean water, each one as real and unfinished as the next.
The Architecture of Unfinished Business
Even my shoulder surgery refuses to end properly. Years later, I still feel it. Not pain exactly, but presence. A reminder that some things don’t heal so much as scar, don’t conclude so much as become part of your structure. The physical therapy ended, the sling came off, the function returned (mostly), but the experience of being that broken, that dependent, that vulnerable—that doesn’t end. It becomes part of the ongoing story of living in a body that remembers everything, even what we’d rather forget.
There are mornings when I reach for something and the shoulder catches, just for a second, like it’s remembering its trauma. Like it’s saying, “Hey, remember when you couldn’t do this? Remember when lifting a glass of water was an achievement? Remember when you needed help to wipe your own ass?” The body doesn’t believe in endings either. It keeps every story it’s ever lived, filed away in muscle memory and scar tissue.
The bidet is still installed in my bathroom. I don’t need it anymore, haven’t needed it for years, but removing it would feel like admitting something ended. And maybe I’m not ready for that kind of finality. Maybe keeping it there is my way of acknowledging that some stories pause but never really conclude.
I was thirty-three when I tried to write my own ending.
I’m not going to walk you through the specifics. Not because I’m protecting you — you’re a grown-up, you can handle it — but because the specifics aren’t the point. The specifics are just mechanics. The point is that I made a decision. A final one. The kind of decision that’s supposed to be irreversible, the kind that comes with a period at the end of the sentence so hard it cracks the page.
Except it didn’t crack anything. The period turned out to be a comma.
And then I had to figure out what the fuck comes after a comma you didn’t plan for.
*»You’re waiting for me to tell you what stopped me. What pulled me back. What shining moment of clarity pierced the darkness and reminded me that life was worth living. But that’s the Hollywood version, and this isn’t that.«*
Here’s what nobody tells you about surviving a suicide attempt: the strangest part isn’t the before. The before almost makes sense — a terrible kind of sense, the logic of someone drowning who’s decided to stop fighting the water. The strangest part is the after. Waking up in a world you had already said goodbye to. Walking through rooms you’d already mentally emptied yourself out of. Looking in a mirror at someone who was supposed to be past tense.
You become a grammatical impossibility. A sentence that was supposed to end but kept going. And the extra words — the ones that come after the place you’d chosen to stop — they feel different. Borrowed. Unearned. Like you snuck back into a movie after the credits started rolling and now you’re watching scenes that weren’t meant for you.
For months after, everything had this strange double-exposure quality. I’d be doing something ordinary — buying groceries, walking the dogs, paying a bill — and this overlay would settle on top of it: *you weren’t supposed to be here for this.* Not in a grateful way. Not in that saccharine "every day is a gift" way that people who’ve never been there love to say. More like a glitch. Like the simulation didn’t account for this version and hadn’t rendered the textures properly.
I kept waiting for someone to notice. To point at me and say, "Wait — weren’t you supposed to be dead?" Like I was trespassing in my own life. Squatting in a timeline that had been allocated to someone else’s story.
The myth of endings cuts deepest when you’ve tried to write one for yourself and it didn’t take. Because now you know something most people get to avoid knowing: endings are harder to come by than you think. Even the ones you engineer yourself. Even the ones you plan and commit to and execute — they can fail. They can turn into continuations. The period you punctured into the page can bleed sideways into an ellipsis, and then you’re stuck with all these extra pages you didn’t outline, didn’t want, didn’t ask for.
And you can’t return them. Can’t hand them back and say, "No thanks, I already finished this book." The pages keep coming whether you want to fill them or not.
I’m forty-six now. Thirteen years of pages I didn’t plan for. Thirteen years of scenes that happened in the epilogue of a story I thought was over. I’ve done outpatient treatment, inpatient treatment, more outpatient. I’ve sat in rooms with other people whose endings didn’t take and we all look at each other with this recognition that’s impossible to explain to anyone who hasn’t been there — the look of people who tried to leave the party and found all the doors locked.
Some of those people tried again. Some of those people succeeded. And I sit with that, too — the knowledge that my failed ending doesn’t guarantee anything about the future. That I’m not cured of wanting to leave just because leaving didn’t work the first time. The door didn’t lock behind me when I came back through it. It’s still there. Still a door. I just don’t stand as close to it as I used to.
*»You’re doing the math right now, aren’t you? Thirty-three. That was after Riley. After all of it. And you want to know what specifically broke me, what singular event pushed me past the threshold. But that’s not how it works. It’s not one thing. It’s the accumulation. It’s compound interest on a debt that started accruing before I could count.«*
The people who knew — the few I told, the ones who figured it out — they wanted it to change things. Wanted it to be a turning point in the narrative. The dramatic low that precedes the triumphant rise. The darkness before dawn. All that bullshit. They wanted my failed ending to become the beginning of something beautiful.
But it wasn’t a beginning. It was a continuation. Just more middle. More of the same story, told from a slightly different angle by someone who now carried one more piece of knowledge he didn’t want: that even endings don’t end. That you can make the most final decision a person can make and still end up here, still existing, still waking up, still staring at a whiteboard that tells you to take a shower like that’s something to be proud of.
And maybe it is. Maybe taking a shower when you weren’t supposed to be alive to take showers is its own kind of thing. Not triumph. Not victory. Not even survival, really. Just continuation. The comma after the period. The sentence that keeps going because it doesn’t know how to stop.
There’s no bow to put on this. I tried to end and didn’t. Now I’m here. The ending became another middle, and the middle keeps generating more middle, and somewhere in all that middle is whatever this is — this life, this book, this paragraph you’re reading that only exists because an ending failed.
Make of that what you will. I still don’t know what to make of it myself.
The relationships that feel most true are the ones that acknowledge this myth. The ones that don’t pretend there’s closure, resolution, a point where everything makes sense. My real friends don’t ask if I’m “over” Riley’s death or if I’ve “moved past” Justin’s overdose. They understand that these aren’t events with endpoints. They’re ongoing processes, continuous presences, permanent alterations to the fabric of existence.
We sit with our unfinished stories, our questions without answers, our grief without resolution. We don’t try to force endings where none exist. We just exist in the endless middle together, carrying our incomplete narratives like shared luggage that’s too heavy for any one person to lift alone.
There’s Sarah, who lost her brother to an overdose five years ago and still sets a place for him at Thanksgiving. Not because she’s in denial, but because acknowledging absence doesn’t mean accepting an ending. The place setting isn’t about him anymore—it’s about the space he left, the conversations that continue even though he can’t participate, the way love doesn’t end just because life does.
There’s Marcus, whose son died by suicide three years ago. He still pays for the kid’s phone plan. “I know it’s stupid,” he says, “but canceling it feels like…” He never finishes that sentence. Doesn’t need to. We all know what canceling it feels like. It feels like writing “The End” on a story that isn’t finished, that will never be finished, that continues in every unanswered call to a number that rings in a drawer somewhere, the phone slowly dying like everything does, but never quite dead enough to stop hoping it might ring.
The Mythology of Closure
The suicide prevention people hate this kind of talk. They want recovery narratives with clear conclusions. Stories where someone was suffering, got help, and now they’re better. The end. Success story. Funding justified. Hope restored. But that’s not how it actually works for those of us living in the aftermath. We don’t get to close the book on suicide. It remains an open question, a possibility that hovers at the edges of consciousness, a door that’s never fully closed even when we’re nowhere near walking through it.
I’ve been to their support groups. Sat in those circles of folding chairs that smell like industrial coffee and desperation. Listened to people try to wrestle their messy, ongoing, unending grief into neat little share-sized portions. “It’s been two years since…” “It’s been five years since…” “It’s been ten years since…” As if the number means anything. As if time creates distance from something that lives inside you now, that has rewritten your DNA, that has become part of your molecular structure.
»Name the thing you know that you wish you could unknow. The thing that changed what every room looks like, what every silence sounds like. You can’t, can you? Because it’s not a single piece of knowledge—it’s the lens you see everything through now.«
The facilitators always want to end on hope. They need the meeting to conclude with something uplifting, something that suggests progress, growth, healing. But sometimes people just cry. Sometimes the stories don’t resolve. Sometimes we leave feeling worse than when we came because we’ve been reminded that this doesn’t end, it just continues, and continuing is the only choice that doesn’t require an ending we’ll never get.
The Christmas after Riley died, I kept expecting him to walk through the door. Not hoping—expecting. My brain couldn’t process the permanence, couldn’t accept the ending. It kept generating scenarios where this was all a mistake, a misunderstanding, a really fucked up prank that would reveal itself any moment. Even now, years later, there are moments when I forget. When I think “I should tell Riley about this” before remembering that’s impossible. The ending my brain accepted and the ending my heart refuses to believe are two different things, existing simultaneously, neither one more true than the other.
I still have his number in my phone. Still have our text thread. Hundreds of messages that stop abruptly on February 28, 2015. The last thing he sent me was a meme about dogs. Stupid. Perfect. Completely inadequate as a final communication. But final communications are another myth. Every conversation we ever had continues in my head, evolving, changing, responding to new information he’ll never have.
Sometimes I write him texts I’ll never send. Tell him about things he’s missing. Update him on people he knew. Share the jokes he would have loved. The messages sit there, unsent, unending, accumulating like snow in a globe that never settles.
The Weight of Carrying Forward
Justin’s recliner sat in my house for three years after he died. Three years of walking past it, of seeing it in my peripheral vision and thinking for just a second that he was there. Three years of being unable to throw it away because throwing it away felt like accepting an ending I wasn’t ready to accept. When I finally had someone haul it away, it didn’t feel like closure. It felt like amputation. Another piece of evidence that endings aren’t clean conclusions but ragged tears in the fabric of what was.
The space where it sat is still visible in the carpet—a permanent depression, a ghost of presence that refuses to disappear no matter how many times I vacuum. Sometimes I catch myself avoiding that spot, walking around it like it’s still there. Because in some way, it is. Everything that’s ever been present leaves its mark, changes the space it occupied, continues affecting the world even in absence.
And then Red died.
I need you to understand something about the way loss works, because the world gets it wrong. People talk about grief like it’s a single-occupancy room. Like you process one loss, clear the space, and then the next one moves in. Neat. Orderly. A waitlist at a restaurant where each party gets seated when the previous one leaves.
That’s not how it works. Loss stacks. It accumulates. Each new death doesn’t replace the last one — it sits on top of it, and the one underneath doesn’t compress or shrink to make room. The structure just bears more weight. And more. And more. Until you stop being surprised that the floor hasn’t collapsed and start being genuinely confused about why it hasn’t.
Red was my dog. Mine and Riley’s, really — Red and Adrian were both there when Riley was alive, both in the house, both part of the architecture of a life that ended when I walked into that dining room and found him. Red had been there for the before and the after. He’d lived in both versions of my life, the one with Riley and the one without, and when he died it was like losing a witness. The last living thing that remembered what that house sounded like when Riley was still in it.
And then Jeff died.
I’m not going to give Jeff the full treatment here. Not because he doesn’t deserve it — he does — but because that’s part of the point. When you’ve already lost Riley, when you’ve already found your best friend’s body, when you’ve already sat next to Justin in that recliner watching him dissolve, when your dog who survived all of it with you is gone — when all of that has already happened, the next death doesn’t get its own chapter. It gets added to the list. And the list is the thing that kills you. Not any single name on it, but the length. The way it keeps growing. The way you start to realize that it’s never going to stop growing.
Riley. Red. Justin. Jeff.
Four names. Four different kinds of loss — a best friend, a dog, a friend who was more like a brother, another friend who didn’t make it. Four griefs that don’t process independently, don’t take turns, don’t wait in line. They show up simultaneously, without warning, triggered by different things on different days, layering on top of each other until you can’t tell which ache belongs to which absence.
*»You have a list too, don’t you? Names you could write down. And you’ve noticed how each new name doesn’t just add to the grief — it multiplies it. How losing someone new makes you grieve everyone you’ve already lost all over again, like each death rips open every previous death and says, "Remember this? This is still happening too."«*
The math of loss isn’t addition. It’s something worse. Something without a clean equation. Jeff’s death didn’t add a unit of grief to the pile — it changed the composition of all the grief that was already there. Made Riley’s loss heavier somehow, made Justin’s absence sharper, made Red’s death mean something different than it had the day before. Each loss retroactively rewrites every loss that came before it. The list doesn’t just get longer. It gets denser. And you carry all of it, all the time, because none of it ends.
That’s the myth, right there, in its most relentless form. You think you’re processing. You think you’re making progress. And then someone else dies and you realize you haven’t processed anything — you’ve just been accumulating. Stacking weight on weight on weight, in a structure that was never designed to hold this much, wondering how much more it can take before something fundamental gives.
His girlfriend—the one who was supposed to manage his pills, who called me that morning in tears—she disappeared after the funeral. Moved away, deleted her social media, became her own kind of ghost. But her absence doesn’t end her part of the story. It just means she’s carrying her version of this unending narrative somewhere else, in silence, without witnesses. Another story that continues without conclusion, just growing quieter with distance.
The $700 he owed me is still outstanding. I joke about it sometimes—“Justin still owes me $700”—but it’s not really a joke. It’s an acknowledgment that debts don’t disappear just because the debtor does. That relationships don’t end just because one person exits. That we’re all walking around collecting IOUs from the dead that will never be repaid but can never be forgiven either.
»You feel it, don’t you? The weight of all your own unfinished business. The conversations that stopped mid-sentence. The apologies never given or received. The questions that will never have answers. They don’t end. They just accumulate, like compound interest on a loan you never took out but have to keep paying anyway.«
The grief books all talk about “complicated grief” like it’s the exception, not the rule. Like most grief follows those neat stages, reaches that acceptance phase, finds its ending. But every grief I’ve ever witnessed or experienced has been complicated. Has refused to follow the prescribed trajectory. Has insisted on its own messy, non-linear, never-ending truth.
They pathologize it if you’re still grieving “too much” after a year. They have diagnostic criteria for when normal grief becomes “complicated” or “prolonged.” As if there’s a statute of limitations on loss. As if love comes with an expiration date. As if the connections we form are supposed to have built-in obsolescence that activates upon death.
But here’s what they don’t understand: the grief isn’t the problem. The expectation that it should end is the problem. The myth that we’re supposed to “get over” loss, “move past” trauma, “find closure” after devastation—that’s the pathology. That’s the dysfunction. That’s the lie that makes suffering worse by adding shame to sorrow.
The Circular Nature of Memory
Memory doesn’t believe in endings either. It loops back on itself, finding new angles on old pain, discovering fresh wounds in familiar territory. Each anniversary, birthday, holiday becomes another revolution in grief’s orbit, bringing you back to the same point but at a different altitude.
Riley’s birthday comes every year, and every year I have to remember again that he’s not getting older. He’s frozen at thirty-two, forever young in the worst possible way. Meanwhile, I keep aging, accumulating years he’ll never have, experiences he’ll never share, stories he’ll never hear. The gap between us widens with each passing year, but somehow he never gets further away. He’s right there, just out of reach, forever present in his absence.
The first time I heard someone else say “You alive?” as a casual check-in, I almost threw up. Just those two words, in that order, and suddenly I’m back in that morning, seeing those words on my phone screen, knowing I’ll never send that text again, never get that response, never have that simple confirmation that he’s still here, still breathing, still possible.
Language becomes a minefield when nothing ends. Certain phrases, songs, smells, dates become permanent triggers, little time machines that transport you back to moments that are supposedly over but never really ended. They just got interrupted, paused at the worst possible moment, frozen in a state of incompletion that no amount of time can resolve.
I can’t watch certain shows we used to watch together. Not because they make me sad—sadness would be simple, sadness would be clean. It’s because watching them alone feels like a betrayal of something that isn’t even there anymore. Like I’m breaking a promise I never made to someone who can’t hold me accountable. The shows continued their storylines, their characters evolved, their plots resolved, but my experience of them is frozen at the moment Riley stopped watching.
Sometimes I wonder what he would think about how things turned out. About the pandemic. About the world going incrementally insane. About all the shared references we would have accumulated if he’d stayed. But that wondering doesn’t go anywhere. It just circles back on itself, creating more questions without answers, more stories without endings.
The Mythology of Moving On
“Moving on” might be the cruelest myth of all. As if grief is a location you can leave. As if loss is a chapter you can close. As if love stops just because life does. The phrase itself implies a kind of betrayal—that healing means forgetting, that progress means abandoning the dead, that health looks like pretending they never existed.
But we don’t move on. We move with. We carry our dead with us, not as burdens but as parts of ourselves that continue evolving, continue affecting our choices, continue shaping who we become. They’re not behind us in some past we’re supposed to leave. They’re in us, with us, part of the ongoing story that has no ending because we’re still here telling it.
I’ve had people tell me I need to “let go” of Riley and Justin. As if I’m holding onto them. As if they’re not holding onto me just as much. As if the connections we form in life come with release clauses that activate upon death. But that’s not how love works. That’s not how memory works. That’s not how human connection works.
The therapists have their theories. “Continuing bonds,” they call it now, like it’s some new discovery that people don’t stop loving their dead. Like it’s revolutionary to suggest that relationships continue evolving even when one person is gone. They’ve pathologized grief for so long that simply acknowledging its endlessness feels like progress.
But those of us living in the aftermath have always known this. We’ve always understood that death doesn’t end relationships—it just changes them. Transforms them from active exchanges to internal dialogues. From shared experiences to carried memories. From present tense to every tense at once.
»When was the last time you caught yourself thinking "they would have loved this"? Not as nostalgia—as reflex. As the permanent filter you didn't install but can't uninstall. The dead don't just live in memory. They become the lens.«
The living move forward in time, accumulating new experiences, new connections, new stories. But the dead remain fixed, preserved in memory like insects in amber, perfect and unchanging while we continue our messy evolution around them. The distance grows, but the connection doesn’t weaken. If anything, it intensifies, distills, becomes more essential even as it becomes more impossible.
The Endless Middle
We’re all living in the middle of stories that won’t resolve. Not just the big traumas, the major losses, the dramatic events that reshape our understanding of the world. But the small uncertainties, the minor questions, the little incompletions that accumulate like dust in corners we can’t quite reach.
That friend who just stopped responding one day—what happened? That relationship that faded without explanation—why? That job that ended badly—what if? That choice you made or didn’t make—how would things be different? These stories don’t end. They just stop being told, but they continue existing in the vast library of unfinished narratives we all carry.
Social media makes it worse. Now we get to watch the stories we’re no longer part of continue without us. See the lives that diverged from ours keep going, keep evolving, keep accumulating experiences we’ll never share. Every update is a reminder that endings are artificial constructs. That life is all middle, all continuation, all ongoing narrative that only stops when we stop observing it.
But even observation doesn’t end things. It just changes our relationship to them. Like quantum physics—the act of observing changes what’s being observed, but it doesn’t conclude anything. It just adds another layer to the endless complexity of what is and isn’t, what was and might be, what ended and continues simultaneously.
The daily rituals know this. Those tasks that seem like they should lead somewhere but don’t. They just repeat, day after day, a meditation on the endlessness of existence—not to achieve something, not to reach some destination, but just to continue. To persist. To remain in the middle of whatever story we’re telling ourselves about our lives.
The Comfort of Incompletion
Maybe that’s why we cling so hard to the myth of endings—because the reality is too much to bear. The reality that grief doesn’t end, it just gets integrated. That trauma doesn’t resolve, it just gets managed. That loss doesn’t get overcome, it just gets carried. That healing isn’t a destination but a direction, and sometimes we’re moving backward, and sometimes we’re standing still, and sometimes we’re just spinning in circles, and all of it is normal. All of it is human. All of it is true.
But there’s something oddly comforting about accepting the endlessness once you stop fighting it. Once you stop waiting for the conclusion that isn’t coming. Once you stop expecting resolution and start accepting continuation. There’s a kind of peace in knowing that you don’t have to reach an ending. Don’t have to achieve closure. Don’t have to tie up all the loose ends into something that makes sense.
The stories continue. All of them. Simultaneously. Riley is still dead and still alive in my memory. Justin is still gone and still present in the space he left behind. Badge is still protecting something in Vanuatu while also living in the echo of his memory here. The men with bush knives are still a threat and also ancient history. My shoulder is healed and still healing and will never fully heal.
All of these things are true at once. None of them have endings. They just have today’s version of the ongoing story, tomorrow’s iteration of the endless narrative, the next chapter that isn’t really next because there’s no sequence, no order, no progression toward conclusion.
The myth of endings would have us believe that someday, somehow, the pain will stop, the questions will be answered, the story will conclude. But that’s not how human experience works. We’re all walking around with our unfinished business, our incomplete stories, our grief that shapeshifts but never fully disappears.
Some people find this depressing. The idea that things don’t end, don’t resolve, don’t wrap up neatly with lessons learned and growth achieved. But I find it oddly liberating. It means I don’t have to race toward some finish line. Don’t have to achieve closure by a certain date. Don’t have to pretend I’m “over it” when I’m not, when I’ll never be, when “over it” is just another myth we tell ourselves.

Living in the Ellipsis
So here we are, in the endless middle of everything. The ellipsis that extends infinitely in both directions, never quite reaching a period. Every loss we’ve experienced, every trauma we’ve endured, every question without an answer—they’re all still here, still happening, still continuing their stories in the background of our daily existence.
Riley’s last text to me still sits in my phone: “You alive?”
I never answered. Can’t answer. Will never be able to answer. The cursor still blinks in that text window sometimes when I accidentally open it, waiting for a response that will never come. The question hangs there, suspended in digital amber, neither answered nor answerable, just continuing to exist in its state of eternal incompletion.
And maybe that’s the most honest thing—that question without an answer. That check-in that will never be completed. That connection that was severed but somehow continues. It’s the perfect representation of everything that doesn’t end, everything that can’t be resolved, everything that we carry forward because we have no choice but to carry it.
Justin still owes me $700. The woman in Vanuatu might still be Baptist. Badge might still be alive somehow, against all odds, old and gray but still watching. The men with knives might have forgotten all about me, or they might remember me as the white missionary who disrupted their world. My shoulder might give out again someday, or it might hold forever. The whiteboard might fade completely, or I might keep rewriting it until I die.
None of these stories will resolve. They’ll just accumulate, layer upon layer, creating the complex geology of a life lived in the middle of things. No clear beginnings, no satisfying endings, just this endless accumulation of incomplete narratives that somehow add up to a life.
And that’s not an ending. It’s just where this particular story stops being told, while continuing to exist, continuing to hurt, continuing to mean something that I’ll never fully understand.
The myth of endings would have us believe that someday I’ll delete that text, clean out those contacts, pack away those memories into boxes marked “resolved.” But that’s not how this works. That’s never been how this works.
Instead, I carry it all. The unfinished conversations. The unanswered questions. The stories that stop mid-sentence. The grief that changes shape but never disappears. The healing that isn’t heading toward health but just toward a different way of being wounded.
We’re sold this idea that stories are supposed to have three acts, rising action, climax, resolution. Beginning, middle, end. But life doesn’t give a fuck about narrative structure. Life is all middle. All messy continuation. All stories bleeding into each other without clear boundaries or clean conclusions.
Even this doesn’t end. It just stops. Here. Mid-thought. Mid-feeling. Mid-existence.
Like everything real does.
Because the myth of endings isn’t just about death or loss or trauma. It’s about the entire human experience of expecting conclusions where none exist. We want our relationships to either work out or end definitively. We want our careers to have clear trajectories. We want our growth to be measurable, our healing to be complete, our stories to make sense.
But that’s not what we get. What we get is this: the endless middle. The continuous present. The stories that keep generating new chapters even when we’ve stopped reading. The connections that persist even when the people are gone. The questions that remain questions forever.
And maybe learning to live with that—learning to exist in the incompletion, to breathe in the unresolved, to find peace in the perpetual middle—maybe that’s the only ending that actually exists. Not an ending at all, but an acceptance of endlessness.
A recognition that we’re all just telling stories that will outlive us, creating narratives that will never conclude, building connections that death can’t sever even though it tries.
We continue. The stories continue. Everything continues.
Without ending.
Without resolution.
Without the period at the end of the sentence that would let us finally close the book and walk away.
Instead, we carry the open books with us. All of them. Every story still being written. Every ending that never came. Every ghost that refuses to stop haunting because haunting is just another word for continuing to matter even when mattering should have stopped.
This is what it means to survive something that doesn’t end: you don’t get to close the book. You just learn to carry it. Learn to live with the weight of all those open stories. Learn to breathe around the incompletion.
And sometimes, in the quiet moments when you’re not trying to force an ending, you realize that maybe the continuation is the point. Maybe the fact that it doesn’t end means it mattered. Maybe the pain of incompletion is just love with nowhere to go, connection that persists despite impossibility, humanity refusing to acknowledge the boundaries we’re told should contain it.
Riley is still dead. Justin is still gone. Badge is still a memory. The grief is still here. The questions still unanswered. The stories still continuing.
And I’m still here too. Still writing. Still remembering. Still refusing to pretend that any of this has ended just because time has passed.
Because time doesn’t end things. It just continues them in different forms. Transforms them. Translates them into new languages of loss and love and the impossible persistence of connection despite every logical reason it should have severed.
We don’t get endings. We get continuations. We get the endless middle. We get the myth of closure and the reality of carrying on despite the lack of it.
And somehow, we keep going anyway. Because it’s all we’ve got. Because it’s all we’ve ever had. Because the myth of endings is just that—a myth we tell ourselves to make the endless bearable.
But once you see through the myth, once you recognize it for what it is, something shifts. Not ending. Not concluding. Just… shifting. Continuing. Persisting.
Like grief. Like love. Like every story that refuses to end just because one of the characters exits the stage.
The show goes on. Without resolution. Without conclusion. Without the satisfaction of a final curtain.
Just… on.
