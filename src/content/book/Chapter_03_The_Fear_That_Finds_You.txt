Chapter 3 - The Fear That Finds You
There’s a specific kind of fear that only exists at 3 AM being in a country you’re foreign to for the first time, when the world has been reduced to you, your luggage, and the crushing weight of complete uncertainty. Not the sharp-edged fear of men with bush knives, not the quiet dread of watching someone slip away piece by piece, but something older. Something primal. The fear of being completely untethered from everything familiar, everything safe, everything known.

I felt it the moment I walked out of the Fiji airport into air so thick it felt like breathing underwater. Alone. Lost. The kind of alone that makes your skin feel too tight and your breath too shallow and your heart too loud in your chest. The kind of lost that makes you question every decision that led you here, every choice that wasn’t really a choice, every moment that set this particular beautiful fucking disaster in motion.

But then… music.

An island string band playing in the darkness. Simple melodies woven from ukuleles and handcarved wooden things I couldn’t name. Voices that somehow made 3 AM feel like high noon, like the sun was already overhead, like warmth existed even in this suspended space between arrival and belonging.

One of the band members noticed me—really noticed me—in that way only island people can. Not just seeing my physical presence, but seeing through it. Through the carefully constructed facade of someone pretending they aren’t terrified, through the missionary name tag that felt like playing dress-up, through every layer of who I was pretending to be into who I actually was: a scared kid standing alone on the dark at the edge of the world.

He stopped playing mid-song, but the music didn’t falter. The band kept going, like waves rolling beneath a boat, while he walked over with a smile so genuine it felt like a lifeline.

“Elder. Hi!” His voice carried the same warmth as the music. “The elders will be here to get you soon. Just relax for a bit. It’s ok.”

Just relax. It’s ok. Like it was that simple. Like being ok was something you could choose.

The thing about being a Mormon missionary is that everyone expects you to be fucking perfect. A walking, talking embodiment of purity and light, dressed in pristine white shirts that somehow never stain, speaking in gentle tones about eternal truths while floating three inches above the ground on a cloud of righteousness.

What a crock of shit.

Nobody tells you about the moments when you’re alone in your flat at midnight, sweating through your garments because the fan broke again, cursing at God in language that would make your bishop faint and your mission president write to him in disappointment. Nobody talks about how sometimes the most spiritual experiences happen right after you’ve screamed “fuck this” at the ceiling, your voice raw with frustration and doubt and the kind of bone-deep exhaustion that comes from trying to be something you’re not.
»You remember what this feels like, don’t you? To wear someone else’s expectations like ill-fitting clothes, to smile through the suffocation, to play at being whole when you’re held together with safety pins and spite. Relax. You’re not the only one.«
The Missionary Training Center was its own special kind of purgatory—a place where they tried to polish away every rough edge, smooth out every wrinkle of individuality, turn us all into perfectly identical representatives of “truth.” Like spiritual bootcamp, but with more forced smiling and fewer curse words. At least fewer spoken ones. The ones in our heads? Those got more creative by the day.

I watched them praise the kid who could recite every discussion word-for-perfect-fucking-word, while I sat there mixing yellow and blue Powerade together just to confuse other missionaries while I walked past them in the cafeteria with green. To see if anyone else might recognize that maybe God was as funny as I saw him. In the simple, everyday things. In the human desire to make something new out of something familiar.

My companion from Grenada got it, I think. Even if we didn’t get along. There were moments when I’d catch him stifling a laugh during the most serious parts of training, and I knew he was fighting the same battle—trying to reconcile the messy, beautiful, complicated humanity inside him with the sanitized version we were supposed to become.

The football player from the Air Force Academy—massive guy, heart even bigger than his biceps—he definitely got it. We weren’t even in the same district, but somehow we found each other. Like magnets drawn to whatever didn’t quite fit the mold. We never talked about it directly, never had to. We just recognized something in each other—that same wrestle between reverence and rebellion, between seeking truth and questioning everything we thought we knew about it.

The waiting period in California was worse, in its own way. At least in the MTC there was structure to push against, rules to bend, boundaries to explore. But in California? Just… waiting. Existing in this liminal space between preparation and purpose, between the missionary I was supposed to be and whatever the hell I was becoming.
»You still feel it sometimes, don’t you? That suspended animation between who you were and who you’re supposed to become. That weightless terror of existing in the space between identities, between certainties, between versions of yourself that feel simultaneously possible and impossible. You fucking faker!«
The other missionaries there wore their resentment like cologne—cheap and obvious and trying too hard to mask something else. They looked at my visa assignment like I’d stolen something from them. Like Vanuatu was some prize I hadn’t earned, rather than a terrifying unknown that woke me up at night wondering what the fuck I’d gotten myself into.

“Must be nice,” they’d say, voices dripping with sarcasm. “Getting to go somewhere exotic while we’re stuck here in the suburbs.”

Must be nice. Like I had any fucking clue what I was walking into. Like I hadn’t spent every night since getting my call wondering if I was making the biggest mistake of my life. Like I wasn’t terrified of going somewhere so foreign that even its name felt strange on my tongue.

California sprawled around me like a maze I wasn’t meant to solve, full of pristine suburbs and perfectly manicured faith that felt about as real as the plastic plants in our apartment. The other missionaries moved through their days like they had it all figured out—or at least like they believed they were supposed to have it all figured out.

But at night, when the lights were out and the facade could finally crack, I’d hear them. Tossing in their beds. Muttering in their sleep. Sometimes crying, though they’d never admit it in the daylight. All of us carrying our own versions of fear, our own flavors of doubt, our own ways of wrestling with this thing we’d signed up for without really understanding what it meant.

One night—must have been around 2 AM, that hour when everything feels both more real and less possible—I found myself in the bathroom, staring in the mirror, trying to recognize the person wearing my face. Eyes slightly wild with all the questions I wasn’t supposed to be asking.

“What the fuck are you doing?” I whispered to my reflection, half expecting some divine rebuke for the profanity. But none came. Just silence. Just my own face staring back at me, carrying all the doubt and fear and wild, desperate hope that no one else seemed to see.

And in that moment—that profane, perfect moment—I felt something that might have been peace. Not the sanitized, Sunday School version of peace. Not the kind you can wrap in scripture mastery verses and pass around like spiritual candy. But something realer. Something that lived in the cracks between doctrine and doubt, between sacred and swearing, between who I was and who I was becoming.

The islands themselves were a study in contradictions. From a distance, it looked like every postcard paradise you’ve ever seen: endless sky melting into crystalline water, palm trees swaying in warm breezes, beaches stretching like ribbons of gold against the blue-green sea. The kind of beauty that makes you believe in God, or at least in the possibility of peace. But here’s the thing about paradise: it tricks you. It lulls you into thinking beauty means safety, that somewhere this gorgeous couldn’t possibly harbor anything truly threatening. The sunsets are too perfect, the ocean too blue, the children’s laughter too pure as they chase each other through paths worn smooth by generations of bare feet.
»When was the last time you trusted something beautiful without waiting for it to turn on you? That’s what I thought. Because you’ve already learned what took me a jungle and a bush knife to figure out: paradise is just another word for somewhere you haven’t been hurt yet.«
But up close, the edges sharpen. The jungle isn’t just green—it’s a thousand shades of green, layered so deep they look black in the shadows. The air doesn’t just feel humid—it feels alive, thick enough to chew, heavy with the smell of rotting fruit and blooming flowers and salt carried in from the sea. The insects don’t just buzz—they scream, a constant chorus that rises with the sun and swells into deafening chaos as darkness falls.

It started with a baptism. One decision. One moment that felt routine—almost mundane—in the context of my mission. A grown woman, fully capable of making her own choices, wanted to be baptized. She’d been attending services for months, sneaking away from her village to sit in the back of our small congregation, soaking in the words like the parched earth soaks in the first rains of the season.

So I baptized her.

I can still feel the water—warm from the equatorial sun, clear enough to see straight to the reef where fish swam and corals and anemones clung to rock. The ocean calmed, the clouds parted for the sun. I can still hear the singing—dozens of voices raised in island-harmony, the kind of singing that comes from somewhere deeper than throats, somewhere closer to souls. I can still see her face as she came up from the water, eyes shining with something that looked like freedom.

But she wasn’t just a woman—she was someone’s daughter. And in the cultural structure of her village, that meant she wasn’t free to make that choice on her own. She was considered property. Her father—a chief with skin like weathered leather and eyes that could freeze blood—was furious. Furious enough to want me dead. Furious enough to send his men after me with bush knives sharp enough to split a coconut in one clean stroke.

You don’t realize how fragile safety is until it’s gone. Until you feel it slipping out from under your feet like sand being pulled away by the tide. Until you understand that all the beauty around you—the perfect sunsets, the crystalline water, the flowers that bloom like fireworks in the jungle—none of it means anything when someone wants you dead.

There’s a specific kind of terror that lives in the body. Not in the mind, not in imagination, but in muscle and bone and blood. The way your shoulders tense when footsteps cross the property line. The way your hand remembers the weight of a bush knife before your brain registers danger. The way your breath learns to move silently through lungs that understand survival isn’t about strength—it’s about going unnoticed.

That machete became part of me in those weeks—never more than arm’s reach away, usually closer. During the day, it hung from my belt like it belonged there. At night, it lay beside me, my fingers learning its weight, its balance, the exact texture of its worn handle. Even when I was teaching, even when I was praying, even when I was trying to pretend everything was normal—it was there. The natives noticed, of course. Saw how I’d shifted from the naive missionary who’d arrived months earlier to someone who understood that paradise has teeth.

At first, it was just whispers. Men walking back and forth on the road that crossed through the chief of chiefs’ property where I was staying. Their bare feet silent in the red dirt, the sharp edges of their knives catching the sunlight like mirrors. These weren’t the tools they used for farming or clearing brush anymore—these were weapons, honed to gleaming edges that spoke of purpose, of intent.

My mind started breaking things down into simple, survivable pieces. Like the whiteboard that would anchor me years later with its four basic instructions for existing, I found myself creating mental lists, reducing the overwhelming into manageable steps: Stay quiet. Watch the shadows. Listen for Badge. Keep the knife close. Breathe shallow. Don’t move unless you have to. Each item a small contract with survival, a way to make the impossible feel slightly more possible.
»Maybe you've never had men with knives outside your door. But you know what it's like to reduce your whole world to a checklist just to keep the panic from swallowing you. To break the unbearable into steps small enough that survival feels almost possible.«
But in that fear—right in the middle of it—there was Badge.

Not because I chose him. Not because I was looking for salvation. But because sometimes protection finds you in the most unexpected forms, wearing patchy black fur and carrying strength that has nothing to do with size and everything to do with heart.

Dogs in the islands weren’t pets. They were pests—scavengers that picked through trash piles and slept in the dirt, their ribs showing through mange-spotted fur, their eyes holding the same desperate hunger you see in anything that has to fight daily just to exist. People didn’t name them. They didn’t feed them. They didn’t trust them.

But Badge… Badge was different.

He wasn’t big—maybe fifty-five pounds of muscle and determination wrapped in patchy black fur that had seen better days. His ears were permanently alert, swiveling like radar dishes to catch every sound. His tail had been docked at some point, leaving just a stub that wagged with surprising enthusiasm when he saw me.

He started following me one day, and he never stopped. Not because I deserved it. Not because I’d earned it. But because sometimes love looks like a scraggly dog placing himself between you and danger, asking nothing in return except the chance to protect something that needs protecting.

When I walked, he walked beside me, perfectly in step, without being told. When I stopped, he stopped. When the men from the other village lingered too close, Badge would place himself between me and them—He didn’t bark. He didn’t even growl—not even a snarl—He just stared.

And they stared back. And they hesitated.

There was one night—one moment—where everything crystallized into perfect clarity. The kind of night where even the jungle holds its breath, where insects fall silent, where darkness itself seems to lean in close to listen. The air was heavy with threat, thick with the kind of humidity that makes breathing feel like drowning.

I heard footsteps outside. Slow. Deliberate. Moving along the edge of the property line where the chief’s land met the jungle. I sat up in my hut, bush knife gripped tight in my hand—that familiar weight that had become as essential as breathing—the other clutching a letter I had been writing. My heart was hammering so loud I was sure they could hear it outside. The bush knife’s edge caught what little moonlight filtered through the gaps in the walls, a thin glint of silver in the darkness.

My mind went back to those simple steps, that mental checklist that kept panic from taking over completely: Listen. Wait. Trust Badge. Hold the knife steady. Remember to breathe. Breaking down terror into tasks small enough to handle.

And then I heard Badge.

That growl—low and sharp and guttural. I had never heard anything like it before. It wasn’t just a warning. It was a promise. A declaration that whatever was out there would have to go through him first. A sound that seemed to come from somewhere prehistoric, somewhere carved into canine DNA from when dogs first decided to stand between humans and the darkness.

The footsteps stopped. The air felt electric, like the moment before lightning strikes. My knife hand was sweating, the handle slick against my palm. I could hear my own breath, too loud in the stillness, too fast, too shallow.

And then, slowly, they retreated. The sound of feet moving through grass, growing fainter, disappearing into the jungle’s endless whispers.

I never saw who it was. Never knew how close they got. But I know this: if Badge hadn’t been there, I don’t think I’d be here to tell this story.

He stayed until the end. Until the day the tiny bush plane landed on that cracked runway—more potholes than pavement. He followed me all the way there, sat a few feet away while I climbed the steps, his ears still alert, his eyes still watching, still protecting.

I wanted to take him with me. God, I wanted to scoop him up and bring him home. But I couldn’t. The logistics alone were impossible—quarantine laws, international transport, medical requirements. But more than that… he belonged there. He had chosen his post, his purpose, and who was I to take that from him?

I knelt down in the red dirt one last time. Scratched behind his ears where he liked it best. Told him he was a good boy. The best boy. His stub of a tail wagged, and for a moment—just a moment—his constant vigilance softened into something that looked almost like a smile.

And then I got on the plane.

I never saw him again. But I named a dog after him when I got back. And every time I look into that dog’s eyes, I see him. The scrappy, loyal, fierce guardian who stood between me and the dark.

This wasn’t the first time something I loved disappeared while I was away.

That’s the thing about standing on that cracked runway watching Badge get smaller through a scratched airplane window — my brain didn’t just file it under "sad thing that happened in Vanuatu." It opened a drawer I’d shoved shut years ago. Decades ago. A drawer full of the same lesson, taught over and over in different rooms, different cities, different versions of a kid who kept making the mistake of getting attached.

We had two dogs when I was little. I don’t remember what kind — mutts, probably, the way everything we owned was a mutt version of something nicer that other families had. What I remember is loving them with that stupid, total, uncomplicated love that only little kids and dogs are capable of. The kind where you bury your face in fur and the whole world shrinks down to just warmth and heartbeat and the smell of something alive that’s glad you exist.

Both of them "ran away."

That’s what my mom said. Ran away. Both times, while we were gone from the house. Convenient, right? Two separate dogs, two separate disappearances, both timed perfectly for when we weren’t home to witness it. I was young enough the first time to believe it. Cried for days, left the back door cracked open at night hoping he’d find his way back, the way dogs do in those movies where the family moves across the country and the golden retriever shows up eight months later, matted and thin but tail still wagging.

The second time, I didn’t cry as long. Not because it hurt less. Because some part of me — some animal part that was learning to read the weather in my mother’s moods — already knew. She didn’t want them. She didn’t want me to have them. And what my mother didn’t want had a way of simply ceasing to exist.

*»You had one too, didn’t you? Something you loved that just vanished one day, and the explanation never quite fit, and you learned not to ask twice because the answer wasn’t going to change?«*

I think about those dogs now — what actually happened to them — and I honestly don’t know. Maybe she drove them to a shelter. Maybe she opened the door and shooed them out and locked it behind them. Maybe they actually did run away and the timing really was coincidence and I’m a grown man building a conspiracy theory out of childhood grief. But here’s what I know for certain: my mother was capable of it. Of deciding something was inconvenient and making it gone. Of looking at a kid’s face afterward and not flinching.

This was the same woman who rationed our food every morning. Not portioned — rationed, the way you’d ration supplies in a bunker. Here’s what you get today. This is it. Don’t ask for more. I remember the specific ritual of it — her standing in the kitchen, parceling things out like a warden distributing commissary. Three crackers. A piece of cheese if we were lucky. The math of hunger becomes automatic after a while. You stop thinking about what you want to eat and start calculating what you can afford to eat now versus what you might need later. I was doing that math before I could do long division.

We moved constantly. I counted once — more moves than years I’d been alive. Try building a life on that. Try making friends, try learning a neighborhood, try figuring out which route home doesn’t take you past the kid who wants to beat your ass, when "home" changes every eight months. You learn not to memorize addresses. You learn not to hang posters because you’ll just have to pull them down. You learn that "your room" is just whatever room you’re sleeping in until you’re sleeping somewhere else.

Everything was temporary. That was the lesson. Not spoken — never spoken — but taught with the consistency of a curriculum. The dogs were temporary. The houses were temporary. The schools were temporary. The food in the cupboard was temporary. The only permanent thing was my mother’s ability to make me feel like all of it — the instability, the scarcity, the chaos — was somehow my fault. Behave, or I’ll send you to live with your father. She said it like a threat. Like my dad was a punishment. She’d spent years telling me how terrible he was — painting him as this monster I should be grateful she was protecting me from — while simultaneously holding him over my head like a hammer every time I acted like a normal kid.

Which, for the record, is what I was. A normal kid. Not a bad one. Not a troubled one. Just a boy who sometimes didn’t want to clean his room or talked back or committed the unforgivable sin of being a child in a house run by someone who didn’t particularly want one.

My favorite babysitter — a guy, one of the few men in my early life who was actually kind — died when he got hit by a garbage truck. He was riding his skateboard, listening to his Walkman, and just... gone. I found out the way kids find out things: suddenly, without context, without anyone sitting me down to explain what death means when you’re seven or eight and the person who made you feel safe just doesn’t show up anymore.

And then there was the bike ramp.

I built it myself — or helped build it, the details blur at this distance — this janky plywood ramp that was going to launch me into glory or the emergency room, and it chose the emergency room. I hit it wrong, went over the handlebars, landed on my head. Lost consciousness. Woke up to my mother laughing. Not nervous laughter, not the kind that people do when they’re scared and don’t know how to react. Laughing. Like it was funny. Like watching your kid crumple unconscious onto asphalt was a punchline.

She barely comforted me. I remember that with the same clarity I remember Badge’s growl, the same sensory precision — the grit of the pavement against my palms, the dizzy wrongness of the world tilting, and my mother’s laughter floating above it all like background music in a scene that’s not supposed to be funny.

So yeah. Standing on that runway in Vanuatu, watching a dog I loved get smaller and smaller through a window — my body knew that feeling. Knew it in the bones, in the marrow, in whatever part of you stores the oldest griefs. The specific flavor of loving something and watching it disappear and knowing you can’t do a goddamn thing about it. Badge was the first time I was old enough to name it. But my body had been rehearsing for that moment since I was five years old, standing in a kitchen doorway, calling for a dog that was never coming back.

The difference — the only difference — is that Badge chose to stay until I left. Badge didn’t get disappeared. Badge stood his post. And maybe that’s why losing him cut so deep. Because for the first time, the thing I loved hadn’t been taken while I wasn’t looking. I had to walk away from it with my eyes open.

I’m not sure which is worse. I keep going back and forth on that one.

There’s something about surviving real danger—the kind you can see, the kind with edges and weight and consequences—that changes how you think about the noise in your head. It doesn’t make it stop. It doesn’t make it easier. But it gives you… perspective.
»Here’s the part no one talks about: it doesn’t end when the danger does. You walk away, you fly home, you build a normal life — and the threat assessment never clocks out. It just gets quieter. More polite. Harder to explain to people who’ve never had to sleep with one eye open.«
Because after you’ve felt real fear—after you’ve carried a knife and listened to footsteps in the dark and wondered if this night might be your last—the thoughts that usually feel so sharp, so overwhelming, so impossible to ignore… they start to feel different.

Not less real. Not less painful. But maybe… less final. Because you’ve survived worse. You’ve faced darker nights. And somehow, you’re still here.

The fear changes shape, transforms from something abstract into something concrete, something you can touch, something with weight and substance and form. It becomes a companion rather than an enemy. A teacher rather than a tormentor.

And maybe that’s the real lesson—not that we ever stop being afraid, but that we learn to carry our fears differently. To hold them like Badge held his watch: alert but not anxious, aware but not overwhelmed, ready but not consumed.

Because sometimes the bravest thing isn’t being fearless. Sometimes it’s just breathing through the terror, letting it move through you like weather, knowing that like all storms, this too shall pass.

Even if it leaves us changed. Especially if it leaves us changed. Because of how being terrified makes the choosing matter more.

Here’s what they don’t tell you about facing death: it never really leaves you. Not in the dramatic way people think—not in nightmares or flashbacks or the kind of trauma that makes for good movies. It lives quieter than that. More subtle. Like the way your body remembers how to dance long after the music stops, the way your hands remember the shape of something they haven’t held in years.
»Isn't that the cruelest part? That sometimes safety feels more frightening than danger. Because at least in danger, you knew who you were. At least the rules were clear. Survive. Everything else was noise.«
The fear transforms, becomes something else entirely. Not the sharp-edged terror of men with bush knives, not the primal panic of footsteps in the dark, but something deeper. Something that lives in the marrow of your bones, in the spaces between heartbeats, in the quiet moments when you forget to remember you’re supposed to be okay.

I still catch myself scanning rooms for exits. Still feel my shoulders tense at unexpected movements. Still find my hand reaching for the pocket knife that’s always there now—a descendant of that bush knife that became an extension of my arm in those jungle nights. Not the same weight, not the same purpose, but the same instinct. The same need to have something sharp between me and whatever might come through the darkness.

People see me check for it—that subtle pat of my pocket, that quick confirmation it’s there—and they don’t understand. They see paranoia where I see preparation. They see fear where I see readiness. They don’t know about the nights when that machete was the only thing besides Badge standing between me and men who wanted me dead. Don’t know how the weight of it in my hand became as familiar as my own heartbeat, how I learned to sleep with my fingers wrapped around its handle, how even now, years later, my hand still remembers exactly how to grip it.

Not because I’m afraid, not really. But because my body learned a truth in those jungle nights that my mind can never unlearn: that paradise can turn predatory in the space of a heartbeat, that beauty can hide teeth, that survival isn’t about being strong—it’s about being ready.

Badge understood this. In the way he slept with one ear always alert, in the way he positioned himself between danger and what needed protecting, in the way he carried his vigilance like a gift rather than a burden. He knew something I’m still trying to learn: that being alert doesn’t mean being afraid. That readiness isn’t the same as fear. That sometimes the bravest thing you can do is simply stand your ground and growl into the darkness.

Sometimes I think that’s what all those prayers were really about—not the proper ones or even the profane ones, but the ones that lived in the space between, in the quiet moments between terror and trust, between faith and fuck-it-all. They weren’t really prayers for safety or salvation. They were prayers for the courage to keep standing, to keep breathing, to keep being exactly who we are even when the world demands we be something else.

Because that’s what survival really teaches you: not how to be fearless, but how to be authentically afraid. How to hold both your terror and your truth, your panic and your purpose, your dread and your determination. How to be fully, messily, imperfectly human in a world that often feels inhuman.

And maybe that’s what Badge was trying to show me all along. Not just how to survive, but how to live with the knowledge that survival leaves behind. How to carry your vigilance like a gift. How to let your readiness be a form of love. How to stand between what matters and what threatens it, not because you’re fearless, but because some things are worth being afraid for.

The fear never really leaves. But it changes shape. Becomes something useful. Something true. Something that reminds you not just of what you survived, but of who you became in the surviving.

And maybe that’s enough. Maybe that has to be enough. Maybe that’s what transformation really looks like:

Not the absence of fear, But the presence of something stronger. Something that growls at the darkness. Something that stands its ground. Something that knows exactly what it’s protecting And chooses to protect it anyway.

Even when it’s terrified. Especially when it’s terrified. Because of how being terrified makes the choosing matter more.
