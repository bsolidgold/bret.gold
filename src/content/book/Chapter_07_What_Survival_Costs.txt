Chapter 7 - What Survival Costs
The Weight of Survival

There’s a peculiar gravity that exists between bone and breath, between consciousness and collapse. Not the clean exhaustion of physical labor or the satisfying fatigue of a good workout - the kind that at least gives you permission to feel tired. No, this is something more fundamental, more fucking insidious. Like existence itself has become dense matter, turning each moment into a complex equation of mass and momentum that your body must somehow solve while your mind catalogs all the ways you’re failing at basic human functions.

Most people think survival should look like something. They want a clean line between drowning and swimming, a moment they can point to and say “Look, that’s when I started healing like a proper fucking adult.” They want it to be cinematic — a montage of recovery set to inspiring music, a series of breakthroughs that build into a story worth telling at dinner parties. Something with an arc. Something you could summarize in a caption.

What a crock of shit.

Real survival, the kind that happens in the quiet hours between midnight and morning when even your demons are too exhausted to keep screaming - it looks like nothing at all. Some mornings, waking up feels like being buried alive. The weight of consciousness presses down like wet cement, making every movement a negotiation between necessity and impossibility. The air feels too thick to breathe, the light too sharp to process, the simple act of opening your eyes a battle between what your body needs and what your mind will allow.

The whiteboard hangs on my wall like a monument to basic existence, its surface marked with four lines that shouldn’t need to be written down, shouldn’t require conscious effort, shouldn’t feel like mountains to climb with ankle weights made of shame:

The same four lines. The same daily negotiation.
»You’re reading this like it’s fiction, aren’t you? Like it’s someone else’s story. But tell me—how many mornings have you stared at the ceiling, counting down from ten, waiting for your body to obey you? Yeah. That’s what I thought.«
Four pathetic steps that have become both compass and cage - a way to navigate through days that feel endless and empty, but also a constant reminder of how far I’ve fallen from normal. From when these basic functions of human existence didn’t require written instructions to accomplish. From when being alive didn’t feel like a full-time job I’m always on the verge of quitting but can’t afford to leave.

The morning light filters through blinds I haven’t bothered to dust in months, turning the room into a study in grayscale. Shadows stretch across walls like accusations, each one a reminder of another day I have to somehow survive. The air purifier wheezes in the corner - a mechanical metronome counting out seconds that feel simultaneously too fast and too slow, its rhythm a mocking reminder that time continues even when you’ve lost the ability to move with it.

Even my dogs seem to understand the weight of these mornings. They don’t bounce around demanding walks or breakfast like they used to. Instead, they lie beside me, their warmth a silent anchor, their steady breathing a reminder that survival sometimes just means continuing to inhale and exhale, even when breathing feels like swallowing broken glass.

The thing about this kind of exhaustion is that it doesn’t show up on any medical tests. No blood work can quantify it, no scan can capture it, no diagnostic criteria can contain it. It exists in the space between measurable symptoms, in the gap between what doctors can see and what you can barely explain without sounding like you’re auditioning for a mental hospital intake interview.

It’s not depression - though that threads through it like a dark ribbon. Not anxiety - though that hums beneath everything like a transformer about to blow. Not grief - though that weights it like lead in your bones. It’s something else. Something that makes simply being awake feel like running an ultramarathon with broken ribs. Something that turns basic tasks into complex equations of energy expenditure versus necessity.

Find cup. Fill cup. Lift cup. Drink water.
»Four steps to drink water. You probably thought that was hyperbole until you counted yours this morning.«
Four movements that shouldn’t require strategic planning or a fucking PowerPoint presentation on proper hydration techniques. Yet here we are, breaking down existence into pieces small enough to lift, into problems simple enough to solve, into weights we might be able to carry if we calculate the physics just right and the universe decides to cut us some slack for once.

The body learns its own mathematics of survival - the precise angle needed to get out of bed without your muscles screaming in protest, the exact pressure required to wash your hair without your arms giving out, the careful calibration of movement that might get you through one more day without completely falling apart at the molecular level.

You start timing your blinks like they’re commercial breaks in consciousness - each one a brief respite from the exhausting work of keeping your eyes open and pointed in the appropriate direction. Start measuring distances in terms of energy cost versus necessity - is getting up to pee worth the expenditure? How about answering that text from three days ago? What’s the conversion rate between basic human decency and the crushing cost of consciousness?

Some days, giving up doesn’t sound as impossible as it should. Some days, the only thing keeping you here is the simple fact that leaving requires more energy than staying. Some days, survival looks like nothing more than inertia - a body in motion staying in motion because changing direction requires more fucking effort than your entire being can muster.

But we keep breathing. Keep moving. Keep existing. Not out of courage or meaning but because the body doesn’t know how to quit. It just keeps running its programming — lungs filling, heart pumping, neurons firing — like an employee who didn’t get the memo that the company went bankrupt.

And maybe that’s the first cost of survival - learning to carry the weight of continuing when continuing feels optional, when existence feels like too much work, when being alive requires written instructions and spite-fueled momentum and the simple, stubborn refusal to give the darkness the satisfaction of winning.
»You’re still reading, aren’t you? Don’t stop. Don’t you dare call this nothing.«
Even if winning just means managing to drink some fucking water today.



Everyone's Running

Everyone constructs their own labyrinths of escape - elaborate systems woven from necessity and instinct, beautiful in their complexity, profound in their inevitability. These aren’t simple exits but intricate geographies of avoidance, paths worn smooth with use until they become familiar as heartbeats, necessary as breath.

I know because I’ve mapped them all. Studied them like a fucking anthropologist observing the varied rituals of human denial. It’s fascinating, really, the lengths we’ll go to avoid looking directly at our own darkness.

Some build entire lives around their escape routes, filling their days with children and career achievements, with responsibilities that leave no room for the shadows to seep in. It’s respectable escapism - socially approved, even celebrated. No one calls it running when you’re running toward something that looks like success. No one questions why you need three kids, two jobs, and seventeen hobbies just to feel like you’re allowed to exist.

I never learned to accumulate things. When you’ve moved more times than years you’ve been alive, everything becomes temporary. Furniture is just weight you’ll have to carry to the next place that won’t feel like home either.

Others throw themselves into adventure or extremes, pushing their bodies so hard their minds can’t focus on anything else. They find peace in exhaustion, sanctuary in the kind of fatigue that makes thinking impossible. CrossFit cultists, ultramarathon masochists, weekend warriors turning self-punishment into social media content. At least they’re honest about their desperation, even if they dress it up in moisture-wicking fabric and call it “personal growth.”

Then there are those who choose more obvious exits - drugs, alcohol, self-destruction in all its varied forms. The escapists whose methods we label as dangerous, whose paths we mark as destructive. As if there’s really such difference between numbing yourself with achievement and numbing yourself with chemicals. As if running toward something is so much better than running away. At least they’re not pretending their escape route is a fucking lifestyle brand.

I used to have jiu-jitsu - that perfect alchemy of physical exhaustion and mental focus that could make everything else disappear for a while. The mats became sanctuary where consciousness could narrow to simple physics: pressure and leverage, position and submission, the clean mathematics of bodies in motion. No room for the noise. No space for the void. Just the pure, honest struggle of trying to solve problems with your body while someone else tries to fold you into unnatural shapes.

But that door closed. That path disappeared. That particular form of exodus became unavailable, leaving me with something both more terrifying and more profound: the necessity of full presence in a world where everyone else gets to look away. Where everyone else gets their carefully constructed escape routes and I’m stuck here, fully fucking conscious, unable to look away from my own void.

Some would call it a gift - this inability to escape, this forced confrontation with everything I carry. They would dress it in the language of opportunity, of growth, of the kind of deep personal work that leads to transformation. Those people have never had to sit with their void without distraction, without relief, without even the possibility of temporary reprieve. Those people still have their exits, their escape hatches, their socially acceptable forms of running away.

The cost isn’t just in therapy bills or lost work hours or the endless mental energy spent processing what others get to ignore. It’s in the constant awareness, the inability to look away, the forced confrontation with every uncomfortable truth that most people get to set down sometimes. Like living in a house made entirely of mirrors, each one reflecting back exactly what you’re trying not to see, and all the lights are stuck on high beam.

Even therapists don’t get it. They sit there with their carefully neutral expressions and their arsenal of coping mechanisms, suggesting mindfulness like it’s some kind of magic spell that will make the void stop staring back. As if being more aware is the solution when awareness is the whole fucking problem. As if what I need is more consciousness when consciousness itself feels like drowning in broad daylight.

There’s a particular kind of exhaustion that comes with this level of awareness - not the clean fatigue of physical exertion or the simple tiredness of a long day, but something that lives in your marrow. Something that makes every breath feel like lifting mountains, that turns basic existence into a physics problem you’re too tired to solve but can’t stop trying to calculate.

At least the people with chemical escape routes get to check out sometimes. At least the achievement addicts get the temporary high of accomplishment. At least the adrenaline junkies get their momentary peace in the space between jumping and landing.

But here I am, stuck in the perpetual present tense of my own consciousness, unable to look away, unable to check out, unable to find even temporary reprieve from the cost of being conscious in a world that keeps taking pieces of me but never quite finishes the job.

And maybe that’s the real cost of survival when escape isn’t an option - not just the energy spent staying alive, but the constant, crushing awareness of exactly how much energy it takes. The inability to pretend this is anything other than what it is: a daily choice to continue existing when every cell in your body is asking why.

A choice that would be a lot fucking easier if I could still find peace in the gentle art of folding clothes while people are still in them.



The Body Keeps Score

The body keeps the score, but not in ways you’d expect. Its accounting shows up in the subtle architecture of how we occupy space - in muscle memory and nerve endings, in the careful mathematics of movement and stillness. Each survival writes itself into bone and sinew, becoming part of our fundamental structure, like new growth rings in an ancient tree that’s been struck by lightning but refuses to die out of pure fucking spite.

Think you’re carrying your trauma well? Try telling that to your shoulders - those complex fortifications holding years of tension like geological strata. Try explaining it to your jaw - that vault of unspoken words and swallowed screams that’s probably keeping your dentist’s kids in private school. Your body’s been keeping receipts for every moment you pretended to be fine, and it’s ready to present the bill with interest.

Survival becomes a physical presence, manifesting in the careful way you navigate rooms, in how your body positions itself in relation to doors. Not because you’re planning an escape - you learned long ago that running isn’t an option - but because your nervous system never got the memo that the war is over. Your muscles remember every battle they’ve ever fought, every impact they’ve absorbed, every moment they’ve had to brace for incoming disaster.

The exhaustion settles into your bones like sediment at the bottom of a still lake - not just tiredness, but a fatigue that seeps into the marrow itself. This isn’t the kind of tired sleep can fix. This is the kind of tired that sleep looks at and says “nah, I’m gonna sit this one out.” The kind that makes gravity feel personal, like the universe has singled you out for its own special brand of fuck you.

In the shower, water traces patterns of memory across skin that has learned to hold more than just sensation. The nylon rope still hangs - no longer necessary for physical support but transformed into something more profound: a tangible reminder that survival sometimes requires surrender, that strength occasionally means allowing something else to hold your weight when your own legs have forgotten how to stand. When staying upright feels like trying to balance on a tightrope made of your last remaining nerve.

Sleep transforms from natural state to foreign territory - a country whose borders have become increasingly difficult to cross. The body forgets the simple architecture of rest, reconstructing itself into a permanent watchtower where every muscle stands ready for signals of threat that ended years ago but echo still in the nervous system’s endless corridors. Your bed becomes both sanctuary and battlefield - a place where exhaustion wages war with hypervigilance, and neither side fights fair.

During the day, your body moves through space like a cartographer mapping hostile territory. Muscles remember their mathematics of protection - the precise angles needed to occupy space while remaining ready for flight or fight or freeze. The hands develop their own intelligence - a wisdom of tremors and tensions that speak in the dialect of what they’ve held and what they’ve had to release.

Every movement becomes a careful calculation of necessity versus capacity. Is standing up worth the energy it will cost? Does answering that text require more resources than you can spare? What’s the exchange rate between basic human interaction and the bone-deep exhaustion that follows? Your body’s economy runs on a deficit, each action borrowed against a future you’re not sure you’ll have the strength to reach.

These physical manifestations aren’t symptoms to be cured but the body’s own language of continuation - the sophisticated grammar of persisting when persistence seems impossible. Each ache becomes a word in this complex vocabulary, each tension a sentence in the ongoing story of how we remain present when presence feels like too much weight to bear.

The doctors don’t understand. They run their tests, check their boxes, stare at their screens looking for proof of what your body already knows: that survival writes itself into flesh in ways medicine can’t measure. That trauma isn’t just psychological - it’s physics. It’s the way gravity changes when you’re carrying the weight of everything you’ve survived. The way space warps around memories your muscles can’t forget.

In the quiet hours before dawn, when sleep remains an elusive diplomat refusing to negotiate terms of peace, the body catalogs its inventory of survival: the precise geography of each muscle’s memory, the careful architecture of bones that have learned to carry more than their designed capacity, the intricate network of nerves that have developed their own intelligence of alarm and alert.

Your ribcage becomes a archive of held breath - every time you’ve had to freeze, every moment you’ve had to make yourself small, every instance you’ve had to swallow your voice to stay safe. Your spine carries the history of every time you’ve had to stand when standing felt impossible. Your hands remember every moment they’ve had to stay steady when everything inside was shaking apart.

The physical cost isn’t just in what breaks or bends or scars. It’s in the fundamental rewriting of how the body understands its relationship with existence. Every system adapts, every cell learns new protocols, every fiber recalibrates its understanding of what’s possible and what’s necessary. Your body becomes a living document of everything it’s had to endure to keep you here.

And yet, somehow, the body continues its essential work. The heart maintains its rhythm, each beat a small act of defiance against impossibility. Lungs keep expanding and contracting, even when each inhale feels like a negotiation your body is losing. Cells keep dividing, blood keeps flowing, synapses keep firing - all the quiet, necessary mechanics of staying alive continuing their work even when staying alive feels like too much to ask.

This too is survival - this intricate dance of systems and signals, this complex choreography of persistence. The body becomes both the vessel of our continuation and the record of its cost, holding in its tissues the precise mathematics of what it means to endure when endurance seems impossible.

And maybe that’s the most honest accounting of all - not the stories we tell about healing, not the narratives we construct about recovery, but the simple, stubborn persistence of a body that continues its work even when that work feels impossible. Even when every movement costs more than we think we can afford. Even when existence itself feels like a debt we can never repay.

The body keeps the score. And the score is written in muscle and bone. In sinew and cell. In the precise tension of a jaw that won’t unclench. In the careful calibration of breath that never quite fills the lungs. In the complex mathematics of continuing when your own skeleton feels like a suggestion.

But we keep breathing. Keep moving. Keep existing.

Not because we’re strong. Not because we’re brave. Not because we’ve found some greater purpose or deeper meaning.

But because the body remembers how to live even when the mind forgets why it should want to. Even when every movement feels like defying gravity. Even when existence itself feels like too much weight to bear.

And maybe that’s all we’ve got left to give. Maybe that’s the whole offering.



The Score You Didn’t Earn

Here’s something nobody tells you about moral injury: it doesn’t require you to pull a trigger. It doesn’t require you to be in the room, or even on the continent. It just requires you to be good at your job — and for your job to be part of a machine that kills people.

I scored a 98 on the ASVAB. Ninety-eight. Out of ninety-nine. College had become unaffordable — the kind of unaffordable where the math doesn’t work no matter how many times you run it, no matter how many meal plans you skip, no matter how many textbooks you try to get by without. So the Air Force it was. And because I scored a 98, they made me an air traffic controller. Because apparently when the military finds out you’re smart, they put you in charge of keeping very expensive things from crashing into each other.

I graduated top of my class. Didn’t study. Everyone else — these sharp, serious people who wanted this, who’d prepared for this — they studied like their lives depended on it, flashcards and study groups and late nights with manuals spread across their bunks. I just showed up and it clicked. Like my brain had been waiting for something that moved this fast, something that demanded this kind of focus. The irony of being naturally gifted at the thing that would eventually break you is a special kind of cosmic joke that nobody laughs at.

They stationed me at Spangdahlem Air Base. Germany. The Eifel region — beautiful, green, impossibly foreign. Two squadrons of F-16 Fighting Falcons. One squadron of A-10 Thunderbolt IIs — the ones they call Warthogs, the ones built around a 30-millimeter cannon designed to turn tanks into scrap metal and anything softer than a tank into something you don’t want to think about. Plus the transports, the tankers, the support aircraft. All of it flowing through my scope, my frequencies, my voice clearing them into position and sending them on their way.

»Ever been really, really good at something you wish you were terrible at? Hold that thought.«

The isolation was its own animal. Germany is beautiful the way a museum is beautiful — you can admire it, but you don’t belong there. I had no community. No family within four thousand miles. No church — I was already pulling away from the LDS faith by then, but at least the church had been a structure, a scaffold of people who showed up on Sundays whether they liked you or not. In Germany, I had a dorm room and a job that ran on shifts that made normal human connection a scheduling impossibility. You work mids, you sleep days, you eat alone in a chow hall where the fluorescent lights make everything look vaguely medical.

So there I was. Twenty-something years old, alone in a country where I didn’t speak the language, directing aircraft with a precision that earned me commendations I didn’t want.

The thing about air traffic control on a military base during wartime — and it was wartime, let’s not dress that up — is that you don’t get briefed on what happens after the aircraft leave your airspace. You clear them for departure. You hand them off. They disappear from your scope. And then they come back. Sometimes they come back lighter — less ordnance on the pylons, fewer munitions on the racks. You notice. You’re trained to notice everything, to track every detail, because details keep people alive. But these details — the ones about what left and what didn’t come back on the wings — those aren’t the kind anyone trains you to carry.

I learned what was really happening the way you learn most ugly truths in the military: sideways. Through awards ceremonies where they pin medals on pilots for missions described in language so sanitized it could be a press release. Through the deductive reasoning that comes from being smart enough to score a 98 on the ASVAB and too smart to not connect the dots. Through the simple math of an A-10 leaving with a full combat load and returning empty. You don’t need a classified briefing to understand subtraction.

»You want me to say I didn’t know. That would make this easier for both of us, wouldn’t it? Cleaner. But I knew. And knowing is its own kind of weight.«

The guilt didn’t arrive all at once. It seeped in. Like groundwater finding cracks in a foundation you thought was solid. I’d be on position, headset on, scanning the scope, and suddenly the routine call — "cleared for takeoff, runway two-three, winds one-eight-zero at seven" — would catch in my throat. Not because I forgot the phraseology. I could do the phraseology in my sleep, and sometimes did, waking up mumbling callsigns into my pillow. It caught because I understood, finally and completely, what I was clearing them to do. What I was a part of. What my voice — calm, professional, top-of-the-fucking-class — was enabling.

I couldn’t concentrate. The scope that used to be a puzzle I loved solving became a map of my complicity. Every blip a reminder. Every handoff a transaction I couldn’t undo. The precision that had made me excellent became the thing that made it impossible to look away from what my excellence was serving.

And then the PTSD started. Except it didn’t feel like it was allowed to be PTSD.

That’s the part that’ll really twist the knife — the shame of being broken by something you didn’t directly do. I didn’t fly the missions. Didn’t drop the ordnance. Didn’t pull any trigger. I just told them which runway to use and which heading to fly and wished them good day in a voice so steady it made me sick. I was the guy in the tower. The guy with the comfortable chair and the air conditioning and the coffee that was always either burnt or cold but never actually dangerous. How do you explain to anyone — to a therapist, to a friend, to yourself at three in the morning — that you have PTSD from a desk job? From headphones and radar screens and weather reports?

You don’t. That’s the answer. You don’t explain it, because the explanation sounds absurd even to you. You hear yourself say "I think I have PTSD" and then immediately add "but I wasn’t in combat" like a disclaimer, like an apology for taking up space in a diagnostic category you haven’t earned the right to occupy. Like trauma has a hierarchy and you’re trying to cut in line.

»If you’re thinking "that’s not real PTSD," you’re thinking exactly what I told myself for years. How’d that work out for you when you tried to logic your way out of something that lives in your nervous system?«

The thing about moral injury — and I didn’t have that term then, didn’t have any term for it, just had the shapeless certainty that something inside me had shifted into a configuration that wouldn’t shift back — is that it’s survival cost in its most invisible form. Nobody sees it. There’s no scar, no limp, no visible wound. You walk around looking fine. You do your job. You clear aircraft for departure with a voice like a robot who’s read too many self-help books about professionalism. And inside, something is eating its way through the load-bearing walls of who you thought you were.

I felt weak. That was the dominant note — not guilt, not horror, not moral outrage. Weakness. Because the people flying those missions seemed fine. The pilots came back and ate dinner and called their wives and slept like humans who hadn’t just done the thing I couldn’t stop thinking about. And I was the one falling apart? I was the one who couldn’t sleep, couldn’t eat, couldn’t stop my hands from shaking during the mid-shift when the tower was quiet and my brain had nothing to focus on but the arithmetic of what I’d helped do?

What kind of broken do you have to be to get PTSD from being good at your job in an air-conditioned room?

That’s the question I asked myself every day. For months. While the insomnia got worse and the guilt got heavier and the space between me and every other human being on that base widened into something I couldn’t bridge with small talk or beer or any of the normal mechanisms that military people use to stay tethered to each other.

I couldn’t continue. That’s the simplest way to say it. I couldn’t sit in that tower and clear one more aircraft and pretend that my hands were clean because they never touched the weapons. Force shaping gave me the exit — a voluntary separation, honorable discharge, the military’s version of "we’ll let you leave without making this ugly." I took it like a lifeline thrown to someone who was already underwater.

But here’s what they don’t tell you about leaving: the job ends, the score doesn’t. The body that kept score of every cleared takeoff, every returned aircraft missing its payload, every awards ceremony where someone got a medal for the thing that was keeping me awake — that body came home with me. Packed itself right alongside my uniforms and my dd-214 and the commendations I’ve never hung on any wall.

The body keeps score. Even when the scorekeeper thinks he doesn’t deserve to be counted among the injured. Even when the injury happened in a tower with central heating and a coffee maker. Even when the war was four thousand miles away and you were just the voice on the radio, calm and professional and slowly coming apart at the frequency only dogs and damaged people can hear.

Especially then.



What Breaks Quietly

Between what breaks and what endures lies a territory of subtle losses - the quiet dissolutions that occur while we’re busy surviving the louder catastrophes. These aren’t the dramatic endings or sharp cessations but the gradual fading of possibilities, the slow dimming of lights we once thought would guide us home.

Nobody warns you about this part of survival - the way it eats the edges of your life first, consuming the non-essential parts so quietly you don’t notice until there’s nothing left but the bare minimum required to continue existing. Like watching a fire consume a building from the inside, leaving the walls standing but gutting everything that made it a home.

Friendships don’t end so much as they dissolve into increasingly longer silences. Each unreturned message becomes another small stone in a wall built not by intention but by the simple physics of survival requiring all available energy. The people who once filled your days with light and laughter slowly transform into social media ghosts - their lives continuing in bright squares of carefully curated moments while you watch from the shadows of a muted account, too exhausted to even double-tap your acknowledgment of their continuation.

You start measuring time in unanswered texts, in missed calls, in the growing space between “we should get coffee soon” and the slow acceptance that soon means never. Each connection requires energy you can’t spare, attention you can’t maintain, presence you can’t fake convincingly enough to keep anyone from noticing how far you’ve drifted from the person they used to know.

Dreams don’t die - they just get deferred so many times they forget how to stay awake. The novel you were going to write becomes a Notes app full of fragments that you can’t bear to delete but can’t find the energy to expand. The degree you planned to finish transforms into a login page whose password you’ve had to reset so many times the security questions feel like mockery. The places you were going to visit become browser bookmarks that sink further down the folder hierarchy with each system update.

»Still waiting for someone to notice you’ve disappeared, aren’t you? Still hoping someone will text first. Spoiler: they won’t. And it’s not their fault. Survival’s isolation is a one-way mirror—you see them, but they can’t see you.«

Even love changes its shape in survival’s crucible. The capacity for connection doesn’t disappear, but it transforms into something more complex - like light bending as it passes through deep water. You still feel everything, perhaps even more intensely than before, but the ability to translate those feelings into actions, into presence, into the kind of sustained attention that relationships require… that becomes another casualty of continuing when continuation costs everything you have.

The hidden price isn’t in the big moments of loss or the obvious absences. It’s in the accumulated weight of all the small surrenders - the coffee dates never scheduled, the celebrations missed, the inside jokes that slowly lose their context as the spaces between connections grow too wide to bridge with simple reference. It’s in the way your world contracts, not through conscious choice but through the simple economics of having less and less energy to invest in anything beyond basic existence.

Your space becomes both sanctuary and prison - a carefully constructed environment where everything is precisely where you need it to be because the energy required to adapt to change is energy you simply don’t have. The outside world continues its chaotic dance of progress and motion while you maintain your holding pattern, caught in the amber of survival’s suspended animation.

The professional toll accumulates in missed opportunities and declined invitations. Each “maybe next time” becomes another small withdrawal from the account of possibility, each “I’ll have to pass” another brick in the wall between you and the career trajectory you once imagined. Colleagues who once sought your input slowly learn to work around your absences, their understanding gradually transforming into a kind of professional pity that tastes like copper on the back of your tongue.

Even your relationship with yourself shifts into something more complicated. The person you were, with all their plans and potential, becomes a kind of ghost that haunts the corridors of your consciousness. Not with malice or accusation, but with a quiet presence that reminds you of everything you once thought possible, everything you might have become if survival hadn’t demanded all your resources.

You start measuring success in increasingly smaller increments. Got out of bed? Victory. Answered one email? Achievement unlocked. Managed to eat something that wasn’t explicitly designed for optimal one-handed consumption while lying down? Fucking gold star for effort.

The world doesn’t stop for your survival. It doesn’t pause its endless parade of birthdays and weddings and promotions and milestones. It keeps generating events you should attend, celebrations you should acknowledge, life changes you should commemorate. Each one becomes another small reminder of what you can’t participate in, what you can’t celebrate, what you can’t acknowledge because acknowledgment requires energy you need for basic functions like remembering to drink water and trying not to dissolve completely into the fabric of your unwashed sheets.

The shame becomes its own kind of gravity - a constant downward pull that makes every attempt at connection feel like trying to launch a rocket with a dead battery. You watch friends drift away, unable to explain that it’s not them, it’s not even really you - it’s the simple mathematics of having nothing left to give after survival takes its daily toll.

Your social circle contracts like a dying star, collapsing inward until all that’s left is the dense matter of absolute necessity. The people who understand without requiring explanation. The ones who don’t need response times measured in hours instead of weeks. The ones who know that sometimes survival looks like silence, like absence, like a text message that says “I’m alive” and nothing more because anything more would be a lie.

Even memory changes shape - becomes less linear, more like a deck of cards scattered across time. You start losing track of when things happened, what order events occurred in, which version of yourself experienced which moment. Time becomes elastic, stretching and contracting according to laws of emotional physics you never studied but have to live by anyway.

The world splits into before and after, but the line between them isn’t clean. It’s more like a gradient, a slow fade from color to grayscale, from participation to observation, from being to merely existing. You can’t even point to the moment it happened - it’s like trying to identify the exact second twilight becomes night.

Your Amazon cart becomes a museum of abandoned potential - books you’ll never read, supplies for hobbies you’ll never start, tools for projects you’ll never begin. Each item a small monument to who you thought you’d be by now, who you might have become if survival hadn’t demanded everything you had to give and then asked for fucking interest on top of it.

The ability to plan dissolves like sugar in rain. Future tense becomes a foreign language you once spoke fluently but can barely remember now. Making commitments feels like writing checks against an account you know is empty, each “maybe” and “we’ll see” another small admission of defeat.

You start constructing elaborate architectures of avoidance - complex systems of excuse and explanation designed to hide the simple truth that existing takes everything you have. That you can’t attend the wedding because putting on real pants and maintaining facial expressions appropriate for celebrating sounds like performing open-heart surgery on yourself while making small talk. That you can’t do dinner because the thought of holding a conversation while also remembering how to use utensils feels like trying to solve differential equations while juggling.

The future becomes a kind of theory - something that exists for other people, something you hear about but can’t quite believe in. Like quantum physics or reliable public transportation. You stop buying green bananas because thinking that far ahead feels like tempting fate. Stop making plans that require future you to be more functional than present you because that’s a bet you’ve lost too many times to keep making.

Yet somehow, the world keeps turning. Keeps demanding participation. Keeps generating events and expectations and obligations that pile up like unopened mail on your kitchen counter. Each one a small reminder of how far you’ve drifted from the person who could handle such things without having to calculate the exact energy cost of every social interaction.

The hidden price of survival isn’t just in what you lose - it’s in what you never get to become. The versions of yourself that might have existed if all your energy wasn’t consumed by the simple, exhausting act of continuing to exist. The potential that dies quietly while you’re busy staying alive.

And maybe that’s the cruelest cost of all - not the dreams that die dramatically, but the ones that fade so slowly you don’t notice they’re gone until something reminds you they were ever there. The gradual surrender of everything non-essential until survival becomes not just your primary occupation but your only one.

Yet here you are. Still paying the price of continuation in small surrenders and quiet losses.

Because what’s the alternative? What else is there but to keep going? What choice do you have when stopping costs more than you can afford to pay?

So you keep breathing. Keep existing. Keep paying the price in missed connections and abandoned dreams and potential that dies quietly in the night.

Because survival doesn’t ask if you can afford its cost. It just keeps sending the bill. And you just keep paying. Until there’s nothing left to pay with except the person you might have been.



What Keeps Us Here

In the complex mathematics of survival, the solutions rarely look like what we expect. They’re found not in grand epiphanies or dramatic breakthroughs, but in the tiny anchors that keep us tethered to existence when existence feels impossible to maintain - the small, often absurd reasons we find to continue when continuation seems like more effort than it’s worth.

These aren’t the kinds of reasons anyone puts on a bumper sticker. They don’t fit on a poster. They’re too small, too mundane, too fucking human to package into something marketable. But they’re what actually keeps us here, these tiny threads of connection to a world that often feels like it’s spinning too fast to hold onto.

Some mornings, I wake up because my dogs need to be fed. That’s it. That’s the whole fucking equation. No profound reason, no deep purpose, no inspiring narrative about the triumph of the human spirit. Just the simple fact that these beings depend on me for their survival, and somehow that dependence becomes enough of an anchor to keep me tethered to existence for one more day.

Dogs don’t understand depression. Don’t comprehend trauma. Don’t recognize the weight of memory or the gravity of grief. They just know they’re hungry, they need to go out, they want their walks and their treats and their belly rubs. Their needs become a kind of compass when every other form of navigation fails - a way to find north when all other directions have lost meaning.

Sometimes it’s spite that keeps me here. Pure, petty, human spite - a quiet fuck you to entropy and chaos and all the forces that should have ended me but somehow didn’t. The simple, stubborn refusal to give the universe the satisfaction of winning. There’s a strange kind of peace in spite - a clarity that comes with choosing to continue not because of meaning or purpose or hope, but just because stopping feels too much like surrender.

And I didn’t survive this fucking long just to give up now. The spite wasn’t born from nothing — surviving alone since seventeen teaches you that sometimes anger is the only fuel that doesn’t run out.

Other days, it’s curiosity that keeps me breathing. What happens in the next episode of that show I’ve been watching? How does that story end? What’s that friend from high school going to name their baby? Tiny hooks that catch on consciousness, giving just enough reason to stay for one more hour, one more day, one more breath.

The anchors shift and change, evolving with the tides of necessity. Sometimes it’s the simple momentum of existence - the physical fact that bodies in motion tend to stay in motion, that stopping requires more energy than continuing, that being here has its own kind of gravity that’s harder to escape than to endure.

These aren’t the kinds of reasons that make inspiring stories. They don’t fit into neat narratives about healing or growth or overcoming. They’re too small, too mundane, too human to be held up as examples of the triumphant spirit. But maybe that’s what makes them perfect. Maybe their very smallness is what gives them power - like the tiny hooks mountain climbers use to scale impossible heights, each one capable of holding weight far beyond its size when properly placed.

The whiteboard watches from its crooked angle, its tasks still both challenge and comfort. Today, spite is its own kind of fuel.

I hung it crooked because finding a level felt like too much effort when everything was already tilted sideways. It’s stayed crooked because sometimes the most honest thing you can do is acknowledge that nothing is quite straight in your world right now, and pretending otherwise is just another drain on energy you can’t spare.

These small victories wouldn’t even register as victories to most people. Got out of bed? Congratu-fucking-lations on achieving the bare minimum of human existence. Took a shower? Want a medal for basic hygiene? Managed to go outside? Should we alert the media about this groundbreaking achievement in leaving your room?

But here’s the thing: when your own pulse feels like a question mark, when breathing feels like dragging anchors, when consciousness feels like a bill you can’t afford to pay - these tiny triumphs become everything. They become the difference between continuing and collapsing, between staying and surrendering, between finding enough reasons to try again tomorrow and letting the darkness win today.

This too is what survival costs: the acceptance that sometimes the smallest reasons are the only ones we get, and somehow, they have to be enough. That continuing isn’t always about grand meanings or profound purposes, but about finding enough tiny anchors to hold us here until we remember - or learn for the first time - how to want to stay.

Even if those anchors are nothing more than:
A dog that needs feeding.
A show that needs finishing.
A spite that needs feeding.
A whiteboard that refuses to judge.

Sometimes that’s all you get. Sometimes that has to be enough. Sometimes survival looks like nothing more than stubbornness and spite and the simple refusal to let the darkness win today.

And we keep breathing. Keep moving. Keep existing. Not because we’re brave or strong or have found some deeper meaning or higher purpose, but because momentum is a cruel fucking mistress. Bodies keep breathing even when souls feel crushed, hearts keep beating even when they should have shattered, minds keep thinking even when thinking feels like drowning.

The cost of survival isn’t just in what we lose or what we have to give up. It’s in what we have to accept as enough. The small victories we have to learn to celebrate. The tiny anchors we have to trust to hold our weight.
