Chapter 7 - What Survival Costs
The Weight of Survival

There's a peculiar gravity that exists between bone and breath, between consciousness and collapse. Not the clean exhaustion of physical labor or the satisfying fatigue of a good workout - the kind that at least gives you permission to feel tired. No, this is something more fundamental, more fucking insidious. Like existence itself has become dense matter, turning each moment into a complex equation of mass and momentum that your body must somehow solve while your mind catalogs all the ways you're failing at basic human functions.

Most people think survival should look like something. They want a clean line between drowning and swimming, some Instagram-worthy moment they can point to and say "Look, that's when I started healing like a proper fucking adult." They want it to be cinematic - a montage of recovery set to inspiring music, a series of breakthroughs that add up to something worth putting on LinkedIn.

What a crock of shit.

Real survival, the kind that happens in the quiet hours between midnight and morning when even your demons are too exhausted to keep screaming - it looks like nothing at all. Some mornings, waking up feels like being buried alive. The weight of consciousness presses down like wet cement, making every movement a negotiation between necessity and impossibility. The air feels too thick to breathe, the light too sharp to process, the simple act of opening your eyes a battle between what your body needs and what your mind will allow.

The thing about this kind of exhaustion is that it doesn't show up on any medical tests. No blood work can quantify it, no scan can capture it, no diagnostic criteria can contain it. It exists in the space between measurable symptoms, in the gap between what doctors can see and what you can barely explain without sounding like you're auditioning for a mental hospital intake interview.

It's not depression - though that threads through it like a dark ribbon. Not anxiety - though that hums beneath everything like a transformer about to blow. Not grief - though that weights it like lead in your bones. It's something else. Something that makes simply being awake feel like running an ultramarathon with broken ribs. Something that turns basic tasks into complex equations of energy expenditure versus necessity.

Find cup. Fill cup. Lift cup. Drink water.
»If your jaw feels tight right now, unclench it. If your shoulders are hunched, drop them. Funny, isn't it? How your body holds stories you thought you'd forgotten?«
Four movements that shouldn't require strategic planning or a fucking PowerPoint presentation on proper hydration techniques. Yet here we are, breaking down existence into pieces small enough to lift, into problems simple enough to solve, into weights we might be able to carry if we calculate the physics just right and the universe decides to cut us some slack for once.

Some days, giving up doesn't sound as impossible as it should. Some days, the only thing keeping you here is the simple fact that leaving requires more energy than staying. Some days, survival looks like nothing more than inertia - a body in motion staying in motion because changing direction requires more fucking effort than your entire being can muster.

Even if winning just means managing to drink some fucking water today.


The Score You Didn't Earn

Here's something nobody tells you about moral injury: it doesn't require you to pull a trigger. It doesn't require you to be in the room, or even on the continent. It just requires you to be good at your job — and for your job to be part of a machine that kills people.

I scored a 98 on the ASVAB. Ninety-eight. Out of ninety-nine. College had become unaffordable — the kind of unaffordable where the math doesn't work no matter how many times you run it, no matter how many meal plans you skip, no matter how many textbooks you try to get by without. So the Air Force it was. And because I scored a 98, they made me an air traffic controller. Because apparently when the military finds out you're smart, they put you in charge of keeping very expensive things from crashing into each other.

I graduated top of my class. Didn't study. Everyone else — these sharp, serious people who wanted this, who'd prepared for this — they studied like their lives depended on it, flashcards and study groups and late nights with manuals spread across their bunks. I just showed up and it clicked. Like my brain had been waiting for something that moved this fast, something that demanded this kind of focus. The irony of being naturally gifted at the thing that would eventually break you is a special kind of cosmic joke that nobody laughs at.

They stationed me at Spangdahlem Air Base. Germany. The Eifel region — beautiful, green, impossibly foreign. Two squadrons of F-16 Fighting Falcons. One squadron of A-10 Thunderbolt IIs — the ones they call Warthogs, the ones built around a 30-millimeter cannon designed to turn tanks into scrap metal and anything softer than a tank into something you don't want to think about. Plus the transports, the tankers, the support aircraft. All of it flowing through my scope, my frequencies, my voice clearing them into position and sending them on their way.
»Ever been really, really good at something you wish you were terrible at?«
The isolation was its own animal. Germany is beautiful the way a museum is beautiful — you can admire it, but you don't belong there. I had no community. No family within four thousand miles. No church — I was already pulling away from the LDS faith by then, but at least the church had been a structure, a scaffold of people who showed up on Sundays whether they liked you or not. In Germany, I had a dorm room and a job that ran on shifts that made normal human connection a scheduling impossibility. You work mids, you sleep days, you eat alone in a chow hall where the fluorescent lights make everything look vaguely medical.

So there I was. Twenty-something years old, alone in a country where I didn't speak the language, directing aircraft with a precision that made people say things like "not bad for a guy who never studied" — the military's version of a compliment, which is just an insult that couldn't commit.

The thing about air traffic control on a military base during wartime — and it was wartime, let's not dress that up — is that you don't get briefed on what happens after the aircraft leave your airspace. You clear them for departure. You hand them off. They disappear from your scope. And then they come back. You're in a dark room staring at radar returns. You don't see anything. You just notice things — the way you're trained to notice everything, to track every detail, because details keep people alive. Except some details aren't the kind anyone trains you to carry.

I learned what was really happening the way you learn most ugly truths in the military: sideways. Through awards ceremonies where they pin medals on pilots for missions described in language so sanitized it could be a press release. Through the deductive reasoning that comes from being smart enough to score a 98 on the ASVAB and too smart to not connect the dots. You don't need a classified briefing when you have pattern recognition and a brain that won't stop running the math on what your voice is enabling.
»You want me to say I didn't know. That would make this easier for both of us. But I knew. And knowing is its own kind of weight.«
The guilt didn't arrive all at once. It seeped in. Like groundwater finding cracks in a foundation you thought was solid. I'd be on position, headset on, scanning the scope, and suddenly the routine call — "cleared for takeoff, runway two-three, winds one-eight-zero at seven" — would catch in my throat. Not because I forgot the phraseology. I could do the phraseology in my sleep, and sometimes did, waking up mumbling callsigns into my pillow. It caught because I understood, finally and completely, what I was clearing them to do. What I was a part of. What my voice — calm, professional, top-of-the-fucking-class — was enabling.

I couldn't concentrate. The scope that used to be a puzzle I loved solving became a map of my complicity. Every blip a reminder. Every handoff a transaction I couldn't undo. The precision that had made me excellent became the thing that made it impossible to look away from what my excellence was serving.

And then the PTSD started. Except it didn't feel like it was allowed to be PTSD.

That's the part that'll really twist the knife — the shame of being broken by something you didn't directly do. I didn't fly the missions. Didn't drop the ordnance. Didn't pull any trigger. I just told them which runway to use and which heading to fly and wished them good day in a voice so steady it made me sick. I was the guy in the radar room. The guy in the dark basement with the scope and the headset and the coffee that was always either burnt or cold but never actually dangerous. How do you explain to anyone — to a therapist, to a friend, to yourself at three in the morning — that you have PTSD from a desk job? From headphones and radar screens and weather reports?

You don't. That's the answer. You don't explain it, because the explanation sounds absurd even to you. You hear yourself say "I think I have PTSD" and then immediately add "but I wasn't in combat" like a disclaimer, like an apology for taking up space in a diagnostic category you haven't earned the right to occupy. Like trauma has a hierarchy and you're trying to cut in line.
»If you're thinking "that's not real PTSD," you're thinking exactly what I told myself for years. How'd that work out for you when you tried to logic your way out of something that lives in your nervous system?«
I felt weak. That was the dominant note — not guilt, not horror, not moral outrage. Weakness. Because the people flying those missions seemed fine. The pilots came back and ate dinner and called their wives and slept like humans who hadn't just done the thing I couldn't stop thinking about. And I was the one falling apart? I was the one who couldn't sleep, couldn't eat, couldn't stop my hands from shaking during the mid-shift when the scope was quiet and my brain had nothing to focus on but the arithmetic of what I'd helped do?

What kind of broken do you have to be to get PTSD from being good at your job in an air-conditioned room?

I couldn't continue. That's the simplest way to say it. Force shaping gave me the exit — a voluntary separation, honorable discharge, the military's version of "we'll let you leave without making this ugly." I took it like a lifeline thrown to someone who was already underwater.

But here's what they don't tell you about leaving: the job ends, the score doesn't. The body that kept score of every cleared takeoff, every returned aircraft, every awards ceremony where someone got a medal for the thing that was keeping me awake — that body came home with me. Packed itself right alongside my uniforms and the dd-214 that says "honorable" like that one word is supposed to make everything okay.

Especially then.


Our Wardrobe

I've been thinking about how we carry this shit. Not the coping mechanisms or the therapeutic frameworks or the five stages of whatever — just the basic, daily question of what you do with the darkness when you have to leave the house.

Because there seem to be two camps, and they're both wrong.

Camp one puts it on a pedestal. Wears their trauma like a badge of honor. Builds a brand around their struggle, starts a podcast, gets a tattoo in a font that screams "I've been through something and I need you to acknowledge it." They treat their darkness like it's special. Like suffering makes them interesting.

Camp two hides it. Shoves it so far down that even their therapist can't find it — assuming they'd ever admit to needing a therapist, which they won't, because weakness isn't part of the brand. They smile and they function and they perform normalcy with the precision of someone who's rehearsed "I'm fine" in every key and tempo. And they fall apart in private, in bathrooms and parked cars and 3 AM bedrooms, where nobody can see the cracks.

Both camps are full of shit. And most people who claim to be self-aware are just world-class jugglers — they've mastered the art of keeping all the avoidance balls in the air so they never have to catch one and look at it. They use substances, or people, or both. They exercise until their bodies break. They identify as adventurous or outdoorsy — jumping off cliffs for the dopamine spike so they don't have to sit in a quiet room with the thing they're running from. They haven't done the shadow work. They've just gotten really good at pretending the shadow isn't there.

Here's what I think — and this might not make any sense, but when has that ever stopped me. We shouldn't hide it. But we shouldn't perform it either. We should wear it the way we wear socks. Only the obnoxious ones wear socks for the world to see. Only the gross ones don't wear socks at all. Just wear them. Like unremarkable jeans that nobody notices because they don't have bedazzled pockets or gaudy manufactured holes in them.

Because our darkness isn't special. That's not an insult — it's a relief. It's not unique. It's not a brand. Everyone's got it. Everyone's carrying the same basic human weight of being alive and having been hurt by it. Some people's jeans are more worn than others. But they're all just jeans.

If that makes any sense.
»Which camp are you in? The one performing their pain for Instagram, or the one hiding it so well that their friends think they're the stable one? You're exhausted either way. Because both performances run on the same fuel: the terror that if people saw the actual truth, they'd leave.«

I almost died from salmonella when I was in my late twenties. Not from the things that were supposed to kill me — not from the men with bush knives in Vanuatu, not from the weight of what I carried out of the Air Force, not from the darkness that had already started pulling at my edges. From salmonella. I was in the ICU for a week and a half, quarantined, septic, with cellulitis spreading through my body while a parade of doctors ran test after test and couldn't figure out what was wrong. They thought it was C. diff. They thought it was colitis. They thought it was half a dozen things it wasn't.

It was fucking salmonella.

My head coach from the gym came to visit. Sat with me for hours. We watched Burn Notice together — just the two of us in the ICU, and I told him people had said I looked like the main character. He said, "Yeah, but you're much more dangerous." It was the first time we'd ever really talked. The first time the relationship felt like something real instead of just a guy in a gi telling me what to do on the mats. I didn't know that relationship would never fully open. That the door I'd been trying to walk through would close for good years later, after someone I'd idolized—a person who doesn't deserve to be named—spent years systematically poisoning everything good about that place. But lying in that ICU bed, watching a spy show with a man who'd just called me dangerous, I didn't know any of that yet. I just knew it felt like belonging, and I was so hungry for belonging that almost dying from food poisoning seemed like a reasonable price to pay for one afternoon of it.


What Breaks Quietly

Between what breaks and what endures lies a territory of subtle losses - the quiet dissolutions that occur while we're busy surviving the louder catastrophes. These aren't the dramatic endings or sharp cessations but the gradual fading of possibilities, the slow dimming of lights we once thought would guide us home.

Nobody warns you about this part of survival - the way it eats the edges of your life first, consuming the non-essential parts so quietly you don't notice until there's nothing left but the bare minimum required to continue existing. Like watching a fire consume a building from the inside, leaving the walls standing but gutting everything that made it a home.

Friendships don't end so much as they dissolve into increasingly longer silences. The people who once filled your days with light and laughter slowly transform into social media ghosts - their lives continuing in bright squares of carefully curated moments while you watch from the shadows of a muted account, too exhausted to even double-tap your acknowledgment of their continuation.

Dreams don't die - they just get deferred so many times they forget how to stay awake. The novel you were going to write becomes a Notes app full of fragments that you can't bear to delete but can't find the energy to expand. The degree you planned to finish transforms into a login page whose password you've had to reset so many times the security questions feel like mockery. The places you were going to visit become browser bookmarks that sink further down the folder hierarchy with each system update.

Your Amazon cart becomes a graveyard of abandoned potential - books you'll never read, supplies for hobbies you'll never start, tools for projects you'll never begin. Each item a small monument to who you thought you'd be by now, who you might have become if survival hadn't demanded everything you had to give and then asked for fucking interest on top of it.
»Still waiting for someone to notice you've disappeared, aren't you? Still hoping someone will text first. Spoiler: they won't. And it's not their fault. Survival's isolation is a one-way mirror—you see them, but they can't see you.«
Even love changes its shape in survival's crucible. The capacity for connection doesn't disappear, but it transforms into something more complex - like light bending as it passes through deep water. You still feel everything, perhaps even more intensely than before, but the ability to translate those feelings into actions, into presence, into the kind of sustained attention that relationships require… that becomes another casualty of continuing when continuation costs everything you have.

The hidden price isn't in the big moments of loss or the obvious absences. It's in the accumulated weight of all the small surrenders - the coffee dates never scheduled, the celebrations missed, the inside jokes that slowly lose their context as the spaces between connections grow too wide to bridge. It's in the way your world contracts, not through conscious choice but through the simple economics of having less and less energy to invest in anything beyond basic existence.

You start measuring success in increasingly smaller increments. Got out of bed? Victory. Answered one email? Achievement unlocked. Managed to eat something that wasn't explicitly designed for optimal one-handed consumption while lying down? Fucking gold star for effort.

The ability to plan dissolves like sugar in rain. Future tense becomes a foreign language you once spoke fluently but can barely remember now. Making commitments feels like writing checks against an account you know is empty. You stop buying green bananas because thinking that far ahead feels like tempting fate. Stop making plans that require future you to be more functional than present you because that's a bet you've lost too many times to keep making.

The hidden price of survival isn't just in what you lose - it's in what you never get to become. The versions of yourself that might have existed if all your energy wasn't consumed by the simple, exhausting act of continuing to exist. The potential that dies quietly while you're busy staying alive.

And maybe that's the cruelest cost of all - not the dreams that die dramatically, but the ones that fade so slowly you don't notice they're gone until something reminds you they were ever there.

Yet here you are. Still breathing. Still existing. Still paying the price of continuation in small surrenders and quiet losses.

Because survival doesn't ask if you can afford its cost. It just keeps sending the bill. And you just keep paying. Until there's nothing left to pay with except the person you might have been.


What Keeps Us Here

In the complex mathematics of survival, the solutions rarely look like what we expect. They're found not in grand epiphanies or dramatic breakthroughs, but in the tiny anchors that keep us tethered to existence when existence feels impossible to maintain - the small, often absurd reasons we find to continue when continuation seems like more effort than it's worth.

These aren't the kinds of reasons that make inspiring Instagram posts. They're too small, too mundane, too fucking human to package into something marketable. But they're what actually keeps us here, these tiny threads of connection to a world that often feels like it's spinning too fast to hold onto.

Some mornings, I wake up because my dogs need to be fed. That's it. That's the whole fucking equation. No profound reason, no deep purpose, no inspiring narrative about the triumph of the human spirit. Just the simple fact that these beings depend on me for their survival, and somehow that dependence becomes enough of an anchor to keep me tethered to existence for one more day.

Sometimes it's spite that keeps me here. Pure, petty, human spite - a quiet fuck you to entropy and chaos and all the forces that should have ended me but somehow didn't. The simple, stubborn refusal to give the universe the satisfaction of winning. And I've been surviving on my own since before I could do long division, and I didn't make it this far to quit because a Tuesday felt too heavy.

Other days, it's curiosity that keeps me breathing. What happens in the next episode of that show I've been watching? How does that story end? Tiny hooks that catch on consciousness, giving just enough reason to stay for one more hour, one more day, one more breath.

Even if those anchors are nothing more than:
A dog that needs feeding.
A show that needs finishing.
A spite that needs feeding.
A whiteboard that refuses to judge.
»You're waiting for some grand conclusion, aren't you? Some neatly tied bow, some profound lesson to make all this worth it. But that's not how survival works. There's no crescendo. No final act. Just this—one more breath, one more step, one more stubborn refusal to stop. So, keep going. I'll be here.«
Sometimes that's all you get. And somehow, it has to be enough. Even when it feels like nothing at all.

Especially when it feels like nothing at all.

Because that nothing? That's everything we've got left. And we're too fucking stubborn to let go of even that.
