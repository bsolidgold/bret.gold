Chapter 4 - Why We Freeze Before We Move
Mornings are the worst. Not because of physical tiredness—that would be too simple, too fixable. It's the moment consciousness seeps in, when the veil between sleep and awake starts to thin, and the first thought forms with perfect clarity: "Shit. Not again. Do I really have to do this again?"
»How many mornings have you opened your eyes and immediately wished you hadn't?«
The whiteboard mocks me from its crooked perch. As if consciousness itself isn't the heaviest weight to lift. The paralysis comes in waves—heavier on days with obligations, present but different on "free" days. But the result is always the same. Stay in bed. Stay still. Stay safe in this cocoon of almost-but-not-quite existing.

There's this wall I didn't build. It lives in the space between my abstract mind—where ideas float in perfect, untranslatable clarity—and the concrete world where things need to make sense to other people. On one side, everything exists in this language only I speak, and even I don't fully understand it. On the other side is everyone else, waiting for me to make sense. The translation feels impossible most days.
»You've tried to explain this to someone before, haven't you? The thing in your head that makes perfect sense until you open your mouth and it comes out wrong, or sideways, or not at all. And they looked at you like you were speaking a language they'd never heard. Because you were.«
I have to force myself to connect. Always. Whether it's walking into a room full of strangers or talking to people I've known for years. The only difference is the flavor of the fear. With strangers, it's simple avoidance—stay far enough away that they can't really see you. With people I know, it's more complex. They've already seen enough that rejection feels inevitable, so I orchestrate it myself. Better to control the how and when of abandonment than to let it surprise you.

Teaching should be different. I know I'm good at it—at taking complex ideas and making them accessible, at using humor to put people at ease, at finding ways to relate to anyone trying to learn. But the voice never shuts up. The one that says I'm not good enough. That I'm condescending. That they only let me teach out of pity or exhaustion. That they'll fix my fuckups later.

Too dumb. That's what my dad called it. Not once, not in anger—just as fact. Like weather. The sky is blue, Bret is too dumb. You carry that long enough, it stops sounding like someone else's voice and starts sounding like your own. I can't push it aside or put it in a box. I have to do everything with it staring me in the face and calling me names the whole time. Every class I teach, every word I write, every movement I make comes with its own Greek chorus of criticism and doubt.

Jiu-jitsu is this strange sanctuary where paralysis meets purpose. Sparring—"rolling," as we call it—is a special kind of therapy. It's like forced meditation. When you're doing it—really doing it—you can't think about anything else or you lose. And the real-world translation of losing is dire. Move or be moved. Act or be acted upon. The quiet power of knowing you could physically dominate almost anyone without needing to prove it—it's better than any anti-anxiety medication.

Most of the time.

Sometimes I sit in my car for what feels like hours before class. Not because I'm physically tired or unprepared, but because my mind is mapping every possible way things could go wrong. Each scenario plays out in vivid detail—the ways I could fail, the ways I could embarrass myself, the infinite variations of disaster that await beyond the driver's seat. The paralysis feeds itself. Each moment of hesitation becomes evidence for future hesitation.

There's this moment between thought and action where time loses all meaning. Where intention meets impossibility. Where the space between wanting to move and actually moving becomes an ocean too vast to cross.
»When was the last time you wanted to do something and just... did it? Without the negotiation? Without the committee meeting in your skull?«
Time becomes elastic in these moments. Stretches and contracts in ways that don't make sense. Minutes feel like hours while hours disappear in the blink of an eye. You look up from your phone and realize six hours have passed while you scrolled through the same three apps in an endless loop. Instagram, Twitter, Reddit, repeat. Like if you check one more time, something will be different. Something will change. Something will finally feel real.

The stillness doesn't announce itself. It seeps in through the cracks of consciousness like water finding its way into a basement. One skipped shower because why bother, you're not going anywhere today. One missed meal because the thought of cooking feels like planning an expedition to Mars. One "I'll do it tomorrow" that stretches into two, into ten, into a hundred tomorrows until you can't remember what it felt like to do anything today.

The shame sits heavy—a constant companion to the stillness. What kind of broken thing needs instructions for existing? Everyone else manages this. Children manage this. But you? You just lay here, burning daylight, wasting oxygen, taking up space that could be used by someone who actually deserves to exist.

The worst part isn't the paralysis itself. It's the awareness of it. The crystal clear understanding of exactly what's happening, why it's happening, how it's happening—and still being unable to break free. Like watching yourself drown in slow motion while calmly cataloging the exact chemical composition of the water filling your lungs.

My mind becomes a maze with no exit. Each thought leads to another darker thought. Each possibility branches into infinite terrible futures until even the simplest decision feels like trying to solve complex calculus while the building burns down around you.

The thoughts circle like vultures:
What if I make the wrong choice?
What if this changes everything?
What if I'm not ready?
What if this is the decision that finally breaks me?

Each question spawns a thousand more. My brain spins like a hamster wheel in hell, generating enough anxiety to power a small city but never actually moving forward.

I try to reason with it sometimes. To argue against the voice with logic and evidence. Look at all the times movement led to something good. Look at all the moments when breaking through the paralysis was worth it. But logic doesn't touch this kind of fear. Because the paralysis isn't logical. It's not rational. It's my body and brain conspiring to keep me safe in the only way they know how. By making movement itself feel impossible. By turning possibility into prison.

Memory lives in muscle and bone before it ever reaches consciousness. Your shoulders tense before your mind names the fear. Your throat closes before words can form. Your chest tightens before thoughts can process why. The body knows—has always known—what the mind tries so hard to forget.

My body learned to freeze long before I had a word for it. Before therapists, before diagnoses, before I understood that the flinch when someone raises their voice wasn't weakness—it was curriculum. Learned in kitchens and schoolyards and every new house that was never going to be home.
»Here's the part nobody warns you about: your body learned the lesson before you did. It flinches at sounds you can't consciously remember. It braces for impacts that happened decades ago. Your mind can forgive all it wants—your nervous system keeps its own records.«
The paralysis writes itself into flesh like braille, readable only through touch and tension. My jaw aches from words I can't say. My shoulders burn from weight I can't put down. My stomach churns with acid that has nothing to digest but anxiety. Every cell becomes a prison of potential energy with no release.

I sleep through most things that aren't emergencies. Seriously. If it's not life-threatening, I'm not even rolling over. But the moment something threatens anyone or anything I truly care about? The paralysis shatters. Movement becomes not just possible but inevitable.

Maybe that's the difference between breakthrough and breakdown—immediacy. Whether something absolutely has to be done or not. Whether the cost of staying still finally outweighs the comfort of paralysis.

Some of my writing will never see light. Some of my translations from that abstract language behind the wall will stay locked away, seen by one person or no one at all. Not because it's bad, but because it's too raw, too close to that untranslatable truth that lives in the spaces between thoughts.

In jiu-jitsu, there's this moment just before a roll begins—this breath of space between stillness and explosion. It's not quite paralysis and not quite movement. It's potential energy incarnate. The body knows what's coming, prepares for it, but hasn't committed to any particular path. That's where I live now—in that space between intention and action, between thought and expression, between paralysis and flight.

The voice is still there, calling me names, listing my failures, predicting disasters. But it's starting to sound tired, like it's been shouting for so long it's losing its conviction. Or maybe I'm just learning to hear it differently—not as prophecy but as protection, a guard dog that never learned when to stop barking.

Most days I lose. Stay still. Stay safe. Stay small. But sometimes—in those rare moments when movement becomes more necessary than comfort—something breaks through. Not because I've conquered the paralysis or banished the voice or become someone different. But because something else became more important than fear.

The whiteboard stays crooked. The voice keeps talking. But something has shifted in the space between intention and action. Not because the weight got lighter or the path got clearer, but because I've learned to carry the heaviness differently. To move not despite the paralysis, but with it.

And see what happens next.
