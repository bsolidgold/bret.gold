Chapter 4 - Why We Freeze Before We Move

Shit. Not again.

That's the first thought. Every morning. Before my eyes are even fully open, before the dogs have stirred, before the light through the curtains has had a chance to remind me what time zone I'm in — that voice. Like an alarm clock I never set and can't turn off. Do I really have to do this again? Do I really have to haul this body through another day of being a person?

The whiteboard stares at me from its crooked perch on the wall. Four tasks. Four simple, stupid, impossible tasks. And already — before I've moved a single muscle — the negotiations have started. The committee in my skull convenes. Arguments for and against getting up are weighed with the seriousness of a Supreme Court deliberation, except every justice is me and they all hate each other.

*»How many mornings have you opened your eyes and immediately wished you hadn't? Not in the dramatic way. In the tired way. The way where consciousness itself feels like an imposition you didn't agree to.«*

There's this wall. I didn't build it — at least I don't think I did — but it's mine now. It lives in the space between my brain and my mouth. On one side, everything exists in perfect, untranslatable clarity. Ideas, feelings, observations, connections — they're all there, vivid and whole and making complete sense in a language only I speak. On the other side is the world, waiting for me to make sense. And somewhere between thinking it and saying it, everything falls apart.

My mouth doesn't speak the same language as my brain.

That's the simplest way I can put it, and even that doesn't capture it. It's like being fluent in a language that has no written form — you can think in it, dream in it, feel in it, but the moment you try to translate it for someone else, the meaning collapses. The best you can manage is an approximation that makes you sound either stupid or crazy, and you can see it in their eyes — that flicker of confusion, of concern, of distance — and you stop trying. You swallow the thing you were going to say and replace it with something smaller, something safer, something that fits the container they're holding out for you.

And there goes the analyzer. The protector. You met him in the preface — he's the part of me that just turned a feeling into a three-paragraph explanation. He does that. He's been doing that since I was a kid. Takes something that lives in the gut and runs it through so many filters that by the time it comes out, it's clean and organized and completely disconnected from the thing it was supposed to be.

He got me through a lot. Scored a 98 on the ASVAB without studying. Graduated top of my class in air traffic control. Figured out jiu-jitsu through pattern recognition while other guys drilled the same move a thousand times. The analytical brain is a machine and it's kept me alive and functioning in a world that rewards people who can think their way through problems.

But here's what it costs: I don't know how to feel things. Not the way other people seem to. The only emotion that gets through the wall is overwhelm — this flooding, drowning, everything-at-once wave that comes without warning and leaves me wrecked. There's no middle gear. It's either the analyzer running the show, cool and competent and completely disconnected, or it's the flood. Nothing in between. No gentle sadness, no quiet happiness, no moderate anything. Just the machine or the wave.

My therapists — the early ones, the ones I'd eventually realize were hacks — couldn't break through to the flood. They got the analyzer. Got the articulate, self-aware, reasonable version of me that could discuss his trauma like he was presenting a case study. They thought I was doing great. They thought I was processing. I was performing. And I was so good at it that even I didn't know the difference.

The voice that runs the committee meeting every morning — the one that says I'm not good enough, that I'm broken, that everyone can see it — that voice has a source. Most voices do. They don't come from nowhere. They get installed by someone specific, in a specific room, on a specific day, and then they just... stay. Like malware that came bundled with your operating system.

Mine was installed in a kitchen with ugly linoleum and a light fixture that buzzed.

Too dumb. That's what my dad called it. Called me. Not once, not in a moment of anger he'd later apologize for — just as fact. Casual. Like reporting the weather. The sky is blue, water is wet, Bret is too dumb. He said it the way you'd tell someone the store is closed — not to be cruel, just to save them the trouble of trying. It wasn't a weapon. It was a diagnosis. And because it came from my dad — the one I'd chosen to live with at eleven, the one who was supposed to be the better option — it had the weight of gospel.

He said it about homework. About projects. About questions I asked that apparently had obvious answers. He said it in front of people and to my face and probably to himself when I wasn't around. And the worst part — worse than the word, worse than the repetition, worse than the way it seeped into my bones — was that part of me believed him. Part of me still does. Part of me hears "too dumb" every time I sit down to write, every time I teach a class, every time I open my mouth to say something that matters. The analyzer can argue against it — can point to the ASVAB score, the certifications, the students who say I changed how they think about jiu-jitsu — but the voice doesn't care about evidence. The voice lives deeper than logic. The voice lives in the linoleum and the light fixture and the particular way his face didn't change when he said it.

I was eleven when I moved in with him. Chose to. My mom had given me the option like a threat — "behave or I'll send you to live with your father" — and one day I called her bluff. Took her up on it. Walked out of one bad situation and into a different kind of bad situation, which is a pattern I'd repeat so many times in my life that it almost became a skill.

Dad's house had its own version of scarcity. Different from mom's food rationing but the same genre. The same constant awareness that there wasn't enough and you'd better not ask for more. I used wrestling as a cover — told people I was cutting weight when the truth was there was nothing in the fridge. Eleven years old, pretending voluntary starvation was athletic discipline. That's some world-class rebranding right there. I should've gone into marketing.

*»You learned young too, didn't you? The art of making lack look like choice. Of turning deprivation into a personality trait. We all have our version. "I'm just not hungry." "I like being alone." "I don't need much." All of it code for something we don't have the words for yet. Or maybe we have the words but our mouths don't speak the language.«*

January 2006. I was twenty-six, freshly separated from the Air Force, freshly separated from an engagement that ended when she cheated on me, freshly separated from the LDS church, freshly separated from any sense of who I was or what I was supposed to do with myself. That's a lot of freshly separated. Every structure I'd built my identity on — military, faith, relationship — had collapsed within about a year of each other, and I was standing in the rubble trying to figure out which direction was forward.

Jiu-jitsu wasn't a plan. It wasn't even really a decision. The UFC was getting popular and I'd seen guys grappling on TV and something about it — the rawness, the problem-solving, the way it looked like a conversation between two bodies — hooked into the part of my brain that never stops analyzing. I found a gym. Absolute MMA. I walked in.

I don't know what I expected. Something organized, probably. Something with a clear structure and a progression I could map out and optimize and control — because that's what the analyzer does. What I got was a room that smelled like sweat and rubber mats and something vaguely chemical, a bunch of guys in pajamas tying knots in each other, and an immediate, visceral awareness that I had no idea what I was doing.

A guy I'd never met choked me unconscious within twenty minutes.

I woke up staring at the ceiling with someone's hand on my shoulder asking if I was okay, and my first coherent thought was: okay. This is honest. This is the first honest thing that's happened to me in a year. No pretending. No performing. No managing anyone's expectations or navigating someone's agenda. Just physics and bodies and the absolute, inarguable reality of being put to sleep by a person who was better at this than me. No room for the analyzer. No time for the committee. Just the mat and the roll and the immediate, binary truth of either you move or you get moved.

I went back the next day. And the next. And the next.

The gym replaced the church. Not spiritually — I was done with that. But socially. The LDS church is a machine of community. It demands your time, your money, your social life, your weekends, your Wednesday nights. When you leave, you don't just lose the theology — you lose the entire social infrastructure. Every friend, every gathering, every sense of belonging. You walk away from God and also from the potlucks and the service projects and the people who knew your name and asked about your week. That loss is sneakier than the theological one. The theological one you can argue yourself through. The social one just leaves you alone on a Saturday night wondering where everyone went.

Jiu-jitsu filled that hole. Not perfectly — nothing fills anything perfectly, and anyone who tells you otherwise is selling something — but enough. It gave me a room to walk into where people knew my name and were glad to see me. It gave me a thing to work at that didn't require me to believe in anything except leverage and timing. It gave me a community that earned its closeness through shared suffering on the mats rather than shared mythology in a chapel.

And it gave me something the church never did: permission to be exactly as fucked up as I actually was. On the mat, there's no performance. You can't fake your way through a roll. Your insecurities, your fears, your patterns, your tendencies — they all show up in how you move, how you grip, how you react to pressure. Jiu-jitsu is the only place I've ever been where the gap between who you are and who you're pretending to be gets closed by force. The mat doesn't care about your story. It just shows you who you are.

But even the sanctuary has a parking lot. And the parking lot is where the freeze lives.

I've sat in my car outside the gym for — I don't know. Forty minutes. An hour. Long enough for the car to get cold. Not because I'm physically tired. Not because I'm unprepared. Because my mind is running every possible disaster scenario with the processing power of a supercomputer and the emotional maturity of a nine-year-old.

What if I gas out in the first round and everyone sees how out of shape I am. What if the new guy is actually better than me and the illusion of competence I've been maintaining for years collapses in front of everyone. What if the coach says something and the "too dumb" voice agrees with him and I can't focus for the rest of the night. What if I get injured and can't train and lose the only community I have. What if what if what if what if —

The what-ifs don't stop. They breed. Each one spawns three more, and those three spawn nine more, and within sixty seconds I've constructed a detailed simulation of every possible catastrophe that could unfold in the next ninety minutes, all from the safety of a driver's seat that smells like dog hair and old coffee.

*»You do this too. The car before the thing. The parking lot deliberation. Maybe it's the gym, maybe it's a party, maybe it's a phone call you've been putting off. You sit there running the math on whether the cost of going in exceeds the cost of driving away. And some days the math doesn't work out. Some days you just drive home. And then you run new math on what that says about you as a person.«*

Some nights I go in. Some nights I drive home. There's no pattern to it, no formula I've been able to identify — and believe me, the analyzer has tried. Sometimes the thing that gets me through the door is spite. Sometimes it's boredom. Sometimes I just remember I told someone I'd be there and the guilt outweighs the dread.

The interesting thing — and here's the analyzer earning his keep — is that it's never once been as bad as the parking lot predicted. Not once. In almost twenty years of training, the catastrophe simulations have never materialized. The parking lot has a perfect record of being wrong. And it doesn't matter. The next time I pull into that lot and put the car in park, the projector starts rolling the same disaster reel like it's never been wrong in its life.

The body doesn't learn from evidence. It learns from experience. And somewhere in my history — in the kitchens and the schoolyards and the houses that were never home — my body learned that the safest response to any situation with an unknown outcome is to freeze. To stay still. To stay small. To wait until the threat has passed or until movement becomes so unavoidable that the cost of freezing finally exceeds the cost of moving.

That's the equation. That's the whole thing. The freeze breaks when the cost of staying still gets higher than the cost of moving. Not when you feel ready. Not when the fear goes away. Not when you've talked yourself into bravery or motivation or whatever word people use for the thing they think gets you off the couch. It breaks when something — a dog that needs to pee, a class you promised to attend, a body that can't lie still any longer — tips the scale.

It's not heroic. It's not even a choice, most of the time. It's math. Ugly, animal, survival math that my body runs without consulting my brain.

Some days the math breaks toward movement. Some days it doesn't.

Today, I'm still in the car.
