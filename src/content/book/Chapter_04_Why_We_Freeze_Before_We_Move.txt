Chapter 4 - Why We Freeze Before We Move
Mornings are the worst. Not because of physical tiredness—that would be too simple, too fixable. It's the moment consciousness seeps in, when the veil between sleep and awake starts to thin, and the first thought forms with perfect clarity: "Shit. Not again. Do I really have to do this again?"
»That empty feeling you get—the one you get just after waking up realizing that you didn't die in your sleep—you just felt it. Because you know this moment. This space between dreaming and drowning where you beg whatever's out there to let it all have been a nightmare. Where you pray to wake up with a different brain—one that doesn't wage war against itself before your eyes are even open.«
The first instruction on the whiteboard mocks me: Wake up. As if that's the easy part. As if consciousness itself isn't the heaviest weight to lift. The paralysis comes in waves—heavier on days with obligations, present but different on "free" days. But the result is always the same. Stay in bed. Stay still. Stay safe in this cocoon of almost-but-not-quite existing.

There's this wall I didn't build. It lives in the space between my abstract mind—where ideas float in perfect, untranslatable clarity—and the concrete world where things need to make sense to other people. On one side, everything exists in this language only I speak, and even I don't fully understand it. On the other side is everyone else, waiting for me to make sense. The translation feels impossible most days.

My mouth doesn't speak the same language as my brain. That's the simplest way I can put it. I can think in this language, dream in it, feel in it—but the moment I try to translate it for someone else, the meaning collapses. The best I can manage is an approximation that makes me sound either stupid or crazy, and I can see it in their eyes—that flicker of confusion, of concern, of distance—and I stop trying. I swallow the thing I was going to say and replace it with something smaller, something safer, something that fits the container they're holding out for me.

Too dumb. That's what my dad called it. Called me. Not once, not in anger—just as fact. Like weather. The sky is blue, water is wet, Bret is too dumb. He said it the way you'd tell someone the store was closed—not to be cruel, just to save them the trouble of trying. I was eleven. I'd just moved in with him because my mom's house had gotten bad enough that his house sounded like an upgrade. The bar was underground and I still had to limbo under it to find something worse. And here was this man I'd chosen—chosen—to live with, looking at me with the same expression you'd give a math problem you'd already decided was unsolvable, installing a voice in my head that would outlive him, outlive every therapist who'd try to uninstall it, outlive every accomplishment I'd ever stack against it.

The voice is always there. I can't push it aside or put it in a box. I have to do everything with it staring me in the face and calling me names the whole time. Every class I teach, every word I write, every movement I make comes with its own Greek chorus of criticism and doubt. Teaching should be different—I know I'm good at it, at taking complex ideas and making them accessible, at using humor to put people at ease. But the voice never shuts up. The one that says I'm not good enough. That I'm condescending. That they only let me teach out of pity or exhaustion. That they'll fix my fuckups later.

And the fucked-up part? I scored a 98 on the ASVAB. Ninety-eight out of ninety-nine. Graduated top of my class in air traffic control without studying. Figured out jiu-jitsu through pattern recognition while everyone else drilled the same move a thousand times. This brain—this analytical, pattern-matching, never-stops-running machine—has gotten me through more than most people could handle. But it doesn't know how to feel things. Not the way other people seem to. The only emotion that gets through the wall is overwhelm—this flooding, drowning, everything-at-once wave that comes without warning and leaves me wrecked. There's no middle gear. It's either the machine running the show—cool, competent, completely disconnected—or it's the flood. Nothing in between. No gentle sadness, no quiet happiness, no moderate anything. Just the machine or the wave.
»Your hands have started to shake. Because you know this wall. This invisible barrier between what lives in your head and what you can force out into the world. This impossible distance between thought and expression that feels wider with every attempt to cross it.«
I have to force myself to connect. Always. Whether it's walking into a room full of strangers or talking to people I've known for years. The only difference is the flavor of the fear. With strangers, it's simple avoidance—stay far enough away that they can't really see you. With people I know, it's more complex. They've already seen enough that rejection feels inevitable, so I orchestrate it myself. Better to control the how and when of abandonment than to let it surprise you.

January 2006. I was twenty-six. Freshly out of the Air Force. Freshly out of an engagement that ended when she cheated on me. Freshly out of the LDS church. Freshly out of any sense of who I was or what I was supposed to do with my life. Every structure I'd built my identity on—military, faith, relationship—had collapsed within about a year of each other, and I was standing in the rubble wondering which direction was forward.

The church thing is its own kind of loss that people who haven't been LDS don't fully understand. The church isn't just Sunday. It's your entire social infrastructure—your friends, your weeknight activities, your service projects, your sense of belonging. When you leave, you don't just lose the theology. You lose every friendship that was built on the assumption that you'd always be there, every gathering that assumed your participation, every person who knew your name and asked about your week. You walk away from God and also from the only community you've ever had. The theological loss you can argue yourself through. The social loss just leaves you alone on a Saturday night wondering where everyone went.

So I found jiu-jitsu. Or it found me. The UFC was getting popular and I'd seen guys grappling on TV and something about it—the rawness, the problem-solving, the way it looked like a conversation between two bodies—hooked into whatever part of my brain never shuts off. I walked into Absolute MMA knowing nothing. A guy I'd never met choked me unconscious within twenty minutes, and I woke up staring at the ceiling with someone's hand on my shoulder asking if I was okay, and my first thought was: okay. This is honest. This is the first honest thing that's happened to me in a year.

Jiu-jitsu is this strange sanctuary where paralysis meets purpose. Sparring—"rolling," as we call it—is a special kind of therapy. It's like forced meditation. When you're doing it—really doing it—you can't think about anything else or you lose. And the real-world translation of losing is dire. Move or be moved. Act or be acted upon. The quiet power of knowing you could physically dominate almost anyone without needing to prove it—it's better than any anti-anxiety medication.

Most of the time.

Sometimes I sit in my car for what feels like hours before class. Not because I'm physically tired or unprepared, but because my mind is mapping every possible way things could go wrong. Each scenario plays out in vivid detail—the ways I could fail, the ways I could embarrass myself, the infinite variations of disaster that await beyond the driver's seat. The paralysis feeds itself. Each moment of hesitation becomes evidence for future hesitation. Each frozen instant proof that stillness is safer than movement. My brain builds an airtight case for why staying in the car is the only logical choice, using every past pain as precedent.

I try to reason with it sometimes. To argue against the voice with logic and evidence. Look at all the times movement led to something good. Look at all the moments when breaking through the paralysis was worth it. But logic doesn't touch this kind of fear. Reason can't reach this kind of resistance. Because the paralysis isn't logical. It's not rational. It's my body and brain conspiring to keep me safe in the only way they know how—by making movement itself feel impossible. By turning possibility into prison.

Memory lives in muscle and bone before it ever reaches consciousness. Your shoulders tense before your mind names the fear. Your throat closes before words can form. Your chest tightens before thoughts can process why. The body knows—has always known—what the mind tries so hard to forget. My body learned to freeze long before I had a word for it. Before therapists, before diagnoses, before I understood that the flinch when someone raises their voice wasn't weakness—it was curriculum. Learned in kitchens and schoolyards and every new house that was never going to be home.
»Your breathing just got shallow, didn't it? Because your body remembers what your mind wants to deny. The way trauma writes itself into flesh, the way fear carves paths through your nervous system, the way memory becomes muscle before it becomes thought.«
The paralysis writes itself into flesh like braille, readable only through touch and tension. My jaw aches from words I can't say. My shoulders burn from weight I can't put down. My stomach churns with acid that has nothing to digest but anxiety. Every cell becomes a prison of potential energy with no release.

I sleep through most things that aren't emergencies. Seriously. If it's not life-threatening, I'm not even rolling over. But the moment something threatens anyone or anything I truly care about? The paralysis shatters. Movement becomes not just possible but inevitable.

Maybe that's the difference between breakthrough and breakdown—immediacy. Whether something absolutely has to be done or not. Whether the cost of staying still finally outweighs the comfort of paralysis.

Some of my writing will never see light. Some of my translations from that abstract language behind the wall will stay locked away, seen by one person or no one at all. Not because it's bad, but because it's too raw, too close to that untranslatable truth that lives in the spaces between thoughts.

In jiu-jitsu, there's this moment just before a roll begins—this breath of space between stillness and explosion. It's not quite paralysis and not quite movement. It's potential energy incarnate. The body knows what's coming, prepares for it, but hasn't committed to any particular path. That's where I live now—in that space between intention and action, between thought and expression, between paralysis and flight.

The voice is still there, calling me names, listing my failures, predicting disasters. But it's starting to sound tired, like it's been shouting for so long it's losing its conviction. Or maybe I'm just learning to hear it differently—not as prophecy but as protection, a guard dog that never learned when to stop barking.

Most days I lose. Stay still. Stay safe. Stay small. But sometimes—in those rare moments when movement becomes more necessary than comfort—something breaks through. Not because I've conquered the paralysis or banished the voice or become someone different. But because something else became more important than fear.

The whiteboard stays crooked. The instructions remain basic. The voice keeps talking. But something has shifted in the space between intention and action. Not because the weight got lighter or the path got clearer, but because I've learned to carry the heaviness differently. To move not despite the paralysis, but with it.
»You're holding your breath. Because you recognize this moment—this subtle shift between paralysis and potential. This whisper of change that comes not from conquering the stillness, but from learning to carry it differently. You can breathe now.«
Wake up.

Just wake up.

And see what happens next.
