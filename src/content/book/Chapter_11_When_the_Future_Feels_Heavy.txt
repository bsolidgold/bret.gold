Chapter 11 - When the Future Feels Heavy
The phone sits silent at 4:47 AM. No notifications. No messages. No missed calls. Not because I told people to stop calling, but because I couldn't make myself answer enough times that they learned to stop trying. Each unanswered text wasn't a decision — it was a paralysis, a complete inability to form the words that connection requires, to bridge the gap between what I feel and what I can express.
My thumb hovers over the screen anyway, that familiar muscle memory of checking for messages that stopped coming months ago. Sometimes I open my texts to stare at conversations where their last message sits unanswered. "Hey, thinking of you." "Want to grab coffee?" "You okay?" Each one a hand extended that I couldn't make myself take, not because I didn't want to, but because something in me just… couldn't.
The coffee beside me went cold hours ago. I made it at 2 AM when I gave up on sleep, thinking maybe caffeine would give me the energy to finally respond to something, anything. Instead, it just sits there, developing that oily film on top, abandoned like every attempt at connection I've watched die from my own inexplicable inability to maintain it.

*»You've got one of those right now, don't you? A message sitting open that you've read eleven times. You know exactly what you'd say. You just can't make yourself say it.«*

The weight isn't from what might happen. It's from knowing the pattern will continue: people will reach out, I'll want desperately to respond, I'll craft perfect responses in my head, and then… nothing. Hours will pass. Days. Weeks. Until it's been so long that responding would require explaining the silence, and explaining the silence would require understanding it myself.

I pick up the cold coffee and take a sip anyway. It tastes like disappointment with notes of oxidation, but at least it's something I can successfully complete. A small interaction that doesn't require me to overcome whatever force field keeps me from human connection.

After Riley died, after Justin died, something broke in the machinery that lets people maintain connections. Not a decision to isolate — that would be easier to fight. A fundamental inability to believe I deserve connection, that I'm worth the effort of maintaining relationships, that people actually want to hear from me rather than just being polite.

But here's the thing — the machinery was never functioning properly to begin with. Riley and Justin didn't break it. They just finished the job.

Seven schools. I went to seven different schools before I graduated. Seven fresh starts. Seven times being the new kid walking into a cafeteria where everyone already has their people, scanning the room for an empty seat that won't get me noticed, trying to calibrate the exact level of invisible that keeps you safe but doesn't make you a target. Spoiler: that level doesn't exist. You're either invisible enough that no one helps you, or visible enough that someone decides you're the one they're going to break. There's no middle ground when you're the kid who just showed up.

Chesterton Elementary is where I learned the first lesson. Not reading or math — the real curriculum. A kid and I were wrestling around, play-fighting the way boys do when they're too young to know the difference between roughhousing and violence. Our teacher didn't bother figuring out the distinction. She grabbed me, hard — physically intervened like I was some kind of threat. I was a kid. I panicked. I ran. And that was it. Expelled. Not suspended, not talked to, not given the benefit of the doubt. Expelled. From elementary school. For playing.

Think about that for a second. My first real lesson in how connection works: you try to engage with another kid the way kids engage, an adult decides you're the problem, and you're removed. Not corrected. Removed. The message isn't subtle — you don't belong here, and the act of trying to connect is itself dangerous.

So they sent me to Emerson Elementary, where I was one of the only white kids in the school. Which would've been fine, honestly, except that being different in any visible way when you're already the new kid is like walking into a room with a target pinned to your back and a sign that says PLEASE FORM AN ORDERLY LINE. The bullying was relentless. Daily. Creative in the way only children can be creative when they've identified someone who won't fight back because fighting back is what got him expelled from the last school.

And here's the detail that still sits in my gut like a stone: the school had food programs for low-income kids. Free lunch, breakfast, the works. I qualified. My family was poor. But nobody knew that, or nobody checked, or nobody cared enough to find out, because I was the white kid and white kids don't need help, apparently. So I sat there hungry while the system designed to catch kids like me looked right through me. Invisible when it would've helped. Visible when it hurt.

Then Sprucewood. Then Indian Hills Middle School, where Mrs. Durfee — a teacher, an adult who was supposed to be safe — decided I was worth bullying too. When the kids who are supposed to hurt you hurt you, that's expected. When the adults who are supposed to protect you join in, something rewires. You stop believing in the category of "safe person" entirely.

Then Crescent View. Then Alta High School. Then Jordan.

Seven schools. Seven times walking through new doors hoping this time would be different. Seven times learning it wouldn't be. Each transfer was supposed to be a reset, a chance to start over, but what actually transferred was me — the same kid who'd learned by now that connection was a trap, that trying to belong was just volunteering to be hurt in a new zip code.

*»How many times did you try before you stopped trying? Before the math got simple enough that even hope couldn't argue with it — every attempt equals pain, so stop attempting?«*

But the moment that really welded the lesson into place — the one I still turn over in my head at 3 AM like a coin I can't stop flipping — happened at scout camp. We were going for the rowing merit badge. Me and another kid in a boat, out on the water, doing whatever twelve-year-olds do in a rowboat, which is mostly everything wrong. The boat overturned. Standard kid stuff, embarrassing but survivable. Except when it flipped, I hit my partner in the head with the oar, and the oar-lock — the metal bracket that holds the oar to the boat — hooked into his life jacket and dragged him under.

He was underwater. Caught. Panicking. And I dove under and unhooked him. Got him free. Pulled him up. We were both gasping and choking and terrified, and then bigger scouts came and hauled us out and everyone was fine. Everyone survived. Because I went under and got him.

No one believed me.

Not a single person. The story that stuck was that I'd flipped the boat and hit the kid and almost drowned us both. The part where I dove under and unhooked his life jacket from the oar-lock and pulled him to the surface — that part didn't exist in anyone else's version. I was the kid who almost killed someone, not the kid who saved someone. The other scouts turned it into ammunition. Another reason to push me to the margins.

I literally saved a kid's life and was called a liar for it.

Let that sit for a second. Not because I need you to feel sorry for me — I don't. But because it maps so cleanly onto everything that came after that it almost feels engineered. You do the thing. The brave thing, the connective thing, the reaching-out thing. And the world looks at you and says: no, that didn't happen. You're not that person. You're the problem.

So when I tell you that something broke after Riley and Justin died — it did. But it broke along fault lines that were carved in elementary school hallways and scout camp lakes and every cafeteria where I sat alone pretending I preferred it that way. The adult losses confirmed what the childhood had already taught me: connection is where you go to get hurt. Belonging is a trick. And no matter what you do — even if you literally pull someone from drowning — no one's going to believe you're worth keeping around.

My phone has maybe twelve contacts I haven't deleted. Not because I decided to cut them off, but because deleting them would make official what my silence has already accomplished. Half of them probably wonder what they did wrong. The other half have probably written me off as someone who doesn't care. None of them know I've typed a thousand messages I couldn't send, that I care so much it paralyzes me, that I don't believe I deserve their continued interest.

The Mental Spiral
It starts with one thought. Something simple. Something reasonable. I should text them back.

But it's been three weeks. They probably hate me now. They probably think I'm an asshole. I am an asshole. Who takes three weeks to respond to "how are you?" I should explain. But how do I explain that I wanted to respond but couldn't? That I typed twenty responses but deleted them all? That sounds insane. I am insane. They deserve better friends. Everyone deserves better than someone who can't even answer a simple text. If I respond now it'll just be awkward. Better to let it die. But then I'll have killed another connection. But it's already dead, isn't it? I killed it by not responding. Like I kill everything—

Twenty seconds. That whole paragraph. That's how long it takes to go from a simple thought about responding to a text to complete shutdown. My brain has gotten terrifyingly good at it — the way a river gets good at carving the same canyon deeper.

Last night it happened at the grocery store. I saw someone I actually like. They smiled, started walking toward me, and I turned down the cereal aisle like I was suddenly desperate for Cheerios. Not because I dislike them — I genuinely like them. But because I couldn't face the conversation where they ask why I never responded to their last five messages. So I hid behind a display of granola bars. Like a fucking child. And then I sat in my car for twenty minutes afterward, not driving, just marinating in it.

*»Which aisle do you hide in? Or is it the thing where you suddenly need to check your phone very intently, or you walk the other direction and pretend you didn't see them? We've all got a version.«*

Three nights ago, I spent four hours staring at my phone, at a message from someone I genuinely love. Five words was all I needed: "I'm here, thinking of you." My fingers wouldn't type. Or they'd type and I'd delete. Type and delete. Four hours. Then I put the phone down and went to bed. Another connection left to die of neglect I never chose.

The scariest thing about the future isn't that people will leave. It's that they'll try to stay and I won't be able to let them. That I'm building my own prison one unanswered message at a time, and I can see every brick going into place, and I still can't stop laying them.

Last week, I made a list of all the people I haven't responded to. Not to finally respond — I still can't — but just to see the scope of it laid out on paper. Twenty-three names. Twenty-three people who reached out and got silence. They fell into three categories:

People who still occasionally try (getting rarer).
People who gave up but would probably respond if I could reach out.
People who've written me off completely. Most of them.

The weight isn't guilt exactly — guilt implies choice. It's more like grief for the person I can't seem to be. The one who responds to texts. The one who maintains friendships. The one whose brain doesn't lock up at the simple act of saying hello back.

So I'm trying something new. Not fixing it — I've tried that. But when I can't respond to a message, instead of spiraling into shame, I try to just sit with the truth: I can't do this right now. Not won't. Can't.

It doesn't make me respond. Doesn't break the paralysis. But it changes the weight from shame to something more like sorrow. And sorrow, at least, is honest.

*»You're thinking this sounds defeatist. Like I'm giving up. But you know the difference between surrendering and drowning, right? One of them is a choice. The other is what happens when you've been treading water so long your legs stop working.«*

Small Moments of Now
Right now, in this exact moment, my dogs are scattered around me like furry anchors. Badge is snoring — named after the original, carrying his own weight of meaning. Huck's sprawled across my feet like he's trying to keep me from floating away. Ripley's curled against my side, occasionally sighing in her sleep. They don't know I have seventeen unanswered messages. They don't understand why no one comes over anymore. They just know I'm here, and for them, that's enough.

Dogs don't need you to respond to texts. They don't require you to maintain the relationship through words you can't find. They don't keep score.

There's a particular quality to morning light that makes dust motes visible. They float like tiny planets in the shaft of sun coming through my window. Each one alone, separate, drifting. They don't try to connect. They don't fail to respond to each other. They just exist.

I reach for my coffee — still cold, still disappointing, but real. The ceramic is smooth against my palm. No words to find. No responses to craft. No fear that the mug will abandon me if I don't maintain our relationship properly.

*»Notice where you are right now. Not where your head went — where you actually are. Whatever surface is holding you up, whatever room you're sitting in. That's real. The unanswered messages aren't going anywhere. They'll still be there in three seconds. But so will you.«*

These moments don't solve anything. The messages remain unanswered. The paralysis remains. But for a few seconds, existence shrinks down to something manageable: just this breath, just this warmth, just this moment of being alive without having to perform aliveness for anyone else.

Ripley stretches, yawns with her whole body, and resettles against my side. Huck readjusts across my feet. Badge continues his snoring symphony. They don't know about the messages I can't answer. Don't care that I hid from someone at the store. They just know that right now, connection exists without effort, without words, without the risk of me failing to maintain it.

I used to think that every day was a battle I was losing. Most days I lose — that was the running tally, the scoreboard I kept in my head. Most days I can't answer the messages, can't make the connections, can't be the person my phone thinks I should be.

But somewhere in the past few months, the math shifted. Not dramatically. Not in a way I could point to and say there, that's when it changed. More like the way seasons turn — you don't notice the days getting longer until suddenly you're standing in light that wasn't there a week ago.

Most days aren't all anymore.

That's it. That's the whole shift. Not "most days I win" — that would be bullshit and we both know it. Just: most days aren't all. There are cracks in the all. Moments where the paralysis loosens its grip for just long enough that I can scratch a dog's ears without the shame of unanswered messages crushing me. Minutes where the cold coffee tastes like something other than failure. Seconds where the morning light hits the dust motes and I forget, briefly, that I'm supposed to be drowning.

I can handle the next five minutes. I can manage three breakfasts and ear scratches and maybe, if I'm feeling particularly brave, I'll open those messages again. Not to answer — I know I probably can't. But just to read them. To remember that people reached out, even if I couldn't reach back.

*»That's not hope. Don't call it that — it'll spook and bolt. It's just a crack in the wall. A hairline fracture in the all. You've probably got one too, if you stop clenching long enough to notice.«*

The future will wait. It always does. And when it arrives, it'll probably look a lot like today — me and three dogs and cold coffee and messages I can't answer. But the all is cracking. Slowly. In places I can't predict and can't control.

That's not trust exactly. But it's something.
Badge shifts, lets out a contented groan, and starts snoring again. Ripley opens one eye to check if anything important is happening, decides it's not, and goes back to sleep. Huck's tail thumps once against the floor for no reason anyone but him understands.

I don't close the messages this time. I just let them sit there, open on the screen, while the light moves across the ceiling and the dogs breathe and the coffee gets even colder.

Still not answering. But not closing them either.
