Chapter 8 - Why Rest Feels Like Failure

I'm in bed. It's the middle of the afternoon and I'm in bed and the blinds are closed and the room is dark and time is just a concept that applies to people who have a reason to track it. I'm not sleeping. I'm not resting. I'm not recovering from anything specific. I'm just here. Metabolizing oxygen. Converting calories I barely consumed into energy I won't use. Lying in a bed that has taken the shape of my body from the sheer number of hours I've spent not leaving it.

And the thought is there. The one that's always there. Quiet. Patient. Waiting for exactly this kind of silence to make itself heard:

You shouldn't be here.

Not "here" as in this bed, this room, this house. "Here" as in alive. As in existing. As in taking up space and resources and oxygen that could be used by someone who actually has a reason to use them. The thought doesn't scream. It doesn't need to. It just sits in the dark with me like a roommate who's been here longer than I have, and it says the thing it always says, in the same reasonable tone, with the same calm certainty:

You should have never been born.
»You know this thought. Not because I described it well. Because you've had it. Sitting in the same dark, breathing the same stale air, arriving at the same conclusion with the same terrible calm. And the scariest part wasn't the thought itself. It was how much sense it made at the time.«
The thing about that voice — the one that says I'm not worth it, that I shouldn't exist, that the world would measurably improve if I subtracted myself from it — is that it doesn't sound like anyone else anymore. That's the trick. That's the long game. It used to sound like my dad. "Too dumb." The kitchen, the linoleum, the light fixture — I told you about that. It used to sound like Becky. The cold frequency. It used to sound like my mom. "Behave or I'll send you to live with your father" — like my existence was a punishment she could assign to someone else when it became inconvenient.

But somewhere along the way — and I can't tell you when, because it happened the way erosion happens, too slow to see and too constant to stop — their voices merged with mine. Or mine merged with theirs. Or there stopped being a difference. The criticism that used to come from the outside started generating from the inside, and now I can't tell if "I shouldn't exist" is a thought I'm having or a thought I was given and just never returned.

That's the cruelest part of growing up in a house where you're not wanted. It's not the pain. Pain fades. It's that the message outlives the messenger. The people who taught you that you were nothing can apologize, can die, can disappear completely from your life — and the curriculum remains. Running on autopilot. Self-sustaining. A voice that sounds so much like your own that fighting it feels like fighting yourself.

Becky. My stepmom. My dad's second wife. I moved in with them when I was eleven because my mom's house had gotten bad enough that my dad's house sounded like an upgrade. Here's the thing about being the kid from the previous marriage: you're evidence. Walking, breathing, taking-up-space-at-the-dinner-table evidence of a life your stepparent wasn't part of. Becky handled this with the kind of quiet, surgical cruelty that doesn't leave marks anyone can point to.

She wasn't dramatic. No screaming, no scenes. It was smaller. And smaller is worse because smaller gets into the cracks.

Two radio frequencies. That's the simplest way to describe the house. When she talked to her son, the frequency was warm. When she talked to me, it was static. Same woman, same kitchen, same sentence structure — but the temperature changed. I was a kid. I didn't have the vocabulary for what I was feeling. I just knew that when she looked at her son, something in her face opened, and when she looked at me, something closed. Like a door I could see but couldn't reach.

The kitchen table was a courtroom. I was always on trial for crimes I couldn't name. Sitting wrong. Eating wrong. Breathing at the wrong volume. Existing with the particular inconvenience of being someone else's child in her house. The charges were never read aloud. The verdict was always the same. And the sentence was this: you don't belong here. Everyone knows it. We're all going to pretend we don't until you figure out how to make yourself small enough to disappear.
»You know the frequency. The one where someone is technically being civil but every syllable carries the weight of wishing you weren't there. You learned to hear it before you could name it. Your body still flinches when it picks up the signal — even now, even in rooms full of people who actually want you there. Because the body doesn't forget which frequency means you're a problem.«
That house didn't teach me that rest is failure. It taught me something worse: that I was an inconvenience. That my presence was a burden. That the space I occupied — at the table, on the couch, in the hallway, in the world — would be better used by someone who deserved it. Becky never said "you shouldn't exist." She didn't have to. The frequency said it for her.

I had a bird. Don't remember what kind — something small, in a cage, in a room where very little else was mine. It died. I was a kid. I didn't have a manual for dead things. I just knew it was gone and I was confused and handled the whole situation the way an eleven-year-old handles things, which is badly.

They thought I killed it.

My dad and Becky. They never said it to my face — that's not how it worked in that house. I figured it out the way kids figure out the things adults think they're hiding: by reading the silences, the glances, the shift in the frequency. They looked at me and saw a kid who would kill a bird.

They don't know I know this. To this day. They walked around with this theory about me in their heads and I walked around knowing they had it, and nobody ever said a word. That's how it works in houses like that. The most important conversations are the ones that never happen. And the kid just absorbs it. Files it alongside "too dumb" and the cold frequency and the general understanding that he is guilty until proven innocent, except nobody explains what innocence looks like, so he just stays guilty. Forever. For everything.
»You were accused of something you didn't do. Maybe not a bird. But something. And the accusation stuck not because there was evidence but because you were the kind of kid accusations stuck to. The kind whose denial sounds like a confession no matter how you say it.«
My dad got me a guitar once. The only gift I can remember him giving me. I don't know why — maybe guilt, maybe a flash of fatherly impulse, maybe it was on sale. It was mine. The one thing in that house that was unambiguously, no-asterisk mine. And I loved it with a fierceness that probably scared me even then, because I already knew what happened to things I loved — they ran away, or they died, or they got taken while I wasn't looking.

His girlfriend smashed it.

Not Becky — they'd divorced by then, because my dad cheated on her. After Becky came the girlfriends. A rotating cast. One drank constantly. Another called the cops on me because she couldn't get me to do something unreasonable. My dad watched all of this with the disinterest of a man who'd already decided I was unsolvable. She smashed the guitar and the world kept turning like it was nothing. Like it was just an object. Like the only gift my father ever gave me wasn't in pieces on the floor and I wasn't standing there understanding, in a way I couldn't have articulated but felt in every cell, that nothing was mine. Nothing would ever be mine. That anything I loved could be destroyed by anyone at any time and there would be no consequence. Just the expectation that I'd clean it up and stop being so sensitive.

He kicked me out when I was seventeen. Put me on the sidewalk with a garbage bag. And I stood there thinking — not feeling, thinking, because the feeling wouldn't come for decades — I stood there thinking: of course. This is how it was always going to end. The equation was right all along.

Years later. My half-brother's wedding. Washington State. Becky was there. She tried to apologize. For how she treated me. Those were approximately her words. An apology for the kitchen-table courtroom and the two frequencies and the way I'd been made to feel like a tenant in a house where I was supposed to be family.

I brushed it off. Told her it was fine. Let her off the hook the way I let everyone off the hook — quickly, cleanly, with a smile that costs more than anyone watching could possibly know. I was already carrying other shit that weekend. Didn't have the bandwidth to stand in a banquet hall and process thirty years of being the wrong kid in the wrong house while someone's photographer waited for us to clear the shot.

She probably walked away thinking it landed. Thinking the account was settled. That a few sentences at a wedding could close the ledger on years of making a child feel like an intruder in his own home.

It didn't land. The ledger isn't closed. But I let her think it was because that was easier than feeling it, and easier has been my operating system since I was old enough to learn that feelings are dangerous and the safest thing to do with them is nothing.
»Someone owes you an apology they'll never give right. Maybe they've even tried. And you did the same thing — took it, smiled, said it's fine, and walked away carrying the same weight you walked in with. Not because you're noble. Because the alternative was breaking open in front of people who haven't earned the right to see you break.«
So here I am. In the dark. In bed. Middle of the afternoon.

The voice says I shouldn't exist. And the voice sounds like mine. And the thing that makes it so hard to argue with isn't the volume or the cruelty — it's the evidence. Because I can trace the logic. I can follow the thread from the rationed food to the dogs that ran away to the cold frequency to the dead bird to the smashed guitar to the sidewalk with the garbage bag to Riley's door to Justin's hospital bed to Dani's Instagram eulogy to this bed, this room, this dark — and the through-line is clear and clean and devastating: everywhere I go, things end. Everyone I love, I lose. Everything I touch, I break or it breaks me. The equation hasn't changed since I was a kid. The only variable is how long I keep trying to get a different answer.

That's not self-pity. That's math. Bad math — math done by a part of my brain that was installed in a kitchen with ugly linoleum by people who didn't want me there, and refined over decades by every loss that confirmed what they taught me. But it feels like my math. It feels like something I discovered, not something I was given.

And that's why I'm still in bed. Not because I'm tired. Not because rest feels like failure. Because some part of me — the part that was built in kitchens and courtrooms and houses where I was never the right kid — genuinely believes that the bed is where I belong. That the dark is where I belong. That taking up as little space as possible is the closest I'll ever get to doing something right.

There's a tired you can sleep off, and then there's the tired that sleeps inside you. The kind that's been accumulating since you were a kid and learned that the people who were supposed to keep you safe were the ones you needed saving from. That tired is structural. Load-bearing. I'm not sure what would happen if I actually extracted it. I'm not sure I'd know how to stand.

I know the voice isn't mine. Most days I know that. The therapists — the real ones, the ones who finally broke through — they showed me where it came from. Showed me the installation date. Showed me the kitchen and the linoleum and the face that didn't change when it said "too dumb."

But knowing where a voice came from doesn't make it stop talking. And some days, in the dark, it's the only voice I hear.

The whiteboard waits on the wall. Its faded letters don't argue with the voice. Don't try to motivate me or inspire me or shame me into moving. They just stay there, patient and crooked and almost invisible, holding space for the possibility that tomorrow might be a day I can reach them.

I don't have an answer for this one. I just have the knowledge that the voice isn't mine, and some days that's enough to get me to the whiteboard, and some days it isn't.

Today it isn't.

The whiteboard doesn't care. It just waits.
