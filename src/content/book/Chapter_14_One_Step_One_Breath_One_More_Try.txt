Chapter 14 - One Step, One Breath, One More Try
The First Breath

Tuesday morning arrives like an unwanted house guest—inevitable, intrusive, and carrying the weight of expectations I can’t meet. The light filters through blinds I haven’t cleaned in months, casting bars of shadow across the ceiling I’ve been staring at for the past hour. Or maybe two. Time moves differently when you’re suspended between sleeping and waking, between existing and actually being alive.
My body feels like it’s made of wet sand—heavy, formless, resistant to any attempt at movement. The thought of getting up feels like considering a marathon. The idea of facing another day feels like agreeing to swim an ocean that doesn't have another shore. Everything feels impossible from here, from this cocoon of sheets that smell vaguely of defeat and the particular kind of sweat that comes from fighting battles nobody else can see.
The dogs are stirring. I can hear their tags jingling as they stretch, their quiet whines that say they need to go out. This—this is what moves me. Not motivation, not inspiration, not some sudden burst of energy or purpose. Just the simple fact that these creatures depend on me, and somehow that dependence becomes the only anchor strong enough to pull me from the gravitational well of my own inertia.
»You know this moment, don’t you? This impossible weight of the smallest movement. The way even breathing feels like lifting mountains when you’re this far down in the hole.«
I don’t leap out of bed. There’s no dramatic moment of decision. Just a slow negotiation with my own body—first one leg, sliding toward the edge of the mattress like it’s testing the temperature of reality. Then the other, following reluctantly. My feet find the floor—cold, solid, undeniably real. And for a moment, just a moment, I let them rest there. Not standing yet. Just… touching ground.
This is what victory looks like sometimes: two feet on the floor.
The whiteboard watches from its crooked angle on the wall. Four simple lines. I glance at the first one—Wake up—and even that feels generous for this state of semi-consciousness. The rest of the list blurs. One impossible thing at a time.
My hands find the edge of the mattress, and I push. It takes three tries—three separate attempts to generate enough momentum to achieve vertical. My knees shake slightly, like they’re surprised to be asked to work. My back protests with a symphony of cracks and pops that would concern me if I had the energy for concern.
But I’m standing.
It’s not graceful. It’s not inspiring. It’s not the kind of thing anyone would put on a motivational poster. But I’m fucking standing, and some days that’s the biggest victory you’re going to get.
The dogs circle my legs, tails wagging with an enthusiasm I can’t fathom possessing. They don’t understand that getting to the back door feels like crossing a desert. They don’t know that each step requires a conscious decision, a deliberate override of the voice in my head that keeps whispering what’s the point?
But I take the steps anyway. One, then another, then another. Not because I want to. Not because I feel better. Not because I’ve suddenly found meaning or purpose or any of the things the self-help books promise. But because the dogs need to pee, and somehow that need is enough to move me forward when nothing else can.
The back door handle is cold under my palm. I turn it, pull, and morning air rushes in—sharp, real, carrying the smell of grass and possibility and another day I didn’t think I’d see. The dogs bound outside, and I stand in the doorway, neither in nor out, suspended between the safety of my cave and the overwhelming reality of the world.
I take a breath. A real one. Deep enough that my lungs remember what they’re for.
It’s not much. In the grand scheme of things, it’s nothing—just a man in yesterday’s clothes, standing in a doorway, breathing. But it’s also everything. It’s movement when stillness felt eternal. It’s action when paralysis seemed permanent. It’s one small, shaky step away from the edge.
And maybe that’s enough for now.
The Weight of Small Movements
The whiteboard has been hanging on my wall for years now, its surface a testament to the long war between intention and capability. What started as recovery instructions after shoulder surgery—simple reminders for a body swimming in painkillers and confusion—became something else entirely. Something more honest: a daily negotiation with the basic requirements of being human.
Most people don’t need written instructions for existing. They don’t need to break down the act of being alive into component parts small enough to lift. But most people haven’t spent years learning that sometimes the distance between lying down and standing up can feel like light-years, measured not in space but in the impossible weight of motivation that refuses to come.
»You’re already doing the math, aren’t you? Calculating how much energy you have versus how much each task will cost. Like your life force is a checking account that’s always overdrawn, and you’re trying to figure out which bills are most urgent.«
Today, getting out of bed took everything I had. Not metaphorically—literally. Every ounce of will, every fragment of determination, every scrap of “fuck it, fine” I could muster. And now I’m standing in my kitchen, staring at a glass that needs filling, and I honestly don’t know if I have enough left in the tank to lift it to the tap.
This is what people don’t understand about this kind of exhaustion. It’s not being tired after a long day. It’s not needing a nap after poor sleep. It’s a bone-deep depletion that makes every action feel like pushing a boulder uphill. Where other people see a simple task—fill glass, drink water—I see a complex equation of movements that each require their own negotiation with a body that’s forgotten how to move without conscious command.
Reach for glass (can I lift my arm that high?) Grasp glass (do I trust my grip not to shatter it?) Move to sink (that’s four whole steps) Turn tap (which direction is on again?) Fill glass (how long can I hold it steady?) Turn off tap (don’t forget this part) Lift glass to mouth (the longest journey) Drink (try not to spill) Put glass down (gently, gently)
Nine separate actions for what should be one fluid motion. Nine individual battles with inertia. Nine tiny victories that no one will ever celebrate, that I can barely acknowledge myself without feeling pathetic for needing to parse existence into pieces this small.
But I do it. Somehow, I do it. The glass gets filled. The water gets drunk. And I stand there afterward, genuinely exhausted from the effort, wondering if this counts as taking care of myself or if it’s just another sign of how far I’ve fallen from functional.
The shower sits twelve feet and a thousand decisions away. I know I need one—can smell the particular cocktail of depression sweat and yesterday’s attempt at being human. But the thought of all those steps—undressing, adjusting water temperature, standing upright for more than thirty seconds, the complex choreography of actually getting clean, then the Herculean task of drying off and finding clean clothes—it feels impossible.
So I negotiate. Maybe just wash my face today. Maybe just change shirts. Maybe just…exist in this liminal space between clean and not, between effort and surrender, between trying and giving up.
»Your shoulders just dropped a little, didn’t they? That tiny release when someone else admits they can’t do the basic shit either. When someone else says out loud what you’ve been too ashamed to admit: that sometimes, existing is the hardest thing you’ll do all day.«
The thing about these small movements is that they carry the weight of everything else you’re not doing. Each tiny victory is shadowed by the ghost of what you should be accomplishing. Got out of bed? Great, but you didn’t exercise. Drank water? Sure, but you haven’t eaten actual food in two days. Managed to shower? Wonderful, but your inbox has 847 unread emails and your phone has been on silent for a week because you can’t handle the idea of human interaction.
The world measures progress in promotions and purchases, in milestones and achievements that look good on social media. But some of us are out here measuring progress in whether we managed to brush our teeth today. Whether we opened the curtains. Whether we responded to one text message, even if it was just “I’m okay” when we’re anything but.
These small movements aren’t small at all. They’re acts of rebellion against the gravity of depression. They’re tiny insurrections against the voice that says “why bother?” They’re proof that somewhere, beneath all the weight and exhaustion and bone-deep tiredness, something in us still wants to live, even if it can only manage to want it in increments measured in seconds.
Sometimes I stand in front of that board, marker in hand, and check off boxes for things I haven’t actually done yet. Like a promise to myself that I’ll get there eventually. It doesn’t judge. Doesn’t care if it takes three hours or if I only manage one task out of four.
Because that’s the secret about small movements: they’re not really about the movement at all. They’re about the choice to move. The decision, however small, however shaky, to push back against the weight. To say “not today” to the voice that whispers about giving up. To prove, if only to yourself, that you’re still here, still trying, still capable of choosing something other than surrender.
Even if that something is just filling a glass with water.
Even if it takes everything you have.
Even if no one else will ever understand what a victory that represents.
The Space Between Steps
There’s a particular kind of purgatory that exists in the pause between efforts—that suspended moment after you’ve managed one impossible thing but before you can gather the strength for the next. I’m living in one right now, standing in my hallway, caught between the bathroom I just exited and the kitchen I need to reach. Six feet of hardwood floor that might as well be an ocean.
My body has gone into some kind of standby mode. I accomplished the shower—a thirty-minute ordeal that felt like running a marathon—and now every cell in my body is demanding payment for that effort. My legs feel like they’re made of static. My arms hang like wet rope. And my mind… my mind is conducting a filibuster against any further movement.
You did the thing. You showered. That’s enough for today.
The kitchen will still be there in an hour. Or tomorrow.
You could just go back to bed now. You’ve earned it.
»You know this space, don’t you? This terrible pause where momentum dies and you’re left standing in the wreckage of your own good intentions. Where one step forward feels like it used up your entire quota of steps for the week.«
This is the space where most of us get stuck. Not in the complete paralysis of can’t-move-at-all, and not in the momentum of actual progress, but in this liminal territory between. Where you’ve proven you can move, but can’t quite remember why you should keep moving. Where the next step feels both possible and impossible simultaneously.
The hallway stretches like taffy in my peripheral vision. I know it’s only six feet. I’ve walked it thousands of times. But right now, in this moment, with my hair still dripping and my body shaking from the effort of standing upright for fifteen minutes, those six feet contain every doubt I’ve ever had about my ability to continue existing.
Time moves differently in these spaces. Minutes feel like hours, but hours disappear without notice. I might have been standing here for thirty seconds or thirty minutes. There’s no way to tell. My phone is somewhere—bedroom maybe, or buried in the couch cushions—but the thought of finding it requires a decision I’m not capable of making right now.
The dogs pad past me, unconcerned with my frozen state. They’ve seen this before. Know that sometimes I just… stop. Like a wind-up toy that’s run out of momentum, standing perfectly still until something winds me up again. They’ve learned to work around these pauses, these system crashes where my body and mind refuse to sync up.
What people don’t understand about these spaces between steps is that they’re not empty. They’re full—overfull—with the noise of every reason not to move, every argument for giving up, every piece of evidence that trying is pointless. It’s like standing in the eye of a hurricane made entirely of your own doubts and fears and exhaustion.
Remember when you used to just… walk places? Without having to have a whole congressional hearing about it?
Other people are out there running marathons and you’re here trying to convince yourself that walking to your kitchen is worth the effort.
What’s even in the kitchen? Dishes you haven’t done? Food you won’t eat? Another room full of tasks you can’t complete?
But here’s the thing about these pauses, these spaces between steps: they’re not failures. They’re part of the process. They’re what recovery actually looks like when you strip away the soundtrack and the slow-motion montage. Recovery isn’t a smooth upward trajectory. It’s not a montage of gradually increasing victories. It’s this—standing in a hallway, dripping, shaking, arguing with yourself about whether six feet is a distance you can cover.
»Take a breath. No, really. Right now. Deep one. Because you’re holding it, aren’t you? Holding everything—breath, tension, the weight of every step you haven’t taken yet. Let it go. Just for a second.«
Something shifts. Not dramatically. Not inspirationally. Just a tiny loosening in my chest, a slight forward lean that might become momentum if I don’t think about it too hard. My foot lifts—barely, maybe an inch off the ground—and moves forward. Not a full step. Just a shuffle. But it’s forward.
And now I’m one shuffle closer to the kitchen. Five feet, eleven inches to go.
This is what progress actually looks like: tiny, reluctant shuffles through spaces that feel impossible. Not because you’ve found strength or purpose or motivation, but because standing still forever isn’t actually an option, even when it feels like the only option. Because somewhere in the machinery of survival, something keeps clicking forward, even when the gears are grinding and the engine is smoking and every warning light is flashing red.
Another shuffle. Another tiny rebellion against inertia. The kitchen doorway is visible now, and with it, the promise of… what? Coffee I probably won’t drink? A chair I can collapse into? Doesn’t matter. It’s a destination, and sometimes that’s all you need—not a purpose, not a goal, just somewhere that isn’t here.
The space between steps isn’t empty. It’s where the real battle happens. Not the dramatic moments of breakthrough, but these quiet negotiations with yourself about whether continuing is worth the effort. Whether moving forward is possible. Whether that tiny shuffle counts as progress.
It does. It all counts. Every shuffle, every pause, every moment you spend frozen in your hallway arguing with yourself about the physics of forward motion. It’s all part of the messy, non-linear, deeply unglamorous process of continuing to exist when your body keeps lobbying for surrender.
Five feet, eight inches to go.
I’ll get there. Eventually. One shuffle at a time.
The Power of Trying Again
The kitchen chair holds me like a confession booth where I’m both priest and sinner, absolving myself for the sin of exhaustion while immediately condemning myself for not pushing harder. I made it here—those six feet of hallway conquered one shuffle at a time—and now I’m facing the reality that making it here doesn’t mean I’ve made it anywhere meaningful.
The coffee maker stares at me from the counter, three feet and seventeen steps away from where I sit. Yesterday’s grounds are still in the filter, growing a civilization of their own. The thought of standing up, walking over there, dumping the old grounds, finding new ones, adding water, pressing the button, waiting for it to brew—it’s a chain of impossibilities that makes me want to laugh or cry or both.
But here’s the thing about trying again: it doesn’t come from hope. It doesn’t spring from some well of determination you’ve been secretly harboring. It comes from somewhere stupider and more stubborn—from the part of you that’s too dumb to know when to quit, too contrary to give the universe the satisfaction of winning.
»You feel that, don’t you? That tiny spark of ‘fuck it’ that lives somewhere behind your ribs? Not hope. Not strength. Just pure, stubborn refusal to let this be where your story ends—sitting in a kitchen chair, defeated by a coffee maker.«
I’ve been here before. Different chairs, different rooms, different impossible tasks, but the same fundamental equation: the effort required feels greater than the energy available. The cost seems higher than any possible reward. The mere thought of trying—again—feels like agreeing to push a boulder up a mountain that’s actively trying to throw you off.
After Riley died, I spent three weeks trying to respond to a single text message. Three weeks. Twenty-one days of opening my phone, staring at the words “Hey, how are you doing?” and closing it again without typing a single letter. Each day I’d tell myself today would be different. Today I’d manage those few words that would let someone know I was still alive, still present, still capable of basic human interaction.
It took three weeks, but I did it. Finally. Sent back “I’m surviving” and immediately threw my phone across the room like it was on fire. But I did it. And the next text was easier—took only five days. And the one after that, three. Not because I was healing or getting better or any of that bullshit, but because I was learning the rhythm of trying again. The particular cadence of failure and retry that becomes its own kind of progress.
That’s what people don’t understand about trying again—it’s not a triumphant return to the field. It’s not a phoenix rising from the ashes. It’s more like a drunk person trying to find their keys in the dark, fumbling with the same lock over and over until something finally clicks. It’s messy and frustrating and deeply undignified, but sometimes the lock opens, and that has to be enough.
I stand up. Not gracefully. Not with purpose. More like a marionette whose puppeteer is also drunk and possibly having an existential crisis. But I’m vertical again, and that’s something. The coffee maker is still three feet away, might as well be three miles, but I’ve covered impossible distances before. Six feet of hallway just this morning. I can do three more feet. Maybe.
The first step is the worst—that initial commitment to movement when every fiber of your being is screaming for stillness. But the second step is easier. Not easy, but easier. Like my body remembers, reluctantly, that it knows how to do this. That it’s done this before. That it can do it again, even if it really, really doesn’t want to.
»Count them if you need to. The steps. Sometimes making things smaller makes them possible. One. Two. Three. You’re already closer than you were.«
The old coffee grounds hit the trash with a wet thud that feels unnecessarily metaphorical. Yesterday’s attempt at normal, discarded. Today’s attempt, pending. Tomorrow’s attempt, uncertain. But right now, in this moment, I’m trying again. Not because I believe it will be different this time. Not because I’ve found some secret well of strength. But because the alternative—not trying—feels like a different kind of death. The slow kind. The kind where you’re still breathing but you’ve stopped living.
Fresh grounds. Water. Button pressed. The coffee maker gurgles to life, and I lean against the counter because standing without support is asking too much right now. But I’m here. I tried. Again. Despite every failure that came before, despite every voice saying “why bother,” despite the bone-deep exhaustion that makes even breathing feel like labor.
This is what trying again looks like: not a triumphant return but a reluctant shuffle forward. Not a breakthrough but a slow grinding through. Not victory but the simple, stubborn refusal to let defeat be permanent.
The coffee drips, each drop a tiny percussion of persistence. I watch it fall—dark liquid collecting in the pot, proof that trying again sometimes yields results, even small ones, even bitter ones that need sugar and milk to be palatable.
Justin would have laughed at this—me treating making coffee like hauling concrete uphill. Would have said something about how I always made everything harder than it needed to be. He would have been right. But he also would have understood that sometimes making things harder is the only way to make them possible at all. That sometimes you need to turn existence into a series of tiny battles just to remember you’re still capable of fighting.
The coffee finishes brewing. I pour a cup, hands shaking slightly from the effort of trying, again, to be a functional human being. It’s too hot, too bitter, too much like every other cup of coffee I’ve forced myself to make on days when making coffee felt impossible.
But I made it. Again. Despite everything.
And tomorrow, if tomorrow comes, I’ll probably have to make it again. Have to try again. Have to shuffle through the same impossibilities and negotiate with the same exhaustion and fight the same voice that says “why bother?”
Because that’s what trying again really means: not solving the problem once and for all, but agreeing to face it again tomorrow. And the next day. And the next. Not because it gets easier—it doesn’t—but because the trying itself becomes a kind of proof. Proof that you’re still here. Still fighting. Still too stubborn to let the coffee maker win.
The Quiet Victory of a Single Step
Nobody celebrates the small victories. There’s no applause for brushing your teeth when brushing your teeth required three separate attempts and a negotiation with your reflection that borders on hostage crisis management. No parade for managing to eat something that isn’t cereal directly from the box while standing over the sink. No medal ceremony for responding to a text within the same week it was sent.
The world reserves its recognition for the big moments—the promotions, the marathons, the transformations that photograph well and fit neatly into before-and-after narratives. But those of us living in the spaces between before and after, we know the truth: the small victories are the only ones that actually matter.
»You’ve got your own collection, don’t you? Those tiny triumphs no one else would understand. The day you opened the curtains. The time you answered the phone instead of letting it ring into silence. That moment you chose to stand up instead of sinking deeper into the couch. They matter. They all matter.«
I’m standing on my front porch, coffee cup in hand, morning air sharp against my skin. To anyone walking by, I’m just a disheveled man in yesterday’s clothes, clutching coffee like a lifeline, staring at nothing in particular. They don’t see the mountain I climbed to get here. They don’t know about the hallway shuffle, the coffee maker battle, the internal war I waged just to open the front door.
Go outside.
The last task on the list, checked off for the first time in… I’ve lost count of the days. Time moves differently when you’re measuring it in small victories and smaller defeats. When your calendar isn’t marked by appointments but by whether you managed to change your clothes or eat something that required more preparation than removing a lid.
The outside world continues its relentless motion—cars passing, dogs barking, the distant sound of someone’s lawn mower starting up even though it’s barely 9 AM and who has that kind of energy anyway? It all feels simultaneously too close and impossibly distant, like watching life through glass you’re not sure you’ll ever be able to break through.
But I’m here. Outside. Breathing air that doesn’t taste like the recycled staleness of my bedroom. Feeling sunlight that isn’t filtered through dusty blinds. Existing in the world instead of just observing it from the safety of my self-imposed isolation.
It’s a small victory. So small it barely registers on any scale that matters to anyone but me. But that’s the thing about these quiet victories—they’re not for anyone else. They’re not performative. They’re not meant to be seen. They’re just tiny proofs of life, little evidence markers that say “I’m still here, still trying, still capable of choosing something other than surrender.”
Three weeks ago, I couldn’t even look at the front door without feeling like I was drowning. The thought of stepping outside felt like agreeing to be visible when visibility was the last thing I could handle. When being seen meant having to pretend to be okay, having to perform normalcy I didn’t have the energy to fake.
Two weeks ago, I made it to the door, hand on the knob, before the panic set in. That peculiar panic that has nothing to do with actual danger and everything to do with the pressure of being visible, of existing where other people might witness you. I stood there for ten minutes, hand on the doorknob, before retreating back to the safety of my bedroom.
Last week, I opened the door. Just opened it. Didn’t step through, didn’t even look outside for more than a second. But I turned the knob, pulled the door open, let the outside air mix with the inside air for exactly three seconds before closing it again like I was shutting out a storm.
And today. Today I’m standing on the porch.
»Three weeks to get to the porch. Tell me that doesn’t mean something. Tell me that kind of stubbornness doesn’t deserve at least a nod. Go ahead—try to dismiss it. You can’t. Because you know what those three weeks cost.«
The neighbor walks by with her dog, waves. I manage something that might charitably be called a wave back—more of a coffee cup salute, really. She doesn’t know this is the first human interaction I’ve had in days that wasn’t mediated by a screen. Doesn’t know that acknowledging her existence took more energy than I thought I had. Doesn’t know that this tiny exchange—two humans recognizing each other’s presence in the world—feels like both too much and exactly what I needed.
This is what we don’t talk about when we talk about healing: how small it is. How quiet. How utterly unremarkable it looks from the outside. We want the dramatic moments, the breakthrough therapy sessions, the sunrise revelations that change everything. But most of healing happens in these tiny, almost invisible moments. In the space between staying in bed and getting up. In the pause before you answer a text. In the breath you take before stepping outside.
My coffee’s gone cold, but I stay on the porch a few more minutes. Not because I’m enjoying it—enjoyment feels like too strong a word for this tentative engagement with the world. But because I can. Because I made it this far. Because going back inside now feels like admitting defeat, and I’ve had enough defeat for one lifetime.
A bird lands on the railing, cocks its head at me like it’s trying to figure out what kind of creature stands perfectly still on a porch at 9 AM on a Tuesday. I don’t have an answer for it. I’m trying to figure that out myself. But we regard each other for a moment—two creatures existing in the same space, neither of us entirely sure why—before it flies away.
The small victories don’t come with rewards. There’s no prize for making it outside, no achievement unlocked for basic human functioning. But there’s something else, something harder to name. A tiny shift in the gravity pulling me down. A microscopic increase in the possibility that tomorrow might be different. Not better, necessarily. But different. And sometimes different is all the hope you need.
I go back inside, but I leave the door open. Just for a few minutes. Just to let the outside air clear out some of the stagnation. It’s a small thing. A tiny rebellion against the isolation I’ve been cultivating. But it matters. To me, it matters.
The whiteboard watches from its crooked angle, all four tasks checked off for the first time in recent memory:
✓ Wake up ✓ Get out of bed
✓ Take a shower ✓ Go outside
Four small victories that add up to one day survived. One day closer to whatever comes next. One day of proving that even when progress is measured in shuffles and stutters and coffee-cup salutes to neighbors, it’s still progress.
Tomorrow I’ll probably have to do it all again. The negotiating, the shuffling, the trying and failing and trying again. But today—today I did it. All of it. And that has to count for something.
Even if I’m the only one counting.
It All Still Counts
Here’s what they don’t tell you about survival: it’s not a destination you reach, it’s a practice you repeat. Every day, every hour, sometimes every minute. It’s not a problem you solve once and move on from—it’s a negotiation that never really ends, just changes shape.
I’m back in my bed now, the day’s small victories already feeling distant and unreal. Did I really make coffee? Did I actually stand on the porch? The evidence suggests yes—the empty cup on my nightstand, the lingering smell of outside air in the hallway—but it all feels like something I might have imagined. Like maybe I dreamed the whole thing and never actually left this bed at all.
»But you did. You know you did. Even if your brain is trying to convince you otherwise, even if the voice is already dismissing what you accomplished today. You did the things. They happened. They count.«
The exhaustion that follows these small efforts is profound. Not the good exhaustion that comes after a workout or a productive day, but something deeper, more fundamental. Like I’ve been mining for signs of life in myself and only found a few tiny sparkles of something that might be worth saving. The effort of extraction has left me hollowed out, empty in a way that feels both terrible and oddly peaceful.
My phone buzzes. A text from someone I haven’t talked to in months: “Hey, thinking of you. Hope you’re doing okay.”
Six months ago, this would have sent me into a spiral. The pressure to respond, to perform okay-ness, to craft some reply that acknowledges their concern without revealing the depth of my not-okay-ness. Three months ago, I would have ignored it, let it sit unread until the notification badge became just another small shame to carry.
Today, I type back: “Surviving. Thanks for checking in.”
Send.
It’s not much. Five words that took five minutes to write, delete, rewrite, delete again, then finally send before I could talk myself out of it. But it’s honest. It’s connection, however thin. It’s proof that I’m still here, still capable of reaching back when someone reaches out.
The person typing those words about survival being messy, about progress being small, about the quiet victories that no one celebrates—that person is still trying. Still fighting their own quiet battles. Still measuring progress in shuffles and breaths and small rebellions against the drag of being alive.
We want our healing to be cinematic. We want the soundtrack to swell as we overcome our obstacles. We want the moment of triumph that everyone can point to and say “that’s when everything changed.” But that’s not how it works. Not for most of us.
Instead, we get this: small movements that don’t look like much from the outside. Tiny victories that we can barely celebrate ourselves, let alone explain to others. Progress so incremental it’s almost invisible, except to those of us who know what it costs to move forward even an inch when you’re carrying this much weight.
But it counts. All of it counts.
The shuffle down the hallway counts. The coffee made with shaking hands counts. The door opened to let in light counts. The text sent after months of silence counts. The breath taken when breathing feels like drowning counts. The decision to try again tomorrow counts.
»You still here? Still reading? That counts too. The fact that you’ve stayed with these words, with these small stories of smaller victories—that’s its own kind of persistence. Its own form of hope.«
Tomorrow, I’ll wake up and the list will be waiting. I might manage all four again, or I might only manage one, or I might spend the entire day staring at the ceiling, negotiating with gravity about whether getting up is worth the effort. There’s no way to know. No guarantee that today’s victories will make tomorrow’s battles any easier.
But I’ll try. Not because I’m strong or brave or healed or any of the things I’m supposed to be by now. But because trying is what I do. It’s what we do—all of us who measure progress in breaths and steps and small acts of defiance against the voice that says we should have given up by now.
We’re still here. Still taking one step, one breath, one more try at this impossible thing called living. And maybe that’s not the inspiring story anyone wants to hear. Maybe it’s not the kind of victory worth celebrating. Maybe it’s just another day of barely managing the basics while the world spins on without us.
But we’re still here.
And that has to count for something.
That has to be enough.
Because right now, in this moment, with everything we’re carrying and everything we’ve survived and everything we still have to face—it’s all we’ve got. These small victories. These tiny rebellions. These quiet proofs that somewhere inside us, despite everything, something still wants to live.
Even if it can only want it one breath at a time.
Even if it can only manage one shuffle forward.
Even if the only victory we can claim is that we’re still here to try again tomorrow.
The sun is setting now, casting long shadows across my room. Another day survived. Another collection of small victories that no one else will ever fully understand. Another chance to practice this strange art of continuing when every instinct says stop.
Tomorrow will bring its own battles, its own impossibilities, its own opportunities for small victories or small defeats. But that’s tomorrow’s problem. Today, right now, I’m here. I did the things. I tried. I survived.
And for today, that’s everything.
For today, I’ll take the win—small as it is, quiet as it is, invisible as it might be to everyone else.
Because I know what it cost. I know what it means. I know that sometimes the biggest victory is simply refusing to stop trying, even when trying feels like the hardest thing in the world.
One step. One breath. One more try.
