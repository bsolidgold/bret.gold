Chapter 16 - The Space Between Almost
There’s this moment when you’re trying to get out of bed—your body has shifted, maybe one leg has even found the floor, but you’re not standing yet. You’re suspended in that space between lying down and getting up, between surrender and action, between giving up and going on. Your muscles know what they’re supposed to do next, your brain has sent all the right signals, but something in between has gone missing. The translation from intention to action gets lost somewhere in your nervous system, leaving you frozen in this awkward half-state that perfectly captures what it feels like to be alive but not quite living.
That’s where I live most days. In the space between almost and done. Between trying and succeeding. Between the person I was and whoever the fuck I’m supposed to become.
»You know this space, don’t you? This purgatory of partial progress. Where you’ve done enough to feel guilty about not finishing but not enough to feel accomplished. Where every step forward feels like it’s happening underwater, and you’re not even sure you remember what breathing normally feels like anymore.«
The whiteboard on my wall—still crooked, still patient—has three checkmarks today. Three out of four. I got up, got out of bed, even managed a shower. The water was too hot and then too cold and I forgot to use soap on my left arm, but I stood under that stream of water for twelve minutes and that counts as clean enough. But going outside? That fourth task sits there unchecked, and somehow that one empty box carries more weight than the three victories combined.
Almost. I almost completed the list. Almost did what normal people do without thinking. Almost managed to be a functional human being for one complete day.
The mathematics of it should be simple: three out of four is seventy-five percent. That’s a passing grade. That’s a solid C. That’s more than half. But my brain doesn’t process it that way. My brain sees that empty box and calculates it as complete failure, as evidence that I can’t even manage the absolute bare minimum of human existence. Three successes become meaningless in the shadow of one incompletion.
Almost is its own kind of hell, and nobody prepared me for how much real estate it would take up in my life.
The Ache of Incomplete Things
My life has become a museum of almost-finished things, each room carefully curated with the artifacts of good intentions that ran out of steam. Half-written emails saved in drafts, their cursors blinking at the end of sentences that trail off into nothing because I ran out of energy mid-thought. Sometimes I open them weeks later and can’t even remember what I was trying to say, can’t reconnect with the person who started typing those words with such purpose before the weight of continuing became too much.
Books with bookmarks permanently stuck at page 47, page 132, page 208—each one marking the exact spot where my concentration finally collapsed under its own weight. I’ve read the first third of maybe fifty books this year. I know dozens of beginnings, understand countless setups, but I couldn’t tell you how any of them end. The characters remain frozen in their conflicts, their resolutions forever out of reach, just like mine.
Text messages typed but never sent, sitting in that limbo where I was almost brave enough to reach out, almost honest enough to say I’m not okay, almost human enough to maintain a connection. My phone is full of these ghost conversations, these one-sided dialogues where I practice being a person who can still connect with other people. “Hey, I know it’s been a while…” they usually start, before dissolving into the digital equivalent of mumbling.
»You have your own collection, don’t you? Your own museum of things you were going to finish, going to complete, going to become. All those almosts that pile up like snow until you can’t see over them anymore.«
The guitar in the corner hasn’t been touched in months. I was almost getting good at that one song—you know, the one everyone learns when they’re trying to prove they’re serious about guitar. My fingers had almost memorized the chord progressions, almost found their way smoothly between G and C and D. Now the strings are probably out of tune, and my calluses have softened back to tender skin that would hurt to press against the frets. Starting over would mean acknowledging how much ground I’ve lost, and I don’t have the energy for that kind of honesty.
The running shoes by the door have dust on them. Not a thick layer, just enough to show they haven’t moved in long enough for the world to settle on them. I almost made it a habit—had a streak going for three weeks, which is about two weeks and six days longer than most of my attempts at self-improvement. I remember the last run, how my legs felt like they were finally remembering what they were made for. Then I missed one day, which became two, which became a month, which became another monument to my inability to maintain momentum.
The journal on my nightstand has entries that stop mid-sentence, thoughts literally abandoned halfway through because finishing them felt like trying to lift a car with my teeth. “Today was…” one entry reads, and nothing more. Today was what? I don’t remember. Today was probably like all the other todays—heavy and blurred around the edges, too much and not enough all at once.
It’s not the grand failures that destroy you. It’s these little incompletions. These tiny surrenders that accumulate like compound interest on a debt you never meant to take on. Each unfinished task becomes evidence in the case your brain is constantly building against you—proof that you can’t even do the simple things, the basic things, the things everyone else manages without this crushing weight of effort.
My apartment is organized in layers of almost: almost clean laundry that made it out of the dryer but not into drawers, almost washed dishes that soaked for so long the water turned gray and cold, almost sorted mail that evolved from a pile to a mountain to a landscape feature I navigate around rather than through. Each layer represents a moment when I almost had the energy to complete something normal, something basic, something that shouldn’t require negotiation with my own executive function.
The worst part about almost is that it’s visible. You can see exactly how close you got. Can measure the distance between where you stopped and where you were trying to go. It’s right there, taunting you with its proximity. Like dying of thirst while looking at a glass of water you can’t quite reach. Like standing at the edge of a pool, fully dressed, knowing you need to jump but unable to make your muscles execute the command.
Some days I sit here cataloging all my almosts, like an accountant of failure, tallying up the incomplete:
Almost got that degree. Just six credits short. They might as well be six thousand for how impossible they feel now. The thought of going back, of sitting in those fluorescent-lit classrooms, of pretending I belong among people who haven’t had their futures derailed by their own brain chemistry—it’s laughable. Those six credits stand between me and a piece of paper that says I finished something, but they might as well be an ocean.
Almost maintained that friendship. Just needed to return one more call, send one more text, show up one more time. But each unreturned message added weight to the next one, until the thought of responding carried the mass of all the previous silences. “Sorry it’s been so long” became “Sorry it’s been months” became “Sorry I disappeared” became nothing at all because how do you apologize for becoming a ghost?
Almost healed from that loss. Just needed a little more time, a little more therapy, a little more something I can’t even name. But healing isn’t linear, and almost-healed is like being almost-pregnant—it doesn’t count. You’re either whole or you’re not, and I’m not. I’m held together with psychological duct tape and spite, and some days the tape doesn’t stick.
Almost wrote that book. Just needed to push through the middle part where everything gets messy and complicated and too real to put into words. The beginning was easy—beginnings always are. It’s the middles that kill you, the parts where you have to sustain effort without the adrenaline of starting or the satisfaction of finishing. The middle is where almost lives, that swampy territory where good intentions go to die.
Each almost carries its own specific weight, its own particular shame. They stack up in my chest like stones, making it harder to breathe, harder to move, harder to try again. Because what’s the point of starting something new when I have this graveyard of almosts proving I don’t know how to finish?
The Tension of Progress
Progress has become this word that tastes like copper in my mouth—like blood from biting my tongue too many times when people ask how I’m doing. “Making progress,” I say, which is technically true if you measure progress in microscopic increments, in movements so small they’re invisible to the naked eye, in the kind of forward motion that looks exactly like standing still unless you’re the one feeling every millimeter of effort it takes.
The world has this obsession with visible progress. With before-and-after photos. With transformation stories that fit neatly into a three-act structure. Beginning, middle, end. Struggle, breakthrough, triumph. But nobody knows what to do with those of us stuck in permanent middle, in endless struggle, in the space where breakthrough feels like a myth other people tell themselves to make the world make sense.
»You’re scrolling through your phone right now, aren’t you? Or you were, before you started reading this. Looking at everyone else’s highlight reels, their victories, their completions. While you sit there in your almost, feeling like you’re the only one who can’t quite close the gap between trying and done.«
I watch other people move through the world like they have access to some secret manual I never received. They set goals and achieve them. Start projects and finish them. Make plans and follow through. Meanwhile, I’m over here celebrating because I managed to respond to a text within the same week I received it, because I ate something that required more preparation than removing a lid, because I put on pants that weren’t pajamas even though I never left the house.
The pressure builds in layers, geological strata of expectation that compress into something diamond-hard and just as cutting:
The societal pressure that says you should always be improving, always be growing, always be becoming a better version of yourself. As if the current version isn’t already hanging on by a thread. As if I have extra resources to dedicate to optimization when I’m barely managing basic maintenance. Somewhere along the way, we all agreed that standing still is the same as moving backward. That if you’re not growing you’re dying. There are entire podcasts devoted to this idea, entire bookshelves, entire careers built on making you feel like who you are right now isn’t sufficient. But what if you’re just trying not to decompose?
The internal pressure that comes from knowing exactly what you’re capable of when you’re not drowning in your own brain chemistry. I remember what it felt like to finish things, to have follow-through, to be a person who could make a plan on Monday and still want to execute it by Friday. That memory feels like it belongs to someone else now, some alternate universe version of me who didn’t get derailed by the weight of their own consciousness.
The external pressure from people who mean well but don’t understand that “just push through” isn’t advice—it’s a reminder that they’ve never had to push through what you’re pushing through. They’ve never had to negotiate with their own mind for permission to do basic tasks. They’ve never had to ration their energy like a survivalist rationing water in the desert, knowing that if they use too much on one thing, there won’t be enough left for the next essential function.
The temporal pressure of time passing while you’re stuck in almost, watching opportunities expire and possibilities close while you’re still trying to get your shoes on. Jobs that require applications by dates that pass while you’re staring at the half-completed form. Relationships that wither from neglect while you’re trying to find the energy to send a simple “thinking of you” text. Dreams that have expiration dates you can hear ticking like bombs in the distance.
Some days I feel like I’m running a race where everyone else is on solid ground and I’m waist-deep in molasses. Every step takes ten times the effort and gets me one-tenth of the distance. And the worst part? From the outside, it looks like I’m not even trying. Because effort isn’t always visible. Struggle isn’t always dramatic. Sometimes the hardest battles are the quiet ones, the ones that happen in the space between your bed and your bedroom door, the ones that look like nothing to everyone else but feel like everything to you.
I’ve become an expert at the mathematics of minimal progress. I can calculate exactly how much energy each task will require, factor in the recovery time needed after, and determine if I can afford the expenditure. Shower: 40 energy units. Getting dressed in real clothes: 25 units. Responding to a text that requires emotional availability: 75 units. Going outside: all remaining units plus whatever I can borrow from tomorrow’s supply.
The weight of almost becomes physical. It sits on your chest like a cat that’s decided you’re its permanent resting place—if the cat weighed three hundred pounds and was made of lead. It wraps around your ankles like chains made of your own good intentions. It fills your lungs like water, making every breath feel like work, which is ironic because breathing is supposed to be the one thing that happens automatically, the one thing you shouldn’t have to think about, but here we are, consciously inflating and deflating our lungs like it’s a part-time job we never applied for.
The world keeps spinning at its regular speed while you’re stuck in slow motion. You watch everyone else living their lives at normal velocity—having conversations that flow, making decisions without three-hour internal debates, completing tasks without needing to collapse afterward like they’ve just donated a kidney. They make it look so easy, this business of being human, while you’re over here needing a strategy meeting with yourself just to decide what to have for breakfast.
And still, somehow, you keep breathing. Keep trying. Keep adding to your collection of almosts because the alternative—giving up entirely—feels like a different kind of death. So you live in this space between, this purgatory of partial progress, this hell of incomplete things.
Lessons in the In-Between
If almost teaches you anything, it’s patience. Not the noble kind they write about in philosophy books, but the grinding, teeth-clenched, white-knuckled kind that comes from having no other choice. The kind that feels less like virtue and more like endurance. Less like wisdom and more like stubbornness. Less like acceptance and more like the grudging acknowledgment that this is what you have to work with, so you might as well get comfortable with discomfort.
You learn that progress doesn’t always look like movement. Sometimes it looks like staying in place when everything in you wants to run backward. Sometimes it looks like holding your ground against a tide that wants to pull you under. Sometimes it looks like simply not getting worse, and calling that a victory because at least you didn’t lose ground today. At least the incomplete things didn’t multiply. At least you’re still here, still occupying space in a world that feels too fast and too bright and too much.
»What are you still holding onto? Not the thing you tell people about—the other one. The one you grip so hard it’s left marks, even though you stopped believing it would save you a long time ago. Even though letting go might actually feel like relief.«
In the space of almost, hope becomes something different than what the motivational posters promised. It’s not bright and shining and full of certainty. It’s more like a pilot light—small, flickering, barely visible, but somehow still burning. Still there. Still keeping the possibility of warmth alive even when everything else has gone cold. Some days you have to cup your hands around it to keep it from being extinguished by the wind of your own doubt. Some days you can barely see it through the fog of exhaustion. But it’s there, stubborn and small and refusing to go out completely.
I’ve learned to recognize the micro-victories that nobody else would even notice, the incremental progress that doesn’t photograph well but still counts:
The day you thought about calling but didn’t, and then the next day when you actually picked up the phone even if you didn’t dial. That’s two steps. Two tiny, almost invisible steps, but steps nonetheless.
The moment you stood up even though you sat back down immediately after. Your body remembered what standing felt like. Your muscles remembered their job. That’s data. That’s information your system can use.
The time you opened the document even though you didn’t write anything. You looked at it. You didn’t run away. You sat with the discomfort of the blank page for thirty seconds before closing it. That’s thirty seconds longer than yesterday.
The morning you put on real clothes even though you didn’t leave the house. You made a choice that wasn’t the easiest choice. You picked the shirt that has buttons even though buttons require fine motor skills that feel impossible some days. That’s rebellion against the gravity of almost.
These aren’t the victories that make inspiring stories. They’re not the kind of progress you can graph or photograph or share on social media. Nobody’s going to congratulate you for almost calling someone. There’s no achievement unlocked for opening a document you didn’t write in. But they’re real. They count. They’re the tiny rebellions against the gravity of almost, the small insurrections against the weight of incomplete.
In this space, you develop a different relationship with time. It’s not linear anymore. It’s more like a spiral—you keep coming back to the same points but at slightly different elevations. The third time you almost finish something feels different from the first time. Not better necessarily, but different. You recognize the terrain now. You know where the difficult parts are. You can prepare for the place where you usually stop, even if you still stop there.
Hope in almost doesn’t announce itself. It whispers. It shows up in the space between giving up and trying again. In that pause where you’re deciding whether three out of four is enough for today or whether you’ve got one more in you. In the moment when you realize that almost finished is still further than not started. That almost there is still somewhere. That almost human is still human.
The thing about living in almost is that it teaches you things you never wanted to learn. Like how to find meaning in the smallest movements. Like how to celebrate victories nobody else can see. Like how to keep breathing when breath feels like a negotiation. Like how to hold hope so gently it doesn’t break under the pressure of your need for it to mean something.
You learn that sometimes courage looks like adding one more word to an unfinished email. Sometimes strength looks like keeping the bookmark in the book even though you haven’t opened it in months. Sometimes resilience looks like not throwing away the running shoes even though they’re gathering dust. These small acts of not giving up completely—they matter. They’re proof that some part of you still believes in the possibility of completion, even if that belief is thin as tissue paper.
Existing in the Space Between
What if almost isn’t a failure? What if it’s just a different kind of existence—a liminal space where everything is still possible because nothing is quite finished? What if the space between almost and done isn’t a void but a cocoon, ugly and restrictive and necessary for whatever comes next?
I’m trying to learn to live here without the constant ache of should be further. To exist in this in-between without the perpetual weight of not enough. It’s like learning to breathe underwater—technically impossible but somehow necessary, so you adapt. You develop gills made of patience and acceptance. You learn to move through this medium that’s thicker than air but not quite solid enough to stand on.
»You want to rush through this part, don’t you? Want to skip to the part where it gets better, where almost becomes done, where the struggle finally means something. But that’s not how this works. That’s never how this works.«
Some days I think about all the people living in their own almosts. The woman at the grocery store who almost smiles but not quite, her face stopping just short of the expression like she’s forgotten the last part of the equation. The man at the coffee shop who almost makes eye contact but looks away at the last second, like completing the connection would cost more energy than he has to spend. The neighbor who almost waves when we pass, their hand starting to rise then falling back to their side like it’s too heavy to lift all the way.
All of us carrying our invisible collections of incomplete things, our museums of efforts that didn’t quite make it across whatever finish line we imagined. We recognize each other sometimes, in those brief moments when our almosts align—a shared understanding in the grocery store checkout line when someone’s card gets declined and they have to put items back, not because they don’t have money but because they almost remembered to transfer it to the right account. A mutual nod of recognition when someone says “I’ve been meaning to…” and then trails off because we all know that meaning to and doing occupy different universes.
Maybe that’s the secret—that we’re all living in various states of almost. Some people are just better at hiding it. Some people’s almosts are less visible, less heavy, less consuming. But nobody is ever really done. Nobody ever really arrives at that mythical place where everything is complete and nothing is left undone. We’re all just managing our incomplete things, curating our museums of almost, trying to make peace with the space between intention and execution.
The space between almost and done isn’t empty. It’s full of all the trying, all the effort, all the small movements that don’t quite add up to completion but still matter. It’s full of the weight of continuing when continuation feels impossible. It’s full of the peculiar courage it takes to stay in this space without knowing when or if you’ll ever leave it.
There’s something profound about accepting where you are without giving up on where you’re going. About acknowledging that almost is your current address without making it your permanent residence. About understanding that some journeys are measured not in miles traveled but in the ability to keep your feet pointed forward even when you’re not moving.
I’m learning to decorate this space, to make it livable if not comfortable. To find small pleasures in the incomplete—the book that’s forever at page 132 means I’ll always have something to read. The half-written email means the conversation isn’t over. The dusty running shoes mean the possibility still exists. These aren’t consolation prizes. They’re not participation trophies. They’re evidence of trying, of reaching, of refusing to give up completely even when giving up would be so much easier.
That fourth task sits unchecked. Go outside. Such a simple thing. Such an impossible thing. But maybe tomorrow. Maybe tomorrow I’ll make it all the way to done. Or maybe tomorrow I’ll still be here in almost, adding another day to my collection of incomplete things, another page to my story of trying.
And maybe that’s okay. Maybe that has to be okay. Maybe the space between almost and done isn’t a punishment but a pause. A breath. A moment of gathering strength for whatever comes next. Maybe it’s not about escaping almost but about learning to exist here without the constant ache of failure, without the perpetual weight of not enough, without the shame that says incomplete means unworthy.
The afternoon light is different than the morning light. It comes in at a different angle, illuminates different corners, casts different shadows. My three checkmarks look different in this light—less like failure, more like effort. Less like incomplete, more like progress. Less like almost, more like still trying.
The Architecture of Almost
Living in almost means restructuring your entire existence around incompletion. Your living space becomes a physical manifestation of all the things you’ve almost done—surfaces covered with half-sorted papers that were definitely going to be filed properly, books arranged in piles based on how guilty you feel about not finishing them, projects in various states of decay like archaeological layers of good intentions.
My desk is a graveyard of productivity. Notebooks with three pages of enthusiastic notes before blank pages take over. Pens that ran out of ink mid-sentence and were never replaced, leaving words literally fading into nothing. Post-it notes with cryptic reminders for tasks I no longer remember, their adhesive failing so they flutter like dying moths when the air moves. Each item a small monument to a moment when I thought I could be someone who finishes things.
The computer is worse. Hundreds of documents with titles like “Untitled_1” through “Untitled_247,” each one containing a paragraph or two of something that felt important at 2 AM but lost its urgency by morning. Browser bookmarks so numerous they’ve become useless, a digital hoard of “I’ll read this later” that later never comes for. Twenty-three draft emails to the same person, each one attempting to explain why I haven’t responded to their message from six months ago, each explanation more elaborate and less sendable than the last.
»You’re looking around your own space right now, aren’t you? Seeing your own archaeology of almost. The layers of attempted organization, the systems you started but couldn’t maintain, the evidence of all the times you tried to be someone who completes things.«
But there’s something else here too, something I’m only starting to recognize. In between all these monuments to incompletion, there’s evidence of survival. Of adaptation. Of finding ways to exist in the almost that actually work. The coffee cup that’s always in the same place because that’s the only way I’ll remember to drink water. The phone charger that snakes across the floor because putting it away means I’ll forget to charge my phone and miss the alarms that remind me to take medication. The pile of clean clothes on the chair because folding them and putting them in drawers adds unnecessary steps between me and getting dressed.
These aren’t failures of organization. They’re accommodations. They’re the physical architecture of a life that’s learned to work with almost instead of against it. Every surface at exactly the right height for leaning when standing feels too hard. Clear paths between the bed and the bathroom, the bathroom and the kitchen, the kitchen and the couch—the essential corridors of minimal existence. Everything else can be chaos, but these paths remain clear, these systems remain functional.
You’re still here. Still reading. Still existing in your own space between almost and arrival. Between who you were and who you’re becoming. Between the last time you fell and the next time you’ll try to stand.
»That emptiness you feel? That’s not failure. That’s just the space waiting to be filled. That’s just the pause between breaths. That’s just the quiet before whatever comes next.«
I don’t know when almost becomes enough. When trying becomes arriving. When the space between fills in and becomes solid ground. Maybe it never does. Maybe we just get better at living in the in-between, at finding beauty in the incomplete, at recognizing that almost is still more than nothing.
The truth about almost is that it’s not the opposite of done—it’s proof that you started. Proof that you tried. Proof that you’re still here, still adding checkmarks to your list even when you know you might not finish it. Every almost is evidence of effort, even if that effort didn’t get you where you meant to go. Every incomplete thing is proof that you haven’t given up completely, that some part of you still believes in the possibility of completion, even if that belief is thinner than thread.
And in a world that keeps demanding completion, demanding perfection, demanding that we transform our struggles into inspiring narratives with clean endings—maybe the most radical thing we can do is admit that we’re still in almost. Still in process. Still becoming. Maybe honesty about incompletion is its own form of resistance, its own kind of strength.
Maybe the most honest thing we can say is: I’m not there yet. But I’m not nowhere either. I’m here, in the space between almost and done, and I’m learning that maybe this counts too. Maybe this is enough. Maybe this is everything.
The whiteboard watches from its crooked angle, patient as always. Three checkmarks. One empty box. Almost done. Almost there. Almost.
And tomorrow, I’ll try again. Not because I believe tomorrow will be different, but because trying is what we do in the space between almost and done. We try. We rest. We try again. We adjust our expectations. We lower the bar. We raise it again. We forget what we were trying to do. We remember. We start over. We continue from where we stopped. We exist in this space that’s neither success nor failure but something more complex, more human, more real.
That’s not nothing. That’s not failure.
That’s survival. That’s continuation. That’s the peculiar courage of living in almost and refusing to let it become never.
Even when it hurts. Even when it’s heavy. Even when nobody else understands the weight of that one unchecked box, the effort hidden in those three checkmarks, the victory disguised as incompletion.
You’re still in your almost. Still moving through the space between.
And maybe—just maybe—that’s exactly where you need to be right now. Not because it’s comfortable. Not because it’s easy. But because almost is still part of the journey, and you’re still on it. Still traveling, even when it looks like standing still. Still moving, even when the movement is invisible. Still becoming, even when becoming feels like being stuck.
Still here. Still trying. Still beautifully, messily, honestly incomplete.
And that’s enough. For today, that’s enough.
Tomorrow, we’ll see about that fourth checkmark. Tomorrow, we’ll see about done.
But today? Today we’re here in almost.
And we’re still going.
And that counts.
That matters.
That’s everything.
