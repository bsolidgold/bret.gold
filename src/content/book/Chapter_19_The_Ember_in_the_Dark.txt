Chapter 19 - The Ember in the Dark
A Fragile Spark
The morning light doesn’t announce itself. It seeps through a tear in the curtain I’ve been meaning to fix for three years now, creating this thin blade of gold that cuts across the darkness of my bedroom. The light catches a single dust mote, transforms it into something almost magical, almost meaningful. I watch it drift—this tiny, illuminated nothing—and for reasons I can’t explain, I can’t look away.
My body feels welded to the mattress. Not sleeping, not quite awake, just existing in that suspended state where consciousness feels optional and movement feels impossible. The dogs are still breathing their steady rhythm beside me, their warmth the only thing anchoring me to the physical world. Everything else feels theoretical—the day ahead, the tasks waiting, the person I’m supposed to be.
The dust mote continues its lazy spiral through the shaft of light, and I find myself holding my breath, watching it like it matters. Like if it disappears, something important will be lost.
»You’ve done this too—fixated on something absurdly small because the big things were all too heavy to look at. A crack in the ceiling. The way ice melts in a glass. Anything that isn’t the thing you can’t face yet.«
It’s nothing. Just a particle of dust catching light at the right angle. But in this moment, in this specific configuration of exhaustion and need, it becomes everything. A reason to keep my eyes open. A point of focus that isn’t pain or memory or the crushing weight of another day I’m not sure I can navigate.
The whiteboard hangs crooked on the wall, its instructions barely visible in the dim light. Tasks that feel insurmountable from this angle, from this depth of tiredness that has nothing to do with sleep and everything to do with the cost of continuing.
But the dust mote keeps floating, keeps catching light, keeps being inexplicably beautiful in its meaninglessness. And something in me—something so small I almost miss it—shifts. Not dramatically. Not with any kind of revelation or breakthrough. Just a tiny adjustment in the gravity that’s been holding me down. Like a single degree of change in temperature. Barely perceptible, but there.
I don’t leap out of bed. Don’t suddenly find the strength to tackle the day. But my hand moves—just slightly—toward the edge of the blanket. The first negotiation with movement. The smallest possible gesture toward continuing.
This is how it starts sometimes. Not with grand gestures or profound realizations, but with dust motes and morning light and movements so small they barely register as choices. With embers so faint you have to look twice to see them glowing.
The world wants our hope to be loud. Wants it to arrive with fanfare and certainty, with clear signs and unmistakable warmth. But sometimes—most times—hope is just this: a dust mote in sunlight, a hand moving toward the edge of a blanket, a tiny adjustment in the weight of being alive.
I watch the light move across my ceiling as the morning progresses, tracking time not in minutes but in the slow migration of shadows. The dogs stir eventually, their bodies warming up to the idea of breakfast, of another day of simple needs simply met. Their movement creates a chain reaction—if they’re getting up, I need to get up. If they need breakfast, I need to provide it. Their basic requirements become my scaffolding for movement.
It’s not inspiration. It’s not motivation. It’s just necessity dressed up as purpose, but sometimes that’s enough. Sometimes that’s all we get—these tiny, practical reasons to keep moving when movement feels impossible.
The dust mote has long since drifted out of the light, disappeared back into the anonymous darkness of my room. But something about seeing it—about witnessing that brief moment of illumination—stays with me. Not as memory exactly, but as possibility. As proof that even in rooms full of darkness, light finds a way to slip through torn curtains and transform nothing into something worth watching.
The Nature of Embers
Here’s what they don’t tell you about hope when everything’s gone dark: it’s not a bonfire. It’s not even a candle. It’s an ember—barely visible, easy to miss, so fragile that a harsh breath could extinguish it. But it’s also stubborn as fuck, glowing there in the ash of everything that’s burned down, refusing to go completely cold.
I’ve become an expert in recognizing these embers, these tiny points of heat in the landscape of loss. They hide in the strangest places—in the weight of a coffee mug warming your hands when everything else feels cold, in the way your dog’s tail still wags even though you haven’t been worth wagging for in months, in the fact that your body keeps breathing even when your mind has given up on finding reasons why it should.
They’re so small, these embers. So easy to dismiss. Nobody writes books about them because they’re not marketable. You can’t package “I drank a glass of water today” as a breakthrough. You can’t sell “I answered one text message” as transformation. But when you’re living in the kind of darkness where even breathing feels like a job you're underqualified for, these tiny sparks of functioning become everything.
»You’re already cataloging your own embers, aren’t you? Those small, almost embarrassing victories that you’d never tell anyone about because they seem too pathetic to count. But they do count. They all count.«
A text from someone who still remembers you exist—not asking for anything, not expecting a response, just a simple “thinking of you.” Three words that somehow cut through three weeks of feeling invisible. That’s an ember.
A song that comes on shuffle at exactly the right moment—not even a song you particularly like, but something about the melody, about the way it fills the silence, makes the next five minutes bearable. That’s an ember.
The way sunlight hits your kitchen counter at 3 PM, turning ordinary formica into something briefly beautiful. You don’t Instagram it. You don’t even really acknowledge it. But you notice it, and for just a second, beauty exists again in your world. That’s an ember.
These aren’t photogenic or shareable. They’re private, almost shameful in their smallness. But they’re real. And when you’re trying to survive in the kind of darkness that swallows everything whole, real matters more than impressive.
I remember a Tuesday—or maybe it was a Thursday, the days had started bleeding together—when the only thing I managed to do was refill the dogs’ water bowl. That’s it. That’s the whole achievement. Noticed the bowl was empty, walked to the sink, filled it, put it back. Maybe thirty seconds of activity in twenty-four hours of existence.
But that was an ember. A tiny proof that I could still see a need and meet it. That I could still take care of something, even if I couldn’t take care of myself. That somewhere in the wasteland of my consciousness, the part of me that gives a shit about other living things was still glowing, however faintly.
The thing about embers is they’re patient. They can glow for hours in the right conditions, neither dying nor growing, just maintaining their small heat against the darkness. They don’t demand attention or care, though they’ll respond to both. They just persist, these tiny points of possible fire, waiting for the right moment to either fade completely or catch something flammable and grow.
Depression wants to convince you that these embers don’t matter. That anything less than a roaring fire of recovery is meaningless. That if you’re not getting better in obvious, measurable ways, you’re not getting better at all. But that’s bullshit. That’s the kind of all-or-nothing thinking that keeps us from recognizing the small victories that actually keep us alive.
Because here’s the truth about surviving in the dark: it’s not about finding the light switch. It’s not about sudden illumination or dramatic rescue. It’s about recognizing the embers that are already there—the tiny, persistent glows that prove something in you is still burning, even if it’s barely visible to the naked eye.
Your heart beating when you wish it would stop—that’s an ember. Your lungs filling with air even when breathing feels pointless—that’s an ember. Your body processing food and water even when eating feels like too much work—that’s an ember.
These aren’t conscious choices. They’re deeper than that. They’re the embers that live in your cells, in your DNA, in the parts of you that remember how to survive even when your conscious mind has forgotten why it should want to.
»Funny thing about your heartbeat—you never asked it to keep going. Never signed a contract. It just does, stubborn and indifferent to your opinion on the matter. That’s not inspiration. That’s mutiny. The good kind.«
Some days, recognizing these embers is the only thing that keeps me tethered to existence. Not in any profound or meaningful way, but in the most basic sense—they’re evidence that some part of me is still trying, even when I’m not conscious of it. Even when I think I’ve given up completely.
The coffee tastes like coffee—that’s an ember. The shower water is the right temperature—that’s an ember. My favorite hoodie still fits the same way—that’s an ember. The dogs still recognize me—that’s an ember.
Tiny, insignificant things that wouldn’t even register in a normal life. But in the landscape of loss, in the territory of trauma, these small consistencies become anchors. They’re proof that not everything has burned down. That somewhere in the ash, something is still glowing.
Protecting the Spark
The morning I couldn’t do it anymore—couldn’t pretend, couldn’t push through, couldn’t manufacture another ounce of “fine”—I sat on the edge of my bed staring at the wall. Four basic tasks hanging there that had become my daily tribunal, my four-count indictment, my quiet reminder of how far I’d fallen from functional.
But that morning, something shifted. Not in the tasks themselves—they remained monuments to impossibility—but in how I looked at them. Instead of seeing four things I had to do, I saw four things I could choose from. Not all of them. Not even most of them. Just one.
Just one.
The negotiation began: Which one could I manage? Which single task could I extract from this list and accomplish without completely depleting whatever microscopic reserve of energy I had left? Waking up was already done—cruel joke that consciousness is when you’d rather be anywhere else. Getting out of bed felt too big, too decisive. A shower required standing, and standing required a kind of balance I wasn’t sure I possessed.
But go outside? Maybe. Maybe I could just open the door. Not walk through it, not venture into the world, just open it. Let some different air in. See if the world was still there.
»You know this negotiation, don’t you? This careful mathematics of survival where you’re calculating the minimum viable existence. Where you’re trying to find the one thing—just one fucking thing—you can do that counts as living.«
I made it to the door that day. Stood there in my boxers and three-day-old t-shirt, hand on the knob, feeling the sun on my face for the first time in… I couldn’t remember how long. Didn’t step outside. Didn’t need to. Just stood there, door open, being a barrier between inside and outside, existing in the threshold between my darkness and the world’s light.
That was my ember that day. That was what I protected and nurtured and counted as victory.
The thing about protecting these small sparks is that it requires a complete recalibration of what success looks like. You have to lower the bar so far that other people would think you’re giving up, but really you’re just being realistic about what you can actually sustain. You’re choosing to protect the ember rather than demanding it become a bonfire before it’s ready.
Some days, protecting the spark means saying no to everything except the most basic survival tasks. It means disappointing people who don’t understand why you can’t just “push through it.” It means accepting that your capacity is what it is, not what you wish it was or what it used to be or what other people think it should be.
I learned to cup my hands around these tiny flames like someone protecting a match in the wind. Gentle. Patient. Not asking for more than they could give. Not forcing them to be bigger or brighter or warmer than they were capable of being in that moment.
The water glass on my nightstand became a protected ember. Not the act of drinking water—that was too much intention—but just filling the glass. Just having it there. Some days I drank from it, some days I didn’t, but it was there. A possibility. An option. A tiny rebellion against the part of me that had stopped believing in tomorrow enough to prepare for it.
Protecting the spark means defending your small victories against the voice that says they don’t count. The voice that compares your “I opened the door today” to someone else’s marathon. The voice that insists that if your recovery doesn’t look good from the outside—polished, presentable, worth photographing—it doesn’t count at all.
Fuck that voice.
Your ember is your ember. Your tiny victory is your victory. Your survival is your survival. It doesn’t need to look like anyone else’s to count.
»When was the last time someone looked at the tiny thing you managed and said "that counts" instead of "is that all?" If you can’t remember, let me be the one: that counts.«
I started keeping a list—not of goals or achievements or any of that productivity porn bullshit—but of embers. Tiny things that proved I was still here, still trying, still capable of something:
Remembered to feed the dogs before they had to remind me
Answered a text within the same week I received it
Ate something that required more preparation than opening a package
Noticed the moon
Laughed at something (even if it was just a stupid meme)
Changed my shirt
Opened a window
Remembered someone’s birthday (even if I didn’t reach out)
Made it through a song without skipping to the next one
Sat outside for five minutes
Looking at that list now, it seems pathetic. These are things that functioning humans do without thought, without effort, without needing to document them as achievements. But when you’re protecting an ember in hurricane winds, when you’re trying to keep something alive in conditions that are actively hostile to life, every small success is enormous.
The protection isn’t just from external forces—from the world that demands you be more productive, more functional, more “normal.” It’s from your own internal weather systems. From the shame that says you should be better by now. From the comparison that measures your ember against other people’s bonfires. From the despair that whispers maybe it would be easier to just let it go out.
But you don’t. You keep your hands cupped around that tiny glow. You feed it the smallest twigs of possibility—not enough to smother it, just enough to keep it alive. You learn the precise balance between protecting it and letting it breathe. You become an expert in the care and feeding of almost-extinct hope.
Some embers need isolation to survive—quiet, dark spaces where they can glow without the pressure of wind or judgment. Others need gentle breath, the smallest encouragement to keep burning. Some need fuel—tiny accomplishments that feed the flame. Others just need time, patience, the space to exist without demands for growth.
Learning which ember needs what kind of protection—that’s the real work. That’s the expertise you develop in the dark, through trial and error, through watching embers die and learning from their loss.
Moments of Unexpected Light
The thing about darkness is that it makes you hyperaware of any light that manages to break through. Your pupils dilate, your whole being becomes calibrated to detect the smallest glow. You become a specialized instrument for recognizing illumination in places where it shouldn’t exist.
It was a Wednesday—I remember because Wednesday was the day I was supposed to be getting better according to some arbitrary timeline I’d created—when Badge (the one I named after my island protector) did something that cracked me open. I was on the floor of my bedroom, not dramatically collapsed but just sitting there because sitting on the floor felt more honest than pretending I could handle furniture. The weight of existing had finally exceeded my capacity to remain vertical.
Badge walked over—not with the bouncing energy of a dog who wants something, but with that careful approach dogs have when they know something’s wrong. He didn’t lick my face or paw at me or do any of the things dogs do in movies when their humans are breaking. He just lay down next to me, pressing his spine against my thigh, and put his head on my knee.
The weight of his head—maybe eight pounds of dog skull and concern—became an anchor. Not pulling me down but keeping me from floating away entirely. He breathed his steady dog breaths, and I found myself matching them without meaning to. In and out. In and out. The simplest rhythm in the world, shared between two mammals who didn’t need words to understand that sometimes, presence is enough.
»Think about who has done this for you. Not the person who gave you advice or told you to be strong. The one who just sat there. Who didn’t try to fix anything. Who was just warm and present and didn’t leave. You’re thinking of them right now, aren’t you?«
That’s unexpected light—not a sunrise or a revelation or a breakthrough, but a dog’s head on your knee when you’ve forgotten you’re worth touching.
These moments don’t follow the narrative rules we’ve been taught about hope and healing. They don’t arrive when you’ve earned them through suffering or growth or any kind of worthiness. They just appear, random as lightning, illuminating everything for a split second before leaving you in a darkness that somehow feels less complete than before.
A stranger in the grocery store who sees you struggling with the self-checkout and just says, “Those things are impossible, right?” Not trying to help, not trying to fix, just acknowledging that yes, this simple task is sometimes too much. That small recognition—that’s unexpected light.
The barista who remembers your order even though you haven’t been there in months. Who doesn’t say anything about your absence, doesn’t make it weird, just makes your coffee and says, “Good to see you.” Four words that somehow mean you still exist in the world, that your absence was noticed but not judged. That’s unexpected light.
The random text from someone you haven’t talked to in years: “Saw this and thought of you.” Attached is something stupid—a meme, a photo of a dog that looks like a cloud, a screenshot of a tweet about how horrible morning people are. It’s nothing. It’s everything. It’s proof that you exist in someone’s mind when you’re not even in the room. That’s unexpected light.
These moments can’t be forced or scheduled or earned. They arrive like gifts from a universe that mostly seems indifferent to your suffering but occasionally, randomly, impossibly, offers you something gentle.
I remember driving home from a particularly brutal therapy session—the kind where you pay someone $200 to professionally confirm that yes, you’re as fucked up as you thought—when my phone shuffled to a song I’d forgotten existed. Something from years ago, from before everything broke, from a time when I could listen to music without every lyric feeling like a personal attack.
But this song—it just washed over me without violence. It was just melody and memory, but the good kind of memory. The kind that reminds you that you haven’t always been this broken. That there was a before, which means there might be an after. That you’ve had different chapters, which means this might not be the final one.
I had to pull over because I was crying, but it wasn’t the usual crying. It wasn’t grief or frustration or the general tears of existing. It was something else—something that felt almost like relief. Like my body remembering it could feel things besides weight.
»You just remembered your own moment, didn’t you? That unexpected crack in the darkness where something else slipped through. Maybe it was a song, maybe it was a stranger’s kindness, maybe it was just the way light hit water. But you remember it. You’re holding it right now like evidence. Like proof.«
The unexpected light doesn’t fix anything. Badge putting his head on my knee didn’t cure my depression. The stranger in the grocery store didn’t solve my anxiety. The song didn’t heal my trauma. But they created these tiny breaks in the narrative of darkness—these small proofs that other things were possible, even if I couldn’t access them yet.
Sometimes the light comes from even stranger places. From the spider in my bathroom that I couldn’t bring myself to kill, that became a weird roommate I’d check on each morning. Still there? Still alive? Me too, spider. Me too.
From the way my coffee maker still worked every morning, reliable in a way that nothing else in my life seemed to be. That consistent gurgle and hiss, that predictable transformation of water and grounds into something resembling purpose.
From the fact that my body kept healing itself in small ways—cuts scabbing over, bruises fading—even when healing felt impossible on any larger scale. My cells didn’t get the memo that we’d given up. They just kept doing their cellular things, dividing and repairing and persisting.
The unexpected light often comes through cracks we didn’t know existed. Through people we didn’t know were paying attention. Through moments so small they don’t even register as moments until later, when we look back and realize that was when something shifted, even if we couldn’t name it at the time.
The Quiet Power of Small Light
You want to know what actually keeps people alive? Not inspiration. Not breakthrough moments. Not sudden clarity or purpose or any of the loud, confident promises the world keeps making about transformation. It’s the accumulation of tiny lights—so small you’d miss them if you weren’t looking, so quiet they don’t even register as hope until you realize you’re somehow still going despite everything that says you shouldn’t be.
Every glass of water I managed to drink when dehydration would have been easier—that was a small light.
Every time I fed the dogs when I couldn’t feed myself—small light.
Every shower I took when staying dirty felt more honest—small light.
Every text I answered when isolation felt safer—small light.
Every time I chose to stay when leaving seemed simpler—small light.
They add up, these tiny rebellions against darkness. Not in any dramatic way, not in any story worth telling at parties, but in the most fundamental way possible: they keep you here. They become the breadcrumbs you follow through the forest of your own dissolution, each one just visible enough to get you to the next one.
»You’re counting your own small lights right now, aren’t you? All those tiny victories no one else saw. All those moments you chose to continue when stopping was an option. All those small lights you’ve been told don’t count. They count. They all fucking count.«
The power isn’t in their individual brightness. One small light barely illuminates anything. But string together enough of them—enough glasses of water, enough answered texts, enough moments of choosing to stay—and suddenly you’ve got a constellation. Not bright enough to navigate by, maybe, but bright enough to prove the darkness isn’t total.
I started to understand this the day I realized I’d been alive for six months since Justin died. Six months of Tuesdays and Thursdays and all the other days that felt impossible to get through but somehow I did. Six months of small lights that I didn’t even recognize as lights at the time. Six months of my body continuing its work while my mind insisted everything was over.
That’s 180 days. 4,320 hours. 259,200 minutes of choosing, consciously or not, to continue.
When you break it down like that, when you do the math on survival, it stops looking like one big choice and starts looking like what it really is: millions of tiny choices. Millions of small lights. Millions of moments where something in you—maybe not even the conscious you, maybe something deeper, more primal, more stubborn—chose to keep going.
The quiet power is in their persistence. These small lights don’t burn out as quickly as the big ones. They don’t demand the kind of fuel that larger hopes require. They can sustain themselves on almost nothing—on the simple momentum of routine, on the basic requirements of keeping other beings alive, on the fundamental stubbornness of cells that keep dividing even when division feels pointless.
Your heart beats approximately 100,000 times per day. That’s 100,000 small lights. 100,000 tiny choices to continue. 100,000 quiet rebellions against the darkness that would swallow you if your heart listened to your head.
Your lungs expand and contract about 20,000 times per day. 20,000 more lights. 20,000 more proofs that something in you believes in the next breath, even when you don’t.
Your cells replace themselves constantly—skin cells every few weeks, liver cells every few months, bone cells every few years. Your body is literally rebuilding itself all the time, creating new light from old materials, refusing to accept decay as permanent.
»Here’s what’s strange about all those tiny continuing things your body does without asking your permission—they’re not betrayals of your desire to stop. They’re evidence of something in you that already made the decision. Something older than your sadness, older than your reasons. Something that votes "yes" a hundred thousand times a day whether you agree with the ballot or not.«
The small lights teach us something that the big flames never could: that survival doesn’t require brightness. It doesn’t require heat that others can feel or light that others can see. It just requires the tiniest glow, the smallest possible ember, the quietest possible “yes” to continuing.
And here’s the thing about quiet power—it’s sustainable in a way that loud power never is. You can’t maintain a bonfire indefinitely. You can’t keep a spotlight running forever. But a small light? An ember? A tiny glow that barely registers as light at all? That can persist almost indefinitely, feeding on the smallest amount of fuel, requiring the least possible maintenance.
When I look back at the darkest periods—the times when I was sure I wouldn’t make it through—what I see isn’t the dramatic moments of choosing to live. It’s the accumulation of small lights. The thousands of tiny choices that didn’t feel like choices. The quiet persistence of a body that kept breathing, a heart that kept beating, dogs that kept needing breakfast.
The crooked frame on the wall I hung at an angle because perfect felt like too much pressure—that’s small light. The glass of water I filled but didn’t drink—small light. The fact that I keep showing up to the same morning even when I can’t do anything with it—small light.
These aren’t the kinds of victories that make good stories. “I filled a glass of water today” doesn’t inspire anyone. “I drank water” doesn’t transform lives. “I kept breathing” doesn’t sell books or fill seminars or generate likes on social media.
But these small lights have a power that the big ones don’t: they’re honest. They’re sustainable. They’re real. They don’t require you to be anything other than what you are in this moment—broken, exhausted, barely holding on, but still here. Still accumulating small lights whether you notice them or not.
The Persistence of Light
The morning light still finds that tear in my curtain. Every day, whether I’m watching or not, whether I care or not, whether I’m capable of seeing it as metaphor or meaning or just fucking light through a hole in fabric. It persists, this daily reminder that illumination doesn’t require permission, doesn’t need invitation, doesn’t wait for readiness.
I’m watching it again now—not the same dust mote, but another one, or maybe the same one, who can tell? It doesn’t matter. What matters is that I’m still here to see it, still capable of recognition, still able to hold both the darkness and this tiny point of light in the same frame of vision.
»You’re still here too. Still carrying your own ember through your own darkness. That’s not nothing. That’s everything.«
The ember isn’t always visible. Some days, the darkness feels so complete that I’m convinced it’s gone out entirely. That whatever small light I’ve been tending has finally succumbed to the weight of too much shadow, too much cold, too much everything. But then something shifts—the angle of vision, the quality of attention, the simple passage of time—and there it is again. Still glowing. Still persisting. Still refusing to go completely dark.
This is what I’ve learned about embers, about small lights, about the tiny persistent glow of continuing: they don’t need us to believe in them to exist. They don’t require our faith or our attention or our gratitude. They just persist, these small rebellions against darkness, these tiny insistences on continuation.
My body keeps proving this. Even on the days when I’m convinced everything has gone dark, my heart keeps its rhythm. My lungs keep their exchange. My cells keep their silent work of renovation and repair. The ember persists at levels I’m not even conscious of, in places I can’t even see.
The dogs still wake up expecting breakfast. The coffee maker still responds to its programmed timer. The sun still finds that tear in the curtain. The world continues its quiet insistence that tomorrow is possible, even when I can’t imagine it.
This isn’t hope in any grand sense. It’s not the kind of light that illuminates paths or reveals destinations or makes everything suddenly clear. It’s just persistence. Just the simple, stubborn continuation of small lights that refuse to be extinguished, even when extinguishing would be easier.
I think about Riley sometimes, about Justin, about all the people whose embers went out. Not dramatically, not all at once, but slowly, quietly, one small light at a time until there wasn’t enough glow left to sustain them. I think about how close I’ve come to that same darkness, that same slow dimming, that same quiet surrender.
But I also think about Badge—the first one, the island protector—who showed me that sometimes survival is just standing guard. Just being present. Just maintaining your position even when you can’t see what you’re protecting or why it matters. He didn’t know he was saving my life. He was just being a dog, doing dog things, following dog instincts. But his simple persistence, his daily presence, his stubborn refusal to abandon his post—that was enough. That was everything.
»Your eyes just got wet. Because you have your own Badge, don’t you? Your own guardian, your own reason, your own stubborn anchor to this world. Maybe it’s a person, maybe it’s a pet, maybe it’s just your own stubborn refusal to let the darkness win. Whatever it is, it’s keeping your ember alive. And that’s enough.«
The ember in the dark isn’t a promise of fire. It’s not a guarantee of warmth or light or transformation. It’s just a possibility. A maybe. A small “not yet” in response to the darkness that whispers “give up.”
Some nights, I sit in my room with all the lights off, just to prove I can. Just to practice being in the dark without being consumed by it. And in that deliberate darkness, I can see it more clearly—the ember that persists despite everything. Not in my chest or my head or any poetic location. Just everywhere. In my cells. In my breath. In the simple fact that I’m still here to practice sitting in the dark.
The whiteboard has become less instruction and more invitation. Not a list of demands but a quiet suggestion that maybe today has room for one small act of continuing.
Not every day. Not perfectly. Not with any kind of consistency. Not in any straight line. But enough. Enough to keep the ember alive. Enough to accumulate small lights. Enough to continue.
The tear in my curtain will probably never get fixed. It’s been three years now, and I’ve accepted it as a permanent feature of my landscape. My daily reminder that broken things can still serve a purpose. That imperfection can be an entry point for light. That sometimes the most important illumination comes through the places where we’re torn.
The morning light will find it again tomorrow. And probably the tomorrow after that. And all the tomorrows I can’t yet imagine but that my ember insists on believing in anyway. Not with faith or hope or any kind of certainty. Just with persistence. Just with the quiet power of small light that refuses to go out.
This is what survival actually looks like: not a triumphant flame but a persistent ember. Not a roaring fire but a quiet glow. Not enough light to see by, maybe, but enough to prove the darkness isn’t total. Enough to navigate by, one small step at a time. Enough to keep going, even when going feels impossible.
The dust mote has drifted out of the light now, back into the anonymous darkness of my room. But I know it’s still there. Still floating. Still capable of catching light when the angle is right. Just like my ember. Just like all our embers. Still here. Still glowing. Still persisting in the dark.
And maybe that’s the only kind of light that really matters—the kind that persists not because it’s strong or bright or impressive, but simply because it refuses to go out. The kind that accumulates in tiny moments, in small victories, in the quiet persistence of bodies that keep breathing even when breathing feels like a vote you keep casting for reasons you can't articulate.
The kind that reminds us, in the darkest moments, that we’re still here. Still carrying our small light. Still adding our tiny glow to the constellation of humans who are all just trying to keep their embers alive, one breath at a time, one day at a time, one small light at a time.
That’s enough. It has to be enough. And somehow, mysteriously, against all odds and logic and reason—it is enough. The ember persists. The small lights accumulate. And we continue, carrying our tiny flames through the darkness, not because we can see where we’re going, but because the alternative is letting the light go out entirely.
And we’re too stubborn for that. Too persistent. Too practiced in the quiet art of keeping embers alive.
So we continue. We persist. We carry our small lights through whatever darkness we’re given. Not because it’s inspiring or impressive or worthy of recognition. But because that’s what embers do.
