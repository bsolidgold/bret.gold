Chapter 2 - The Moment Before the Fall

The music was wrong.

That's the first thing. Not what I saw — what I heard. Sad country songs bleeding through the house on a Saturday morning, tinny and small from my Bluetooth speaker in the kitchen, playing on a loop that no one was listening to. The kind of music that shouldn't have meant anything except it meant everything, because Riley didn't listen to sad country music. Not ever. Not at ten in the morning. Not at any hour of any day in the twelve years I'd known him. Riley blasted hip-hop in the shower so loud I'd bang on the wall. He sang off-key to classic rock while he cooked breakfast he'd inevitably burn. The day Riley started listening to sad country was a Friday night in February while I was asleep fifteen feet below him, and I didn't hear it then.

I heard it in the morning.

But I'm getting ahead of myself. That's what trauma does to timelines — puts the worst part first and makes everything else arrange itself around it like furniture around a black hole. The friendship, the nights at the bars, the cereal arguments and road trips and the bathroom floor at three AM — it all orbits that Saturday now. It all gets pulled toward it. So let me try to start at the beginning, even though the beginning doesn't feel like the beginning anymore. Even though my brain has rewritten the story so many times that the opening keeps wanting to be the ending.

---

I met Riley in 2006 when I started training at Absolute MMA. He was nineteen. I was twenty-six, which felt like a bigger gap back then than it probably was. I'd already served a mission in the South Pacific, done four years in the Air Force, lived in Germany, had an engagement blow up in my face, and was trying to figure out what to do with myself now that all the things I thought were going to be my life had turned out not to be. Riley was just getting started — hadn't figured out what he wanted yet, but in that way that nineteen-year-olds haven't, where the uncertainty is still exciting instead of exhausting. We were both white belts, both brand new to jiu-jitsu, both terrible at it in the same enthusiastic, know-nothing way. The jiu-jitsu world was like that — easy to make friends from all walks of life and all ages. Something about voluntarily learning to be uncomfortable together strips away the bullshit.

It wasn't until Riley turned twenty-one that we became really good friends. There were several nights at the bars and clubs, but one sticks out.

We went to Lumpy's to watch the UFC fights with some friends from the gym. I don't remember who fought or very much about that night, but Riley got a lot more drunk than he usually did. A lot more. I was the DD, so I drove him home.

Long story short — that was the start of a long night making sure he didn't aspirate on his own vomit while he slept. We ended up in his bathroom, him puking in the bathtub because the toilet was too small a target. Poison Control was called. Riley eventually slept it off.

I found out later that one of our "friends" had sexually assaulted him that night.

I want to sit with that sentence for a second because I spent years not sitting with it. I found out and I filed it and I moved on because that's what you do when someone tells you something that rearranges everything you thought you knew about a night you were present for. You file it. You move on. You don't revisit it because revisiting it means asking questions you don't want answered — like where the fuck were you when it happened, and could you have stopped it, and did you miss something, and is this the thing that started the fuse burning. Because trauma has a fuse. And sometimes the fuse is years long. And sometimes you don't know it's burning because the person carrying it is too good at being fine.

That bathroom night was the start of a pretty solid friendship. Maybe surviving something ugly together just does that — makes you family in a way that normal friendship can't. He knew I'd hold his head over the bathtub without asking questions. I knew he'd do the same. That was enough.

After that it was everything. Fight nights. Road trips to Vegas doing 120 because Riley was behind the wheel and he had that grin — the one that said he knew exactly how fast he was going and didn't give a single shit. Wendover. Camping and fishing. Just sitting on the couch doing absolutely nothing, which is what the best friendships are actually made of — not the big moments, the nothing moments. The ones where you're just existing in the same room and it's fine. It's better than fine. It's the only place you don't have to perform.

»You have someone like this. Had someone. Someone you didn't earn through effort or compatibility or shared interests — someone who just showed up and fit. The kind of friendship that doesn't need explaining because there's nothing to explain. You just are. And either you still have them, in which case stop reading this and text them right now, or you don't, in which case you already know where this story goes.«

We became roommates around 2011, after we both went through breakups at the same time. Got our purple belts shortly after. Riley was a firefighter by then — tested for and become an engineer. Dream job. He saved people for a living. That was his whole thing. He ran toward what the rest of us ran from, came home after 48-hour shifts and acted like it was nothing. Because firefighters don't admit it's something. They pack it into dark humor and cheap beer and an unspoken pact with the guys at the station that nobody's going to be the one who says how bad it really was.

He wasn't sure he could afford his house on his own. I was renting. So I told him I'd pay half his mortgage if I could use half of the house. No-brainer.

We were pretty awesome roommates. I chalk it up to Riley's easy-going nature. Burned eggs every morning. Blasted hip-hop. Sang off-key. Ate the last of my cereal and argued about whose turn it was to buy more. Normal shit. Roommate shit. The kind of unremarkable, mundane, beautiful daily life that you don't realize is the best thing you'll ever have until you're sitting in someone else's driveway watching paramedics wheel it out on a gurney.

You were more than just a friend, Riley. We were like brothers. We had each other's backs. For real.

---

After his breakup, Riley's drinking changed.

This is the part where I'm supposed to catalog the warning signs with the clarity of hindsight, lay them out like evidence, show you how obvious it all was. But it wasn't obvious. That's the thing about warning signs — they only look like warning signs after the thing they were warning you about has already happened. Before that, they just look like a guy going through a rough patch.

He went from a happy-go-lucky drunk — always buying people drinks, always the life of the party, the kind of drunk that makes you want to be drunk too — to something quieter. Sadder. He'd sit in the living room by himself, TV on but not watching, beer in hand but not really drinking. Just existing in that space between fine and not fine that people like us know how to inhabit so well that nobody around us can tell the difference.

A lot of the drinking happened when I wasn't around. He had friends from the fire station. I only made it out when his schedule lined up with my weekends. Then the drinking shifted to the house. Parties that got out of control. Guys who were cool about keeping it down when I reminded them it was a school night.

I should have seen it. The transition from party drunk to alone drunk. The way the laughter got thinner. But I did what everyone does — told myself he was going through something, trusted him to come out the other side. He was a firefighter. He was tough. He was fine.

That's the story we tell ourselves about the people we love. They're fine. They're tough. We tell ourselves that because the alternative — that they're drowning right in front of us and we're too scared to reach in — is something we can't live with. So we don't look too hard. We don't ask the question we're afraid to answer. We accept the performance because questioning it means admitting something is wrong, and admitting something is wrong means doing something about it, and doing something about it means confronting the possibility that maybe we can't fix it. Maybe the fuse has been burning since a night at Lumpy's that we were supposed to be watching over, and there's nothing we can do about the length of it now.

»You're thinking about someone right now. The one you didn't ask. The one where you saw the warning signs and chose the comfortable explanation. I'm not pointing fingers — I'm standing right next to you in it. We all do it. We're all complicit in the fiction that the people we love are fine.«

---

The last party Riley had wasn't on a school night. I didn't have to remind anyone.

That whole week stands out from the rest. In hindsight, it was strange — but in the way that only hindsight makes strange, because at the time it was good. One of the best weeks we'd had in a while. We were both busy, but we found time to watch SportsCenter together before bed at least three nights. Just sitting on the couch, arguing about highlights, dogs at our feet. Normal. Easy. The kind of evening that makes you think everything is fine because it feels so much like everything is fine.

Riley seemed happier that week than he had in months. Lighter. Like something had settled.

I thought it was because we had a seminar with Grand Master Flavio Behring that weekend — Flavio was a legend in jiu-jitsu, and mat time with him was a big deal. Riley loved that stuff.

Then I found out he wasn't planning on going.

That was odd. I didn't think about it. I should have thought about it. I should have sat with that one detail — Riley, who loved jiu-jitsu, who loved seminars, who would've been first on the mat — deciding not to go. I should have asked why. But I didn't, because it was a good week, and asking why would've been pulling a thread, and sometimes we don't pull threads because we're afraid of what unravels.

Here's the thing about the moment before the fall that took me years to understand: sometimes it doesn't look like a moment at all. Sometimes it looks like three good nights of SportsCenter and a friend who seems happier than he has in months. Sometimes the warning signs look exactly like the absence of warning signs. Because the person who's already decided stops fighting. The burden lifts. They seem better because they've stopped carrying the weight of the choice. The choice is made, and what's left is something that looks, from the outside, like peace. Like resolution. Like the person you've been worrying about has finally turned a corner.

They have turned a corner. Just not the one you think.

He had his usual impromptu party Friday night. I went to bed early for the seminar. Woke up a few times to party noises. Brushed them off because it wasn't a school night.

I wish I would have gone upstairs anyway.

---

The next morning I got up and went through my routine as usual. Kept the dogs in my room while I did my bathroom stuff. Got dressed. Gathered my jiu-jitsu stuff for the seminar. Went upstairs to let the dogs out for the day.

I want to slow down here because my brain always wants to speed through this part — to skip ahead to where it's over, to where the knowing has already happened and I can stop holding both versions of reality in my hands. But the thing is, it's never over. And the worst part isn't the knowing. The worst part is right here, in the space between the routine and the truth. In the last few seconds of a world.

Everything changed when I turned the corner on the landing before the final three steps upstairs.

My body knew before I did. That's something nobody tells you about moments like this — your body runs the math before your brain even opens the textbook. There's no conscious calculation, no logical deduction. Just this animal certainty that arrives in your marrow and your gut and the base of your skull all at once. Something is wrong. Something is already wrong. Something has been wrong while you were sleeping and now you're awake and it's still wrong and you can't un-wrong it.

The music. Sad country, still playing, still looping, tinny from the speaker in the kitchen. Playing for no one. Or playing for me — I'll never know which, and that question alone has cost me more sleep than I can count.

Usually Red and Adrian would sit by the sliding glass door in the morning, waiting for me to let them out. They weren't there.

I came all the way upstairs and saw the kitchen. Pantry doors ripped off their hinges and thrown across the room. Food everywhere. Empty bottles on every surface. Whatever happened at that party — whatever broke something in Riley that was already cracked — it happened while I slept through it fifteen feet below.

And then I saw why Red and Adrian weren't at the door.

They had found Riley.

At first I thought he'd passed out. He was leaned up against his bedroom door, and Riley passed out after parties sometimes — not usually against his door, but drunk people end up in strange places, and my brain was still running alternate explanations, still bargaining, still trying to hold onto the world where this was a hangover and not a catastrophe. Because as long as I hadn't checked, as long as I hadn't touched him, there was still a version of reality where he was fine. Schrödinger's roommate, slumped against a door, simultaneously okay and not okay until someone closes the distance and collapses the possibility into a single, irreversible fact.

I called the dogs over and let them out. Dropped everything. Ran to him.

It was pretty clear he was not okay.

I knew he was gone. My body had known since the landing. But there is a distance between knowing and accepting that your hands can occupy for a very long time, and mine were already moving — checking, undoing, trying, because doing nothing meant it was real and I wasn't ready for it to be real.

The strap went from around his neck, over the top of the door, and down to the doorknob on the other side. The length exactly right. Just enough tension. He knew exactly what he was doing. Planned it the way he planned a rescue — methodically, with the competence that made him good at his job. The same attention to detail that saved other people's lives, applied to ending his own.

It took me a while to figure out his rigging, but I got him down. I gave him chest compressions. Air went in and out of his mouth. There was nothing left. He was so far gone.

I thought I could save you, Riley.

»I'm not going to describe what that looks like. What someone looks like after. You either know or you don't, and if you don't, I hope you never learn. If you do — if your hands just went cold because you've been in that room, you've touched that skin, you've tried to push life back into something that's already empty — I don't need to describe it. Your hands remember. They'll always remember. They remember in the shower and on the steering wheel and in the middle of the night when you wake up pressing against something that isn't there.«

You were gone.

I didn't know what to do. The firefighter visits in elementary school — the ones where they come to your class and teach you what to do in emergencies — those paid off. They ingrained it deep enough that I barely remember making the call. I picked up Riley's phone first by accident, couldn't unlock it. Mine was by the sliding door where I'd dropped everything when I saw him. Riley had connected to my Bluetooth speaker from his phone, queued up the sad country playlist, and thrown his phone far enough away that he couldn't reach it. Even that was part of the plan. Remove your own ability to change your mind.

I told the operator what happened. Told them he was a firefighter. One of them. I don't know if it mattered. I just wanted them to know who they were coming for.

Police and fire were there in seconds. I was a mess. Couldn't stop crying. In the way.

One of the firefighters pulled me into the living room and asked if I had anyone I could call. I called Matt.

While I waited, my phone kept ringing. Cameron called — one of our mutual friends from the gym. He needed directions to the seminar.

I interrupted him.

"Riley's dead."

Silence. A few seconds that lasted a year.

"No way. What the fuck? What happened?"

I told him. I don't remember how that conversation ended.

---

I sat in the driveway when Matt showed up. We sat there while the paramedics wheeled Riley out on the gurney. We sat there as teammates started arriving. The word was out.

There's a specific quality to sitting in a driveway while something like this unfolds around you. Time stops doing the thing time is supposed to do. People come and go. Cars pull up and doors open and close and someone is talking to you and someone else is on the phone and the sky is the same sky it was yesterday and the concrete is the same concrete and everything is exactly the same except nothing will ever be the same, and you're sitting in the overlap between before and after, and both are closing, and you can't stop either one.

Adam pulled up with his truck. "Load the dogs up. You're staying with us."

No discussion. No options. No "let me know if you need anything" — that useless sentence people say when they don't know what else to say. Just: load the dogs up. That's the kind of person Adam is. That's the kind of people jiu-jitsu people are, mostly. When the world collapses, someone shows up with a truck and tells you what to do next because you've forgotten how to decide things.

The rest of that day was a blur. Adam's house. His family. What felt like the entire team cycling through. Everyone wanting to talk, offer condolences. Grand Master Flavio came to sit with us. The seminar was canceled — the seminar Riley had already decided not to attend, because he'd already decided something else entirely.

I wanted to ask Flavio how to do this. How to survive losing someone you love. His son was murdered in the '90s. His daughter died of cancer when she was young. He'd walked this road ahead of me and he was still standing and I needed to know how. But I couldn't form the words. I sat next to a man who had buried two children and couldn't figure out how to say the simplest thing: How do you keep going?

I don't know what he would have said. I wish I'd been able to ask.

For a few weeks, everyone wanted to hang out. Bars, shots dedicated to Riley, toasts, encouragement. It was irritating — not because they didn't mean well, but because every shot was another moment where I had to stand upright in public and act like I wasn't back on the landing every time I blinked. Every toast assumed I was moving through this, processing, doing the work of grief the way you're supposed to. I wasn't processing anything. I was frozen on three stairs turning a corner over and over in a loop not much different from the one playing sad country songs in the kitchen.

The invitations faded. I didn't want to go out anyway.

Riley's parents were devastated. They tried to rationalize his death any way their minds could find, bouncing every theory off me for verification. I don't think they realized how badly this destroyed me too. Of course they were devastated — he was their baby boy.

I was just his friend. Who found him. So they didn't have to.

I never went back into that house. Friends and family packed up my stuff. I was a few weeks into a new job and went back to work just to have somewhere to point my body that wasn't the living room or the driveway or the inside of my own head. They offered an open-ended leave. I turned it down. If I stopped moving, I'd be back on the landing.

It forced me into buying my own place. So that's good, I guess.

---

I've gone back and forth with my explanation of the choices Riley made in his final moments.

Why me?

It was clear he intended for me to find him. He knew my routine. Knew I'd wake up early. Knew I'd come upstairs, turn the corner, take those three steps. He positioned himself against his bedroom door — directly on the path between the stairs and the kitchen. There was no way to miss him.

But why? Why the fuck would you do that to me?

Was it to get back at me for my own attempt? He knew about mine — two years earlier, when I was thirty-three, when I'd gotten close enough to the edge that I could see over it. Did he think I'd understand? Some fucked-up version of solidarity that made it acceptable?

Was it because he didn't want his family to find him? Because he loved his parents too much to put that image in their heads? So he gave it to me instead, because I was — what? Tougher? Already cracked enough that one more fracture wouldn't show?

Did he not want to end up like his brother?

Or did he trust me? Trust me to be steady enough to make the calls, take care of the dogs, hold it together long enough for the professionals to arrive. Trust me to absorb it.

Here's the thing about being trusted with someone's worst moment: nobody asks you first. Nobody says, "I'm going to put something in your hands that will never come out, is that okay?" They just do it. They make a choice and you carry the consequences and there's no returning it, no setting it down, no transfer of custody. It's yours. Forever.

Fuck you too, bro. I mean that with every bit of love I have.

Fuck you for leaving. Fuck you for making me the one who found you. Fuck you for being so goddamn good at planning things that you planned your own exit with the same precision you used to plan a rescue. Fuck you for the sad country music. For throwing your phone so you couldn't change your mind. For the pantry doors ripped off the hinges — evidence of whatever storm tore through you that night while I slept through it because it wasn't a school night. Fuck you for not coming to the seminar. For being so happy that last week. For giving me three good nights on the couch — SportsCenter, arguing about highlights, dogs at our feet, everything normal, everything fine — and making me think you'd finally turned a corner when you'd already decided which corner it was.

And fuck you for not knowing how much you were loved. By everyone. Your funeral was packed. Standing room only. Firefighters in dress uniforms. Gym guys trying to hold it together. Every person in that room would have done anything — anything — to have had one more conversation. To ask the question nobody asked. To not brush off the signs. To go upstairs on a Friday night when it wasn't a school night and sit down and say, "Hey. You okay? Like, actually okay?"

We were supposed to be black belts together.

We were supposed to watch so many more fights. Take so many more trips. Drive too fast to Vegas and argue about SportsCenter and eat each other's cereal and grow old telling stories about the time I held your head over the bathtub while Poison Control told me what to watch for.

We were supposed to have so much more time.

---

That morning changed me in ways I'll probably never understand. Not because the understanding isn't available — but because understanding it would require going back to the landing and staying there long enough to study it, and my body won't let me. It goes there involuntarily — in dreams, in flashbacks, in the half-second assessments I do every time I walk past a closed door in my own house — but it won't stay voluntarily. It touches the memory and recoils like a hand on a hot surface. Quick enough to register. Not long enough to hold.

I think about it constantly. I have nightmares — not the metaphorical kind, not "it haunts me" in that soft way people use to describe things that are actually eating them alive. I mean I'm on the landing. Turning the corner. The dogs aren't at the door. The music is playing. I'm running. Chest compressions. Air in and out and nothing working. And the music — the fucking music I can't make stop, looping in my sleep the same way it looped in the kitchen — and I wake up with my hands pressing into the mattress the way they pressed against his chest, and for a few seconds I don't know which is real, the bed or the body, and that uncertainty is its own kind of hell.

It changed the way I see myself. Something shifted on that landing. Like the person I was before that morning died there too, and whoever came down those stairs was someone else wearing the same skin.

I don't feel safe anywhere. Not in my own house. Not in anyone's. I notice doors now — not in some poetic, metaphorical way. Literally. I notice them. The weight of them, whether they're open or closed, what might be behind them. I notice doorknobs. I notice the specific quality of sound that bleeds through a closed door — muffled, distorted, changed by the wood and the air and the distance between you and whatever's on the other side. And I notice music. If there's music playing somewhere in my house that I didn't put on, my whole system goes to alert. It's not a choice. It's not something I can reason through. It's firmware. Hardcoded into whatever my nervous system became on February 28, 2015. Music I didn't start equals something is wrong. That's the equation now. That's the permanent math.

Sometimes I walk through my house and pass a closed door — bathroom, closet, doesn't matter — and my body runs the program. Half a second. Is something wrong. Is the air different. Is there a sound. Are the dogs acting normal. Badge and Huck and Ripley — my dogs now, not my dogs then — they notice. Not the scan itself, but the aftermath. The way I pause too long before opening a door. The way I go still in a hallway, head tilted, listening for something I can't name. They look at me with that head-tilt thing dogs do, trying to read me. Then I open the door and it's nothing — coats, towels, the ordinary guts of an ordinary closet — and I exhale. And they exhale. And we move on.

But my body already ran the math. Already stood on the landing. Already turned the corner and counted the steps.

Every single time.

I don't know how to let go. Actually — it doesn't feel like I'm the one holding on. It feels like it's holding on to me. Like that morning wrapped itself around my nervous system and fused there, and no amount of time or therapy or distance will pry it loose. I'm not carrying it. It's carrying me — dragging me back to the landing every night, making me count the steps every morning, playing that playlist on repeat in the back of my skull in every quiet moment when there's nothing loud enough to drown it out.

It won't let me go.

»You don't have to have found someone to understand this. You just have to have lost someone in a way that rewired you. If you have — and you know if you have, because your body just told you — then you know what I mean. It's not a memory. It's a resident. It moved in and it's not leaving and some days it's quiet and some days it rearranges all the furniture while you sleep.«

Riley's gravesite is literally half a mile from my house. I drive past the road that leads to it almost every day.

I've never been.

I don't have a reason that makes sense. Maybe going there would mean closing the door I've been standing in front of for eleven years — hand on the knob, holding onto the version of reality where he's still somewhere. Not here, but not gone. Just away. Maybe as long as I don't visit, I can keep him on the landing. In the before. In the world where the door hasn't opened yet and both possibilities still exist and I haven't had to choose which one is real.

Or maybe I just can't stand the idea of visiting him in the ground when the last time I saw him was on the floor of his own house, leaned against his bedroom door, with sad country music playing for no one.

We were supposed to be black belts together.
