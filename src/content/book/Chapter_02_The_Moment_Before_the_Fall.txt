Chapter 2 - The Moment Before the Fall
The music was wrong.

That's the thing I remember first. Not the silence you'd expect, not the absence — the presence. Sad country songs bleeding through a closed door on a Saturday morning in February, tinny and small from a Bluetooth speaker, the kind of sound that shouldn't have meant anything except it meant everything. Because Riley didn't listen to sad country music at ten in the morning. Riley listened to whatever was loud and stupid and made him laugh while he cooked eggs he'd inevitably burn. Riley blasted hip-hop in the shower so loud I'd bang on the wall. Riley sang off-key to classic rock while he cleaned his gear after a shift, still smelling like smoke and adrenaline, dropping ash on the kitchen counter while I told him he was an animal and he told me to relax.

Riley didn't play sad country songs on loop. Nobody plays sad country songs on loop unless they're performing heartbreak for an audience or unless something has gone very, very wrong.

My hand was on the doorknob. I know this because I remember the temperature of it — cold, which was strange, because it was his room and the heat was on and doorknobs in heated houses shouldn't be that cold. But maybe that's a detail my brain invented after the fact. Maybe the doorknob was room temperature and my hand was just cold and my body had already started cataloging everything wrong before my mind could catch up. Trauma does that — encodes details that may or may not have existed, stitches sensation into memory with such precision that you can't tell which threads are real and which ones your nervous system manufactured to fill the gaps in a story too big for linear recall.

Red was behind me. Or maybe it was Adrian. One of the dogs was behind me in the hallway, and the other was somewhere else in the house, and I remember the one behind me making this sound — not a whine exactly, not a bark, something in between. Something I'd never heard before. The kind of sound a dog makes when it knows something you don't. Or something you do know but haven't admitted to yourself yet. Dogs don't lie about these things. They don't have the capacity for denial that we do, that elaborate human machinery for looking at something terrible and thinking maybe it's fine, maybe I'm overreacting, maybe the sad country music at ten in the morning is just a phase.

The hallway was maybe fifteen feet long. I'd walked it a thousand times — to the bathroom, to the kitchen, to knock on his door and ask if he wanted to grab food or if he'd already eaten the last of the cereal again (he always ate the last of the cereal). Fifteen feet. Four seconds of walking, five if you're not in a hurry. But that morning it felt like a corridor in a dream, the kind that stretches as you move through it, where the door gets farther away the closer you get. The carpet was the same carpet. The walls were the same walls. The light coming through the window at the end of the hall was the same late-winter light, pale and noncommittal, the kind of light that doesn't warm anything, just illuminates it. Everything was the same except everything was different, and the difference lived in the music and the cold knob and the dog behind me and the way my body had gone heavy and electric at the same time.

I didn't open it right away. That's the part I keep coming back to. I stood there with my hand on that knob and I listened to the music — some guy singing about whiskey and headlights and someone who left — and I knew.

Not knew like a thought. Knew like a bruise. Knew the way your body knows it's about to throw up before your stomach even turns. Every cell in me was already reacting to information my conscious brain hadn't processed yet. My knees did this thing where they didn't buckle but they — loosened, I guess. Like the structural integrity of my legs became a suggestion rather than a fact. My chest compressed. Not pain, exactly. Pressure. Like the air in the hallway had thickened into something you had to push through rather than simply breathe.

I stood there for — I don't know. Thirty seconds. A minute. A year. Time had already stopped doing the thing time is supposed to do, which is move forward in a way that makes sense. Instead it pooled. Collected in that hallway like water with nowhere to drain. And part of me — the part that was still functioning enough to form something like a coherent thought — was bargaining. Running alternate explanations. He fell asleep with the speaker on. He's in a mood. He had a bad shift. He's just sitting in there, being dramatic, the way he got sometimes after calls that went sideways, when the person they were trying to save didn't make it and he'd come home quiet and wrong and need a day before he was Riley again.

But the dog behind me wasn't buying any of that. And honestly, neither was I.

And the whole time, the music kept playing.

---

Here's what nobody tells you about the moment before everything changes: your body runs the math before your brain even opens the textbook. There's no conscious calculation, no logical deduction, no rational assessment of available evidence. There's just this — this knowing that arrives in your marrow and your gut and the base of your skull all at once, this animal certainty that bypasses every higher function you've spent your whole life developing and says: something is wrong. Something is already wrong. Something has been wrong while you were sleeping and now you're awake and it's still wrong and you can't un-wrong it.

I've had that feeling more times than seems statistically fair.

I had it at the Fiji airport at 3 AM when I was twenty years old, standing in humidity so thick it felt solid, waiting for missionaries who were supposed to pick me up, slowly realizing that the mission I'd prepared for — the one in Fiji, the one I'd studied for, the one I'd spent months in California getting ready for — wasn't my actual mission. That I was going to Vanuatu. Which, at the time, I couldn't have pointed to on a map if you'd offered me a thousand dollars and a compass. My body knew before anyone told me. Something in the wrongness of the air, the strangeness of the dark, the way none of the details matched what I'd been promised. My stomach dropped and my skin prickled and some ancient part of my brain started quietly rearranging everything I thought I knew about what was coming. I was standing in an airport in the South Pacific feeling the floor tilt under me, and fifteen years later I'd be standing in a hallway in Utah feeling the exact same thing, and the distance between those two moments would collapse into nothing.

I had it the night my phone rang at 2 AM and I saw Justin's girlfriend's name on the screen. Nobody calls at 2 AM with good news. This is a universal truth, one of the few things every human being on earth understands regardless of language or culture or religion: a phone ringing at 2 AM is a door with music behind it. The phone hadn't even finished its first full ring and my body was already in the hallway with Riley, already standing outside that door, already running the math on another ending I couldn't stop. My hand shook picking up the phone the same way it had shaken on the doorknob. Same hand. Same tremor. Different year.

I had it scrolling through Instagram on an ordinary afternoon — one of those nothing days where you're just killing time, thumb moving on autopilot, barely registering what you're seeing — and then a post from Dani's brother that didn't make sense until it did, and then made too much sense, and then the floor wasn't under me anymore. Not a phone call. Not a hallway. Just a screen and a thumb and a post that rearranged the entire architecture of what I thought was true. She was gone, and I'd been texting her check-in — "You alive?" — and she hadn't answered, and I'd assumed she was busy or ghosting or being Dani, and the whole time she was already gone.

*»You've had it too. That specific moment when information arrives in your body before your brain. When your hands go cold or your vision narrows or your hearing changes — everything either too loud or too quiet — and you think, irrationally, if I just don't look, if I just don't answer, if I just don't open this door, then whatever's on the other side won't be real. As if reality needs your participation to exist. As if you could keep someone alive just by refusing to learn they're dead.«*

But reality doesn't wait for you to be ready. That's maybe the cruelest part.

---

The thing about anticipation — real anticipation, not the butterflies-before-a-first-date kind but the kind that comes from having been destroyed by what's behind enough doors — is that it rewires you. Permanently. You become a different machine. Your operating system gets an update you didn't consent to, and suddenly you're running threat-detection software on every interaction, every silence, every unanswered text, every closed door in every house you'll ever walk through for the rest of your life.

Riley was a firefighter. He ran toward the things the rest of us ran from. He'd seen shit I can't imagine — or maybe I can imagine it, that's the problem, maybe I imagine it too well and too often — and he carried it the way firefighters carry it. Silently. Stoically. Wrapped in dark humor and cheap beer and an unspoken pact with the other guys at the station that none of them would ever admit how bad it really was. They'd joke about it. Make it small. Fit it into a format that wouldn't break them. He saved people for a living. That was his whole thing. He pulled people out of burning buildings and mangled cars and all the other catastrophes that happen while the rest of us are eating dinner and watching TV. He saved people.

And he planned it. That's the part that rearranges everything you think you know about seeing things coming. He didn't do it in a moment of desperation or a blind, impulsive lurch toward ending. He set up the Bluetooth speaker. He chose the playlist — and I've wondered about this more than is probably healthy: how long did it take him to make that playlist? Was it days? Weeks? Did he add songs one at a time, curating his own soundtrack the way you'd curate a playlist for a road trip? Did he listen through it first, testing it, making sure the order was right? This is the kind of thing I think about at 3 AM, the kind of question that doesn't have an answer and never will and still demands to be asked.

Sad country songs — the kind where guys sing about loss with a twang that makes sorrow sound almost beautiful, almost bearable, like grief set to a melody could somehow be domesticated. He queued them up and set them to loop and then he —

He planned it the way he planned a rescue. Methodically. With the competence that made him good at his job. The same attention to detail that saved lives applied to ending his own. And I was on the other side of the wall, asleep, fifteen feet away, while the music played.

I keep getting stuck on that. The music. The deliberateness of it. How he chose what would be playing when someone found him, and that someone was me, and the songs were chosen with care by a person who was being careful about his own ending while I slept through it. And I wonder if the music was kindness or cruelty or both — if he thought the songs would somehow soften what I was about to see, or if the music was just for him, one last thing that was just for him, and I wasn't part of the calculation at all.

Or maybe I did hear it. Maybe that's why I woke up that morning feeling wrong before I had any reason to feel wrong. Maybe the music had been seeping through the wall all night, threading itself into my dreams, and my body was processing the wrongness of it even before I opened my eyes. Maybe my body was standing in the hallway long before the rest of me got there.

---

February 28, 2015. A Saturday. The last Saturday that would ever mean just Saturday.

I've thought about this so many times that the memory has developed layers, like geological strata. There's the surface memory — the facts, the sequence, the sensory data. Then there are the layers underneath: the interpretations, the re-examinations, the thousands of times I've replayed those seconds between hand-on-knob and door-opening, looking for something I missed, something I could have caught, some version of events where I open the door and he's just sitting there, listening to music, and I say "dude, sad country? really?" and he laughs and says something about a girl and we go get breakfast and it's a Saturday and not The Saturday.

But that version only exists in the layers. Not on the surface. On the surface there's just the knob and the cold and the music and the dog making a sound I'd never heard before.

And my body, knowing.

The thing that fucks me up the most — more than what I saw when I opened that door, more than the calls I had to make after, more than the way Red wouldn't leave the hallway for hours, more than the weeks and months that followed where that house stopped being a home and became a crime scene I was still living in — is the in-between. The fifteen feet between my room and his. The time it took to walk those fifteen feet that morning versus every other morning. How a distance I'd covered thousands of times without thinking became, in those few seconds, the longest walk of my life. How the hallway itself seemed to understand what was at the end of it and stretched itself out, giving me more time, more seconds, more of that in-between space where he was still alive because I hadn't opened the door yet.

As long as I stood in that hallway with my hand on the knob, Schrödinger's roommate was still a possibility. Still alive. Still just listening to weird music on a Saturday morning. The moment I opened the door, one reality collapsed and another one took its place, and I could never go back. And I knew that. Standing there. I knew I was in the last seconds of a world, and the next world was already waiting on the other side of that door, patient and permanent, and all I had to do to enter it was turn my wrist.

I think some part of me was trying to stay. Not in the hallway — in the world. In the version of reality where Riley was alive and playing weird music and I was about to give him shit about it. Some part of me was gripping that doorknob not to open the door but to hold onto the last solid thing in a universe that was about to become very, very liquid.

But you can't stay in the hallway forever. You can try. God, you can try. But eventually your hand turns, and the door opens, and the world you were holding onto evaporates like it was never solid at all.

---

The moments before accumulate. That's something I didn't understand until I'd collected too many of them to ignore.

You think each one is singular. Each crisis, each phone call, each doorknob — its own isolated event with its own discrete beginning and end. But they're not. They stack. Your body keeps a running total, and each new moment before the fall gets added to the ledger, and the weight compounds. The third time your phone rings at 2 AM, your body doesn't just react to that call — it reacts to every call, every door, every hallway it's ever walked down knowing something terrible waited at the end.

I felt it in the MTC before Vanuatu, lying awake in a room full of sleeping missionaries, my body buzzing with a certainty I couldn't name — that whatever was coming wasn't what I'd been told, that the Fiji I'd prepared for was a fiction, that I was heading somewhere I couldn't imagine and no amount of preparation would be enough. I was twenty years old and my body was already keeping a ledger I wouldn't be able to read for another fifteen years.

I felt it sitting in a hospital room with Justin, watching him try to be the person he was before the cancer and the opiates and the slow erasure of everything that made him Justin. Each visit, my body ran the same program — the hallway program, the doorknob program, the this-is-wrong-and-you-know-it program. My hands would start shaking before I even got out of the car in the hospital parking lot. Not from cold. From recognition. My body recognized the shape of what was happening even when my brain was still insisting this time would be different, that the next treatment would work, that Justin was tough enough, stubborn enough, too much of an asshole to die.

I felt it the night before my shoulder surgery, standing in my bathroom, hands trembling — not because of the surgery but because my body had learned to associate the night-before-something with catastrophe. Every night before had become Riley's Friday night. Every tomorrow had become a Saturday morning. My body couldn't tell the difference between "tomorrow the surgeon will fix your shoulder" and "tomorrow your world will end." Same trembling. Same pooling of time. Same hallway feeling, even standing still.

The body doesn't differentiate between the moments. It doesn't rank them by severity or sort them by outcome. It just files them all under the same heading: BRACE. And after enough entries in that file, bracing becomes your neutral state. You walk around braced. You sleep braced. You have conversations and eat meals and pet your dogs with this low-grade tension running through you like a current, always humming, always ready, always waiting for the next door with music behind it.

*»When was the last time your shoulders weren't up around your ears? Can you even remember what your body felt like before it learned to expect the worst? That thing you do — that constant scanning, that involuntary assessment of every room for exits and every silence for danger — you think it's just who you are. It's not. It's who you became. There's a difference, and it matters, even if it doesn't change anything.«*

---

I think about doors a lot now. Not in some poetic, metaphorical way — literally. I notice doors. The weight of them, the sound they make, whether they're open or closed, what might be behind them. I notice doorknobs — their temperature, their resistance, whether they turn easy or stiff. I notice hallways. I notice the specific quality of sound that comes through a closed door — muffled, distorted, changed by the wood and the air and the distance between you and whatever's on the other side.

Mostly I notice music. If there's music playing somewhere in my house that I didn't put on, my whole system goes to alert. It's not a choice. It's not something I can talk myself out of. It's firmware. Hardcoded into whatever my nervous system became on February 28, 2015. Music I didn't start playing = something is wrong. That's the equation now. That's the permanent math.

Sometimes I'll be walking through my house and I'll pass a closed door — a bathroom, a closet, doesn't matter — and my body will do the thing. The quick inventory. The micro-assessment. Is something wrong. Is the air different. Is there a sound that shouldn't be there. Are the dogs acting normal. Is the light right. Is the temperature of the doorknob what it should be.

It takes maybe half a second. Most of the time I don't even consciously register it anymore. But it's there. Running in the background like antivirus software that never stops scanning. The hallway program. The doorknob program. The forever program.

Badge and Huck and Ripley — my dogs now, not my dogs then — they notice. Not the scanning itself, but the results of it. The way I'll pause for a beat too long before opening a door. The way I'll go still in a hallway, head tilted, listening for something I can't name. They'll look at me with that head-tilt thing dogs do, trying to read the situation, trying to figure out if this is a thing they should be worried about or just another one of the weird things their human does that doesn't involve food. And then I'll open the door and it'll be nothing — a closet full of coats, a bathroom with a dripping faucet — and I'll exhale, and they'll exhale, and we'll move on.

But my body already ran the math. Already stood in that hallway. Already put its hand on the knob and listened for music that shouldn't be playing.

Every single time.

---

Here's the thing I want to say about the moment before the fall, the thing that took me years to understand and that I still don't fully have language for: the fall isn't the worst part. Everyone thinks the fall is the worst part. The impact. The finding. The knowing for certain. But the fall has a kind of horrible clarity to it — once you're in it, there's nothing to do but fall. The decisions are over. The anticipation is over. You're just in it.

The moment before is where the real damage lives. Because in the moment before, you're still holding everything. The hope and the dread. The bargaining and the knowing. The world where Riley is alive and the world where he isn't, superimposed on top of each other, occupying the same fifteen-foot hallway, and you have to carry both of them for as long as you can stand in front of that door without opening it.

That weight — the weight of two simultaneous realities — is heavier than any single truth. I know this because I've carried both. I've stood in the hallway and I've opened the door and I can tell you with absolute certainty that the hallway was worse. The hallway is always worse. Because in the hallway, you still have to choose. You still have to turn the knob. You still have to participate in the destruction of the world you're trying to hold onto.

After the door opens, the world is just the world. Terrible. Broken. But singular. One reality. One set of facts. One direction to fall in.

It's the before that splits you.

---

I don't know how to end this chapter. Which feels right, actually, because the moment before the fall doesn't really end either. It just — converts. Transforms into something else. The before becomes the during becomes the after, and then the after becomes the new before, because there's always another door, another hallway, another moment when your body knows something your brain refuses to accept.

February 28, 2015. Saturday morning. A hallway. A doorknob. Country music on a Bluetooth speaker, playing for no one.

(Playing for me, I guess. Though I'll never know if he thought about that. If, while he was setting it up, he thought about who would hear it first. If the music was for himself or for whoever came after. I'll never know. That's one of the doors I can't open, no matter how many times I stand in front of it.)

The dogs are asleep at my feet right now. The house is quiet. No music I didn't put on. No sounds that don't belong. Just the refrigerator humming, the heater clicking, the ordinary noises of a house being a house and not a hallway.

And still my body listens. Still my body waits. Still, somewhere in the wiring that got rewritten on a Saturday morning eleven years ago, there's a program running that will never fully shut down.

Not because I want it to. Not because I choose it. Because my body walked a fifteen-foot hallway that became infinite, put its hand on a cold doorknob, and learned something it will never unlearn:

That the moment before the fall is where you actually live now. Not as a visitor. As a resident. Furniture arranged, mail forwarded, dogs on the couch.

And the music plays on.
