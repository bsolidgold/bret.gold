Chapter 2 - The Moment Before the Fall

I met Riley in 2006 when I started training at Absolute MMA. He was nineteen. I was twenty-six, which felt like a bigger gap back then than it probably was. I'd already served a mission in the South Pacific, done four years in the Air Force, lived in Germany, had an engagement blow up in my face, and was trying to figure out what to do with myself. Riley was just getting started — hadn't figured out what to do with himself either, but in that way nineteen-year-olds haven't, where the whole thing is still ahead of you and that's exciting instead of exhausting. We were both white belts, both brand new to jiu-jitsu, both terrible at it in the same enthusiastic, know-nothing way. The jiu-jitsu world was like that — easy to make friends from all walks of life and all ages. Something about voluntarily learning to be uncomfortable together strips away the normal barriers.

It wasn't until Riley turned twenty-one that we became really good friends.

There were several nights at the bars and clubs, but one sticks out like a sore thumb from the others. We went to Lumpy's to watch the UFC fights with some friends from the gym. I don't remember who fought or very much about that night, but Riley got a lot more drunk than he usually did. A lot more. I was the DD, so I drove him home.

Long story short — that was the start of a long night making sure he didn't aspirate on his own vomit while he slept. We ended up in his bathroom, him puking in the bathtub because the toilet was too small a target. Poison Control was called. Riley eventually slept it off.

I found out later that one of our "friends" had sexually assaulted him that night. Which explained the drunkenness. Which would explain a lot of things that came later, actually, but neither of us had that language yet. Nobody tells you that trauma has a fuse, and sometimes the fuse is years long, and sometimes you don't even know it's burning because the person carrying it is too good at being fine.

That bathroom night was the start of a pretty solid friendship. I don't know why, exactly. Maybe surviving something ugly together just does that — makes you family in a way that normal friendship can't. He knew I'd hold his head over the bathtub without judging him. I knew he'd do the same. That was enough.

There were many more nights after that. Fight nights. Road trips to Vegas doing 120 in his car just because we could, just because Riley was behind the wheel and he had that grin — the one that said he knew exactly how fast he was going and didn't care. Wendover. Camping and fishing. Just hanging out at the house doing nothing in particular. There have been SO many UFC fights we should have watched together. So many more trips we should have taken.

»You have someone like this. Had someone. Someone you didn't earn through effort or compatibility or shared values — someone who just showed up in your life and fit. The kind of friendship that doesn't need explaining because there's nothing to explain. You just are. And either you still have them, in which case stop reading this and text them right now, or you don't, in which case you already know where this is going.«

We became roommates around 2011, when we both went through breakups at the same time. We both got our purple belts in jiu-jitsu shortly after. Riley was established as a firefighter by then — he'd tested for and become an engineer. It was his dream job. He saved people for a living. That was his whole identity: the guy who runs toward the thing the rest of us run from. He'd come home smelling like smoke and adrenaline, drop ash on the kitchen counter, and act like it was no big deal, because that's what firefighters do. They pack everything into dark humor and station-house bullshit and an unspoken pact with the other guys that none of them will ever admit how bad it really was.

He wasn't sure if he could afford to buy a house on his own. I was renting an apartment. So I told him I'd pay half his mortgage if I could use half of the house. No-brainer.

We were pretty awesome roommates. I chalk it up to Riley's easy-going nature. He burned eggs every morning. Blasted hip-hop in the shower so loud I'd bang on the wall. Sang off-key to classic rock while he cleaned his gear. We'd eat the last of each other's cereal and argue about whose turn it was to buy more. Normal shit. Roommate shit. The kind of unremarkable daily life that you don't realize is the best part until it's gone.

You were more than just a friend, Riley. We were like brothers. We had each other's backs. For real.

---

After his breakup, Riley's drinking changed.

He went from a happy-go-lucky drunk — always buying people drinks, always the life of the party — to a sad and depressed drunk. I should have seen the warning signs. A lot of his drinking happened when I wasn't around. He had a ton of friends from the fire station who he'd go out with. I only made it out when his schedule lined up with my weekends. Then most of the drinking started to be at the house. Sometimes by himself. The parties would get pretty out of control, but the guys were always cool when I had to remind them I was trying to sleep — it was a school night.

But between the parties, there was something else. The nights he'd be sitting in the living room alone, TV on but not watching, beer in hand but not really drinking. Just existing in that gray space between fine and not fine that people like us inhabit when we don't want to burden anyone with the truth.

I should have seen it. The transition from party drunk to alone drunk. The way his laughter got thinner. The way he started staying home more. I should have asked. But I did what we all do — I filed it under "he's going through something" and trusted him to come out the other side because Riley always came out the other side. He was a firefighter. He was tough. He was fine.

»You're thinking about someone right now. The one you didn't ask. The one where you looked at the warning signs and chose the comfortable explanation. I'm not pointing fingers — I'm standing right next to you in it. We're all complicit in the fiction that the people we love are fine, because the truth is too heavy to carry for two.«

---

The last party Riley had wasn't on a school night. I didn't have to remind anyone that I needed to sleep.

That whole week stands out from the rest.

In hindsight, it was strange. We were both pretty busy, but we found time to watch SportsCenter together before bed at least three nights that week. Just sitting on the couch, dogs at our feet, arguing about highlights. Normal. Except that Riley seemed way more happy than usual. Lighter. Like something had settled.

I thought it was because we had a seminar with Grand Master Flavio Behring that weekend. Flavio was a legend — a coral belt, one of the most respected figures in Brazilian jiu-jitsu — and getting mat time with him was always a big deal. Riley loved that stuff.

Then I found out he wasn't planning on going. That was odd.

I didn't think about it. I should have thought about it.

That's the thing about the moment before the fall — sometimes it doesn't look like a moment at all. Sometimes it looks like three good nights of SportsCenter and a friend who seems happier than he has in months. Sometimes the warning signs look like relief. Because they are. The person who's already decided stops fighting. The burden lifts. They seem better because they've stopped carrying the weight of the choice that hasn't been made yet. The choice is made, and what's left is something that looks, from the outside, like peace.

He had his usual impromptu party Friday night. I went to bed early so I could wake up in time for the seminar. I woke up a few times during the night to party noises. Brushed them off because it wasn't a school night.

I wish I would have gone upstairs anyway.

---

The next morning I got up and went through my routine as usual. Kept the dogs in my room while I did my bathroom stuff. Got dressed. Gathered my jiu-jitsu stuff for the seminar. Went upstairs to let the dogs out for the day.

Everything changed when I turned the corner on the landing before the final three steps upstairs.

There was music still playing from my Bluetooth speaker in the kitchen. Sad country songs, tinny and looping. The kind of music Riley didn't listen to. Not at ten in the morning. Not ever. Riley blasted hip-hop. Sang off-key to classic rock. He didn't play sad country songs on loop.

Nobody plays sad country songs on loop unless something has gone very, very wrong.

Usually Red and Adrian would sit by the sliding glass door waiting for me to let them out. They weren't there.

I came all the way upstairs and saw the kitchen. The pantry doors had been ripped off their hinges and thrown across the room. Food everywhere. Empty bottles on every surface. Riley had been very upset about something the night before. Something that happened while I slept through it.

And then I saw why Red and Adrian weren't at the door.

They had found Riley.

At first I thought he'd passed out. He was leaned up against his bedroom door, and Riley passed out after parties sometimes. I called the dogs over and let them out the sliding door. Dropped everything and ran over to him.

It was pretty clear he was not okay.

I didn't want to believe it. I knew. But I didn't want to believe it, and there is a distance between knowing and believing that your body can occupy for a very long time if it has to. It's the same distance as three stairs on a landing or the space between a hand and a doorknob. It's not far. But it's everything.

I tried to help anyway.

The strap went from around his neck, over the top of the door, and down to the doorknob on the other side. The length was exactly right so that he could sit down with enough tension to do what he intended. He knew exactly what he was doing. He planned it the way he planned a rescue — methodically, with the same competence that made him good at his job. The same attention to detail that saved other people's lives, applied to ending his own.

It took me a while to figure out his rigging, but I eventually did and got him down. I gave him chest compressions. Air went in and out of his mouth. There was nothing left. He was so far gone.

I thought I could save you, Riley.

There was nothing I could do.

You were gone.

»I'm not going to describe what that looks like. What someone looks like after. You either know or you don't, and if you don't, I hope you never learn. If you do — if your hands just went cold because you've been in that room, you've touched that skin, you've tried to breathe life into something that's already gone — I don't need to describe it. Your hands remember. They'll always remember.«

I didn't know what to do. All the visits by the firefighters in elementary school paid off — they ingrained in me what to do in an emergency well enough that I barely remember calling 911. I picked up Riley's phone by accident first because I couldn't get it to unlock. Mine was over by the sliding door where I'd dropped everything. Riley had connected to my Bluetooth speaker from his phone, put on the sad country playlist, and thrown his phone far enough away that he couldn't reach it. Even that was planned. Take away your own ability to change your mind.

I told the operator what happened. I told them he was a firefighter. One of them. I don't know if it mattered. I just wanted them to know who they were coming for.

Police and fire were there in what seemed like seconds. I was a mess. Couldn't stop crying. I was in the way.

One of the firefighters pulled me into the living room and asked if I had anyone I could call. I called Matt.

While I waited, my phone kept ringing. Cameron called — he needed directions to the seminar. I interrupted him.

"Riley's dead."

Silence. A few seconds that lasted a year.

"No way. What the fuck? What happened?"

I told him. I don't remember how that conversation ended.

---

I sat in the driveway when Matt showed up. We sat there while the paramedics wheeled Riley out on the gurney and put him in the ambulance. We sat there as teammates who knew where we lived started showing up. The word was out.

Adam pulled up with his truck and said, "Load the dogs up. You're staying with us."

No discussion. No options. Load the dogs up. That's the kind of person Adam is. That's the kind of people jiu-jitsu people are, mostly. When the world collapses, someone shows up with a truck.

The rest of that day was a blur. Adam's house, his family, what felt like the entire team coming through. Everyone wanting to talk and offer condolences. Grand Master Flavio came over to sit with us. The seminar I'd woken up early for — the one Riley had decided not to attend — was obviously canceled.

I wanted to ask Flavio how to deal with this. The death of someone close to you. His son was murdered in the '90s. His daughter died of cancer when she was young. He'd survived what I was only just starting. But I couldn't form the words. I sat next to a man who had buried two children and couldn't figure out how to ask, "How do you keep going?"

I don't know what he would have said. I wish I could have asked.

For a few weeks everyone wanted to hang out with me. Bars, shots dedicated to Riley, toasts, words of encouragement. It was irritating. Not because they didn't mean well — they did. But every toast was a reminder. Every shot was another moment I had to stand in a bar and pretend I wasn't back on the landing. Every word of encouragement assumed I was further along in processing this than I was, which was not at all.

The invitations faded. I didn't want to go out and deal with everyone anyway. That's how grief works in social circles — the swarm, then the thinning, then you're alone with it. Which is what you wanted and also what you feared.

Riley's parents were devastated. They tried to rationalize his death any way their minds could think of, bouncing every theory off me for verification. I don't think they realized how badly this devastated me. Of course they were devastated — he was their baby boy. I was just his friend. Who found him. So they didn't have to.

I never went back into his house. Friends and family packed up all my stuff. I was only a few weeks into a new job and went back to work just to distract from everything. They offered an open-ended leave of absence. I just needed to not be still. If I stopped moving, I was back on the landing.

It forced me into buying my own place. So that's good, I guess.

---

I've gone back and forth with my explanation of the choices Riley made in his final moments.

Why me?

It was clear he intended for me to be the one to find him. He knew my routine. Knew I'd wake up early for the seminar. Knew I'd come upstairs. He positioned himself against his bedroom door — directly on the path between the stairs and the kitchen. There was no way to miss him.

But why? Why the fuck would you do that to me?

Was it to get back at me for my own attempt? He knew about mine. Did he think I'd understand — some fucked-up version of solidarity that made it okay?

Was it because he didn't want his family to find him? Because he loved his parents too much to put that on them? So he gave it to me instead — because I was what? Tougher? More expendable? Already broken enough that one more thing wouldn't matter?

Did he not want to end up like his brother?

Or did he trust me? Trust me to handle it. To call the right people. To take care of the dogs. To be the one who could absorb it without shattering completely.

Fuck you too, bro. I mean that with every bit of love I have.

Fuck you for leaving. Fuck you for making me the one who found you. Fuck you for being so good at planning things that you planned your own exit with the same precision you used to plan a rescue. Fuck you for the sad country music. Fuck you for throwing your phone so you couldn't change your mind. Fuck you for the pantry doors. Fuck you for not coming to the seminar. Fuck you for being so happy that last week — for giving me three good nights of SportsCenter and making me think things were looking up when you'd already decided.

And fuck you for not knowing how much you were loved. By everyone. Your funeral was packed. Standing room only. Firefighters in dress uniforms. Gym guys trying not to cry. Every person in that room would have done anything — anything — to have had one more conversation. To ask the question nobody asked. To not brush off the signs. To go upstairs on a Friday night when it wasn't a school night and sit down next to you and say, "Hey. Are you okay? Like, actually okay?"

We were supposed to be black belts together.

We were supposed to watch so many more fights. Take so many more trips. Drive too fast to Vegas and argue about SportsCenter and eat each other's cereal and grow old telling stories about the time I held your head over the bathtub at three in the morning while Poison Control told me what to watch for.

We were supposed to have so much more time.

---

That morning changed me in ways I'll probably never fully understand.

I think about it constantly. I have nightmares — not metaphorical ones, not "it haunts me" in the vague way people say that. I mean I close my eyes and I'm on the landing. Turning the corner. The dogs aren't at the door. I'm running across the kitchen. Chest compressions, air going in and out, nothing working, and the music still playing. The fucking music still playing, and I can't make it stop.

It changed the way I see myself. Something shifted on that landing. Like the person I was before that morning died there too, and whoever came down those stairs was someone else wearing the same body.

I don't feel safe anywhere. Not in my house. Not in anyone's house. Every closed door is a question now. Every unexpected sound is evidence. I notice doors — the weight of them, whether they're open or closed, what might be behind them. I notice music. If there's music playing somewhere in my house that I didn't put on, my whole system goes to alert. It's not a choice. It's not something I can reason my way out of. It's firmware. Music I didn't start equals something is wrong. That's the equation now. That's the permanent math.

Sometimes I walk through my house and pass a closed door — bathroom, closet, doesn't matter — and my body does the thing. The quick scan. Is something wrong. Is the air different. Is there a sound that shouldn't be there. Are the dogs acting normal. It takes half a second. Most of the time I don't even consciously notice. But it's there. Running in the background. The landing program. The forever program.

I don't know how to let go. Actually — it doesn't feel like I'm the one holding on. It feels like it's holding on to me. Like that morning wrapped itself around my nervous system and fused there, and no amount of time or therapy or distance will pry it loose.

It won't let me go.

»You don't have to have found someone to understand this. You just have to have lost someone in a way that rewired you. If you have — and you know if you have, because your body just told you — then you know what I mean when I say it won't let go. It's not a memory. It's a resident. It moved in and it's not leaving and some days it's quiet and some days it rearranges all the furniture while you sleep.«

Riley's gravesite is literally half a mile from my house. I drive past the road that leads to it almost every day.

I've never been.

I don't have a reason that makes sense. Maybe standing there would mean admitting that the version of reality where Riley is still somewhere — not here, but not gone, just away — is finally, completely over. Maybe as long as I don't go, some part of me can keep him on the landing. In the before. In the version where the door hasn't opened yet.

Or maybe I just can't stand the idea of visiting him in the ground when the last time I saw him was on the floor of his own house, leaned against his bedroom door, with sad country music playing for no one.

We were supposed to be black belts together.
