Preface
Here’s the thing about minds: they don’t stay quiet. They don’t follow rules. They don’t respect the boundaries between past and present, between what happened and what might have happened, between what we can bear to remember and what we can’t stop remembering.  Your brain is already doing it, isn’t it? Even as you read these words, there’s another voice in there—the one that never stops, the one that knows where all your bruises are, the one that remembers everything you wish you could forget. The one that’s about to remind you of that thing you did, that person you lost, that moment when everything changed and you didn’t move fast enough to catch it.
»You know exactly which voice I mean, don’t you? The one that’s speaking right now, in that tone that feels like fingers pressing on a bruise. The one that knows how your hands shake sometimes for no reason, how your throat closes around words you can’t say, how your skin remembers things your mind tries to forget.«
I wish someone had told me it wasn’t just me. That we all carry this second consciousness, this witness, this keeper of every moment we’ve tried to bury. That it’s not madness or weakness or failure. That it’s just what minds do when they’re trying to heal something that won’t heal clean.  That voice isn’t me. That voice is you. I’m just the one writing it down.  And maybe, if we’re both very quiet, we can hear what it’s trying to tell us:
About survival.
About memory.
About the way trauma lives in our bodies like a second language we never meant to learn.  About how sometimes healing looks like four simple words on a whiteboard, like basic tasks that become mountains, like victories so small they’re invisible to everyone but us.
»You’re nodding. I see you. I am you. And I wish I could tell you it gets easier. But what I can tell you is this: you’re not alone in that darkness. We’re all here, carrying our own voices, learning to speak their language, learning to hold their truth.«
This isn’t a guide. There are no instructions for this. No map through the territory of your own mind. But maybe, in sharing how I navigate mine, you’ll feel less alone in yours.
 Maybe that’s enough.  Maybe that’s everything.  Maybe that’s where healing starts.

Not in silence. But in recognition. In witness. In finally being heard.
